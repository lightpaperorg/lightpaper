# The Production Function, Rewritten

## How Word Machines, Thought Machines, Doing Machines, and Perceiving Machines Are Transforming the Oldest Equation in Economics

---

Everything an economy produces — every good shipped, every service rendered, every line of code committed — can be described by the oldest equation in economics: the production function. Output is a function of capital and labour. *Y = f(K, L)*. That equation has survived two and a half centuries of industrial revolutions without fundamental modification. It needs rewriting now.

This paper argues that artificial intelligence is not a single technology but four distinct types of machines, each analogous to a different category of industrial revolution machinery, and each transforming the production function in specific, measurable ways. These are *word machines* (language models), *thought machines* (reasoning systems), *doing machines* (autonomous agents), and *perceiving machines* (computer vision and robotics). Together, they represent the first time in history that capital can substitute for cognitive labour — not just physical labour — and they demand a new production function to describe what is happening to individual workers, firms, and entire economies.

But to understand where we are going, we must first understand where we have been.

---

## Part I: The Production Function Through History

### The Equation That Explains Everything

In its simplest form, the production function states that output (*Y*) is a function of two inputs: capital (*K*) and labour (*L*). Capital encompasses all non-human inputs — machines, tools, buildings, infrastructure, software. Labour encompasses human effort, time, and skill.

The most famous specific form was developed by mathematician Charles W. Cobb and economist Paul H. Douglas in their 1928 paper, *A Theory of Production*. They established a statistical relationship between physical production in American manufacturing from 1899 to 1922 and the corresponding changes in labour and capital employed:

> Y = A · K^α · L^β

Where *A* is total factor productivity (a scaling constant capturing technology and efficiency), *α* is the output elasticity of capital (approximately 0.3 for most developed economies), and *β* is the output elasticity of labour (approximately 0.7). Labour's share of output has hovered near 70% for a century — a remarkable empirical regularity.

The function was not original to Cobb and Douglas. Swedish economist Knut Wicksell had produced a virtually identical form around the turn of the century, but never published it in a journal. What Cobb and Douglas contributed was the empirical methodology — the first time an aggregate production function had been developed, estimated, and presented to the profession for analysis.

Robert Solow's 1956 growth model added the crucial insight: when he applied growth accounting to the U.S. economy for the 1909–1949 period, he found that approximately one-eighth of the doubling in labour productivity could be attributed to increased capital per worker. The remaining seven-eighths was the residual — "technical change" in the broadest sense. Moses Abramovitz memorably called it "a measure of our ignorance." Most economic growth, it turned out, could not be explained by simply accumulating more capital or adding more workers. Something else — technology, broadly conceived — was doing most of the work.

The production function operates at every scale. For an individual worker, *K* is a laptop and *L* is their time and cognitive effort. For a firm, *K* is factories and equipment, *L* is the workforce. For a nation, *Y* is GDP, *K* is the national capital stock, and *L* is the entire labour force. The equation works at all three levels, though with important caveats about aggregation. And at every level, the history of economic progress is the history of capital substituting for labour — and labour being redeployed to new tasks that the new capital makes possible.

### The Great Substitutions

#### From Hand to Machine: Textiles and the First Industrial Revolution

The textile industry was the epicentre of the Industrial Revolution, and its sequence of inventions provides the clearest historical illustration of capital progressively substituting for labour.

In 1760, the British cotton industry used only 2.6 million pounds of raw cotton and represented just 2.6% of national value added. The industry was organised as a cottage system — spinning and weaving done by hand in workers' homes with tools that had changed little in centuries.

Then capital arrived. James Hargreaves's spinning jenny (1764) allowed one worker to operate eight spindles instead of one. Richard Arkwright's water frame (1769) could spin 96 threads at once and produced stronger thread — but it was too large for a cottage, so it created the factory system itself. Samuel Crompton's spinning mule (1779) started at 48 spindles and eventually enabled one person to work more than 1,000 simultaneously. Edmund Cartwright's power loom (1785) worked at least ten times faster than a skilled handloom weaver and could replace up to 30 hand-operated looms.

The cumulative impact was staggering. By 1800, raw cotton consumption had grown from 2.6 million to 52 million pounds. Cotton yarn prices collapsed — the real price of coarse weft yarns fell to a third of their 1780s level by 1815. Lancashire's mills eventually produced eight billion yards of cloth per year, nearly half the world's total output. Cotton textiles accounted for half of Britain's exports by 1830.

In production function terms: in 1760, *Y_textiles = f(K_simple_tools, L_cottage_workers)*. By 1850, *Y_textiles = f(K_factories + K_machines, L_factory_workers)*, where *K* had increased enormously, *L* had been partially displaced and partially redeployed, and *Y* had increased by more than two orders of magnitude.

#### From Horse to Engine: Steam and the Liberation of Geography

Before steam power, water-powered factories had to be located near fast-flowing rivers. Steam liberated manufacturing from geography. James Watt's improved engine (1769, with rotary motion added by 1781) made steam power suitable for driving factory machinery. By 1800, Boulton & Watt had constructed 496 engines generating about 11,200 horsepower total.

Watt invented the unit of "horsepower" as a marketing tool — a deliberate rhetorical strategy to make the value proposition tangible. A coal-mine pony could pull 22,000 foot-pounds per minute; Watt increased this by 50% to get 33,000 foot-pounds per minute as one horsepower. Capital was literally priced in units of the animal labour it replaced.

#### From Craftsman to Converter: Steel and the 90% Cost Collapse

Henry Bessemer's process (1856) could make 30 tonnes of high-grade steel in half an hour — a feat previously requiring days or weeks of laborious, expensive, small-batch production. The price of steel fell from approximately $200 per tonne to $20 per tonne by 1900 — a 90% decline. Annual U.S. steel production increased from 13,000 tonnes in 1860 to over 24 million tonnes by 1910. By the turn of the century, "there were not a dozen men on the floor of a mill rolling 3,000 tons a day — as much as a Pittsburgh rolling mill of 1850 rolled in a year."

#### From Scythe to Reaper: Agriculture and the Longest Substitution

The U.S. Department of Agriculture documented a 95% decline in the human labour required to produce one bushel of wheat between 1830 and 1894: from three hours and three minutes to just ten minutes. Cyrus McCormick's mechanical reaper (1831) allowed a farmer to harvest ten acres per day instead of two or three.

The agricultural employment numbers tell the most dramatic story of capital-labour substitution in economic history:

| Year | Agricultural Employment (% of US workforce) |
|------|---------------------------------------------|
| 1800 | ~80–90% |
| 1900 | 40–41% |
| 1950 | 10% |
| 2000 | 1.9% |
| 2024 | ~1.5% |

One American farmer in 2024 feeds 155–168 people, compared to three to five people in the 1800s. Agricultural output nearly tripled between 1948 and 2017 while employment fell from 40% to under 2% of the workforce. The displaced workers did not become permanently unemployed — they migrated to cities and became the labour force for the industrial economy.

#### From Central Shaft to Individual Motor: Electricity and the Assembly Line

The electrification of industry fundamentally restructured the factory production function. Before electricity, a single large steam engine connected to all machinery via belts and pulleys dictated factory layout. Machines were arranged by proximity to the drive shaft, not by workflow logic. If the engine stopped, everything stopped.

Electric motors could be attached to individual machines. Unlike steam engines, motors don't lose efficiency as they get smaller. This enabled Henry Ford's assembly line (December 1913): the conveyor belt that moved car chassis from station to station was powered by distributed electric motors. Production time for a Model T dropped from 12 hours to 93 minutes. The price fell from $780 (1910) to $290 (1924). Annual production soared to 735,000 by 1917. Ford didn't simply add more capital or more labour — he reorganised the relationship between them, increasing *A* (total factor productivity) rather than shifting the *K/L* ratio alone.

#### From Adding Machine to Spreadsheet: Computers and the White-Collar Revolution

Every prior revolution primarily affected the production of physical goods. The computer was the first technology to alter white-collar production functions. VisiCalc (1979) compressed work that took twenty hours into fifteen minutes. Lotus 1-2-3 (1983) was used by one in four of the 15 million Americans who used a personal computer at work by 1987. The United States lost roughly 400,000 accounting clerk positions between 1980 and 2000 — but the number of accountants *grew* by 600,000 during the same period. When calculation became trivial, the value shifted to interpretation, strategy, and judgement.

Robert Solow observed in 1987: "You can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics." This paradox — massive IT investment with no visible productivity gain — was resolved in the late 1990s when complementary organisational investments matured. Productivity growth surged from 1.0–1.5% annually to 2.8% per year between 1995 and 2005. The lesson: new technology requires new organisation before its production function effects materialise. The lag was 15–20 years for computers. For electricity, it was even longer.

### The Recurring Pattern

Viewed through the lens of *Y = A · K^α · L^β*, economic history reveals a consistent five-act structure:

1. **New forms of capital** are invented (spinning machines, steam engines, converters, assembly lines, computers)
2. These **shift the production function** — either by increasing *A* or by changing the *K/L* ratio required for a given output
3. **Labour is displaced** from specific tasks (spinning by hand, harvesting by scythe, calculating by ledger)
4. **Displaced labour is redeployed** to new tasks that the new technology makes possible or valuable
5. **Total output increases** dramatically, and the **price of the affected good falls** — often by 90% or more

The cost collapse is the crucial mechanism. When cotton cloth becomes 90% cheaper, the world doesn't consume the same amount of cloth at lower cost — it consumes vastly more cloth. When steel becomes 90% cheaper, railroads become economically viable, and skyscrapers become possible. When computation becomes essentially free, entirely new industries (social media, e-commerce, streaming) emerge from nothing.

## Part II: Production Functions at Every Scale

The production function isn't merely an abstraction of macroeconomics. It operates concretely at the level of the individual worker, the firm, and the nation — and the history of how capital has transformed each level illuminates what AI is about to do.

### The Individual Worker

A medieval scribe in 1400 produced approximately three pages per day. A quill, ink, parchment, a desk — capital barely registered. Output scaled linearly with labour hours, with an effective ceiling of about three pages regardless of skill. The production function was approximately *Y = f(L)*, with capital negligible.

Then Gutenberg's press (1450s) produced 3,600 pages per workday — a 1,200x improvement. Before 1450, there were perhaps tens of thousands of books in all of Europe. By 1500, printing presses had produced more than 20 million volumes. The price of books fell by roughly two-thirds in fifty years. The production function for written documents shifted from *Y = f(L)* to *Y = f(K, L)* virtually overnight.

A clerk in 1900 with a typewriter could sustain 60–80 words per minute versus handwriting's practical maximum of perhaps 20–30. Philip G. Hubert claimed typewriters could "save 40 minutes out of an hour." But the production function was still fundamentally labour-constrained — the typist's speed was the bottleneck.

A knowledge worker in 2000 with a computer experienced something deeper. Word processing eliminated retyping for revisions, changing the economics of quality — the marginal cost of improvement dropped to near zero. Spreadsheets meant tasks previously requiring teams of accountants could be performed by a single person. U.S. worker productivity surged from 1.0–1.5% annually to 2.9% per year during 1995–2000.

A developer in 2024 with AI coding assistants represents a qualitative shift. GitHub Copilot's controlled experiment with 95 professional developers found the treatment group completed tasks 55.8% faster (statistically significant at P=0.0017). AI is now writing 46% of the average developer's code, reaching 61% in Java projects. The worker becomes more architect than bricklayer.

This progression — scribe to typist to computer user to AI-augmented worker — is the story of capital deepening at the individual level. At each stage, the ratio of capital to labour increases, and the nature of the remaining labour changes. The scribe's labour was physical reproduction. The typist's labour was transcription. The computer user's labour was synthesis and analysis. The AI-augmented worker's labour is direction and judgement.

### The Firm

At the firm level, the production function becomes concrete and measurable. Five case studies reveal how dramatically capital can reshape the *K/L* ratio — and how counterintuitive the effects can be.

**Tesla vs Toyota**: Tesla produces approximately 14.2 vehicles per employee; Toyota produces approximately 27. Despite being more automated, Tesla is *less* labour-efficient — partly because it vertically integrates more functions (batteries, software, charging infrastructure) and partly because it is a younger manufacturer still optimising processes. More automation does not automatically mean more labour efficiency. The relationship between capital and output is mediated by organisational knowledge — the *A* in the production function.

**Amazon and the ATM Paradox**: Amazon acquired Kiva Systems for $775 million in 2012. The robots cut "click to ship" time from 60–75 minutes to 15 minutes and reduced operating costs by 20% per warehouse. But Amazon now has over one million robots *and* 1.5 million employees — having added over a million jobs since the acquisition. Robots dramatically reduced the cost per warehouse, which allowed Amazon to open vastly more warehouses, expand into same-day delivery, and process exponentially more orders. The scale effect overwhelmed the substitution effect. This mirrors James Bessen's research on ATMs and bank tellers: from the 1970s through 2010, approximately 400,000 ATMs were installed in the United States, yet bank teller employment grew from 500,000 to nearly 600,000. ATMs reduced tellers per branch from 20 to 13, but cheaper branches meant 43% more branches in urban areas.

**Netflix vs Blockbuster**: Perhaps the cleanest case of capital replacing labour. At its peak in 2004, Blockbuster had 9,094 stores, 84,300 employees, and $6 billion in revenue — roughly $71,000 per employee. Netflix in 2024 has zero stores, 14,000 employees, and $39 billion in revenue — $3.0 million per employee. Revenue per employee is 42 times higher. Netflix replaced Blockbuster's labour-intensive distribution model (store clerks, shelf stockers, physical logistics workers) with capital-intensive digital infrastructure (servers, CDN networks, recommendation algorithms).

**Alphabet**: Revenue per employee of $2.20 million — five times the communication services sector average. Most of Google's revenue comes from automated advertising auctions. The marginal cost of serving one more search query or one more ad is near zero. This is a production function where capital (algorithms, data centres, accumulated data) has almost completely substituted for the labour that traditional advertising required.

**Agriculture**: The most dramatic long-run transformation. One American farmer in 2024 feeds 155–168 people, up from three to five in the 1800s. Output nearly tripled between 1948 and 2017 while agricultural employment fell from 40% of the workforce to under 2%. Virtually all growth came from total factor productivity — technology, better seeds, fertilisers, mechanisation, GPS-guided precision agriculture.

### The Nation

At the national level, the production function becomes the Solow model's core equation. GDP is output. The national capital stock is *K*. The labour force is *L*. And the story of national development is the story of capital deepening.

The United States transitioned through four regimes: agricultural (66% of workers in agriculture circa 1800), industrial (manufacturing expanding 4x from 2.5 million to 10 million workers between 1880 and 1920), services (rising from 31% in 1900 to 78% by 1999), and knowledge/digital (79% services by 2023, with the fastest-growing capital being intangible — software, data, trained AI models).

China's transformation from 1980 to 2020 represents the most dramatic capital deepening any nation has ever undertaken. GDP rose from approximately $191 billion to $14.7 trillion — a 77-fold increase. Gross capital formation was sustained at close to 45% of GDP, roughly double the U.S. rate and unprecedented in economic history.

Japan's post-war miracle (9.3% average annual growth from 1956 to 1973) was fuelled by savings rates of 36% of national income versus 16% in the United States. Investment in capital equipment exceeded 30% of GNP in the late 1960s. South Korea compressed the same trajectory into a single generation — from $79 GDP per capita in 1960 to $31,721 in 2020, with R&D spending reaching 5.21% of GDP by 2022, second highest in the world.

Government spending functions as capital in the national production function. Infrastructure has a fiscal multiplier of approximately 1.6. Education returns $10–15 per $1 invested over time. A 1% of GDP increase in R&D expenditure raises total factor productivity growth by 5.0 to 7.7 percentage points — though with an average lag of 11 years.

### The Counter-Example: Construction

Not every industry has been transformed. Construction stands as the critical counter-example — an industry where the production function has barely moved in half a century.

Real output per worker in construction in 2020 was more than 30% lower than in 1970. Since 1965, labour productivity has been falling at an average pace of 0.6% per year, while productivity in the wider economy has grown at about 1.6% per year. In value added per hour worked between 1947 and 2010, agriculture improved by 16.1x, manufacturing by 8.6x, and construction by just 1.1x.

Why? Extreme fragmentation (83–94% of firms employ fewer than 20 people), R&D spending at least 10x lower than manufacturing as a fraction of revenue, site-specific work resisting standardisation, and regulatory barriers to prefabrication and automation. McKinsey estimates that if construction productivity caught up to the economy-wide average, it would add $1.6 trillion to the sector — 2% of global GDP.

The construction case demonstrates that technology does not rewrite production functions automatically. It requires an institutional and market structure that allows capital to effectively substitute for and complement labour. Where those structures are absent, the production function remains stuck. This lesson matters profoundly for the AI revolution: the technology alone is not sufficient. Organisational, regulatory, and market conditions must evolve in parallel.

## Part III: Four New Machines

### The Taxonomy

AI is not one thing. It is four distinct types of machines, each with a different industrial revolution analogue and each transforming a different dimension of the production function.

### Word Machines

**What they are**: Large language models that read, write, translate, summarise, and generate text at scale. GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini — general-purpose engines for the production of language.

**The analogue**: The printing press mechanised the production of written words — 3,600 pages per workday versus a scribe's three, a 1,200x improvement. The power loom mechanised the production of cloth — a Jacquard operator could produce two feet of complex fabric per day versus a traditional weaver's one inch, a 24x improvement. LLMs mechanise the production of *all* written content — emails, reports, code, legal documents, marketing copy, translations, analyses.

Cities that adopted the printing press between 1450 and 1600 saw economic growth 60 percentage points higher than those that did not. The economic value of word machines may follow a similar pattern, accruing disproportionately to early adopters.

**The evidence**:
- GitHub Copilot (95 professional developers): 55.8% faster task completion, P=0.0017
- AI-produced content is 4.7x cheaper than human-written content ($131 vs $611 per blog post)
- Klarna's AI assistant handled 2.3 million conversations in its first month — equivalent to 700 full-time agents — cutting resolution time from 11 minutes to under 2 minutes. Cost: $2–3 million versus the salary of 700 agents. Projected $40 million profit improvement.
- Lawyers using AI save an average of 32.5 working days per year. AI contract review achieves 94% accuracy.
- 25% of Google's new code is now AI-assisted. 46% of the average developer's code is AI-generated, reaching 61% in Java.

**Who is affected**: Anyone whose primary output is words — writers, lawyers, analysts, programmers, marketers, translators, customer service agents. McKinsey estimates approximately 75% of the $2.6–4.4 trillion in annual generative AI value comes from four word-intensive functions: customer operations, marketing and sales, software engineering, and R&D.

### Thought Machines

**What they are**: Reasoning systems that solve problems, plan multi-step solutions, make decisions under uncertainty, analyse trade-offs, and synthesise evidence. Chain-of-thought models, extended thinking, reasoning architectures.

**The analogue**: Charles Babbage's Difference Engine (1822) automated the tedious work of calculating mathematical tables. His Analytical Engine (1837) was far more ambitious — the first design for a general-purpose computer, incorporating an arithmetic logic unit, conditional branching, loops, and integrated memory. Ada Lovelace recognised that it could manipulate symbols of any kind, not just numbers. The leap from the Difference Engine to the Analytical Engine mirrors the leap from calculators to reasoning AI. The Difference Engine could compute; the Analytical Engine could *think* — conditionally, iteratively, and in ways not explicitly programmed for each case.

**The evidence**:
- OpenAI o3 scored 96.7% on the American Invitational Mathematics Exam (AIME 2024), up from o1's 74.3%
- Harvard/BCG study (758 BCG consultants): AI-augmented consultants produced 40%+ higher quality output. Below-average performers improved by 43%; above-average by 17% — a massive skill-levelling effect.
- GPT-4T achieved 94% diagnostic accuracy in medical studies (2025), up from 57.49% in 2023
- Garry Kasparov's "centaur chess" showed that human-AI teams beat both pure AI and pure human players. This pattern has replicated: Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond's study of 5,179 customer support agents found AI increased productivity by 14% on average, with 34% improvement for novice workers.

**Who is affected**: Anyone whose value comes from thinking — analysts, strategists, researchers, managers, doctors, engineers. The centaur model suggests the near-term effect is augmentation: the human provides judgement and context; the thought machine provides analytical power and breadth.

### Doing Machines

**What they are**: AI agents that take actions, execute multi-step tasks, operate tools, manage workflows, and complete complex sequences of knowledge work autonomously or semi-autonomously.

**The analogue**: The Jacquard loom (1804) was the first programmable automation system. By changing punched cards, it could execute different sequences of operations — not just a single task but a *workflow*. Ford's assembly line (1913) broke a complex manufacturing task into a sequence of simpler, automated or semi-automated steps. AI agents combine both concepts: they accept natural language instructions, break complex tasks into steps, and execute those steps using tools. They are the assembly lines of knowledge work.

**The evidence**:
- Claude Code leads SWE-bench at 72%+, with 83.8% of pull requests merged by maintainers. It currently authors 4% of all GitHub public commits.
- Rakuten reduced time-to-market by 79% (24 days to 5 days). TELUS shipped 30% faster while accumulating 500,000 hours in total time savings across 57,000+ team members.
- Block (formerly Square) cut headcount nearly in half, directly attributed to AI automation.
- C.H. Robinson eliminated approximately 1,400 jobs after deploying AI-driven pricing, scheduling, and shipment tracking.
- IBM laid off approximately 8,000 employees, primarily in HR, replaced by the AskHR chatbot.

**The economics**: A coding agent running on frontier models costs roughly $1,000–4,000 per month of heavy usage — compared to a junior software engineer costing $8,000–15,000 per month fully loaded in the U.S. Inference costs are declining 10x annually.

**Who is affected**: Anyone whose role centres on executing sequences of steps rather than making high-level judgements — project managers, coordinators, administrative workers, HR administrators, logistics coordinators.

### Perceiving Machines

**What they are**: Computer vision systems, robotics, multimodal AI that can see, hear, sense, navigate, manipulate physical objects, inspect surfaces, and respond to physical environments.

**The analogue**: The steam engine brought mechanical power to fixed locations — factories had to be built around a single, large engine. The electric motor distributed power to *any* location and *any* scale. Unlike steam engines, electric motors don't lose efficiency as they get smaller. This enabled a fundamental reorganisation of production. As economist Paul David documented, this transition took decades because factory owners initially just replaced their steam engine with a single electric motor — failing to grasp that the revolution was in *distributed* power. Only when factories were redesigned from scratch around distributed electric motors did the full productivity gains materialise.

Perceiving machines bring intelligence to any physical context — warehouses, roads, factories, farms, hospitals. Just as the electric motor distributed mechanical power, perceiving AI distributes cognitive perception.

**The evidence**:
- Waymo: 79% fewer any-injury crashes at 56.7 million rider-only miles, 81% fewer airbag deployments versus human benchmarks
- Amazon: over 1 million robots across 300+ fulfilment centres, approaching 1:1 ratio with its 1.5 million human employees. Packages handled per worker surged from 175 (2016) to 3,870 (2025) — a 22x improvement.
- AI visual inspection: 97–99.5% defect detection accuracy versus 60–80% for human inspectors. AI detected 37% more critical defects than expert humans in controlled studies.
- Intuitive Surgical (da Vinci): Procedures grew 18% in 2025. Revenue: $2.87 billion in Q4 2025 alone.
- Construction robotics: market growing from $1.4 billion (2024) to $3.66 billion (2030). The one major industry that has resisted capital deepening for five decades may finally be reached by perceiving machines.

**Who is affected**: Anyone whose value comes from perceiving and responding to physical environments — drivers (3.5 million in the U.S. alone), warehouse workers, factory inspectors, security guards, agricultural workers.

## Part IV: The Combined Effect

### The N-of-1 Worker

A farmer in 1800 with manual tools could feed one to two families. A farmer in 1900 with horse-drawn equipment could feed five or six. A farmer in 2024 with a combine harvester, GPS-guided planters, and precision irrigation feeds 155–168 people. The capital-to-labour ratio increased by two orders of magnitude.

The AI equivalent is already visible. A solo founder in 2026, equipped with word machines for writing code and content, thought machines for strategic analysis, doing machines for executing development tasks, and perceiving machines for product analytics — can produce output that previously required a team of 5–15 people.

The evidence is concrete. Base44's solo founder built an AI platform to 300,000 users and $3.5 million ARR in six months, selling to Wix for $80 million. Midjourney reached $500 million in annual revenue with fewer than 150 employees — over $5 million per head. Pieter Levels runs multiple seven-figure businesses with zero employees. Solo-founded startups now represent 36.3% of all new ventures, up from 22% in 2015. Sam Altman has predicted the first one-person billion-dollar company.

The individual production function is being rewritten: *Y = f(human_judgement, AI_word, AI_thought, AI_doing, AI_perceiving)*. The human provides taste, ambition, domain expertise, ethical judgement, and creative vision. The four machines provide everything else.

### The Firm: Smaller Teams, Larger Ambitions

When electricity replaced steam in manufacturing, did factories get smaller? Yes and no. Individual factories became more efficient, requiring fewer workers per unit of output. But the overall scale of industry grew dramatically as manufacturing costs fell and new products became viable.

The same dual pattern is emerging with AI. Microsoft reports AI tools write up to 30% of new code. Klarna's revenue per employee rose 73% year-on-year. Block halved its headcount. But total corporate AI investment reached $252.3 billion in 2024, up 44.5% in private investment. The Jevons Paradox is already visible: as AI makes cognitive work cheaper, companies don't just do the same work with fewer people — they do vastly more work.

McKinsey estimates approximately 75% of the $2.6–4.4 trillion in annual generative AI value comes from four functions: customer operations (word machines plus doing machines), marketing and sales (word machines), software engineering (word machines plus thought machines plus doing machines), and R&D (thought machines plus word machines). The production function is being restructured function by function, not all at once.

### The Nation: A New Factor of Production

AI is not merely an improvement in total factor productivity (*A*). It is a new form of capital — one that substitutes for cognitive labour in ways that traditional capital never could. Traditional capital substitutes for physical labour. AI capital substitutes for *thinking*.

The GDP impact projections range enormously. Goldman Sachs projected +7% global GDP over ten years (2023) but acknowledged "basically zero" actual impact in 2025. Acemoglu estimated a cautious +1.1–1.6% GDP over ten years. ARK Invest projected an explosive +$200 trillion in economic output by 2030. The range itself is informative: we are in a period of radical uncertainty about magnitude, even as the direction is clear.

The Goldman Sachs finding — essentially zero impact so far — is not discouraging. It mirrors the electric motor story precisely. Paul David showed that electrification took 20–30 years to show up in productivity statistics because factories had to be redesigned from scratch around the new technology. Erik Brynjolfsson identified the same J-curve for computers: IT investments only produced productivity gains when combined with complementary organisational changes — redesigned business processes, retrained workforces, new management practices. The lag was 15–20 years.

Which countries will benefit most? The pattern from industrialisation: those that adopt earliest and most aggressively benefit disproportionately. The United States leads in AI investment ($109.1 billion in 2024), foundational model development (61% of global output), and controls 73% of global AI compute. China's AI is projected to contribute 26.1% to GDP by 2030. Low-income countries are projected to see output gains of just 2.7% — the AI revolution risks widening the global development gap, just as industrialisation created the Great Divergence.

## Part V: The Pattern Repeats — And the Equation Breaks

### The Five Stages, Applied to AI

Every major machine revolution in history has followed the same five-stage sequence. There is no reason to believe AI will be different in structure, even if it is different in scope.

**Stage 1 — Displacement**: Workers doing the old task lose their jobs. Handloom weavers' wages fell 58% between 1806 and 1820. Employment among young software developers (ages 22–25) fell nearly 20% between 2022 and 2025. Forty-one per cent of companies plan to reduce workforces by 2030 due to AI. The transition has begun.

**Stage 2 — Cost Collapse**: The cost of the output drops by 90% or more. AI-produced content is 4.7x cheaper. AI customer service costs $0.50–0.70 per interaction versus $8–15 for a human agent — a 90–95% reduction. Inference costs are declining 10x per year. GPT-4-equivalent performance: $20 per million tokens in 2022, $0.40 in 2025 — a 50x decline.

**Stage 3 — Demand Explosion**: Because costs fall, demand explodes far beyond prediction. Businesses report 77% increases in content output volume within six months of AI adoption. The AI code tools market is projected to grow from $4.91 billion (2024) to $30.1 billion (2032). As NPR reported in February 2025, "AI becoming accessible to startups, schools and small businesses would expand the market and, in turn, increase — not lessen — energy demand and AI use."

**Stage 4 — New Job Creation**: Entirely new categories of work emerge. After the typewriter: clerical worker (women went from 5% of office workers in 1880 to 75% by 1900). After the automobile: mechanic, filling station attendant, traffic engineer, suburban developer. After AI: prompt engineer, AI content strategist, agent orchestrator, human-AI collaboration designer, AI safety engineer, multi-agent system architect.

**Stage 5 — Net Positive**: More total employment, higher wages, vastly more output — but with significant disruption during transition. The World Economic Forum projects 170 million new jobs created versus 92 million displaced by 2030 — a net positive of 78 million.

### Why This Time Might Be Different

The "this time is different" argument has intellectual force. Previous machines replaced muscles; AI replaces minds. Every previous revolution left cognitive work untouched: steam engines replaced physical power but not thinking; electric motors distributed physical power but not judgement; computers automated calculation but not reasoning. AI automates reasoning itself.

Supporting evidence: AI now affects high-skill work, not just routine tasks. The elasticity of substitution for intelligence tasks is 2.2 — meaning AI substitutes for human cognitive work relatively easily, compared to capital-labour substitution in manufacturing (elasticity approximately 0.6). Employment among young software developers has already fallen 20%.

### Why It Probably Isn't

Six forces argue against permanent displacement:

**Comparative advantage**: Even if AI is better than humans at every task in absolute terms, humans will have comparative advantage in tasks where AI's relative advantage is smallest. The question is never "can AI do this?" but "is AI's advantage here bigger than its advantage elsewhere?"

**The Jevons Paradox**: When cognitive work becomes cheaper, we do vastly more of it. Every small business can now afford consultant-quality analysis. Every rural clinic can access specialist diagnostics. Every student can have a personalised tutor. Demand expands to absorb and exceed the displacement.

**Human preferences for human interaction**: Even Klarna, after replacing 700 agents with AI, reversed course to ensure customers always have the option to speak with a human. Healthcare, education, therapy, leadership, negotiation — all involve trust relationships where human presence matters.

**New desires emerge**: In 1900, no one wanted a social media presence. In 2000, no one wanted a mobile app. Human desires are not fixed — they expand as technology creates new possibilities.

**David Autor's "Rebuilding the Middle"**: Autor argues AI could actually reverse four decades of wage polarisation. As a "force multiplier for expertise," it enables ordinary workers to do work previously requiring expensive specialists — potentially expanding the middle class rather than hollowing it out.

**Erik Brynjolfsson's "Turing Trap"**: The trap is optimising AI to replicate and replace human labour rather than augment it. When AI automates, the marginal value of workers' contributions is reduced. When AI augments, more of the gains go to workers. The trap is a choice, not an inevitability.

The honest assessment: the academic literature is genuinely divided. Acemoglu sees modest GDP impact (1.1–1.6% over ten years) and real risk of "wrong direction" automation. Brynjolfsson and ARK see transformative gains if augmentation prevails. The key variable is the elasticity of substitution between AI and human labour — high for intelligence tasks (2.2), low for physical tasks, effectively zero for creative and empathetic tasks. The most likely outcome is heterogeneous: massive displacement in some task categories, massive augmentation in others, and massive creation of entirely new categories.

### The Equation Breaks

The classical production function was adequate when machines were dumb tools that amplified human physical effort. It is inadequate for an era where machines can think.

The standard equation treats technology as a single residual (*A*) — everything that isn't capital or labour. But AI is not merely a boost to total factor productivity. It is a new form of capital that substitutes for specific types of labour in ways that traditional capital never could. A bulldozer substitutes for digging labour. An LLM substitutes for writing labour. A reasoning model substitutes for analytical labour. An agent substitutes for coordination labour. A vision system substitutes for inspection labour. These are categorically different substitutions operating on different parts of the production function simultaneously.

The production function needs rewriting:

> **Y = f(K_physical, K_word, K_thought, K_doing, K_perceiving, L_human, A)**

Where:
- **K_physical** = traditional capital (machines, buildings, infrastructure)
- **K_word** = word machine capacity (LLM tokens, content generation, translation, coding)
- **K_thought** = thought machine capacity (reasoning compute, analysis, planning, diagnosis)
- **K_doing** = doing machine capacity (agent hours, workflow automation, task execution)
- **K_perceiving** = perceiving machine capacity (computer vision, robotics, sensors, navigation)
- **L_human** = irreducible human labour (creativity, judgement, empathy, physical presence, taste)
- **A** = total factor productivity (institutional quality, culture, coordination, legal framework)

Each AI capital type has its own price trajectory, its own elasticity of substitution with human labour, and its own adoption curve. The prices are collapsing — inference costs declining 10–50x per year. The elasticities are task-dependent, not occupation-dependent. And the adoption curves are staggered: word machines are already widely deployed, thought machines are scaling rapidly, doing machines are emerging, and perceiving machines are still early outside warehouses and self-driving cars.

The production function at the individual level becomes *Y = f(human_judgement, K_word, K_thought, K_doing, K_perceiving)* — one person commanding four types of capital that previously required a team of specialists.

At the firm level, it becomes a choice: deploy AI capital to do the same work with fewer people, or deploy it to do vastly more work with the same people. History's lesson — from ATMs to Amazon's robots to the explosion of cloth after the power loom — is that the second effect usually dominates.

At the national level, it becomes a new growth equation. Countries that invest early in AI capital, reorganise their institutions around it, and develop the complementary human capital to direct it will capture disproportionate gains — just as Britain captured disproportionate gains from the first Industrial Revolution, and the United States captured them from the second.

---

## Conclusion: The Oldest Lesson

The production function has been rewritten before. It was rewritten when the spinning jenny turned one thread into eight. When the Bessemer converter turned expensive steel into cheap steel. When the assembly line turned twelve hours into ninety-three minutes. When the spreadsheet turned twenty hours into fifteen minutes.

Each time, the new capital displaced workers from specific tasks. Each time, the cost of the affected output collapsed. Each time, demand exploded in ways no one predicted. Each time, entirely new categories of work emerged. Each time, total output and total employment eventually exceeded anything the previous era could have imagined.

The four AI machines — word, thought, doing, perceiving — are rewriting the production function again. But this time, the capital substitutes for cognitive labour, not physical labour. The scribe's quill became a printing press; now the printing press becomes a language model. The calculating engine became a computer; now the computer becomes a reasoning system. The Jacquard loom's punched cards became factory automation; now factory automation becomes autonomous agents. The electric motor distributed power everywhere; now perceiving machines distribute intelligence everywhere.

The pattern is the same. The domain is different. And the oldest lesson in economics — that capital deepening creates more than it destroys, but only when institutions, organisations, and markets evolve to match — has never been more relevant.

The production function is being rewritten. The question is not whether, but how fast, how broadly, and who captures the gains.
