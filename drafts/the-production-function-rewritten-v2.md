# The Production Function, Rewritten

## Four Machines, One Equation, and the Biggest Substitution in Economic History

---

Everything an economy produces — every good shipped, every service rendered, every line of code committed — can be described by the oldest equation in economics: the production function. Output is a function of capital and labour. *Y = f(K, L).* Two inputs, one output. The equation has survived two and a half centuries without fundamental modification.

It needs rewriting now.

Not because artificial intelligence is impressive. It is. But because AI is not one thing. It is four machines that evolved in rapid succession — writing machines, thinking machines, doing machines, and perceiving machines — each one strictly more useful than the last, each one substituting for a different type of human labour in the production function. Together, they represent the first time in history that capital can replace not just muscle but mind.

To understand what that means, we need to understand the equation. And to understand the equation, we need to start with a single person.

---

## Part I: One Equation at Every Scale

### The Individual

Consider a knowledge worker. She has two inputs: her time and skills (labour), and her tools (capital). A laptop, a phone, some software. Her output — documents, analyses, decisions, code — is a function of both:

*Y = f(K_tools, L_time × H_skill)*

where *H* is her human capital: education, experience, judgement. The production function at this scale is intimate and concrete. She can feel when it changes. When she got her first computer, she felt it. When she got broadband internet, she felt it. Each new piece of capital shifted what she could produce in an hour.

A medieval scribe felt it too, though the shift was more dramatic. A scribe in 1400 produced approximately three pages per day. His tools were a quill, ink, and parchment — capital that barely registered. His production function was almost pure labour: *Y ≈ f(L)*. Then Gutenberg's press arrived, producing 3,600 pages per workday — a 1,200x improvement. The production function for written documents shifted from *Y = f(L)* to *Y = f(K, L)* in a generation. Capital went from negligible to dominant.

A clerk in 1900 with a typewriter could sustain 60–80 words per minute versus handwriting's 20–30. Philip G. Hubert claimed typewriters could "save 40 minutes out of an hour." But the clerk was still the bottleneck. The production function was labour-constrained. Capital made her faster; it didn't replace her.

### The Firm

At the firm level, the same equation scales up but gains complexity. A company combines its total capital stock with its total workforce:

*Y_firm = A · f(K_physical, K_intangible, L, H)*

where *A* is total factor productivity — management quality, organisational capital, technology. The firm's *K/L* ratio (capital per worker) determines its character. Tesla has roughly 125,000 employees producing 1.8 million vehicles (14 vehicles per worker), but controls 80% of its supply chain in-house. Toyota has 380,000 employees producing 10.8 million vehicles (28 per worker), but outsources most component production. Tesla's lower vehicles-per-employee doesn't mean lower efficiency — it means a different production function, with more capital and more of the value chain captured internally.

The most instructive firm-level comparison is Netflix versus Blockbuster. At its peak, Blockbuster had 9,094 stores, 84,300 employees, and $6 billion in revenue — about $71,000 per employee. Netflix in 2024 has zero stores, 14,000 employees, and $39 billion in revenue — $3.0 million per employee. Revenue per head is 42 times higher. Netflix replaced labour-intensive distribution (store clerks, shelf stockers, physical logistics) with capital-intensive infrastructure (servers, algorithms, content delivery). Same output category (entertainment delivery), completely different production function.

### The Nation

At the national level, the production function becomes the Solow growth model:

*Y = A · K^α · L^(1-α)*

where *Y* is GDP, *K* is the national capital stock, *L* is the labour force, and *A* is total factor productivity. Cobb and Douglas estimated in 1928 that *α* ≈ 0.3 — capital's share of output is about 30%, labour's about 70%. That ratio has held for nearly a century.

When Solow applied this to the U.S. economy for 1909–1949, he found that only one-eighth of the doubling in labour productivity could be attributed to capital deepening. The remaining seven-eighths was the residual — technology, broadly conceived. Moses Abramovitz called it "a measure of our ignorance." Most growth comes not from accumulating more capital or more workers, but from finding better ways to combine them.

### The Aggregation Problem

Can you build up from individual to national using the same equation? The honest answer from economics is: not cleanly. Felipe and Fisher (2003) showed that even when every firm has a Cobb-Douglas production function, you cannot simply add them up to get an aggregate Cobb-Douglas. The conditions required — identical technology across all firms, or very special distributions of inputs — never hold in practice.

And yet the aggregate production function works remarkably well as an empirical tool. The Cobb-Douglas regression produces excellent fits, and estimated elasticities closely match observed factor shares. The resolution of this paradox is subtle: the aggregate function may be capturing an accounting identity rather than a structural relationship. But as a *conceptual* framework — the same logic at every level, even if the parameters change — it remains the most powerful lens economics has for understanding what capital and labour do.

## Part II: The Great Substitutions

The history of economic progress is the history of capital substituting for labour — and labour being redeployed to new tasks that the new capital makes possible.

### From Hand to Machine

James Hargreaves's spinning jenny (1764) allowed one worker to operate eight spindles instead of one. Richard Arkwright's water frame (1769) spun 96 threads simultaneously. Samuel Crompton's spinning mule (1779) eventually let one person work more than 1,000 spindles. The power loom (1785) worked at least ten times faster than a skilled handloom weaver. Raw cotton consumption grew from 2.6 million pounds (1760) to 52 million (1800) to 588 million (1850). Cotton yarn prices collapsed to a third of their 1780s level. Lancashire eventually produced eight billion yards of cloth per year — half the world's total.

### From Scythe to Reaper

The U.S. Department of Agriculture documented a 95% decline in the labour required per bushel of wheat between 1830 and 1894: from three hours and three minutes to ten minutes. Agricultural employment fell from 80–90% of the U.S. workforce (1800) to 40% (1900) to 10% (1950) to under 2% (2024). One American farmer now feeds 155–168 people. The displaced workers migrated to cities and became the industrial workforce.

### From Craftsman to Converter

Bessemer's process (1856) dropped the price of steel from $200 to $20 per tonne — a 90% decline. U.S. steel production went from 13,000 tonnes (1860) to 24 million (1910). "There were not a dozen men on the floor of a mill rolling 3,000 tons a day — as much as a Pittsburgh rolling mill of 1850 rolled in a year."

### From Central Shaft to Assembly Line

Ford's assembly line (December 1913) cut Model T production time from 12 hours to 93 minutes. Price fell from $780 to $290. Annual production reached 735,000 by 1917. Ford didn't just add capital or labour — he reorganised their relationship. This was an increase in *A* (total factor productivity), not just a shift in the *K/L* ratio.

### The Pattern

Every substitution follows the same five acts:

1. New capital is invented
2. Labour is displaced from specific tasks
3. The cost of the affected output collapses
4. Demand explodes beyond all prediction
5. Displaced labour is redeployed to new tasks that the old economy couldn't have imagined

The critical mechanism is act three. When cloth becomes 90% cheaper, the world doesn't consume the same cloth at lower cost — it consumes vastly more. When steel becomes cheap, railroads and skyscrapers become possible. When computation becomes free, social media, e-commerce, and streaming emerge from nothing.

But there is a crucial number that determines whether capital *can* substitute for labour at all: the elasticity of substitution.

---

## Part III: The Number That Changes Everything

The elasticity of substitution (σ) measures how easily capital can replace labour as their relative prices change. When σ > 1, capital and labour are gross substitutes — you can increasingly use one in place of the other. When σ < 1, they are complements — you need both, and removing one collapses output.

For most of economic history, the measured elasticity of substitution between capital and labour has been **below 1** — typically 0.3 to 0.8. Traditional capital (machines, buildings, infrastructure) *complements* human labour. A tractor makes a farmer more productive; it doesn't replace the farmer. A factory makes workers more productive; it doesn't eliminate them. This is why the Luddites were ultimately wrong: as capital deepened, labour became more valuable, not less, because the two are complements.

But recent research reveals something startling. When the capital is intelligent — specifically, when it is automation or AI — the elasticity of substitution jumps to **3.0–4.5**. AI capital is a strong substitute for labour, not a complement. The mathematical character of the production function changes qualitatively.

This is the number that changes everything. With σ < 1 (traditional capital), capital deepening makes labour *more* valuable. With σ > 1 (AI capital), capital deepening makes labour *less* necessary. The production function shifts from one that requires both inputs to one that can, in principle, operate on capital alone.

The question is: what does this look like in practice?

## Part IV: Four Machines

### The Evolutionary Progression

AI is not one machine. It is four, and they evolved in rapid succession — each one building on the last, each one strictly more useful.

The GPT moment gave us **writing machines**: models that take in words and produce words. Then chain-of-thought gave us **thinking machines**: writing machines that could reason. Then agentic frameworks gave us **doing machines**: thinking machines that could act. And now computer vision and robotics are giving us **perceiving machines**: doing machines that can sense and interact with the physical world.

This progression is not arbitrary. It mirrors the hierarchy of what humans do. We write. We think. We do. We perceive and manipulate the physical world. Each machine type substitutes for a different layer of human labour in the production function, and each one disrupts a different type of production.

### Writing Machines

A writing machine takes in words and produces words. It is useful for any task where text is the output: drafting contracts, writing code, composing emails, generating marketing copy, translating documents, summarising research.

**What fraction of knowledge work is writing?** More than most people realise. Lawyers spend 37% of their day on billable work — much of which is drafting — and 63% on administration, much of which involves correspondence and documentation. Knowledge workers spend 28% of their week on email and 20% searching for information. Across roles, 25–60% of knowledge work involves composing, editing, or reviewing text.

**What are the measured productivity gains?** The MIT Noy-Zhang study (2023, published in *Science*) tested 444 professionals on business writing tasks: 37% faster completion, with quality increasing by 0.45 standard deviations. GitHub's controlled experiment with 95 professional developers: 55.8% faster task completion (P=0.0017). Lawyers using AI save an average of 32.5 working days per year. AI-produced content costs 4.7 times less than human-written content.

But the overall productivity multiplier for the *whole worker* is more modest: typically 1.2–2x. Writing machines accelerate the writing, but the non-writing components — judgement, negotiation, relationships, court appearances, architectural decisions — remain at human speed.

**The honest comparison to industrial machines:** The printing press gave a 1,200x gain on copying. The typewriter gave 3–6x on transcription speed. Writing machines give 1.4–2x on *composition* speed — comparable to the typewriter, not the printing press. But the printing press didn't make composition faster; it made copying faster. LLMs accelerate the harder part: the thinking-through-writing that was previously bottlenecked by the speed of human thought.

**A sobering finding:** The METR study (July 2025) tested 16 experienced open-source developers working on their own repositories. AI tools made them 19% *slower*, not faster — while the developers *believed* they were 20% faster. Writing machines are most powerful for routine, well-defined writing tasks. For expert-level work on complex, familiar codebases, the gains evaporate or reverse.

### Thinking Machines

When models gained chain-of-thought reasoning, the writing machine became a thinking machine. This was a qualitative leap: a machine that doesn't just produce text but reasons through problems — conditionally, iteratively, step by step.

**The landmark evidence:** The Harvard/BCG study (2023) tested 758 BCG consultants. Those with AI completed 12.2% more tasks, 25.1% faster, with 40% higher quality. But the most important finding was the **skill-levelling effect**: below-average consultants improved by 43%; above-average by only 17%. The thinking machine compresses the expertise gradient. Junior + AI approaches senior performance.

This pattern replicated everywhere. Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond's study of 5,179 customer service agents: 14% average productivity increase, but 34% for novice workers, with minimal impact on experts. The MIT writing study: the biggest quality gains went to weaker writers. GPT-4T achieved 94% medical diagnostic accuracy, matching or exceeding non-specialist physicians.

**The centaur question:** Kasparov's Advanced Chess showed human-AI teams beating either alone. But the centaur advantage may be narrowing. A 2025 California Management Review meta-analysis found that human-AI collaborations on average *underperform* both the AI alone and the best human decision-makers — except in creative tasks. The thinking machine doesn't always need the human in the loop.

**The production function implication:** Thinking machines don't just speed up analysis — they *democratise* it. When a junior analyst plus a thinking machine approximates a senior analyst, the wage premium for expertise compresses. David Autor's NBER paper (2024) argues this could reverse four decades of wage polarisation: AI as "a force multiplier for expertise" that rebuilds the middle class.

### Doing Machines

When thinking machines gained the ability to take actions — to execute multi-step workflows, operate tools, manage processes — they became doing machines. This is the leap from suggestion to execution, from autocomplete to autonomy.

**The progression in software is the clearest:** GitHub Copilot (2021) is a writing machine for code — it predicts the next tokens. Claude Code (2025–2026) is a doing machine — it reads a codebase, plans an approach, writes code, runs tests, fixes failures, and submits a pull request. Copilot substitutes for typing. Claude Code substitutes for an engineering workflow. SWE-bench scores went from 1.96% (2023) to 80.8% (2026) — a 41x improvement in autonomous software engineering capability in 2.5 years.

**The economics are striking.** An AI agent costs roughly $0.20–2.74 per hour, running continuously. A median U.S. knowledge worker costs approximately $35 per hour for an eight-hour day. The doing machine is 1/35th to 1/175th the cost. And the cost is falling 10x per year.

**The measured gains approach industrial-revolution class:** Nubank achieved a 12x efficiency improvement in engineering hours. Amazon saved an estimated 4,500 developer-years of effort ($260 million) in a single migration project. Block is targeting $2 million in gross profit per employee, up from $500,000 — a 4x increase.

**The N-of-1 company:** When doing machines handle execution, the minimum viable team approaches one. Solo-founded startups rose from 23.7% (2019) to 36.3% (H1 2025) of all new ventures. 52.3% of successful startup exits were achieved by solo founders. Base44's Maor Shlomo built an AI platform alone, reached $3.5 million ARR in six months, sold to Wix for $80 million.

**The honest comparison:** The Ford assembly line gave an 8x gain (12 hours to 93 minutes). Doing machines appear to deliver 2–12x gains in knowledge work — comparable to the assembly line. This is genuinely industrial-revolution-class disruption, but for *cognitive* production rather than physical production.

### Perceiving Machines

When doing machines gain the ability to see, hear, and interact with the physical world — through computer vision, robotics, and multimodal AI — they become perceiving machines. This is the leap from the digital to the physical.

**The author's key question:** Will perceiving machines give order-of-magnitude productivity gains like industrial revolution machines did?

The honest answer is: it depends on what was already mechanised.

A mechanical excavator gave 40–130x gains over a human with a hand shovel. Will an *autonomous* excavator give another order of magnitude over a *human-operated* excavator? No. Caterpillar's autonomous mining trucks show approximately 30% improvement. Komatsu's AI-assisted excavators: 18%. The biggest gain was the machine itself — going from human muscle to mechanical power. Making the machine intelligent adds 15–30%, not 10x.

**But where human perception was the bottleneck, the gains are enormous:**

AI visual inspection achieves 97–99.5% defect detection accuracy versus 60–90% for human inspectors, at 30x the speed (2 seconds per unit versus 60 seconds). Waymo's autonomous vehicles show a 10x reduction in serious injury crashes. Amazon's warehouse robots improved packages-per-worker from 175 (2016) to 3,870 (2025) — a 22x improvement over a decade.

The pattern: perceiving machines don't multiply the power of existing machines by 10x. They multiply the *availability and consistency* of human perception by 10–100x in domains where perception was the rate-limiting step.

### The Honest Comparison Table

| Machine Type | What it replaces | Measured gain | Industrial revolution comparison |
|---|---|---|---|
| Writing machine | Human writing labour | 1.4–2x composition speed | Typewriter: 3–6x (comparable) |
| Thinking machine | Human analytical labour | 1.1–1.4x overall; 43% for juniors | Calculator/spreadsheet: 10–50x (not yet comparable) |
| Doing machine | Human coordination/execution | 2–12x in knowledge work | Assembly line: 8x (comparable) |
| Perceiving machine | Human perception in physical work | 15–30% on mechanised tasks; 10–100x where perception was bottleneck | Mechanical shovel: 40–130x (not comparable on mechanised tasks) |

The critical insight: writing and thinking machines give modest per-worker gains (1.2–2x), but they apply across *all knowledge work simultaneously* — 50%+ of GDP. A 2x gain across half the economy is historically unprecedented even if no single worker sees a 10x improvement. Doing machines are where the gains approach industrial-revolution scale for individual workflows.

## Part V: Tesla and the Redefined Product

### Why Tesla Goes After Ride-Sharing

Tesla's evolution illustrates the deepest production function insight: AI doesn't just make existing products cheaper — it makes *new products* economically viable.

The traditional car manufacturing production function: *Y_cars = f(K_factory, K_robots, L_workers)*. Output is vehicles. Revenue is captured once at the point of sale.

The ride-sharing production function with human drivers: *Y_rides = f(K_vehicles, L_drivers, A_platform)*. Uber and Lyft's take rate is approximately 40% of gross bookings; the remaining 60% goes to drivers.

Tesla's autonomous ride-sharing production function: *Y_mobility = f(K_vehicles, K_AI, K_charging, K_maintenance)*. With *L_drivers → 0*, the cost structure transforms:

| Component | Uber (per mile) | Tesla robotaxi (projected) |
|---|---|---|
| Driver compensation | $1.00–1.50 | $0 |
| Vehicle + operations | Driver-borne | $0.20–0.37 |
| **Total cost** | **$2.00–3.00** | **$0.20–0.37** |
| Platform margin | ~40% of fare | ~100% of fare |

By replacing *L_drivers* with *K_AI*, Tesla doesn't just reduce costs. It captures 100% of the margin instead of 40%. The entire driver compensation pool becomes profit. The product changes from "a car you buy" to "per-mile mobility you subscribe to."

This pattern — technology eliminating a labour input, which changes what the product *is*, which unlocks a larger market — has played out before. Netflix replaced store clerks with algorithms; the product changed from "video rental" to "streaming entertainment." Google replaced media buyers with automated auctions; the product changed from "advertising" to "targeted attention." In each case, the production function changed not just in its inputs but in its *output variable*.

### The Coasean Question

Ronald Coase's theory of the firm (1937): firms exist because internal coordination is cheaper than market transactions. The firm's boundary sits where the cost of one more internal transaction equals the cost of conducting it externally.

AI reduces coordination costs in *both* directions — inside the firm and in the market. This creates a genuine ambiguity. In industries with high physical integration (vehicles, construction, manufacturing), AI likely favours larger, more vertically integrated firms. Tesla controls 80% of its supply chain. In information-intensive industries that can be modularised (software, consulting, creative), AI may favour smaller firms and freelancers — hence the rise of the N-of-1 company.

The doing machine is what makes this possible. When an AI agent can manage procurement, scheduling, compliance, and subcontractor coordination, the overhead of vertical integration drops dramatically. A Tesla-style company becomes feasible in industries where it previously wasn't.

## Part VI: Construction — The Worked Example

### The Fifty-Year Decline

Construction is the critical counter-example to the narrative that technology inevitably transforms production functions. Real output per worker in U.S. construction in 2020 was more than 30% lower than in 1970. The sector has been declining at 1% per year for fifty years while the broader economy grew at 1.6%. Construction alone accounts for approximately one-third of the decline in trend U.S. GDP growth since World War II.

In Australia, the situation is equally stark. Physical productivity has more than halved over thirty years. The lost opportunity costs $62 billion per year. The structural root cause is fragmentation: 462,939 construction businesses in Australia, 91% of which are micro-businesses with fewer than five employees. With 91% micro-businesses, there is essentially no capacity for R&D, technology investment, or process innovation.

### The Two Constraints

The author identifies two constraints that keep housing expensive and addressable markets small:

**The capital constraint:** Australia's 5-year major public infrastructure pipeline has reached $242 billion — its highest ever. The machines that do land development (excavators, graders, earthmoving equipment) are disproportionately deployed on roads, rail, and mining. Residential land development competes for the same scarce capital stock.

**The labour constraint:** Australia is projected to be short 300,000 construction workers by 2027. Bricklaying, tiling, roofing, carpentry, and plastering are all severely short-staffed. Only 57% of advertised trades roles are filled. Wages are growing at 5.5%, nearly double the broader economy.

Both constraints push up costs. The National Housing Accord target of 1.2 million homes over five years is already projected to fall short by 262,000.

### The Numbers

Median house price in Sydney: $1.6 million. Price-to-income ratio: 13.8x — "impossibly unaffordable" (second globally, behind only Hong Kong). A median-income household needs 10.6 years to save a 20% deposit. Home ownership among 25–29 year olds: 36%. The proportion of suburbs affordable for first home buyers has fallen from 57% (2017) to 16% (2025).

Construction cost for a standard 200m² home runs $1,800–2,800 per square metre in regional areas, $3,200–4,300 in Sydney. Add land ($280,000–$410,000), site preparation, approvals, and connections, and a realistic total for a new build is $700,000–$1.2 million in capital cities.

### The Tesla of Construction

What would an AI-native, vertically integrated construction company look like? It would deploy all four machines across the entire value chain — from land development through to finished home:

**Writing machines:** Permit applications, contracts, specifications, marketing, compliance documentation. Construction technicians spend 30% of working hours on administrative tasks. This is immediately addressable.

**Thinking machines:** Design optimisation, structural analysis, cost estimation, supply chain planning, site selection. AI can optimise designs for manufacturability and cost while adapting to local conditions.

**Doing machines:** Project management, procurement, scheduling, quality control workflows, subcontractor coordination. This may be the most valuable application. Construction's core pathology is fragmentation — 462,000 micro-businesses, each with their own margins, each needing coordination. Doing machines could orchestrate the entire value chain, eliminating the overhead of managing 10–20 subcontractors per home.

**Perceiving machines:** Autonomous earthmoving, robotic construction (Hadrian X: 500 blocks per hour versus a human mason's 60), drone monitoring, quality inspection. The Australian robotics company FBR's Hadrian X is already building homes in demonstration programmes.

### Can You Halve the Cost?

The author proposes: larger homes, on larger land parcels, at half the cost, 1.5–2 hours from a city on a train line. Is this feasible?

Current cost for a 250m² home in a regional area 1.5 hours from Sydney: approximately $930,000 (land $280,000, construction $550,000, site preparation and approvals $100,000).

At 50% reduction: approximately $465,000. This moves the price from "impossibly unaffordable" to roughly 5x median household income — still expensive by international standards but a massive improvement.

Where could savings come from? Design and approvals (60–70% savings through AI automation), land development (30–40% through autonomous earthmoving at scale), materials (15–25% through bulk purchasing and waste reduction), labour (40–60% through prefabrication, modular assembly, and robotic construction), and overhead (30–50% through elimination of subcontractor markup chains). Cumulative: 35–50%. The 50% figure is aggressive but not impossible, given that construction productivity has already halved — meaning there is enormous waste to recapture — and that modular approaches already demonstrate 25–30% savings before AI optimisation.

### TAM Expansion: The Strong Argument

The author draws a sharp distinction between Tesla's TAM expansion argument for ride-sharing (weak) and the TAM expansion argument for cheaper housing (strong).

**Ride-sharing TAM expansion (weak):** "Because autonomous driving exists, more people will use ride-sharing." This requires a massive behavioural shift — giving up car ownership — with limited empirical evidence. Most robotaxi users were already using rideshare or personal vehicles. Car ownership is deeply embedded in culture and infrastructure.

**Housing TAM expansion (strong):** "Because homes cost half as much, far more people will buy homes." The evidence is overwhelming:

- Housing is a deep human need. The demand exists by definition.
- Price is demonstrably the constraint. Surveys consistently show renters want to buy but can't afford to.
- Structural estimates of the long-run price elasticity of housing demand in Sydney are approximately **-1.3** — demand is *elastic*. A 50% price reduction would increase quantity demanded by roughly 65%.
- Millions of Australians are in the latent demand pool. The 14-percentage-point decline in home ownership among 30–34 year olds represents hundreds of thousands of households who would own homes at historical affordability levels.
- Unlike ride-sharing, buying a home requires **no behavioural change**. People already want to buy. They just can't afford to.

Is total addressable market largely a function of price? For housing in Australia, the answer is emphatically yes. The rate of change of market size is approximately proportional to (and likely greater than proportional to) the rate of change of price, because the price elasticity exceeds unity in the long run.

## Part VII: The Equation, Rewritten

### Why the Old Equation Breaks

The classical production function treats technology as a single residual (*A*) — everything that isn't capital or labour. This was adequate when capital was dumb and could only amplify physical effort. A tractor amplifies digging. A loom amplifies weaving. But neither a tractor nor a loom can *think*.

When capital can write, reason, execute, and perceive, it stops being a complement to labour and becomes a substitute. The elasticity of substitution jumps from below 1 to above 3. The mathematical character of the production function changes qualitatively.

Treating writing machines, thinking machines, doing machines, and perceiving machines as a single "A" residual misses the point. Each substitutes for a different type of human labour. Each has its own cost trajectory, its own elasticity, and its own adoption curve. A lawyer needs writing machines. A consultant needs thinking machines. A project manager needs doing machines. An inspector needs perceiving machines. Lumping them together obscures what is actually happening in the production function.

### The Rewritten Equation

*Y = f(K_physical, K_write, K_think, K_do, K_perceive, L_human, A)*

Where:

- **K_physical** = traditional capital (machines, buildings, infrastructure)
- **K_write** = writing machine capacity (language model tokens, content generation, translation, code completion)
- **K_think** = thinking machine capacity (reasoning compute, analysis, planning, diagnosis)
- **K_do** = doing machine capacity (agent hours, workflow automation, autonomous task execution)
- **K_perceive** = perceiving machine capacity (computer vision, robotics, sensors, autonomous navigation)
- **L_human** = irreducible human labour (creativity, judgement, empathy, taste, physical presence, trust relationships)
- **A** = total factor productivity (institutions, culture, regulation, organisational design)

The prices of K_write, K_think, K_do, and K_perceive are collapsing. Inference costs are declining 10–50x per year. GPT-4-equivalent performance went from $20 per million tokens (2022) to $0.40 (2025). At $0.20–2.74 per hour, a doing machine costs 1/35th to 1/175th of a median knowledge worker.

But the equation doesn't just change in its inputs. It changes in its *outputs*. When Tesla replaces *L_drivers* with *K_perceive*, the product changes from cars to mobility. When a construction company replaces fragmented subcontractor coordination with *K_do*, the product changes from bespoke projects to standardised, scalable housing. The output variable *Y* itself is redefined.

### What Remains Human

The variable *L_human* doesn't go to zero. It changes in character. The scribe's labour was physical reproduction. The typist's labour was transcription. The computer user's labour was synthesis. The AI-augmented worker's labour is direction, taste, and judgement — choosing what to build, for whom, and why.

The irreducibly human contributions are: creative vision (conceiving what doesn't yet exist), ethical judgement (deciding what *should* exist), empathy (understanding what people actually need), taste (distinguishing good from adequate), and physical presence (being there, in trust relationships where it matters). These are not lesser contributions. They are the contributions that capital, however intelligent, cannot replicate — because they require not intelligence but *caring*.

## Conclusion: The Oldest Lesson

The production function has been rewritten before. It was rewritten when the spinning jenny turned one thread into eight. When the Bessemer converter dropped the cost of steel by 90%. When the assembly line cut production time from twelve hours to ninety-three minutes.

Each time, capital displaced workers from specific tasks. Each time, the cost of the affected output collapsed. Each time, demand exploded in ways no one predicted. Each time, new categories of work emerged. Each time, total output eventually exceeded anything the previous era could have imagined — though the transition was painful and slow, and the gains were unevenly distributed.

The four machines — writing, thinking, doing, perceiving — are rewriting the production function again. But there is an important asymmetry to notice. Writing and thinking machines give modest per-worker gains (1.2–2x) across the vast breadth of knowledge work. Doing machines give industrial-revolution-class gains (2–12x) in specific workflows. Perceiving machines give modest gains (15–30%) where physical work is already mechanised, but transformative gains (10–100x) where human perception was the bottleneck.

The gains are not uniform. They are not automatic. Construction's fifty-year productivity decline proves that technology alone is not sufficient — institutions, market structure, and regulation must evolve in parallel. The Solow Paradox proved that computers needed twenty years of complementary organisational investment before their productivity gains materialised. Goldman Sachs reports that AI contributed "basically zero" to U.S. GDP in 2025. The pattern is consistent: the production function is rewritten slowly, then all at once.

The biggest prize may not be in the industries where AI is currently most visible — software, customer service, content production — but in the industries where the production function has been stuck for decades. Construction. Healthcare administration. Government services. Education. These are the sectors where productivity has stagnated, where fragmentation prevents capital deepening, and where the combination of all four machines — writing, thinking, doing, and perceiving — could finally shift the equation.

The production function is being rewritten. The question is not whether. It is how fast, how broadly, and whether we build the institutions to match.
