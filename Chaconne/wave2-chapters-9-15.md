## Chapter 9: "Kothen, July 1720"

The road from Carlsbad was dust and heat and the smell of horses. Bach sat with his manuscripts on his knees and his wig in his bag and the white cotton cap he preferred for travelling pulled low against the afternoon sun, which came through the carriage window in a solid shaft and heated the leather until it was almost too hot to touch. He had been riding for three days. His stockings were grey with road dust. The coachman, a Saxon with a harelip whose name Bach had forgotten and did not intend to relearn, had been whistling the same four bars of a dance tune since Jena, and Bach had spent the last hour trying to determine whether the man was sharp on the third note or whether the intervals were simply unfamiliar. He concluded it was both. The man was sharp, and the mode was Mixolydian, and the combination produced a sound that was not wrong, exactly, but unresolved -- a phrase that wanted to go somewhere and could not find the door.

This was the quality of Bach's attention. He could not hear sound without hearing structure. The rhythmic clatter of hooves on packed earth laid down a ground bass over which the creaking of the chassis and the driver's tuneless whistle arranged themselves into a kind of rough polyphony. He did not choose to hear this way. It was simply how the world entered him -- not as noise but as pattern, not as chaos but as counterpoint waiting to be recognised.

He thought of Maria Barbara now with the absent, functional gratitude of a man thinking of a floor that holds his weight. She would have the household in order. The children would be fed. He would need to speak with her about Friedemann's shoes -- the boy was growing again -- and the organ at the Jakobskirche wanted tuning. Small things. The things a man thinks about when the large things -- the four-bar descent in D minor that he had been turning over for weeks like a stone in his pocket -- are too vast to hold consciously for long.

The partitas were nearly finished. The fair copy of the Sei Solo lay in the portfolio on his knees. The first four movements of the second partita were complete: Allemande, Courante, Sarabande, Gigue. The fifth -- the final movement, the one that would not behave -- existed in sketches and in the phrase that had lodged in his inner ear sometime during the winter and refused to leave. A chaconne. A ground bass, descending, D minor, four bars over which the violin would build everything. He could hear it: the voices moving against each other, the polyphony implicit in a single line, the moment when the key would lift from minor to major and the darkness would break open into light.

The carriage lurched through a village. A church steeple, a well, a woman hanging linen. Wheat fields beyond, the grain heavy and gold. Germany in high summer: the rich smell of turned earth and horse dung and something sweeter underneath, linden blossom. He thought of supper, of the study in the evening when the children were asleep and the candle burned and the only sound was the scratch of the pen. His fingers moved involuntarily on the portfolio's leather cover, finding positions that were not there.

The carriage turned. Kothen appeared, and Bach leaned forward to look for the smoke from his own chimney. He did not find it. The house had its shutters closed against the heat, which was ordinary, but the front door was open, which was not. Maria Barbara kept the door closed in summer. He had told her this was unnecessary. She had ignored him. The door was always closed.

It was open now. A woman stood in the doorway -- not Maria Barbara. A neighbour, Frau Schreiber. She was wearing black. She was holding a cloth in her hands, wringing it, the way a person wrings a cloth when their hands need something to do because the rest of their body has been told to stand still and wait.

Bach's hand tightened on the portfolio. The carriage had not yet stopped. But the music in his head -- the chaconne, the descending bass, the four bars that had been sounding without interruption for three hundred miles -- went silent, and in the silence he understood, before the words came, before Frau Schreiber's face resolved into its full and terrible sympathy, that the house was wrong, that the woman in the doorway was wrong, that everything he had been composing toward had arrived ahead of him and changed its key.

## Chapter 10: "London, Paris"

The light at Heathrow was the colour of dishwater. They came through the arrivals gate into it -- Bob pushing the trolley with four suitcases, Jeannie already scanning for the driver she had booked, Chase with his headphones on and his passport still in his hand, Shanel trailing behind them all with the overnight bag and the particular expression of a sixteen-year-old who has been awake for twenty-two hours and holds every adult in her vicinity responsible. The terminal smelled of floor cleaner and fried food and something else, something Bob had forgotten existed: the damp, composite, centuries-deep smell of a place where it had been raining for a thousand years.

"There." Jeannie pointed. A man in a dark suit held a card: HARRINGTON. She walked toward him with the brisk authority of a woman who had been rehearsing this arrival since the Qantas lounge in Perth, her heels clicking on the polished floor, her handbag swinging, her posture communicating to the terminal at large that she was not a tourist but a person with arrangements. Bob followed. He had learned, over twenty years, that following was the path of least resistance, and that the energy required to lead was never repaid in the currency Jeannie valued, which was the appearance of effortlessness.

In the car -- a Mercedes, black, the leather cool against the backs of their legs -- Jeannie's phone came out. She had been composing the first London post in her head since somewhere over the Indian Ocean, and now she tapped it into existence: a photograph of herself at the gate, the grey London sky behind her, the caption "And so it begins" with a single white heart emoji. She posted it before the car reached the M4. She checked the likes before Hammersmith.

Bob looked out the window. London in late June: the motorway giving way to terraced houses, their chimneys dark against a sky that could not decide between cloud and sun, the trees in their full summer heaviness leaning over garden walls, the red buses, the black taxis, the sheer stacked layered density of a city that had been building on top of itself for two thousand years. After Perth it was like stepping from a single-storey house into a cathedral. Perth was wide and flat and flooded with light; London was vertical and shadowed and everything was close. The buildings pressed in. The sky was a thin grey strip between rooflines.

Chase had fallen asleep, his head against the window, his mouth open. Shanel was scrolling her phone with the glazed incuriosity of a body present and a mind elsewhere. Jeannie was talking to the driver about the hotel, about Harrods, about the best route, and the driver answered with the measured politeness of a man who had been asked these questions by women like Jeannie every working day of his life.

Bob closed his eyes. He was not tired, exactly. He was displaced -- the feeling of arriving in a place where everything, the weight of the air, the colour of the light, the rhythm of traffic, was calibrated to a different frequency. Perth hummed at one pitch and London hummed at another, lower, more complex, a chord where Perth was a single note. He opened his eyes and looked at his hands on his thighs. The left one had curled again, the fingers closing softly around nothing. He straightened them, one by one, and put his hand flat on his leg.

Jeannie glanced at him in the rear-view mirror. "You're not even here," she said.

"I'm here," Bob said.

She held his gaze for a moment -- the flat, appraising look she used when she suspected him of an interior life she could not access or approve -- and then turned back to her phone. Twenty-three likes. She refreshed.

## Chapter 11: "Absam"

The road from Innsbruck ran east along the valley floor with the Inn River on the left and the mountains on both sides, close and enormous, their lower slopes dark with pine forest and their upper reaches bare grey rock that caught the morning sun and held it. Bob drove. Beside him Jeannie slept, her face in the unguarded softness of sleep looking younger and kinder than it did when she was awake and composing it. In the back seat Chase had his earbuds in and Shanel was reading -- actually reading, a paperback from the hotel lobby, her hair falling across her face in a way that made Bob think, with a small sharp ache, of the girl she had been at six.

He had told them he wanted to stop at a village for coffee. The mildest possible lie, and yet his hands on the steering wheel were damp. He had been carrying this destination for eleven days -- since the Musee de la Musique in Paris, since the moment on the Metro when he had typed "violin makers Tyrol" into his phone. Eleven days of knowing he would come here. Eleven days of not knowing why.

He passed Hall in Tirol, and then the sign: ABSAM. White letters on green. Houses built in the Tyrolean style, white plaster and dark timber, balconies heavy with geraniums. The mountains rose directly behind the village, so close that the upper houses seemed to lean against them, and the air through the open window carried the cold mineral smell of altitude and snowmelt and stone.

He parked on Stainerstrasse. The engine ticked in the silence. His heart was doing something it did not normally do -- a fast shallow rhythm he associated with the fund, with the moments when a client called and the number was a number he could not cover -- but this was not dread. This was the feeling of standing at a door you have been walking toward without admitting you were walking toward it.

"I'll catch up," he said. Jeannie opened her eyes. "Where are we?" "Absam. I just want to stretch my legs." "Fine. We need to be back for lunch."

He got out of the car. The air hit him -- cold for July, the mountains holding winter in their upper reaches -- and he stood on the cobblestones breathing. The children had not looked up.

He walked up the street. Past a brass plaque in German he could only half understand -- Jakob Stainer, violin maker, born in this village, 1617. And then the workshop. A low building, older than the houses around it, with small windows set deep in thick walls, and in the nearest window, catching the morning light: the scroll of a violin, its curve precise and dark against the glass.

The door was wooden, heavy, paint-stripped to grey. A brass bell on a spring. He pushed it open.

The bell rang -- bright, small, quickly dead, absorbed by the room. And then the smell. Spruce shavings, hide glue, varnish, rosin, something resinous and ancient that he could not name but that his body knew -- not from memory but from some deeper recognition, the way a migrating bird knows a direction it has never flown. The smell entered him and opened something, a chamber behind his sternum that had been sealed so long he had forgotten it was there.

The workshop was small and cluttered and organised in a way that made sense only to its inhabitant. A bench ran the length of one wall, covered in tools. Wood shavings curled on the floor like pale script. Violins hung from the ceiling at various stages of completion, and the light came from a large north-facing window -- constant, even, the light of a craftsman.

A man appeared from the back room. Small -- five foot six, perhaps less -- with white hair and a checked flannel shirt and a leather apron and hands that were disproportionately large, the fingers thick and extraordinarily steady. His face was deeply lined, brown as leather, with pale blue eyes that had the clarity of a person who has spent decades looking at things closely and has learned that most of what matters is invisible until you learn where to look.

"Guten Morgen." Then, registering something in Bob's clothes or posture: "English?"

"Australian," Bob said.

The man nodded. He had had Australians before. "Come in," he said. "Close the door. The cat goes out."

## Chapter 12: "Vienna"

The Musikverein rose from the Ringstrasse like a golden reliquary, its facade lit by the evening sun in a way that made the stone seem to generate its own warmth. Bob stood on the pavement and looked at it. He had never seen a building that looked like music sounded -- that had the proportions and the colour and the contained intensity of something you were meant to hear rather than see. Jeannie had booked the tickets because the building photographed well. She had declined to attend because two hours of classical music was, in her formulation, "not my idea of a holiday." Chase had laughed. Shanel had hesitated -- he had seen it, the brief flicker of interest before she read her mother's face and sided with the majority. Bob had taken the single ticket and walked here alone, with the violin case in his left hand and the lightness of a man who has been released, for a few hours, from the obligation to be witnessed.

Inside, the Goldener Saal. He had read about it on his phone but the reading had not prepared him. The hall was smaller than he expected, smaller than the Perth Convention Centre, but every surface was gold. Gold caryatids holding up the balcony, gold rosettes in the ceiling, gold mouldings framing painted panels. The seats were red velvet, worn to a nap. The stage was bare except for a music stand. No piano, no orchestra, no amplification. Just a wooden stage in a golden room, and the air had a quality he could feel against his skin -- a density, a readiness, as though the room itself were an instrument waiting to be played.

He found his seat. Third row from the back, left side -- far enough to be invisible, close enough to hear. Around him the audience settled: the rustle of programmes, the murmur of German and Japanese and English, the particular collective hush of a room full of people who have come to listen. He had never been in a room that hushed like this. At the yacht club, at dinner parties, at investor events, silence was a failure -- a gap that someone needed to fill. Here, the silence was the point.

The violinist walked onstage. A woman, young, in a black dress that left her arms bare. She carried her violin in her left hand and her bow in her right and she walked to the centre of the stage with the unhurried certainty of a person who has been going there her whole life. She did not smile. She stood, lifted the instrument to her shoulder, and waited.

The room went silent with a silence Bob had never experienced -- not the absence of sound but the presence of attention, six hundred people breathing together, opening together to receive whatever was about to come. She raised the bow. She held it above the string for a moment that lasted longer than a moment, a held breath, a suspension -- and then she drew it across and the first note entered the room and Bob's body responded before his mind could name what it was hearing.

The sound filled the Goldener Saal the way light fills a church through stained glass -- not all at once but in layers, the fundamental tone and then the overtones blooming above it, each one finding a surface in the golden room and reflecting back, so that the sound was not coming from the stage but from everywhere, from the walls and the ceiling and the three centuries of music that had seeped into the plaster and the gilt. One woman. One instrument. No safety net.

He did not know what she was playing. He knew only that the sound was reaching a place in him that nothing else had reached -- not the fund's terrors, not Jeannie's contempt, not twenty years of living inside a lie. The music walked through every door he had ever closed and stood in the room behind them and said, simply: here.

He wept. The tears came without permission, without sound, running down his cheeks and into the corners of his mouth, and he did not wipe them away because wiping them would have meant moving his hand and he could not move. He was held by the sound the way a river holds a stone. He had not cried in years. Not at his father's funeral, not during the worst nights of the fund. He cried now because the sound had found the thing he had buried and had called it by its name.

## Chapter 13: "Home"

The Indian Ocean was the colour of pewter in the early light and the Norfolk pines threw their shadows west across the terraced grass and everything was exactly as he had left it. The lawn mowed. The pool filter humming. The limestone facade of the house catching the first sun, the windows dark, the garden beds mulched and edged with the geometric precision of a place maintained by people who were paid to maintain it. Marine Parade at six in the morning: the joggers, the dog walkers, the retired couples performing their constitutional along the esplanade. Perth. The light that was not London's light or Paris's light or Vienna's light but its own -- wide, white, unforgiving, the light of a sky so large and so empty it made everything beneath it look provisional.

Bob stood in the kitchen in his socks, making coffee. The Breville -- Jeannie had chosen it because a woman she followed on Instagram had one -- hissed and thumped and produced a sound he had heard ten thousand times and that now, after three weeks in Europe, registered as the sound of a particular life resuming. The beans were stale. He ground new ones. The ritual of it -- the grinder, the tamping, the extraction, the milk -- was so deeply worn into his mornings that his hands performed it without instruction.

But his left hand was doing something. When it was not gripping the portafilter or steadying the cup, it was curling. The fingers closing around a shape that was not there -- the thumb pressing against the index finger, the remaining fingers falling into a curve that mapped precisely to the neck of a violin. He noticed it this time. He had been noticing it for weeks, but this morning, in the kitchen, with the sound of the ocean coming through the open window and the violin case standing upright in his study behind a closed door, the curling felt less like a twitch and more like a statement. His hand knew something his mind was still negotiating. His hand had already decided.

Jeannie was asleep. She would sleep until nine -- the combination of jet lag, wine, and sertraline thickening her sleep into something closer to unconsciousness. The children were in their rooms, similarly buried. The house was his for three hours, and the silence of it -- the particular silence of a large house in which everyone is sleeping and no one is performing -- was a mercy he had not felt since before Europe, before Franz, before the workshop and the smell of spruce and the moment he pushed open a door in a village he had never heard of and walked into the rest of his life.

He took his coffee to the study. A desk, a bookshelf, a window facing east. He had set the violin case here last night while Jeannie directed the unpacking from the hallway. She had not noticed. The case was black, cheap, anonymous, and it sat on the carpet with the mute patience of a thing that contains something extraordinary and does not need you to know it.

He closed the door. He knelt and opened the case. The violin lay wrapped in the cloth Franz had used -- soft cotton, old, smelling faintly of varnish and the workshop's particular air. He unwrapped it. In the Perth light, harder and less forgiving than Absam's, the instrument looked worse than he remembered. The cracks were visible, running from the f-holes in fine dark lines. The varnish was dull, its reddish-amber depth clouded. No strings. No bridge. The soundpost lay on its side against the back plate, a small wooden dowel dislodged from its position in the dark interior. It looked like junk.

He held it anyway. The weight against his palms, the curve fitting the space between his hands. The wood was cool and smooth and he could feel the grain beneath his fingers -- three hundred years of growth rings, each one a year of rain and sun in a forest he had never seen. His left hand curled around the neck. His chin lowered toward where the chinrest should be. The muscle memory fired -- not skill, not technique, but the pure animal knowledge of what this object was and where it belonged against his body.

He was home. He was holding a broken violin in a room in a house he had bought with money that was not his, in a marriage that was not a marriage, in a life that was not a life. And for the first time, standing there in his socks with the ocean audible through the wall and the coffee growing cold on the desk, the word home meant something other than an address.

## Chapter 14: "The Restoration"

The luthier's workshop was in Fremantle, in a lane behind the cappuccino strip, in a building that had once been a sail loft and still smelled, beneath the varnish and the glue, faintly of canvas and salt. Bob had buckled the violin case into the passenger seat. He did not notice he had done this -- the seatbelt drawn across the case the way you buckle a sleeping child -- until he was halfway down Stirling Highway and glanced left and saw it sitting upright, secured, held, as though the instrument inside it were a passenger who needed protecting from the sudden stops of a Tuesday morning in Perth.

The luthier was named David. Fifties, bearded, with the quiet deliberate manner of a man who had spent his professional life handling objects that could not be hurried. He had trained at the Newark School of Violin Making in England and worked a decade in London before returning to Perth because his wife missed the ocean and because, he said, the light was better. He meant this literally. The workshop's north-facing windows had been cut to his specification, flooding the benches with the even, shadowless light that a restorer needs to see what he is seeing rather than what he hopes to see.

Bob opened the case, lifted the violin with both hands -- gently, as though the instrument were asleep -- and set it on the cloth David had spread across his workbench. David looked at it.

His first reaction was professional scepticism. He picked it up the way a doctor picks up an X-ray -- with detachment, with the intention of seeing what was there rather than what someone wanted to be there. He turned it in the light. He looked at the scroll from the side, from below. He ran his thumb along the purfling -- three strips of wood, black-white-black, set into a channel a millimetre from the edge -- and his thumb slowed at the upper bout, slowed again at the corners, and stopped.

He reached for a loupe. A small brass eyepiece on a chain around his neck. He fitted it to his eye and leaned in, and for the next minute he did not speak. He examined the f-holes, their shape, the way the cuts tapered at each end. He examined the varnish -- sniffing, then tilting the instrument so the light caught the surface at different angles. He turned it over and studied the back, the maple's figured grain rippling like water beneath the surface as he tilted the wood.

He set the violin down. He removed the loupe. He looked at Bob.

"Where did you get this?"

Bob told him. Austria. A village called Absam. A luthier named Franz Kaltenbrunner.

David nodded. He did not speak for a moment. His hands rested on either side of the violin, not touching it, framing it, and his face had the expression of a man recalculating something.

"Do you know what you have?" he said.

"I know it's old," Bob said.

"It's more than old." David picked the violin up again, turning it slowly. "The purfling is hand-cut. Not machine, not stamped -- hand-cut, by someone who knew exactly what they were doing. The f-holes are --" He paused, choosing his words. "Consistent with something very fine. The varnish, the way it sits on the wood -- that's not a nineteenth-century imitation. That's not German trade work. That's something else."

He set it down. His hands had changed. They had been professional hands, competent, unhurried. Now they were careful hands. Reverent. The hands of a man who has been told that the object on his bench might be by someone whose name he knows.

"This needs work," David said. "The cracks need internal studs, glued from inside -- weeks of careful work. The soundpost reset. New bridge, new strings, the pegs checked, the seams re-glued. It's substantial." He looked at Bob. "But it's worth doing. It's very worth doing."

## Chapter 15: "First Lesson"

Katarina had twenty minutes between the twelve-year-old and the new student, and she used them the way she used every interval in her teaching day: cleaning up. The twelve-year-old -- Mia, quick fingers, the attention span of a budgerigar -- had left rosin dust on the music stand and the Suzuki Book 4 open to the Seitz concerto they had been dismantling for three weeks. Katarina closed the book, wiped the stand, straightened the chair. The studio was a converted room in Subiaco, off Rokeby Road: one north-facing window, the light at five-thirty turning the warm gold of a Perth autumn. A music stand, two chairs, a shelf of scores, a poster from a Brno Philharmonic concert she had played in 2010 -- Dvorak, the New World, second desk second violins, her hands shaking the entire first movement.

She took out her phone and reread the email.

It had arrived three days ago. The subject line: "Violin lessons -- adult beginner (sort of)." She received these monthly -- men in their forties or fifties who had played as boys and wanted to play again because they were navigating a divorce or needed a project that signalled renewal without requiring genuine change. They lasted three months. They wanted to play the theme from Schindler's List, and when they discovered that the violin demanded everything and returned nothing for weeks, they stopped coming.

This email was different. Four sentences. He had played from twelve to seventeen. He had stopped. He had recently started again. He would like a teacher. No bluster. Just the facts, with the economy of a person embarrassed to be writing. The last line: "I don't want to waste your time." Most people who said that were about to. But the way he wrote it suggested a man who meant it. Who was asking permission to want something.

She had said yes. Wednesday at six. His office on St George's Terrace -- odd, but she had taught at stranger locations. A garage in Dalkeith. A boardroom in West Perth where a mining executive practised scales with his tie loosened and his door locked. The western suburbs were full of people pursuing private passions in rooms designed for other purposes.

She packed her teaching case -- the Prokop violin, Czech-made, resting in its velvet; spare shoulder rest; rosin; Suzuki books; Schradieck exercises. She did not expect to need anything beyond Book 2. Three months. Then the slot would fill with another child.

She drove to the Terrace. The city in early evening: glass towers catching the last sun, suits streaming toward the train station, the peculiar emptiness of a CBD that empties at five-thirty as though someone has pulled a plug. She found the building, took the lift. HARRINGTON CAPITAL. She knocked.

The door opened, and the man behind it had the expression of someone who had rehearsed this moment for hours and forgotten every word. Tall, sandy hair greying at the temples, a face handsome in the way bank advertisements are handsome. His clothes were expensive and chosen by someone else. His right hand gripped the door handle. His left was doing something she recognised instantly: curling around an invisible neck, the fingers finding positions on strings that were not there.

"Come in," he said, too loud. "Thanks for coming. Can I get you a water?"

The office was large. A desk pushed aside -- she could see the marks on the carpet. In the cleared space: a music stand, new, the chrome shiny. A chair. A window showing the Terrace and the flat blue river beyond. He had prepared this room the way a man prepares a confession -- removing everything that did not belong, making room for the thing that did.

He was holding a violin case. Not a student case. Old, battered, with brass clasps polished recently and unevenly, the effort visible, the care visible. He set it on the desk and opened it, and Katarina glanced at the instrument inside and then looked again, and the glance became a gaze, and the gaze became the long arrested attention of a professional encountering something that does not match the room it is in.

She filed it away -- the wood, the varnish, the scroll -- and turned to the man and said: "Play for me."
