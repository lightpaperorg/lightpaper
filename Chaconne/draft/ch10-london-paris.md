# Chapter 10: London, Paris

The light at Heathrow was the colour of dishwater. They came through the arrivals gate into it -- Bob pushing the trolley with four suitcases, Jeannie already scanning for the driver she had booked, Chase with his headphones on and his passport still in his hand, Shanel trailing behind them all with the overnight bag and the particular expression of a sixteen-year-old who has been awake for twenty-two hours and holds every adult in her vicinity responsible. The terminal smelled of floor cleaner and fried food and something else, something Bob had forgotten existed: the damp, composite, centuries-deep smell of a place where it had been raining for a thousand years.

"There." Jeannie pointed. A man in a dark suit held a card: HARRINGTON. She walked toward him with the brisk authority of a woman who had been rehearsing this arrival since the Qantas lounge in Perth, her heels clicking on the polished floor, her handbag swinging, her posture communicating to the terminal at large that she was not a tourist but a person with arrangements. Bob followed. He had learned, over twenty years, that following was the path of least resistance, and that the energy required to lead was never repaid in the currency Jeannie valued, which was the appearance of effortlessness.

In the car -- a Mercedes, black, the leather cool against the backs of their legs -- Jeannie's phone came out. She had been composing the first London post in her head since somewhere over the Indian Ocean, and now she tapped it into existence: a photograph of herself at the gate, the grey London sky behind her, the caption "And so it begins" with a single white heart emoji. She posted it before the car reached the M4. She checked the likes before Hammersmith.

Bob looked out the window. London in late June: the motorway giving way to terraced houses, their chimneys dark against a sky that could not decide between cloud and sun, the trees in their full summer heaviness leaning over garden walls, the red buses, the black taxis, the sheer stacked layered density of a city that had been building on top of itself for two thousand years. After Perth it was like stepping from a single-storey house into a cathedral. Perth was wide and flat and flooded with light; London was vertical and shadowed and everything was close. The buildings pressed in. The sky was a thin grey strip between rooflines.

Chase had fallen asleep, his head against the window, his mouth open. Shanel was scrolling her phone with the glazed incuriosity of a body present and a mind elsewhere. Jeannie was talking to the driver about the hotel, about Harrods, about the best route, and the driver answered with the measured politeness of a man who had been asked these questions by women like Jeannie every working day of his life.

Bob closed his eyes. He was not tired, exactly. He was displaced -- the feeling of arriving in a place where everything, the weight of the air, the colour of the light, the rhythm of traffic, was calibrated to a different frequency. Perth hummed at one pitch and London hummed at another, lower, more complex, a chord where Perth was a single note. He opened his eyes and looked at his hands on his thighs. The left one had curled again, the fingers closing softly around nothing. He straightened them, one by one, and put his hand flat on his leg.

Jeannie glanced at him in the rear-view mirror. "You're not even here," she said.

"I'm here," Bob said.

She held his gaze for a moment -- the flat, appraising look she used when she suspected him of an interior life she could not access or approve -- and then turned back to her phone. Twenty-three likes. She refreshed.

---

Jeannie's London was a series of interiors designed to be photographed. Harrods on the second morning: the gleaming counters, the chandelier in the perfume hall, the hushed abundance of a place that existed not to sell things but to make you feel that being near things was itself a form of achievement. She and Shanel moved through the ground floor in a practised formation -- Jeannie leading, Shanel half a step behind, both of them touching fabrics, testing scents, holding up sunglasses and tilting their heads at the same angle. They did not buy much. The prices were beyond even Jeannie's comfort, and she had the Perth woman's instinct for value, which meant she could appreciate what she could not afford without feeling diminished by the gap. She photographed Shanel trying on a hat in the millinery department. She photographed the food hall from an angle that made the displays look infinite. She photographed her own hand holding a teacup in the café, the Harrods logo visible, the caption already forming: "London mornings."

Chase had gone to Camden. He had announced this at breakfast with the air of a man escaping a burning building, and Jeannie had let him go because Chase on the cultural itinerary was Chase at his most sullen -- arms crossed, phone out, radiating the specific contempt of a nineteen-year-old boy who considers himself too old for tourism and too young for appreciation. Without him the trio functioned more smoothly. Jeannie could direct, Shanel could mirror, and Bob could carry the bags.

Bob carried the bags. He followed them through Harrods and then through the Victoria and Albert Museum, where Jeannie curated their route around the photogenic rooms -- the Medieval Gallery with its stained-glass light, the Fashion Gallery where she stood Shanel in front of a McQueen gown and photographed her photographing it, the jewellery rooms that Jeannie moved through with the reverent attention she brought to nothing else in her life. She was good at this. Bob could see it -- the eye for composition, the instinct for light and angle, the ability to frame a moment so that it looked effortless when in fact every element had been chosen. In another life, with less money and more necessity, it might have been a real skill. A career. Instead it was an Instagram account with four thousand followers, most of whom were women she knew or women who knew women she knew, and the difference between a career and an account was the difference between making something and performing something, though Jeannie would not have recognised the distinction and would have been wounded by it if she had.

At the V&A, Bob lingered. Jeannie and Shanel had moved on -- they were in the shop now, Jeannie debating a silk scarf -- and Bob stood alone in a room full of musical instruments. A gallery he had not planned to enter. He had followed a sign -- MUSIC -- and found himself in a space of glass cases and silence, the kind of room that museums keep at the margins, popular with school groups and a certain kind of solitary visitor.

Keyboards, mostly. Harpsichords with painted lids showing pastoral scenes. A clavichord so small it might have been a child's toy. And in a corner case, a violin. Italian, eighteenth century, the label read. The varnish was dark and cracked and, beneath the cracks, a colour that was not brown or red or orange but something that contained all three, a depth that the overhead light could not fully penetrate. The scroll was carved with a precision that seemed to exceed the capacity of human hands. Bob stood in front of the case and looked at it. He looked at it for a long time.

He did not feel awe. He did not know enough to feel awe. What he felt was simpler and more disturbing: his left hand, at his side, had curled again. The thumb pressed against the index finger. The remaining fingers fell into a curve that mapped to nothing in his current life and everything in his former one -- the neck of a violin, the position of the hand in first position, the shape his body had held five afternoons a week for five years in a music room at Scotch College while Mrs Albrecht's metronome ticked on the piano and the afternoon light came through the high windows and the sound of his own playing filled the space between his ears with something that was not thought and not feeling but a third thing, a thing that had no name, a thing he had not experienced since.

He straightened his hand. He left the room. On the street outside, the noise of London closed around him: buses, taxis, the particular urban murmur of a city that never quite stopped talking to itself. He walked back toward the shop where Jeannie was buying the scarf.

He did not mention the instrument room.

---

The next afternoon, while Jeannie and Shanel visited a restaurant in Mayfair that someone had recommended on Instagram, Bob told them he was going for a walk. "Clear my head," he said, and neither Jeannie nor Shanel questioned it, because Bob going for a walk to clear his head was the blandest, most Bob-like activity imaginable, the kind of thing a man does when he has nothing better to do and the women in his family are doing something better without him.

He walked to the Royal Academy of Music. He had Googled it in the hotel room that morning, on the toilet, the door locked, the search history later deleted with the reflexive caution of a man who understood, without quite articulating why, that what he was doing needed to be private.

The museum was small and free and nearly empty. A room of instruments. A room of portraits -- men in wigs, mostly, their faces stern with the particular sternness of people who have been told to sit still for a long time. And then the display cases.

A Stradivarius. The label said 1709. The varnish glowed under the museum lights -- not reflected light but something that seemed to originate within the wood itself, an amber warmth that Bob felt rather than saw, the way you feel a radiator before you touch it. The instrument was behind glass. It was impossibly beautiful. Not beautiful the way a painting is beautiful -- not composed, not arranged -- but beautiful the way a bone is beautiful, or a shell: a form that has been refined by function until function and form are indistinguishable, until the thing exists in the precise and only shape it could exist in.

He stood in front of the case. Two minutes. Five. A schoolboy brushed past him, bored, heading for the keyboards. The room was quiet. The Stradivarius sat in its glass case and did not move and did not make a sound, and Bob stood before it with his hands at his sides and his left hand curling and his throat tight with something he would not have been able to name if you had asked him, something that was not grief and not longing and not regret but a compound of all three, the way a chord is not three notes but the space between them.

He left. On Marylebone Road the traffic roared and the students streamed toward the Academy carrying instrument cases -- violin cases, cello cases slung over backs, trumpet cases swinging -- and they were all young, all twenty or twenty-two, walking into their musical lives with the careless assumption that the lives would be there when they arrived. Bob watched them. He was forty-seven years old. He was a stockbroker from Perth with greying hair and an extra ten kilos and a left hand that would not stop curling around the ghost of something he had abandoned at fourteen. He was pressing his face against a window, looking in at a room he had left three decades ago, and the glass was cool against his skin and the room was exactly as he remembered it and he could not get back in.

He walked back to the hotel. He did not mention the Academy to anyone.

---

Paris was Jeannie's city. She had been three times before -- twice with girlfriends, once on a couples' weekend with Bob that she remembered as a success and he remembered as a performance -- and she spoke of it with the proprietary fondness of a woman who believed that appreciating a place was the same as understanding it. She wore a new dress from a boutique in the Marais. She had planned every meal. She had a list of cafés that photographed well.

The Instagram operation intensified. In London, Jeannie had posted once or twice a day -- a controlled output, curated, each image considered before it went live. In Paris the frequency doubled. The café crème on the marble table, the wicker chair, the street behind. The croissant, torn open, the steam (or what she imagined was steam -- the croissant had been cool for five minutes, but the angle suggested warmth). The zinc bar at a restaurant in Saint-Germain. The Seine at dusk. Shanel, always Shanel -- Shanel in front of Notre-Dame's scaffolding, Shanel on the Pont des Arts, Shanel in the Tuileries with her hair catching the light and a scarf tied around her neck that Jeannie had bought her at Le Bon Marché, knotting it with a gesture that was both tender and possessive, adjusting it, stepping back, assessing.

"You look French," Jeannie said, and Shanel smiled, because her mother's approval was the only sun she knew.

Chase had detached. He texted "going out" each morning and disappeared into the city, returning late, smelling of beer and cigarettes and the sweet, acrid funk of a body that had walked all day in summer heat. He offered no account of where he had been and Jeannie did not press. His absence simplified the composition. Without Chase, the family photographs resolved into the image Jeannie preferred: herself and Shanel, elegant, European, the mother-daughter narrative that was her most successful content stream.

Bob drifted. He accompanied Jeannie to the restaurants, carried the shopping, agreed to the gallery visits, said the right things. "That looks great on you." "Should we try that place?" "Another wine?" He had been saying these things for twenty years and the mechanism was so deeply worn into his social surface that the words came without requiring thought, the way a pianist's left hand maintains the bass while the right hand attends to the melody. His left hand. Always his left hand.

On the third morning he told Jeannie he was going for another walk. She was doing her makeup in the bathroom, the mirror surrounded by the cosmetics she had arranged on a white towel in the order she would use them, the ritual that preceded every public appearance and that took between forty and sixty minutes depending on the complexity of the day's Instagram requirements. She did not look up.

"Where?"

"Just wandering. There's a museum I saw on the map."

"Which museum?"

"Musical instruments. Historical ones."

She paused. The mascara wand hovered. "Are you going through a thing?" she said.

"What kind of thing?"

"I don't know. A museum thing. A middle-aged man looking at old things thing."

"It's a museum, Jeannie. We're in Paris."

"Fine." She returned to the mirror. "Be back by one. I booked lunch."

He took the Metro. Line 5 to Jaurès, then the short walk to the Cité de la Musique in the 19th arrondissement, the Musée de la Musique, a place he had found on his phone and that nobody in his family would voluntarily visit. The museum occupied the upper floor of a building designed by Portzamparc, all glass and curves, and the collection was arranged chronologically: Renaissance instruments, Baroque, Classical, Romantic. Lutes, viols, harpsichords, organs, and everywhere violins -- behind glass, on stands, hung from walls, the wood darkened by centuries, the varnish cracked and beautiful, the shapes identical and individual the way faces are identical and individual, each one recognisably a violin and each one unmistakably itself.

He put on the headphones the museum provided. A recording played: a violin, unaccompanied, a single line that moved through the space between his ears with a clarity and a closeness that made the sound seem to originate not from the headphones but from inside his skull. He did not know what it was. The piece was slow, then not slow -- a melody that turned in on itself and found, within the turning, a kind of freedom, a depth that exceeded what a single instrument should be able to contain. One violin. One voice. But the voice contained multitudes -- harmonics, implied harmonies, the shadow of a bass line beneath the melody and the ghost of a soprano above it, as though the single line were not one voice but three, or four, or the memory of all the voices that had ever played this instrument in this room in this city.

He closed his eyes. The museum fell away. The other visitors, the glass cases, the chronological march from Renaissance to Romantic -- all of it receded, and he stood in the dark behind his eyelids and the sound filled him the way water fills a vessel, not displacing what was there but finding the shape of it, occupying the exact contours of the emptiness he had been carrying for thirty years without knowing it was empty.

The piece ended. He opened his eyes. The museum reassembled itself around him -- the lights, the cases, a Japanese couple reading a plaque, a guard standing by the door with the vacant patience of a man who had stood by that door for years. Bob took off the headphones. His hands were shaking. His eyes were wet and he could not account for it -- the emotion had come from somewhere below language, below thought, from the same place the curling of his left hand came from, the body's knowledge of what the mind refused to acknowledge.

He wiped his face. He told himself it was jet lag. The wine at dinner last night. The strangeness of being in a foreign city, the sensory overload, the accumulated tiredness of two weeks of travel. He told himself these things and did not believe them, the way a man with a lump tells himself it is nothing and does not believe himself and does not make the appointment.

He left the museum. On the street outside, the Parc de la Villette spread green and sunlit and full of people who were not experiencing a crisis of recognition on a Tuesday morning. He walked to the Metro. He descended the stairs. He stood on the platform and the train came and he got on and sat down and took out his phone.

He typed "violin shops Austria." He deleted it. He typed "violin makers Tyrol." He stared at the results. He typed "Absam." A Wikipedia article. A village in the Inn Valley. Population three thousand. Birthplace of Jakob Stainer, violin maker, 1617--1683. A luthier's workshop. Stainerstrasse. He tapped through to a website -- simple, almost primitive, a few photographs of instruments and a workshop and an old man in a checked shirt. The family was going to Innsbruck in four days. Absam was fifteen minutes east along the valley floor.

The train rocked. The tunnel lights flashed. Bob sat on the Metro with his phone in his hand and his left hand curled around the phone's edge as though it were something else, and he knew -- not in words, not as a decision, but as a certainty that had arrived from outside thought -- that he would go to that village. That he would walk into that workshop. That something was waiting for him there, or that he was waiting for himself there, and that the two things were the same.

He arrived at the restaurant at ten past one. Jeannie had already ordered. She was composing a story about the coquilles Saint-Jacques, photographing the plate from an angle that made the portion look larger than it was.

"Where were you?" she said. Not looking up.

"The museum. Took longer than I thought."

"Was it good?"

"It was fine."

She lost interest. She always lost interest when his answers were short and bland, which was why he kept them short and bland. The exchange was their native language: he offered nothing and she accepted nothing and the nothing sat between them on the starched tablecloth alongside the bread basket and the butter and the two glasses of Chablis she had ordered, one for each of them, though Bob would barely touch his and Jeannie would finish both.

---

The trip continued. Milan, briefly -- the Duomo, which even Jeannie found overwhelming, though her response was to photograph it rather than to stand in its presence; the Galleria Vittorio Emanuele, where she bought Shanel a pair of sunglasses that cost more than some of Bob's school shoes; a restaurant near the Navigli where Chase reappeared, sunburned and suspiciously cheerful, having spent the day at a lake with backpackers he had met at the hostel bar the night before, though he was not staying at a hostel, he was staying at the Park Hyatt, a detail he had evidently omitted from the conversation.

Venice. Two nights. The gondola -- Jeannie insisted, Chase refused, Shanel agreed with the weary compliance of a girl who had learned that resistance cost more than acquiescence. Bob and Jeannie and Shanel in a gondola on the Grand Canal at sunset, the buildings gold and pink in the dying light, the water dark and iridescent, and Jeannie photographing it all with the concentrated urgency of a woman preserving evidence of a life she was living.

Bob saw the city differently. He saw it the way a man sees a city when something has been opened in him that was not open before: not the surfaces but the sounds. The splash of the gondolier's oar. The echo of footsteps in a narrow calle. The church bells -- layer upon layer of them, each one a different pitch, a different decay, the bronze voices overlapping in a polyphony that was not composed but was, to a certain kind of ear, more beautiful for being accidental. Venice was a city built on water and sound. Every surface reflected both.

On a bridge near the Rialto, on their last evening, Bob stopped. A young man was playing a violin. Badly. His intonation wandered, his bowing arm was stiff, and the piece -- something Baroque, a dance, Bob did not know it -- emerged from his instrument the way a butterfly emerges from a chrysalis: crumpled, wet, struggling, but alive. The violin case was open at his feet. A few coins, a couple of notes. The young man played with his eyes closed and his face turned slightly upward, toward the evening sky, and the expression on his face was not the expression of a performer but the expression of a person doing the only thing he knew how to do.

The family walked on. Jeannie called his name from the foot of the bridge -- "Bob!" -- the one-word summons she used, the tone of a woman recalling a dog that had wandered from the path. Chase and Shanel were already across. Bob stood on the bridge and listened for another minute, watching the young man's left hand, the fingers moving on the strings with a certainty that his ear could not match, the effort and the feeling and the inadequacy and the courage of it. He reached into his pocket. He dropped a twenty-euro note into the case. The young man opened his eyes, nodded, closed them again, and kept playing.

Bob walked to his family. He did not look back. But the sound followed him across the bridge and down the calle and through the evening and into the hotel room, where Jeannie was posting a gondola sunset and Chase was on the phone to someone in Perth and Shanel was in the bathroom with the door locked, and Bob sat on the edge of the bed in the dark and held his left hand in his right and pressed the fingers flat and they curled back, gently, insistently, finding the positions on strings that were not there.

In four days they would be in Innsbruck. Absam was fifteen minutes away. He had the address in his phone. He had the name of the street. He had the image of an old man in a checked shirt in a workshop full of violins.

He did not know what he would find there. He knew only that he was going.
