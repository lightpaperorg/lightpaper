# Chapter 16: Cremona, 1715

The hands woke before the man. They always did. In the dark of the bedroom above the workshop, Antonio Stradivari lay on his back and felt his fingers stir against the linen sheet -- the knuckles swollen, the joints stiff with the particular cold of late autumn in the Po Valley, where the damp rose from the river and settled into the stone walls and from the stone walls into the bones of anyone old enough to have stopped resisting it. He was seventy-one. He had been making violins for fifty-three years. Every morning now began with this negotiation between his will and his hands, and every morning the hands won eventually, loosening by degrees, the index finger of the left hand uncurling from the permanent crook that decades of holding a chisel against maple had carved into the tendon. He waited. Beside him, Antonia breathed the slow, even breath of a woman twenty years younger who did not need to bargain with her body for the right to begin the day.

He rose without waking her. Down the stairs, the stone cold under his bare feet -- a cold he had felt ten thousand times, and which no longer registered as discomfort but simply as the texture of descent, of the passage from sleep to work, from the horizontal life to the vertical one. The workshop occupied the ground floor of the house on Piazza San Domenico, the same room he had worked in since he was a young man copying the arching templates of his teacher Niccolo Amati and thinking, with the quiet certainty that was his defining characteristic, that he could do better. He had done better. He had done better for half a century, and he was not finished.

He lit a candle. Then a second, placing it on the far side of the bench so the light fell across the work from two angles, the shadows cast by the instrument's curves doubling and shifting as the flames steadied. The smell of the workshop entered him the way a prayer enters a man who has prayed the same words for fifty years: not through attention but through the body, through the lungs and the skin. Spruce shavings. Hide glue, its faintly animal warmth. The mineral tang of the varnish ingredients in their stoneware jars along the back wall -- the dragon's blood, the turpentine, the linseed oil, the colophony, and the others, the ones he did not name even to his sons, the ground coat that penetrated the wood and did something to its voice that he could hear but not explain and would never write down.

He put on the white linen apron. The white cap. He stood before the bench.

On it, clamped in a cradle of his own design -- two curved blocks of walnut lined with felt, holding the body without touching the plates -- was a violin in the late stages of construction. The top plate of Alpine spruce was fitted and glued, the bass bar set, the purfling inlaid in its narrow channel around the edge, three strips of wood -- black, white, black -- bent and laid with a precision that would survive three centuries of handling and still be identifiable as his. The back plate was maple, the flame figure running diagonally across the surface in a pattern that caught the candlelight and released it, caught it and released it, as though the wood were breathing. Today he would work on the scroll.

He picked up the gouge. He tested the edge against his thumb -- a habit so old it preceded thought, the way breathing precedes thought. The steel was sharp. He turned the scroll toward the light, studied the line of the volute where he had stopped yesterday, and began to cut.

The maple parted without sound. The curl fell to the bench in a tight, pale spiral.

---

The scroll was the signature. Every maker's hand could be read in the volute's curve, in the depth of the fluting, in the tightness of the final turn -- the way every man's character could be read in his handwriting, if you knew how to look. Stradivari's scrolls were deeper-cut than Amati's, more assertive, the chamfers sharper, the eye of the volute slightly larger. He had been carving them for fifty years. Each one was different. Each one was unmistakably his.

He did not think of this as artistry. The word would not have occurred to him, and if it had, he would have found it imprecise. He was a maker of instruments. His father had been a maker of instruments. The genius -- if it was genius, and he would not have used that word either -- was in the hands, in the accumulated knowledge of tools and materials and the ten thousand small decisions that separated a good violin from a great one, and a great one from whatever his instruments were, the ones that left his workshop and went into the world and never came back because no one who owned one would willingly let it go.

He worked for two hours. The candles burned low. The light in the street-side window changed from black to grey to the thin amber of a Lombard autumn dawn, the sun filtering through the narrow streets and finding his hands. He did not notice the transition. He noticed only the wood. The curl of maple falling from the volute in a continuous spiral, each cut removing exactly the amount he intended, no more and no less.

---

The sons arrived at eight. Francesco first, forty-four, heavy and deliberate, opening the shutters and taking his place at the far bench. Omobono at half past -- thirty-six, leaner, quicker. He picked up a back plate and began planing, the maple curls falling to the floor in tight spirals that mixed with the spruce shavings, two different woods, pale and golden, accumulating in drifts against the bench.

They did not speak. The workshop assumed its working rhythm -- the scrape of the plane, the soft percussion of the gouge, the occasional creak of a clamp -- and the silence between the sounds was not absence but presence, the shared understanding of men who communicated through the quality of their cuts rather than the content of their conversation. Stradivari did not look up. He was carving the final turns of the volute, the tightest part of the spiral, where the gouge had to follow a radius so small that the slightest tremor would ruin the line, and his hands, despite the swelling, despite the cold, despite seventy-one years, did not tremble.

At mid-morning he climbed to the attic. The attic ran the length of the house, low-ceilinged, smelling of dry wood and dust and the faint sweetness of resin. Here the spruce blanks were seasoned -- wedges of Alpine spruce cut in the high valleys, split along the grain, stacked with spacers, left to dry for years. Some had been here for a decade. He ran his thumb along a wedge, feeling the growth rings, tight and uniform -- the wood of a tree that had lived through cold winters and short summers where the air was thin and the timber, as a consequence, was dense and even and possessed of an acoustic clarity that no lowland wood could match.

He tapped the wedge with a knuckle. Listened. The wood rang with a particular sustain, a brightness that was not sharpness but projection. He tapped another. The sound was duller, the ring shorter. He returned to the first, tapped again. Nodded. Set it aside for the next instrument.

---

In the afternoon a visitor arrived. A musician from Milan -- young, vain, rings on his fingers that Stradivari noted with distaste. A player should not wear rings.

The musician had come to collect a violin completed the previous month. He lifted it with the easy familiarity of a man who handled violins daily, tuned it with practiced speed, and began to play. Something from Corelli -- showy, the bow arm working the string crossings with a flashy facility that impressed audiences and said nothing. The sound filled the workshop, bouncing off the stone walls, off the jars of varnish, off the hanging instruments in their various states. The workshop became, for a moment, a concert hall.

Stradivari listened. But he did not hear the music. He heard the instrument.

He heard the top plate's response in the upper register -- slightly bright, the spruce a fraction thin at the treble f-hole, a compromise he had made in the graduation and regretted. He heard the bass bar's resonance -- good, full, the wolf tone at the G string managed but present, a faint growl at the fifth position that he could suppress further on the next instrument by adjusting the bar's taper at the lower end. He heard the projection -- how the sound pushed against the stone walls, filled the corners, carried. And he heard a flaw. A slight hollowness in the mid-register, around the A above middle C, where the arching of the top plate and the volume of air inside the body interacted in a way that produced a fractional dead spot, a place where the sound thinned and lost its warmth for the span of a single note.

No one else in the room heard it. The musician heard only his own playing, which was competent and flashy and entirely focused on the surface of the sound rather than its architecture.

Stradivari heard the dead spot again. It would be there for as long as this instrument existed, and it would cost him sleep tonight, and he would correct it on the next instrument -- adjusting the graduation of the top plate by half a millimetre at the position where the air resonance and the plate resonance interfered. Half a millimetre. The difference between adequate and what he was reaching for, the thing that had no name but that he could hear the way a man with perfect pitch hears a note that is a quarter-tone flat -- not as a concept but as a physical sensation, a wrongness in the ear, an itch that could only be scratched by the next instrument.

The musician finished. He lowered the violin. He beamed.

"Maestro," he said. "It is perfection."

Stradivari, not looking up from the scroll, said: "It is adequate."

The musician laughed, taking this for the false modesty of a great man. It was not false modesty. It was not modesty at all. It was the plain statement of a man who could hear the gap between what the instrument produced and what he had intended, and who would spend the remaining years of his life -- twenty-two more years, twenty-two more years at this bench, at this work -- closing that gap by increments so small they could be measured only by the ear and not by any instrument of science.

The musician paid. He left. The bell on the door rang and fell silent. Stradivari returned to the scroll.

---

Evening. The sons had gone. Antonia had called him for supper twice. He had not gone. The workshop was candlelit again, the two flames steady in the still air, the shadows of the instruments on the walls shifting as the flames breathed. The scroll was finished.

He set down the gouge. He lifted the scroll in both hands and held it to the candle. Turned it slowly. The volute tightened in its spiral, the fluting clean and even, the chamfers sharp, the eye precise. The shadow it cast on the wall moved like a living thing -- the spiral tightening, loosening, a form that had no beginning and no end, the same geometry that governed seashells and galaxies and the uncoiling frond of a fern, rendered in maple by a man's hands and a chisel and fifty years of practice.

He fitted the scroll to the pegbox. Tested the alignment -- the angle of the neck, the way the scroll's centre line continued the instrument's axis. It was correct. Tomorrow the neck would be shaped, and the week after that the varnishing would begin -- the ground coat first, brushed on in thin layers, penetrating the grain, and then the coloured coat, the mixture he had refined over decades and guarded absolutely, the orange-red that was not painted onto the wood but seemed to rise from within it, as though the colour had always been there and the varnish merely made it visible. And in a month, or six weeks, this instrument would leave the workshop and go into the world and he would never see it again.

He held the violin -- the body with its new scroll, its unvarnished wood pale and naked in the candlelight. He held it the way he held every instrument before it left his hands: not as a product or a commodity or a thing he had made, but as something that was about to begin a life he could not imagine and would not be present to witness. He had made over a thousand instruments. Each one carried his hands' knowledge into the world. He did not think about posterity. He did not imagine that three centuries from now, people would speak his name with a reverence that bordered on the sacred, that his instruments would sell for more than palaces, that scientists would scan them with machines he could not conceive, trying to discover in the grain and the varnish and the geometry of the f-holes the secret of a sound they could hear but not reproduce. He thought about the scroll. He thought about the dead spot he had heard that afternoon. He thought about the next instrument, which would be better.

He set the violin down on the bench. He blew out one candle. He stood in the half-dark, his hands resting on the bench's edge, the wood worn smooth by his forearms over forty years, and he looked at the instrument that was almost complete. It did not yet have a name. It would acquire many names, over many years, as it passed through many hands -- musicians, dealers, collectors, the occasional man or woman who played it not for display but for love, and whose hands would leave their own invisible mark on the wood, their own sweat and rosin and warmth absorbed into the grain alongside his.

One of those hands, three hundred and eight years from now, would belong to a man in a small shop in the Austrian Alps who would hold this violin to the north light of a workshop window and see in the grain and the varnish and the scroll the unmistakable signature of the maker who stood here now in the candlelight, an old man in a white apron, in a city on the Po, in the autumn of a year that would later be called the heart of the golden period, though the man himself would not have known what that meant and would not have cared. He would have cared only that the scroll was right and the dead spot would be corrected and the next instrument would be better, because the next instrument was always better, and the reaching was the point, and the reaching never ended, not at seventy-one, not at eighty, not at ninety-three when they finally carried him from this room for the last time and the workshop went dark and the bench went cold and the tools lay still.

He blew out the second candle. He climbed the stairs. His hands ached in the dark. He did not mind. The ache was the proof that the hands had worked, and the work was the proof that the hands were alive, and the life in the hands was all there was. Tomorrow he would shape the neck. And then the next instrument. It was always the next instrument.

The workshop below him was silent. The violin on the bench waited, as wood always waits -- patient, resonant, full of a sound that would not be heard for centuries, already there in the grain, in the rings, in the years of cold winters and short summers stored in the fibres of a tree felled and split and carried to a city on a plain and shaped by an old man's hands into something that would outlive them both, the tree and the man, by three hundred years and counting.
