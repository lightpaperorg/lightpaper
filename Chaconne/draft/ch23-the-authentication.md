# Chapter 23: The Authentication

He sat at the desk in the outer office -- the fund side, the side with the Bloomberg terminal and the blinking phone and the compliance folders and the photograph of Jeannie and the children at Rottnest -- and he composed an email. He had been composing it for three days. Not writing it -- composing it, the way a man composes a letter to a lover, or a resignation, or an apology that might save his life: choosing each word, weighing each sentence, reading it back and deleting and starting again, because this email mattered more than any communication he had ever sent, more than the quarterly reports with their fabricated returns, more than the investor letters with their reassuring prose, more than the love note he had written Jeannie on their first anniversary that she had kept in a drawer for a decade and then, during one of her reorganisations, thrown away.

The recipient was a name he had found on a website: David Saunders, Saunders & Associates, Melbourne -- one of the foremost authenticators of fine stringed instruments in Australia, a man who had trained at J & A Beare in London and established his firm with connections to the major European laboratories. Bob had also drafted a version addressed to Bein & Fushi in Chicago, and a third to Tarisio, the auction house that had sold more Stradivari instruments than anyone alive. He would send only one. He did not want the enquiry to circulate, to become gossip in a world that was, he had learned from his reading, surprisingly small -- a few hundred people worldwide who could tell a Stradivari from a Guarneri from a Stainer from a good Cremonese copy, and who talked to each other constantly, the way people in any rarefied field talk, trading rumours and sightings and the occasional breathless story of a lost instrument surfacing in an attic or a garage sale or a small shop in a village in the Austrian Alps.

*Dear Mr. Saunders,*

*I am writing to request your professional assessment of a violin in my possession. The instrument was purchased in 2023 from a luthier in Absam, Austria, who described it as a fine Tyrolean instrument of uncertain age. Since acquiring it, I have had it examined by a Perth-based luthier who believes it to be of early eighteenth-century Cremonese origin.*

He stopped. He had written "possibly from the school of Stradivari" and deleted it. Too much. Too soon. Saunders would stop reading, or worse, he would sigh the weary sigh of an expert who receives a hundred emails a year from hopeful amateurs who think their grandfather's violin is a Strad because the label says *Antonius Stradivarius Cremonensis Faciebat Anno 17--* and a date that may or may not be legible. Bob knew the label meant nothing. Millions of violins carried Stradivari labels -- they had been pasted into cheap factory instruments since the nineteenth century, a tradition of aspirational forgery so widespread that it was not even considered fraudulent, merely conventional, the violin world's version of a designer knockoff. He was not writing about the label.

He was writing about the wood. The varnish. The scroll.

He opened the photographs on his laptop. He had taken them two nights ago, alone in the soundproofed room after the last practice session, using the desk lamp to create raking light across the surfaces, the kind of oblique illumination that revealed what direct light concealed. The scroll from four angles -- front, left, right, and from below, looking up into the pegbox where the chisel marks were visible, the precise cuts of a hand that knew exactly how deep to go and exactly when to stop. The f-holes in close-up, their elegant S-curves slightly asymmetric in the way that all hand-cut f-holes are asymmetric and that the best authenticators can read like a signature. The varnish in raking light, its surface crazed with a network of fine cracks -- craquelure, the word was, the pattern of ageing that forms over centuries as the organic compounds in the varnish oxidise and contract, a pattern that cannot be faked because it cannot be hurried, because it is the signature not of a maker but of time itself.

The back plate. The maple's flame figure, the tight curls of grain running diagonally across the surface, catching the light and releasing it in waves. The interior, photographed through the left f-hole with a phone torch: the bass bar, the linings, the corner blocks, the subtle graduation marks where the maker had carved the plates to their final thickness, the wood thinner at the edges and thicker at the centre in a pattern that determined the instrument's voice as surely as the shape of a larynx determines a singer's.

He attached the photographs. He resumed writing.

*I attach photographs taken in controlled lighting conditions. I am particularly interested in your assessment of the scroll carving, the arching profile, the varnish composition and craquelure pattern, and the f-hole geometry. I understand that a definitive attribution would require physical examination and dendrochronological analysis, and I am prepared to arrange both.*

He read it back. It was too formal, too stiff, the language of a man who had spent his career writing investor correspondence and could not shed the register. But it was also precise, and precision was what these people respected -- not enthusiasm, not hope, but the careful, evidence-based language of someone who understood the difference between asking "Is this a Stradivarius?" and asking "What can you tell me about this instrument?"

He changed the subject line. He had tried *Instrument Assessment Request* and *Authentication Enquiry* and *Violin -- Possible Early 18th Century Cremonese*. He settled on: *Request for Assessment -- Violin of Unknown Provenance, Possibly Cremonese*.

His finger hovered over the send button. His hand was shaking. Not from tendonitis -- that had receded, managed now, a chronic companion rather than an acute crisis. His hand was shaking because this email would begin a process he could not control and could not take back, a process that would end in one of two verdicts: the violin was what he suspected it was, and everything changed, or the violin was not what he suspected, and nothing changed except the loss of a hope he had not admitted to himself he was carrying.

He pressed send.

The email left his outbox and entered the world, irretrievable, travelling at the speed of light to a server in Melbourne where it would sit until a man named David Saunders opened it and looked at the photographs and either deleted it with the other hopefuls or -- and this was the possibility that made Bob's chest tight and his breathing shallow -- or paused. Leaned in. Looked again.

---

Three days passed. Bob did not check his email obsessively. He checked it constantly, which was different -- the checking was not the frantic refreshing of a man waiting for news but the steady, metronomic monitoring of a man who could not stop his thumb from swiping down on the inbox the way he could not stop his fingers from finding the positions on the fingerboard, the body moving of its own volition, the conscious mind merely a passenger. He checked in the morning before practice. He checked between Chaconne passages. He checked at red lights on the Mitchell Freeway and in the bathroom at home and in the garage at midnight when he could not sleep.

On the third day, Saunders replied.

The email was brief. Professional. Bob read it standing at the window of the soundproofed room, the violin still warm in his left hand, the bow hanging from his right, the sweat of practice drying on his forehead.

*Mr. Harrington,*

*Thank you for your enquiry. I have examined the photographs you provided with considerable interest.*

Considerable interest. Bob read the phrase twice. He had spent enough time in the authentication forums -- the late-night reading, the threads on maestronet and violinist.com where collectors and dealers and the occasional authenticated owner discussed provenance and attribution with the intensity of theologians debating doctrine -- to know that "considerable interest" was not the language of polite rejection. Polite rejection said "interesting instrument" or "well-made example" or "the label is of course not a reliable indicator." Considerable interest meant the photographs had shown him something.

*The scroll carving, arching profile, and varnish craquelure patterns are consistent with an instrument of significant age and quality. Several features -- particularly the f-hole geometry and the apparent two-layer varnish system visible in your raking-light photographs -- merit closer examination.*

*I would strongly recommend a physical inspection at our Melbourne laboratory, together with dendrochronological analysis of the top plate. We work with the Berger Laboratory in Hamburg, which maintains the most comprehensive master chronology for Alpine spruce used by the Cremonese workshops. I should note that the dendrochronology alone will take six to eight weeks from the date of sampling.*

*I must be candid: I receive many enquiries of this nature, and the vast majority of instruments submitted for examination prove to be later copies, however well made. But the photographs you have sent are -- if they are an accurate representation of the instrument's current state -- among the more compelling I have seen from a private owner.*

*Among the more compelling.* Bob sat down. The chair caught him the way it had caught him after the D major breakthrough, his legs deciding on their own that standing was no longer possible. He read the email again. He read "among the more compelling I have seen from a private owner" and the words did something to his chest -- a tightening, a heat, the physical sensation of hope arriving in a body that had been braced against it.

*I attach a preliminary overview of our examination process and fee schedule. The full battery -- dendrochronology, CT scanning, varnish analysis via infrared spectroscopy and scanning electron microscopy, and expert panel assessment -- runs to approximately AUD $18,000. I appreciate this is a significant investment. However, given the potential implications of the instrument's features, I would consider it a prudent one.*

*I look forward to hearing from you.*

Bob put the phone on the desk. He looked at the violin in his hand -- the varnish glowing in the afternoon light, the reddish-orange that deepened to amber in the scroll, the craquelure catching the light the way the surface of water catches the light, with a depth that suggested layers beneath. He had been playing this instrument for more than two years. He knew its body the way a lover knows a body -- every curve, every grain line, every place where the varnish was thin and the wood showed through. He had pressed his jaw against it for thousands of hours. He had drawn the bow across its strings ten thousand times. The sound it produced had changed his life, had given him the only honest experience of his adult existence, had opened a door into a room where the person he was supposed to be had been waiting for thirty years.

And now a man in Melbourne was telling him, in the measured language of professional caution, that the instrument might be what he had secretly, desperately, impossibly hoped it was.

He picked up the phone. He typed a reply.

*Mr. Saunders, thank you. I would like to proceed with the full examination. Please advise on logistics for shipping the instrument to your Melbourne laboratory. I will arrange insurance and transport.*

He did not hesitate over the fee. Eighteen thousand dollars. He would pay it from the fund's operational account, the way he had paid for the instrument itself -- ten thousand euros wired to Franz Kaltenbrunner's bank in Innsbruck, recorded in the fund's books as "Musical instrument -- investment purpose," a line item so absurd that any competent auditor would have flagged it, except that the fund had not had a competent audit in three years because Bob had been filing the returns himself, signing the compliance declarations, maintaining the fiction with the methodical diligence of a man who understood that a Ponzi scheme was, at its core, an administrative exercise.

He pressed send. Then he sat in the soundproofed room with the violin in his lap and thought about what he had just done. He had spent eighteen thousand dollars of his investors' money to authenticate an instrument he had bought with ten thousand euros of his investors' money. The violin was a fund asset. If it proved to be what Saunders suspected, its value would dwarf the fund's entire portfolio. The most reckless investment Bob had ever made -- a violin bought on impulse from a man in a leather apron in a village in the Austrian Alps -- might prove to be the most spectacularly successful. The irony was so precise it felt designed, as though the universe were constructing an elaborate joke whose punchline Bob could not yet see.

---

The violin went to Melbourne on a Tuesday, packaged by a specialist courier in a temperature-controlled case lined with museum-grade foam, insured for $100,000 -- a figure that felt both excessive and absurdly inadequate, depending on what the instrument turned out to be. Bob drove it to the airport himself. He carried the case through the freight terminal with both hands, the way he imagined parents carried newborns -- with a care that was physical and complete, the body arranged entirely around the protection of the thing it held. He watched the courier load it into the climate-controlled section of the cargo hold. He stood at the terminal window and watched the plane taxi and take off, and then he drove back to the office and sat in the soundproofed room and the room was silent in a way it had never been silent before, a silence that was not the presence of the foam but the absence of the instrument, the missing weight against his collarbone, the missing pressure on his jaw, the missing voice that had been speaking to him for two years and was now three thousand kilometres away in the hands of strangers.

He could not practise. For the first time since the tendonitis, the room was empty of music. He had the student violin -- the old Chinese factory instrument, still in the wardrobe at the house on Pearse Street where his mother lived alone now, Jim dead of a heart attack in 2019 -- but he could not bring himself to play it, not after two years with Franz's violin, the way a man who has tasted great wine cannot go back to cask. He sat in the chair. He ran the Chaconne in his mind -- the mental practice that Katarina had taught him, hearing each note, feeling each shift in his fingers without an instrument, the body rehearsing in the absence of the thing it rehearsed with. It was not the same. It was not close to the same. But it was all he had.

Three weeks passed. Four. The silence of the room pressed on him.

---

The fund could not wait. The fund had no interest in dendrochronology or provenance or the careful, unhurried process by which a man in Melbourne determined whether a piece of wood was three hundred years old or one hundred. The fund operated on a different clock -- the quarterly clock of redemption requests and performance reports and the relentless arithmetic of a Ponzi scheme in its final months, when the inflows had slowed to a trickle and the outflow demands were multiplying and the gap between what the fund owed and what the fund held was widening with the terrible, accelerating logic of compound deception.

Bob sat at the desk on the fund side of the office and did the mathematics he had been avoiding. The fund held approximately three million dollars in liquid assets -- equities that could be sold, cash in the operational account, a small position in a listed mining company that was itself barely solvent. The outstanding redemption requests -- formal and informal, acknowledged and deferred, the ones Bob had answered and the ones he had not -- totalled approximately eight million dollars. The gap was five million. Five million dollars that existed on the fund's books as assets but did not exist in reality, because the assets had been fabricated -- returns that were never earned, performance that was never achieved, the accumulated fiction of three years of fraudulent reporting compressed into a single, irreducible number.

Five million. The number sat on the spreadsheet and Bob looked at it the way he sometimes looked at a difficult passage in the Chaconne -- with the detached, analytical clarity of a man assessing an obstacle that could not be avoided but might, with sufficient ingenuity, be navigated. Except that there was no navigating this. The passage could not be simplified, the notes could not be changed, the music was written and the arithmetic was fixed and five million dollars was five million dollars, and no amount of smooth investor calls or deferred redemptions or fabricated quarterly returns could make it anything other than what it was: the distance between Robert James Harrington and prison.

The phone rang. He looked at the caller ID. ASIC. Not the junior inquiry officer who had called months ago with "routine questions" -- a different name, a more senior title, an 02 number that placed the caller in Sydney rather than the Perth regional office.

He answered. He had no choice -- not answering ASIC was itself a regulatory offence, a fact Bob knew because he had looked it up at three in the morning on a night when the fund's arithmetic had kept him awake and the Chaconne could not put him to sleep.

The voice on the line was measured, professional, unhurried. A woman. She identified herself -- senior investigator, enforcement division. She was calling in relation to the preliminary inquiry opened several months ago regarding the affairs of Harrington Capital Pty Ltd. She understood that Mr. Harrington had cooperated with the initial inquiry, and she appreciated that cooperation. She wanted to advise Mr. Harrington that the Commission had determined to escalate the matter. A formal investigation had been authorised under section 13 of the ASIC Act. The Commission would be requesting the production of documents. A formal notice would follow by registered post.

Bob listened. He made the appropriate sounds -- the mmm, the yes, the I understand -- the sounds of a man who was receiving information rather than absorbing it, the sounds of a body on autopilot while the mind went somewhere else. The mind went to the soundproofed room. The mind went to the empty stand where the violin should have been and was not, three thousand kilometres away, being examined by men with loupes and callipers and dendrochronological databases, men who were looking at the wood the way Bob looked at the Chaconne, note by note, ring by ring, searching for the truth inside the grain.

The woman asked if he had any questions. He said no. She said a formal document production notice would arrive within the week. She wished him a good afternoon. She hung up.

Bob put the phone on the desk. He sat very still. The fund side of the office was quiet -- the Bloomberg terminal dark, the message light blinking its tireless red, the compliance folders stacked in the wire tray where they had sat, unopened, for weeks. Through the wall, the soundproofed room waited. Empty. Silent.

He stood and walked to the window. The Swan River was visible between the glass towers of the Terrace, a strip of grey water catching the flat winter light. A ferry crossed from Elizabeth Quay to South Perth, white against the grey, leaving a wake that spread and flattened and disappeared. The city was ordinary. The afternoon was ordinary. A woman in Sydney had just told him that the full institutional machinery of the Australian government was about to descend on his life, and the city outside the window did not know and did not care and would not pause for even a moment to acknowledge that Robert James Harrington, fund manager, was standing at the edge of an abyss.

He walked to the soundproofed room. He sat in the chair. He could not play -- the violin was in Melbourne. But he could hear. He closed his eyes and the Chaconne played itself in his mind -- the opening chord, the descending bass, the variations building, the D major section opening like a door into the light -- and he sat in the music that was not there, the music that existed now only in his body, in the neural pathways that two years of obsessive practice had carved into his brain, and the music was the only real thing in his life and it was absent and the fund was collapsing and ASIC was coming and the violin was being measured by strangers and everything was converging on a point he could not yet see but could feel, the way you feel a wave building behind you before it breaks -- the lift, the surge, the moment when the water decides.

---

The email from Saunders arrived six weeks after the violin had left. A preliminary update. Not the final report -- that would take longer, he wrote, because the dendrochronology results from Hamburg were still pending and the expert panel had requested additional varnish sampling. But he wanted to share an interim observation.

Bob read the email in the car park of the Claremont IGA, where he had gone to buy milk because Jeannie had texted him to buy milk and he had driven there without thinking because the body did what the body was told, the habits of accommodation operating beneath the surface of a mind that was elsewhere, always elsewhere, always in the soundproofed room or the Chaconne or the space between the two.

*Mr. Harrington,*

*A brief update on the examination of your instrument (Ref: SA-2025-0847).*

*The preliminary dendrochronological results from the Berger Laboratory are, I must say, remarkable. The youngest tree ring in the top plate has been dated to approximately 1695 -- well within the working period of the major Cremonese workshops. The ring pattern shows a preliminary match of 94.2% against the master chronology established from authenticated Stradivari instruments produced between 1700 and 1725. The Hamburg laboratory has requested a second sample to confirm, which accounts for the delay in the final report.*

*Additionally, our varnish analysis has identified a two-layer system comprising a mineral ground coat and an organic coloured upper layer. The ground coat contains trace elements -- potassium silicate, iron oxide, zinc -- that are consistent with the chemical profile of authenticated Stradivari instruments from the so-called "Golden Period" (c. 1700-1720).*

*I want to be measured in my language, as I always am at this stage. These are preliminary results and the final report will incorporate the full panel assessment. But I would be remiss if I did not tell you that, in twenty-two years of examining stringed instruments, I have rarely seen an unprovenanced instrument present this consistently across multiple analytical dimensions.*

*I anticipate the final report within three to four weeks.*

Bob read the email twice. Three times. He sat in the IGA car park with the engine off and the milk warming on the passenger seat and he read the words *preliminary match of 94.2% against the master chronology* and *consistent with the chemical profile of authenticated Stradivari instruments* and *rarely seen an unprovenanced instrument present this consistently* and his hands began to shake, and the shaking was the same shaking as the D major breakthrough, the same shaking as the workshop in Absam, the same trembling of a body encountering something too large for its nervous system to process through any channel other than the involuntary firing of motor neurons.

He put the phone down. He picked up the milk. He walked into the IGA because he had forgotten something -- what had he forgotten? Bread. Jeannie wanted bread. He walked through the aisles in a daze, past the canned goods and the cleaning products and the magazines with their cover stories about renovations and diets and celebrity breakups, and he selected a loaf of sourdough and paid for it and carried it to the car and sat behind the wheel and did not start the engine.

Ninety-four point two percent. Against the Stradivari master chronology.

The violin he had bought for ten thousand euros from a man in a leather apron in a village in the Austrian Alps. The violin that Franz Kaltenbrunner had insured for thirty thousand dollars because that was what it was worth if it was what Franz had told him it was -- a fine Tyrolean instrument. But it was not a fine Tyrolean instrument. It was not a Stainer copy, not a nineteenth-century reproduction, not a well-made example of the Austrian school. The wood in the top plate had been alive in the seventeenth century, had grown in the Alpine forests during the Little Ice Age, had been harvested and split and seasoned and shaped by hands that might -- might, the word Saunders used was "consistent" -- have belonged to a man in a white apron in a workshop in Cremona who had been dead for three hundred years and whose instruments were worth more than the houses they were played in.

If the final report confirmed what the preliminary results suggested, the violin was worth between fifteen and twenty million dollars. He knew this because he had researched it with the same obsessive thoroughness he had brought to the Chaconne recordings -- the auction records, the private sales, the handful of Golden Period Stradivari instruments that had changed hands in the last decade. The Lady Blunt, sold in 2011 for $15.9 million. The Da Vinci ex-Seidel, sold in 2022 for $15.34 million. The Baron Knoop, sold in 2025 for $23 million. These were the comparables. These were the neighbourhood his violin might occupy.

The fund's deficit was five million dollars. The violin might be worth fifteen to twenty million. The arithmetic was so clean it felt like a parable -- the kind of story where the thing that saves you is the thing you least expected, the investment that no rational person would have made, the ten thousand euros spent on impulse in a moment of desperate hope turning out to be the most profitable transaction in the history of Australian financial mismanagement. Except that the violin was bought with fund money, which made it a fund asset, which meant that its value belonged not to Bob but to the investors he had defrauded, and the authentication would not save him -- it would save them, and the saving would be the final, definitive proof that the fund had been insolvent all along, because you do not sell a fund's assets to cover a fund's debts unless the debts are real and the reported returns are not.

Bob started the car. He drove home to Marine Parade with the milk and the bread and the phone in his pocket containing an email that might change everything and could change nothing, because the violin was not his to sell and the fund was not his to save and ASIC was coming and the formal investigation notice would arrive any day now, and the only thing he could do -- the only thing left -- was play.

He parked in the garage. He carried the groceries inside. Jeannie was at the island bench with a glass of wine and her iPad, scrolling through caterer portfolios for the birthday party, and she looked up and said, "Did you get the sourdough?" and he said yes, and she said, "The caterer can do the Saturday," and he said good, and the conversation was exactly the conversation it had always been, the exchange of domestic logistics between two people who shared a house and a mortgage and two children and twenty years of marriage and not a single true thing, and Bob put the milk in the fridge and the bread on the counter and went to the study and closed the door and sat in the chair and the empty violin stand looked back at him, and the room was silent, and the Chaconne played itself in his mind, and the preliminary dendrochronology results from a laboratory in Hamburg said that the wood in his violin had been growing when Antonio Stradivari was alive, and the ASIC investigation was coming, and the birthday was eight weeks away, and everything -- everything -- was converging on a single point, and the point was approaching, and Bob could feel it the way you feel a wave building, and he sat in the silence and waited.
