# Chapter 2: Nedlands, 1987

He found it on a Tuesday night, or a Wednesday -- a nothing evening in late November, the kind of Perth evening where the heat has broken but the air still holds the memory of it, and the house is quiet because Jeannie is upstairs and the children are elsewhere and the television is on but muted, the way Bob always has the television, the images washing over the room like light through water. He was on his phone. He was always on his phone in the evenings now, scrolling with the glazed purposelessness of a man who does not know what he is looking for but cannot stop looking, and the YouTube algorithm -- which had learned things about him he had not learned about himself -- had begun sliding violin performances between the market commentary and the football highlights. Thumbnails of women on stages, instruments tucked beneath their chins, bows poised. He scrolled past them. He scrolled past them every night. But tonight a thumbnail stopped him: a girl, young, twelve or thirteen, playing on a stage that looked like a school hall, and the angle of her left hand on the neck of the instrument -- the wrist flat, the fingers curving down over the strings with that particular concentrated stillness -- sent something through him that was not thought and not memory but something older than both: a sensation in his own hand, a weight beneath his own chin, the pressure of a string against the pad of his index finger, and he was eleven years old and it was 1987 and the bus was grinding up the hill to Scotch College with the sun coming through the windows and his school bag wedged between his knees and inside the bag, in a case that smelled of rosin and old cloth, was a violin.

He did not tap the video. He put the phone face-down on the couch and sat in the flickering light and let the memory arrive, which it did not as narrative but as a series of physical impressions: the weight of the case on his shoulder. The bus jolting over the speed bumps on Stirling Highway. The music room at Scotch -- its particular smell, floor polish and brass cleaner and the dusty warmth of instruments stored in cupboards that were never quite closed. And the teacher, the first teacher, a young woman whose name he could not retrieve -- not Mrs Albrecht, that was later, this was earlier, Year Seven, the beginning -- placing the violin under his chin and adjusting the chin rest with her thumb, and showing him how to draw the bow across the open D string.

The sound had been horrible. A scraping, nasal whine that made the other boys laugh and made his face burn and made the teacher say something encouraging that he did not hear, because beneath the horrible sound, transmitted through the chin rest into his jawbone and from his jawbone into his skull and from his skull into some chamber of his brain that had never been opened, was a vibration. Not a musical vibration -- not yet, not for months -- but a physical one, as if the string and the wood and the bone were all the same material and the bow had set all of it resonating at once. He did not know what to do with this sensation. He was eleven. He stored it in the place where children store things they cannot explain, below language, below thought, in the body itself, where it would remain for thirty-eight years like a seed in dry ground, waiting for water.

The phone buzzed. A notification from the fund's administrator -- something routine, something about quarterly filings. Bob picked it up, read the message, typed a brief response, and the room reassembled itself around him: the muted television, the empty couch, the Marine Parade house with its ocean views and its silence. He put the phone down. He sat in the dark for a long time.

---

The house on Pearse Street was three blocks from the beach and three decades from Marine Parade. A four-bedroom limestone cottage with a corrugated iron roof and a front garden where Wendy's roses competed with the native grevillea for space along the fence line, and neither won, which gave the garden a democratic quality that reflected nothing about the family inside except perhaps Wendy's refusal to impose hierarchy on living things. The carport held Jim's Volvo wagon, a beige 240 that he washed every second Saturday and drove with the cautious precision of a man who believed that predictability was the highest form of responsibility. The house was comfortable. It was not grand. This was a distinction that would matter to Jeannie when she first saw it, years later, and that did not matter at all to the boy who grew up in it, because when you are eleven years old in Cottesloe in 1987, three blocks from the beach is three blocks from the centre of the known world, and everything else is detail.

Jim Harrington wore a tie every day of his working life. He was a stockbroker at Patersons Securities, which meant he sat in an office on St George's Terrace and spoke to clients on the telephone and read the financial pages of the West Australian with the concentration of a man deciphering scripture. He was not a large man. He was not a loud man. He was a man whose emotional range ran from mild approval to mild concern, with nothing at either extreme, and he navigated the world by holding to the centre the way a sailor holds to the channel: not because the centre was exciting but because the centre was safe, and safety, for Jim, was the point. He loved his son. He demonstrated this through attendance -- at football matches, at swimming carnivals, at school assemblies where Bob sat in the middle of the middle row and received no awards and caused no trouble -- and through the application of his hand to Bob's shoulder at moments of moderate significance, a gesture that conveyed warmth without risking excess.

Wendy had been a legal secretary before Bob was born and never went back. She kept the garden. She played bridge on Thursdays at the Cottesloe Civic Centre with three women she had known since primary school. She cooked the same six meals in rotation and hung the washing on the line in the backyard where it dried in the salt air that came up from the beach and gave everything a faint, clean smell of ocean. She loved her son the way she loved everything: quietly, without drama, with a competence that asked for nothing in return and expected nothing in particular except that things continue as they were. When Scotch offered instrumental lessons beginning in Year 7, it was Wendy who suggested Bob take one. She thought it would round him out, which was the phrase she used, meaning: give him something beyond football and the beach, something that signalled cultivation without requiring commitment, something she could mention to the bridge ladies as evidence that the Harrington boy was developing nicely.

Bob chose the violin. The reasons, as he remembered them, were logistical. The piano could not be carried. The trumpet seemed aggressive, the instrument of boys who wanted to be noticed, and Bob did not want to be noticed. The cello was too big for the bus. The violin fit in a case he could sling over his shoulder and carry in one hand, a case that took up no more room than a school bag and attracted no more attention, and so the most consequential decision of his life was made for the same reason most consequential decisions are made: because it was the easiest thing to do at the time.

---

Mrs Albrecht's studio was a converted sunroom in a house on Monash Avenue, Nedlands, near the university. The house was brick, unremarkable, set back from the street behind a hedge that needed trimming, and the studio was at the rear -- a narrow room with frosted glass on three sides that let in a diffuse, pearly light that made the room feel like the inside of a cloud. A music stand, adjustable, scarred with years of clips and clamps. A metronome on a side table, the old mechanical kind, its pendulum motionless until Mrs Albrecht set it swinging with a flick of her finger. A poster of Yehudi Menuhin on the wall, torn at one corner and attached with yellowed tape, the great violinist caught in the middle of a bow stroke, his eyes closed, his face transported. Shelves of sheet music, arranged in no discernible order. The faint smell of cats, and of rosin, and of the peppermint tea Mrs Albrecht drank from a mug that said WORLD'S BEST OMA.

Her real name was Ingrid. She was German-born, from Hannover, and had come to Perth in the 1960s with a husband who taught mathematics at UWA and who had died in 1982 of a heart attack at his desk, mid-equation, a death so characteristic that Mrs Albrecht mentioned it sometimes with a faint smile that was not quite fond and not quite bitter but occupied some German territory between the two. She had supplemented the household income by teaching violin to western suburbs children whose parents wanted them rounded out, and after her husband's death the supplementary income became the primary income, and she taught with the patience of a woman who had been disappointed by life and had made her accommodation with the disappointment. She was not inspired. She was not severe. She was observant, in the way that people who spend their lives listening are observant, and she noticed things that other teachers -- busier, more ambitious, more invested in exam results and competition trophies -- might have missed.

Bob came to her in Year 8, after the group lessons at Scotch had done what group lessons do: taught him the basics, shown him that he was neither the best nor the worst, and deposited him at a level where further progress required the focused attention of a private teacher. Wendy found Mrs Albrecht through another bridge lady. The lessons were Tuesday afternoons, after school, and Bob rode the bus from Scotch to Nedlands and walked from the bus stop to Monash Avenue carrying the violin case on his shoulder, and for the four years between Year 8 and Year 11 this journey was the fixed point of his week, the one appointment he did not cancel and did not dread and did not think about in advance, because thinking about it would have meant acknowledging that it mattered, and acknowledging that something mattered was, for Bob, already a form of vulnerability.

The progression was unremarkable. Year 8: squeaking gave way to something approaching melody. The Suzuki books, those patient architectures of incremental difficulty, carried him from open strings to first position to the tentative beginnings of second position. Year 9: a Handel gavotte, simplified, that Mrs Albrecht assigned and Bob practised with an inconsistency that drove her to the only sharp remark he ever heard from her: "Robert, the music does not care whether you feel like practising. It waits for you, but not forever." Year 10: Grade 3, then Grade 4. A Bach minuet that came and went. A Vivaldi slow movement that came and stayed, that lodged in his fingers and his memory and would not leave, and that he played alone in his room at night with the door closed and the sound turned down to a whisper, as if the music were something he was stealing.

He was not gifted. Mrs Albrecht knew this. His intonation was unreliable -- he could hear when a note was wrong, which was more than most of her students could do, but the distance between hearing and correcting was a distance his fingers could not always cross. His bowing arm was stiff. He held the bow as if it might escape, a tension that transferred to the string and gave his playing a pinched, careful quality that was the opposite of what music required. He lacked the discipline for scales and the competitive instinct for examinations. Boys with less natural ability but more structure had passed him. He reached Grade 4 by Year 10, which was respectable. Respectable was the word Jim would have used. It was not the word that described what happened when Bob played.

What Mrs Albrecht saw was this: when Bob played -- not exercises, not scales, not the examination pieces he prepared with the grudging diligence of a boy completing homework, but actual music, a slow movement, a real piece, something with a melody that moved and breathed -- something happened to him. He disappeared. Not into competence. Not into the trance of technical mastery that her best students entered, their bodies efficient, their brains calculating intervals and bow distribution. Bob disappeared into the sound itself. His body relaxed. The stiffness in his bowing arm released. His face changed -- the careful, pleasant blankness that she had come to recognise as Bob's public face fell away, and what was underneath was not a face at all but a condition, a state of being, an absorption so complete that she could have left the room and he would not have noticed. He was not performing the music. He was inside it. He was living in it the way you live in a room that fits you perfectly, a room you did not know existed until you walked through its door.

She told Wendy, at the end of Year 10, that Bob had a genuine musical ear but not the discipline to develop it. This was accurate. She did not say what she also believed, which was that the ear was the rarer thing, and that discipline could be taught but the ear could not, and that it was a small tragedy -- not a large one, not a newsworthy one, but a tragedy nonetheless, the kind of quiet tragedy that happens a thousand times a year in a thousand music studios where children with real ears are rounded out and sent home -- that no one would teach him what his ear was worth.

The key moment, the one Bob would remember and not remember for thirty years, happened on a Tuesday evening in his bedroom on Pearse Street. He was fourteen, or fifteen -- the year did not matter, the evening mattered. Homework was done. The door was closed. He had taken the violin from its case and tuned it -- badly, by ear, without a tuner, because he had never owned a tuner and did not know they existed in electronic form -- and he played the slow movement of a Handel sonata. Not the whole sonata. Just the slow movement, the second movement, a larghetto in G major that Mrs Albrecht had assigned and that he had prepared adequately for his lesson and that he now played, alone, in his room, with the door closed, for no one.

The sound filled the small space. It was not a beautiful sound -- his intonation was imperfect, his vibrato rudimentary, the violin itself a Czech factory instrument that had been designed to be adequate and was -- but it was his sound, the sound of his fingers on those strings and his bow on that instrument and his breath and his body and the vibration that passed through the chin rest into his jaw and from his jaw into his skull and from his skull into the chamber that had been opened that first day in Year 7 and that had been slowly, quietly, expanding ever since. And the experience was this: the music opened a door into a room he had not known existed. A room where he was not Robert Harrington of Pearse Street, not Jim's son, not a B-average student at Scotch who played First XVIII without distinction and sat in the middle of every classroom. In this room he was not anything. He was a consciousness inside a sound, and the sound was the room, and the room was infinite, and the door stayed open as long as the music lasted and closed the moment he stopped.

He played the phrase again. And again. And the door stayed open.

He never spoke of this. Not to Wendy, who would have been pleased and puzzled and would have mentioned it to the bridge ladies. Not to Jim, who would have nodded and patted his shoulder and said "Good on you, mate," which would have been worse than silence because it would have reduced the thing to the scale of Jim's understanding. Not to Mrs Albrecht, who might have understood but who occupied the role of teacher, and the role of teacher was a public role, and this was not public. The music was private in the way that certain essential things are private -- not because they are shameful but because they cannot survive exposure. He understood this without articulating it. He practised when he felt like it, which was often. Always alone. Always with the door closed. Always stopping when someone knocked. The knocking was the world, and the world did not know about the room, and the room existed only as long as the door was closed and the music was playing and no one was listening.

---

The school concert was in the chapel at Scotch, a building that smelled of wood polish and hymn books and the particular institutional warmth of a place where boys were expected to be serious. It was evening. The chairs were arranged in rows and the parents sat in them and the overhead lights were too bright, the way institutional lights are always too bright, as if the building were trying to compensate for the fact that nothing of genuine consequence usually happened inside it. Bob was sixteen. Year 11. He had been persuaded -- by Mrs Albrecht, who believed in performance the way Jim believed in consistency, as a thing that was necessary whether or not it was pleasant -- to play in the annual music evening. He would play the Handel sonata. The slow movement. The one from his room.

He sat backstage in his school uniform with the violin on his knee and felt the wrongness of it like a change in altitude. The instrument was the same. The music was the same. But the room was different. The room was two hundred people: parents in good clothes, students slumped in the back rows, teachers standing against the walls with the alert boredom of adults supervising other people's children. Jim was out there somewhere, in his tie, with his hand ready for Bob's shoulder. Wendy was beside him, her handbag on her lap, smiling her quiet smile. Mrs Albrecht was in the second row, where she always sat at her students' performances, her hands folded, her face still.

He walked to the centre of the stage. He set the music stand. He placed the violin under his chin and raised the bow and in the half-second before the bow touched the string he knew, with a certainty that was physical, that was located not in his mind but in the tremor of his left hand and the tightness of his throat and the roar of his own blood in his ears, that the door would not open. Not here. Not with these lights and these eyes and this silence that was not the silence of his room but the silence of expectation, of assessment, of two hundred people politely waiting to hear something pleasant before the interval tea and biscuits.

He played. His hands shook. His intonation wobbled on the opening phrase and did not recover. The bow skated across the strings with the stiff, controlled pressure of a boy who was not making music but surviving exposure. He got through it. He did not stop. He did not break down. He played the notes in the correct order and at approximately the correct tempo and with a sound that was technically adequate and musically dead, because the thing that made his playing real -- the disappearance, the room, the door -- could not exist in front of people. The thing he loved most was the thing he could not share. The concert proved this with the blunt efficiency of an experiment confirming its hypothesis.

The applause was kind. Jim found him afterward in the corridor outside the chapel and patted his shoulder and said, "Well done, mate," and the words fell on Bob like dirt on a coffin. Wendy smiled. She said something about how proud she was. Mrs Albrecht caught his eye from across the crowd -- she was leaving, pulling on her coat, and their eyes met across the heads of the milling parents, and she gave him a look that he did not understand and that he would carry with him, unexamined, for thirty years: not pity, not disappointment, but recognition. She had been there. She had stood on a stage somewhere in Hannover or in Perth or in some other room where the lights were too bright and the silence was wrong, and she had played the notes and the door had not opened, and she had understood that there are people for whom music is not a performance but a condition, and that for such people the stage is not a home but a punishment.

He never performed again. This was not a decision he announced or explained. He simply did not sign up for the next concert, and no one asked why, because no one had expected anything in particular from Robert Harrington's violin playing, and the absence of an unremarkable thing is itself unremarkable.

Year 12 arrived like the future: inevitable, impersonal, concerned only with outcomes. Football. Exams. The TEE, as it was then, with its numerical rankings and its brutal simplicity: a number that determined where you went and therefore, in the logic of Perth in the early 1990s, who you became. Jim's expectation was clear without being stated. Commerce at UWA. Then Patersons. Then a life. The violin receded. Not violently -- it simply became less frequent, the way a language you do not use becomes less fluent, the words still there but harder to reach, the grammar softening, the accent fading. He practised sometimes. Less. Less again. Schoolies happened -- a week in Dunsborough with the boys, beer and bonfires and girls and the extravagant, unearned freedom of eighteen-year-olds who had finished exams and not yet started lives, and the violin did not come to Dunsborough, and when he returned it was January and the results were out and the number was fine and the future was settled.

The last time was a night in the summer after Year 12. The house on Pearse Street was quiet. Jim and Wendy were in bed. The air held the leftover warmth of a December day, and through his bedroom window Bob could see the stars above the Norfolk pines on the beach, and the street was still, and the silence was the right silence -- his silence, the silence of a room with a closed door.

He took out the violin. The Czech factory instrument, worth perhaps four hundred dollars, with its scratched varnish and its slightly warped bridge and the strings that had not been changed in a year. He tuned it. He placed it under his chin. The weight of it. The smell of rosin. The pressure of the chin rest against his jaw, the same pressure that had opened the chamber in his brain seven years earlier. He played the Handel. The slow movement. The door opened. He stood in the room for the duration of the music, and the room was the same room it had always been -- infinite, private, the place where he was no one and everything -- and he played the last phrase and the bow lifted from the string and the sound died and the door closed and he stood in his bedroom in the quiet and knew, without naming the knowledge, that he would not do this again.

He put the violin in its case. He closed the clasps. He put the case on the top shelf of his wardrobe, behind the sports trophies and the school blazer and the accumulated evidence of a boyhood that was, in every measurable way, successful. He did not play again for twenty-seven years.

---

The living room on Marine Parade. The television muted, its scrolling images throwing pale light across the ceiling. His phone on the couch beside him, the YouTube thumbnail still visible: the woman with the violin, her eyes closed, the bow raised, the posture of a body that has given itself entirely to something invisible.

Bob picked up the phone. He opened the video.

The sound filled the room. A violin, playing something he did not recognise, something in a minor key, something that moved with a deliberation that was not slowness but gravity, each note placed with the care of a stone being set into a wall, and the sound was not the sound of his student violin in a bedroom on Pearse Street but it was the same language, the same door, the same room heard from a great distance through thirty years of closed doors and sealed chambers and a life built on the principle that the things you love most are the things you must put away.

He listened for thirty seconds. The music moved. His left hand, resting on his thigh, moved with it -- the fingers pressing, finding positions, the muscle memory not gone but sleeping, stirring now, turning over in the deep place where the body stores what the mind refuses. He listened for a minute.

Jeannie's footsteps on the stairs.

He closed the app. The screen went dark. The sound stopped. The room was the living room again, the Marine Parade living room with its white walls and its Italian tiles and its photographs of a family that smiled and the television murmuring its muted, ceaseless commentary on a world that had nothing to do with the world behind the door.

Jeannie's footsteps came closer, reached the hallway, paused -- she was checking something, her phone, the thermostat, the lock on the front door -- and receded. The stairs again. Her retreat. The door closing. Silence.

Bob sat in the dark. The phone was warm in his hand. The screen was dark. The room was dark. The house was quiet in the way it was always quiet, the way that had nothing to do with sound, and somewhere beneath the quiet, beneath the not-thinking, beneath thirty years of a life built to the specifications of other people, a door that had been sealed shut was beginning, imperceptibly, to swell.
