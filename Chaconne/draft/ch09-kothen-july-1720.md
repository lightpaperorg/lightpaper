# Chapter 9: Köthen, July 1720

The road from Carlsbad was dust and heat and the smell of horses. Bach sat with his manuscripts on his knees and his wig in his bag and the white cotton cap he preferred for travelling pulled low against the afternoon sun, which came through the carriage window in a solid shaft and heated the leather until it was almost too hot to touch. He had been riding for three days. His stockings were grey with road dust. The coachman, a Saxon with a harelip whose name Bach had forgotten and did not intend to relearn, had been whistling the same four bars of a dance tune since Jena, and Bach had spent the last hour trying to determine whether the man was sharp on the third note or whether the intervals were simply unfamiliar. He concluded it was both. The man was sharp, and the mode was Mixolydian, and the combination produced a sound that was not wrong, exactly, but unresolved -- a phrase that wanted to go somewhere and could not find the door.

This was the quality of Bach's attention. He could not hear sound without hearing structure. The rhythmic clatter of hooves on packed earth laid down a ground bass over which the creaking of the chassis and the driver's tuneless whistle arranged themselves into a kind of rough polyphony. He did not choose to hear this way. It was simply how the world entered him -- not as noise but as pattern, not as chaos but as counterpoint waiting to be recognised.

He thought of Maria Barbara now with the absent, functional gratitude of a man thinking of a floor that holds his weight. She would have the household in order. The children would be fed. He would need to speak with her about Friedemann's shoes -- the boy was growing again -- and the organ at the Jakobskirche wanted tuning. Small things. The things a man thinks about when the large things -- the four-bar descent in D minor that he had been turning over for weeks like a stone in his pocket -- are too vast to hold consciously for long.

The partitas were nearly finished. The fair copy of the Sei Solo lay in the portfolio on his knees. The first four movements of the second partita were complete: Allemande, Courante, Sarabande, Gigue. The fifth -- the final movement, the one that would not behave -- existed in sketches and in the phrase that had lodged in his inner ear sometime during the winter and refused to leave. A chaconne. A ground bass, descending, D minor, four bars over which the violin would build everything. He could hear it: the voices moving against each other, the polyphony implicit in a single line, the moment when the key would lift from minor to major and the darkness would break open into light.

The carriage lurched through a village. A church steeple, a well, a woman hanging linen. Wheat fields beyond, the grain heavy and gold. Germany in high summer: the rich smell of turned earth and horse dung and something sweeter underneath, linden blossom. He thought of supper, of the study in the evening when the children were asleep and the candle burned and the only sound was the scratch of the pen. His fingers moved involuntarily on the portfolio's leather cover, finding positions that were not there.

Two months he had been away. Two months at Carlsbad with the Prince, playing for the waters -- the little concerts in the pump room, the evening entertainments, the endless polite sociability of court life at a spa where the nobility came to be seen taking the cure and the musicians existed to provide agreeable sound while they did so. Bach did not resent Leopold for it. The Prince was the best employer he had known or was likely to know: a man who understood music not as decoration but as substance, who played the viola da gamba with genuine skill, who sat in with his own ensemble and asked intelligent questions about voice leading. Leopold paid four hundred thalers a year. Leopold stood as godfather to the child they had named after him -- the boy who had lived ten months and then stopped living, as children did, as three of his children had done, and the grief of that was a room Bach entered sometimes and left again because the room had no doors and no windows and staying in it served nothing.

But Carlsbad was tedious. The waters smelled of sulphur. The company was gilded and empty. Bach had spent the weeks composing in his head -- the partitas, always the partitas, the solo violin works that were becoming the most extraordinary things he had ever written, though he would not have described them so, because Bach did not think in terms of extraordinariness. He thought in terms of craft, of problems and solutions, of the specific technical question of how a single violin could sustain the illusion of multiple voices without accompaniment. The Italians wrote flashy solo pieces -- Corelli, Vivaldi -- designed to display the performer's agility. Bach was not interested in display. He was interested in architecture. A single line that contained within it the implication of an entire harmonic world. A chaconne that was simultaneously a ground bass, a set of variations, a three-part structure moving from darkness through light and back to darkness, and -- if he could manage it, if the form would permit what he heard in his mind -- something larger than all of these, something that used the constraints of a single instrument and four strings to achieve a freedom that an orchestra with its unlimited resources could not.

The carriage turned. Köthen appeared in the afternoon light, the familiar silhouette of the town against the flat Saxon plain: church towers, rooftops, the low wall that had not served a defensive purpose in generations but that marked the boundary between the fields and the streets. Home. He could see the Jakobskirche. He could see, or imagined he could see, the roof of his own house on the Holzmarkt, and smoke from a chimney, though on a day this warm no fire should be burning.

He leaned forward. He looked for the particulars of homecoming -- a familiar cart, a known face in the street, the sign of the bakery near the market square where Maria Barbara bought the black bread he preferred. The streets were quiet in the heat. A dog slept in the shade of a wall. The carriage clattered over cobblestones now, the sound changing from the dull rhythm of the road to the sharper, more complex percussion of stone, and Bach's ear registered the change automatically, filing it alongside everything else -- the shift in resonance, the higher pitch, the way the sound bounced off the housefronts and returned with a small delay that told him something about the width of the street and the density of the stone.

The carriage stopped. He gathered his portfolio, his bag, his wig case. He stepped down into the street, his legs stiff from sitting, and stood in the heat breathing the air of Köthen, which smelled of bakeries and horse dung and the river and the particular dusty sweetness of a German town in July. The house was thirty paces away. The front door was open.

He noticed this. Maria Barbara kept the door closed in summer. He had told her, on more than one occasion, that the practice was unnecessary -- the heat did not enter through a door the way it entered through a window, and the draft was welcome. She had ignored him, as she ignored most of his domestic opinions, which were offered without conviction and received without rancour, the household being her domain as the composing desk was his. The door was always closed.

It was open now. A woman stood in the doorway. Not Maria Barbara. A neighbour, Frau Schreiber. She was wearing black. She was holding a cloth in her hands, wringing it, the way a person wrings a cloth when their hands need something to do because the rest of their body has been told to stand still and wait.

Bach's hand tightened on the portfolio.

---

He would remember, later, that the music stopped first. The chaconne, the descending bass, the four bars that had been sounding without interruption for three hundred miles -- they went silent, and the silence was so abrupt and so complete that it registered as a physical sensation, a pressure in his ears, as though he had descended rapidly from a great height. The world's sound continued -- the coachman unhitching the horses, a child's voice somewhere down the street, the thud of his own bag set down upon the cobblestones -- but the interior sound, the sound that was always present, the polyphony that accompanied his every waking moment and much of his sleep, had ceased.

Frau Schreiber spoke. He understood the words individually. Together they formed a sentence that his mind received and could not assemble. Maria Barbara. A sudden illness. The seventh of July. Already buried. The children were well. The children were with her, with Frau Schreiber, she had been looking after them. The court had sent someone. The Prince had been informed.

Bach stood in the street with his manuscripts on his arm and heard the words and they were sounds without structure, noise without counterpoint, the one form of sound he had never learned to parse.

She was buried. The fact arrived last and weighed the most. Not that she was dead -- death was a commonplace, death had visited this household three times already, he had held a dead infant in his hands not two years past and sung the hymn himself at the graveside -- but that she was buried. That the earth had closed over her. That the ritual had been performed, the prayers said, the body committed to the ground, and he had not been present. He had been at Carlsbad. He had been playing the viola for a prince in a pump room while his wife was being lowered into the churchyard. The thought produced not grief, not yet, but a disorientation so profound that his body swayed and Frau Schreiber reached out a hand to steady him and he did not feel it.

The children appeared. Catharina Dorothea first, twelve years old, standing in the hallway behind Frau Schreiber with a composure that was not a child's composure. She had her mother's face -- the wide-set eyes, the firm mouth -- and she stood very straight, her hands folded in front of her, and the straightness told Bach what words could not: that his daughter had been holding this household together for weeks, that she had assumed a role no twelve-year-old should assume, and that she had done it well, and that the cost of doing it well would not be apparent for years.

Behind her, Wilhelm Friedemann. Ten years old, his father's brow, his father's build already thickening toward the broad-shouldered frame he would carry as a man. His eyes were red. He was trying to stand as straight as his sister and failing, the effort visible in the set of his jaw and the trembling of his lower lip, and the combination of effort and failure was the thing that nearly broke Bach's composure -- not the death, not the burial, but this: his ten-year-old son trying to be brave and not quite managing it.

Carl Philipp Emanuel. Six. He stood behind his brother and looked at his father with the bewildered gravity of a child who understands that something terrible has happened and does not understand what it is, who has been told that his mother is with God and who believes this in the literal way of children, imagining a journey she has taken and from which she will presumably return when the visit is concluded.

Johann Gottfried Bernhard. Five. He was holding a wooden horse. He looked at his father and said, "Papa," and the word was cheerful and ordinary and it landed in the silence of the hallway like a stone dropped into a well.

Bach set down his portfolio. He knelt. He opened his arms, and the children came to him -- not all at once but in sequence, the youngest first because he understood the least and therefore feared the least, then Emanuel, then Friedemann with his red eyes and his trembling lip, and last Catharina Dorothea, who came slowly and who, when she reached her father, did not embrace him but placed her hand on his shoulder, the way a woman comforts a man, the way her mother might have done, and the gesture was so precise in its borrowed maturity that Bach closed his eyes against it.

He held his children in the hallway of his house and above their heads the rooms were clean and orderly and the flowers on the table were wrong -- someone else's flowers, someone else's idea of comfort -- and the house smelled of soap and candle wax and the absence of the person whose presence had given it its particular domestic scent, a smell he had not known he knew until it was not there.

Frau Schreiber stood at a distance, wringing her cloth. The coachman had brought Bach's bags inside and retreated. The street outside was quiet. The afternoon light came through the open door in a long, slanting beam that illuminated the dust motes suspended in the hallway air, each one turning slowly in the light, weightless and aimless and beautiful in the way that only purposeless things are beautiful.

---

He went to the churchyard at dusk. Alone. Catharina Dorothea had offered to accompany him, and the offer, with its quiet authority, its implicit assumption that he might need her guidance, was another of the small devastations of the afternoon. He said no. He said he would not be long.

The churchyard was behind the Jakobskirche, bounded by a low stone wall over which the wheat fields were visible in the failing light, the grain dark now, the golden colour leached by the dusk into something closer to bronze. The graves were arranged in rows, the older ones marked by worn stones, the newer ones by wooden crosses that would be replaced in time. Maria Barbara's grave was near the east wall, beneath a linden tree whose leaves were motionless in the still evening air. The earth was dark and settled, not freshly turned but not yet overgrown, the soil having had two weeks to compact under its own weight and whatever rain had fallen since the seventh of July.

He stood before it. He removed his cap. He held it in both hands at his waist, the way one stands at a grave, the posture prescribed by custom and by the body's own instinct, which knew -- even when the mind did not -- that in the presence of the dead one should be still.

Maria Barbara Bach, née Bach. Born 20 October 1684, in Gehren. Died 7 July 1720, in Köthen. His second cousin. His wife of thirteen years. Mother of seven children, three of whom lay in this same earth, in smaller graves, marked or unmarked, he could not remember now which, the burials having been conducted in the usual way, with grief and hymns and the expectation that God's purpose, though inscrutable, was good.

Three dead children. Now a dead wife. The arithmetic of early-eighteenth-century life, ordinary and brutal, the cost of living measured in graves. One did not protest. One submitted to the will of God, as Luther taught, as the hymns he set to music every Sunday declared. One trusted in the resurrection. One went home and fed the living children and slept in the bed that was now too wide and rose in the morning and continued, because continuing was what the living did, as composing was what a composer did, as breathing was what a body did until it stopped.

Bach stood at the grave and did not pray. Not because his faith had failed -- his faith would not fail, not here, not ever; it was too deeply built into the structure of his mind, as integral as his sense of pitch -- but because the words would not come. The prayers he knew were formal, liturgical, designed for the pulpit and the page, and what he felt at this graveside was not formal. It was a ragged, shapeless thing that had no metre, no key signature, no harmonic resolution. It was the sound of a man standing in a silence he could not fill.

The birds were still singing. Finches in the linden tree, a blackbird somewhere beyond the wall, the evening chorus that the countryside produced every day at this hour regardless of what had happened beneath it. The birds did not know that Maria Barbara Bach was dead. The wheat did not know. The sky, purpling now toward the west, the first stars appearing above the church tower, did not know. The world's indifference was not cruelty; it was simply the world, proceeding, as it always proceeded, through its seasons and its hours, carrying the living and the dead alike without distinction.

He replaced his cap. He touched the earth once -- not the grand gesture of a man in a painting, kneeling and weeping and pressing his face to the ground, but a small, private movement, the fingers of his right hand brushing the surface of the soil the way one might touch a sleeping child's forehead, briefly, to confirm the warmth. The earth was cool. He withdrew his hand.

He walked back to the house. The streets were dark. A lantern burned in the window of the bakery. The smell of bread. The sound of his own footsteps on the cobblestones -- a rhythm, a pattern, the irreducible fact of a body moving through space, producing sound, leaving silence behind it.

---

The study. Night. The children in bed, their sleep overseen by Frau Schreiber, who had assumed the management of the household with the brisk efficiency of a woman who understood that usefulness was the only appropriate response to grief. Bach had thanked her. He had eaten the supper she placed before him without tasting it. He had sat with the children and spoken to them in the ordinary way, about the journey, about Carlsbad, about what they had done while he was away, and they had answered in the ordinary way, and the ordinariness was a kind of mercy, a structure they could inhabit while the larger structure of their lives was being rebuilt around them.

Now he was alone.

The study was a small room at the back of the house, facing the garden. A desk, dark oak, scarred with ink stains. A chair. A shelf of manuscripts and books -- the Bible, a treatise on thorough-bass, the volumes of Buxtehude he had copied as a young man, the score of a Vivaldi concerto he was in the process of transcribing for harpsichord. A candlestick, the tallow already burned low, the flame wavering in a draft from the window that Maria Barbara would have sealed with a cloth had she been alive to do it.

On the desk: the manuscript of the Sei Solo. He had carried it from the carriage to the house to this room without setting it down, without releasing it, the portfolio held against his chest like a thing that might be taken from him if he loosened his grip. He set it on the desk now and opened it. The pages were orderly, the notation precise, the handwriting the assured, unhurried script of a man who knew what he intended before the pen touched the paper. Sei Solo. A Violino senza Basso accompagnato. Six solos. For violin without bass accompaniment.

Alone.

He had not considered the title in this light before. It was a description, not a statement -- the Italian a convention, the "solo" a technical term indicating the absence of a continuo part. But sitting in the study with the candle burning and the house quiet and the bed upstairs empty, the word acquired a second meaning that lay alongside the first like a shadow, not replacing it but darkening it. Sei solo. You are alone.

He turned to the second partita. The Allemande was clean. The Courante was clean. The Sarabande, with its grave, stately rhythm, the dance of a court that took its pleasures seriously -- clean. The Gigue -- clean. Four movements, complete, the ink dark and even. And then the fifth movement. The final movement. The pages of sketches, the fragments, the four-bar theme that he had been carrying in his inner ear for months, the descending bass in D minor that would not resolve itself into a complete architecture no matter how many times he turned it.

He picked up the violin that hung on the wall beside the desk. Not the instrument he performed on -- that was in its case, packed with the travelling things -- but the house violin, the one he used for working out passages, for testing the fingerings of a phrase that looked different on the page than it felt under the hand. It was a decent instrument, Tyrolean, the varnish worn at the shoulder where his chin rested, the neck smooth from decades of his grip.

He placed it under his chin. He drew the bow. He played the theme. Four bars. D minor. The simple, descending figure -- tonic to dominant, the bass moving stepwise down, the pattern that had served a hundred chaconnes before this one and would serve a hundred after. He played it once, twice, three times, each repetition identical, the ground bass establishing itself the way the earth establishes itself beneath the feet of a man who is learning to stand again.

He stopped. The last note faded. The room was silent, and the silence was not the absence of sound but the presence of everything that the sound had contained and now, having ceased, had left behind. He knew this quality of silence. He had encountered it before, in empty churches, in rooms where music had just ended, in the space between the last note and the first breath. It was the silence that came after. Not before. After.

He set down the bow. He set down the violin. He pulled the candle closer and uncapped the ink, and the smell of it -- iron gall, sharp and faintly metallic -- was the smell of his working life, unchanged, reliable, the one constant. He took up the pen.

He did not write the chaconne that night. He was not ready. The piece existed in fragments, in the theme and its implications, in the harmonic architecture he could hear but had not yet committed to the page. He would write it in the days and weeks that followed, not in a fever of grief but in the patient, methodical way he wrote everything, bar by bar, voice by voice, the craft shaping the material as a luthier's tools shape wood -- not imposing a form upon it but finding the form that was already there, waiting, implicit in the grain.

What he wrote that night, if he wrote anything at all, is not recorded. The manuscript that survives is the fair copy, the final version, the clean and meticulous pages that bear the date 1720 and no other annotation. No dedication. No explanation. No title beyond the technical description: Ciaccona. A chaconne. A dance over a ground bass.

The candle burned. The pen scratched. The house was quiet, the children asleep, the bed upstairs empty, the grave in the churchyard settling under a sky full of stars that neither knew nor cared that Maria Barbara Bach was dead, or that the man sitting in the study with his pen and his ink and his four-bar theme was about to write something that would outlast every church and every churchyard in Köthen, that would outlast Köthen itself, that would be played three hundred years later in concert halls and workshops and soundproofed offices and on beaches at dawn by people who had never heard the name Maria Barbara and who wept without knowing why.

He did not know this. He knew only that the piece was not finished, and that he must finish it, because finishing things was what he did.

He wrote until the candle guttered and died. Then he sat in the darkness for a time. Then he went upstairs to the bed that was too wide and lay down and slept, and if he dreamed, the dreams are not recorded, and the music -- when it came, in the days and weeks that followed, in the two hundred and fifty-seven bars of the Ciaccona that would become the single greatest movement ever written for solo violin -- the music is all we have.

It was enough.
