# Chapter 13: Home

The Indian Ocean was the colour of pewter in the early light and the Norfolk pines threw their shadows west across the terraced grass and everything was exactly as he had left it. The lawn mowed. The pool filter humming. The limestone facade of the house catching the first sun, the windows dark, the garden beds mulched and edged with the geometric precision of a place maintained by people who were paid to maintain it. Marine Parade at six in the morning: the joggers, the dog walkers, the retired couples performing their constitutional along the esplanade. Perth. The light that was not London's light or Paris's light or Vienna's light but its own -- wide, white, unforgiving, the light of a sky so large and so empty it made everything beneath it look provisional.

Bob stood in the kitchen in his socks, making coffee. The Breville -- Jeannie had chosen it because a woman she followed on Instagram had one -- hissed and thumped and produced a sound he had heard ten thousand times and that now, after three weeks in Europe, registered as the sound of a particular life resuming. The beans were stale. He ground new ones. The ritual of it -- the grinder, the tamping, the extraction, the milk -- was so deeply worn into his mornings that his hands performed it without instruction.

But his left hand was doing something. When it was not gripping the portafilter or steadying the cup, it was curling. The fingers closing around a shape that was not there -- the thumb pressing against the index finger, the remaining fingers falling into a curve that mapped precisely to the neck of a violin. He noticed it this time. He had been noticing it for weeks, but this morning, in the kitchen, with the sound of the ocean coming through the open window and the violin case standing upright in his study behind a closed door, the curling felt less like a twitch and more like a statement. His hand knew something his mind was still negotiating. His hand had already decided.

Jeannie was asleep. She would sleep until nine -- the combination of jet lag, wine, and sertraline thickening her sleep into something closer to unconsciousness. The children were in their rooms, similarly buried. The house was his for three hours, and the silence of it -- the particular silence of a large house in which everyone is sleeping and no one is performing -- was a mercy he had not felt since before Europe, before Franz, before the workshop and the smell of spruce and the moment he pushed open a door in a village he had never heard of and walked into the rest of his life.

He took his coffee to the study. A desk, a bookshelf, a window facing east. He had set the violin case here last night while Jeannie directed the unpacking from the hallway, her voice carrying through the house with the familiar cadence of a woman restoring order to her domain -- suitcases upstairs, dirty clothes to the laundry, souvenirs arranged on the hall table where they would remain for three days before being absorbed into the house's taxonomy of surfaces. She had not noticed the case. It was black, cheap, anonymous, and it sat on the carpet with the mute patience of a thing that contains something extraordinary and does not need you to know it.

He closed the door. He knelt and opened the case. The violin lay wrapped in the cloth Franz had used -- soft cotton, old, smelling faintly of varnish and the workshop's particular air. He unwrapped it. In the Perth light, harder and less forgiving than Absam's, the instrument looked worse than he remembered. The cracks were visible, running from the f-holes in fine dark lines. The varnish was dull, its reddish-amber depth clouded by age or grime or the absence of the north-facing mountain light that had made it glow in Franz's hands. No strings. No bridge. The soundpost lay on its side against the back plate, a small wooden dowel dislodged from its position in the dark interior. It looked like junk.

He held it anyway. The weight against his palms, the curve fitting the space between his hands. The wood was cool and smooth and he could feel the grain beneath his fingers -- three hundred years of growth rings, each one a year of rain and sun in a forest he had never seen. His left hand curled around the neck. His chin lowered toward where the chinrest should be. The muscle memory fired -- not skill, not technique, but the pure animal knowledge of what this object was and where it belonged against his body.

He was home. He was holding a broken violin in a room in a house he had bought with money that was not his, in a marriage that was not a marriage, in a life that was not a life. And for the first time, standing there in his socks with the ocean audible through the wall and the coffee growing cold on the desk, the word *home* meant something other than an address.

---

Jeannie appeared at half past nine, moving through the kitchen in her dressing gown with the slow deliberation of a woman reassembling herself. She opened the fridge, assessed the contents with the expression of someone reading a disappointing text message, and closed it. She filled the kettle. She stood at the bench and looked at the garden -- the frangipani, the couch grass, the limestone wall -- and said, without turning, "God, it's good to be home."

She meant it. He could hear in her voice the particular satisfaction of a woman returned to the place where her systems worked, her network operated, her Instagram had context. Europe had been a performance -- three weeks of curated experience, the photographs and the meals and the constant low-grade effort of appearing effortless in foreign cities. Home was where the effort could stop. Home was where she already knew the angles, the light, the audience.

For Bob the statement landed differently. He stood at the kitchen bench with his second coffee and said nothing, because to say what the word *home* now meant to him would require explaining Austria, and a village, and an old man with enormous hands, and a broken violin that smelled of spruce and centuries, and none of these things had a place in the kitchen on Marine Parade where the morning light fell on the marble benchtop and the Breville gleamed and the house maintained its careful equilibrium of surfaces.

"What's the plan today?" she said.

"Might go to the office. Check in."

"On a Saturday?"

"Just for an hour."

She looked at him. The flat, appraising look she used when she suspected him of an interior life she could not access or approve. Then she turned back to the kettle. "Fine. We're out of milk."

He drove to Claremont and bought milk and thought about the violin the entire way there and back.

---

That night, after everyone was in bed -- Jeannie by nine, the wine and the jet lag and the sertraline pulling her under like a weighted blanket; Chase in his room with the door closed, music thumping faintly through the wall like a second heartbeat; Shanel's light off since ten -- Bob went to his study. He closed the door. He turned the lock, a small brass latch that made a sound so quiet it might have been a thought.

He opened the case and took out the violin and held it against his shoulder. The collarbone. The chin. The ghost of the position. His left hand wrapped around the neck where the strings would be, the fingers falling into first position -- index, middle, ring, pinky, each one finding its place on the fingerboard with the approximate certainty of a man returning to a house he left thirty years ago and finding the rooms smaller but the doorways still where he remembered them.

He could not play. The violin had no strings, no bridge, no capacity for sound. It was a body without a voice, a throat without breath. But his right hand rose to the position where the bow would be and drew across the air above the strings, a slow, weighted stroke that followed the curve of an imaginary bow from frog to tip. His arm knew the angle. His wrist knew the turn. The muscle memory was there -- crude, degraded by three decades of disuse, the signals arriving through static like a radio station at the edge of its range -- but there. His body remembered what it was to play, even without an instrument that could produce sound.

He stood in his study at midnight, air-bowing a broken violin, and he was happy. Not the performative happiness of the holiday or the managed contentment of the marriage but a quiet, animal happiness, the happiness of a body doing what it was made to do. His right arm moved through the air in slow strokes, shaping the silence into phrases that existed only in his head, and the silence was a kind of music -- the music of intention, of desire, of the space between what he wanted and what he could not yet have. He played four bars of nothing. Eight bars. He played an entire scale of air, his left fingers pressing against strings that did not exist on a fingerboard that was smooth and empty, and the precision of the ghosting -- the way his hand knew where the notes would be if the notes were there to be found -- told him something he had not known until this moment: that the music had never left him. It had only gone underground, the way a river goes underground in a dry landscape, flowing beneath the surface, invisible, still moving, waiting for the place where the rock thins and the water breaks through.

He put the violin back. He went to bed. Jeannie did not stir. He lay in the dark and held his left hand above his face, flexing the fingers, and the fingers curled and uncurled around the shape of a neck that was not there, and he thought: *I will play again. I will learn. I will find someone to teach me.* The thoughts were simple and clean and they cut through the accumulated complexity of everything else in his life -- the fund, the marriage, the fraud, the twenty years of surfaces -- like a blade through paper.

---

Monday was the office. The glass towers of St George's Terrace, the suited men walking with the particular pace of Perth finance -- unhurried but purposeful, the walk of people who believe their money is working even when they are not. Bob parked in the building garage. He took the lift. He walked into Harrington Capital and the associate was waiting for him with a face that was the second wrong face Bob had encountered that summer -- the first had been Frau Schreiber's, in the doorway of another man's house, in another century, in a chapter Bob had never read.

The associate was in his mid-thirties, smooth, the kind of man who wore his shirt collar open because he had read somewhere that it projected approachability. His name was Marcus, and his face was doing the thing it did when he had been carrying something alone for too long: a tight, overly composed expression that was trying to look normal and looked instead like a man about to sit an exam he had not studied for.

"Welcome back," Marcus said. "Good trip?"

"Great. What's happened?"

Marcus told him. While Bob had been in London buying Jeannie's shopping and in Paris visiting the Musee de la Musique and in Absam pushing open a door that changed his life, a significant investor -- David Tran, mining money, $1.8 million in the fund, a man with a lawyer on retainer and the temperament of someone who checked his balances -- had requested a full redemption. Not a partial withdrawal. A complete exit. The amount was substantial. The fund did not have the liquid assets to cover it.

"I've been stalling," Marcus said. "Told him you were travelling. But he called again Friday. He wants to meet."

Bob sat behind his desk and felt the old machinery engage -- the gears of the fraud, the smooth, practised mechanisms of reassurance and deflection that had kept the fund running for eleven years. He picked up the phone. He called David Tran. His voice was warm, unhurried, the voice of a man who had just returned from a European holiday refreshed and in control.

"David. Bob Harrington. Just got back from Vienna -- sorry for the delay. How about coffee this week? I'll have the paperwork ready by Friday."

The voice worked. It always worked. David Tran softened. The meeting was set. Bob hung up and immediately called another investor -- Andrew Ng, who had been making noises at the last quarterly dinner about increasing his position, his wife's inheritance sitting in a term deposit earning nothing. Bob leaned into the phone, said the words that needed saying, and secured an informal commitment for new capital that he could redirect to cover Tran's redemption. Robbing Peter to pay Paul. The Ponzi mechanism, executed with the smoothness of long practice.

Marcus stood in the doorway watching. He asked a question -- something specific about the discrepancy between the fund's reported holdings and its actual cash position. Bob deflected. "The unlisted positions are illiquid. Valuations lag. It'll wash through by quarter end." Marcus nodded. His face said he knew this was insufficient. His silence said he needed the job, needed the salary, needed to not know. He left the room.

Bob sat alone. The Terrace stretched outside his window -- glass, sunlight, the distant blue of the river. He should have been terrified. The fund was a house of cards, and David Tran had just tugged at the base. But he felt nothing -- or rather, he felt only the residual vibration of what had happened in Absam and Vienna and the Goldener Saal, the sound of a solo violin still ringing in some chamber behind his sternum that the fund could not reach. For the first time, the fraud felt like an interruption. The thing that kept him from the thing that mattered. The noise between the notes.

He opened his laptop and searched for "violin restoration Perth." He found three luthiers within thirty minutes. One in Fremantle. One in Subiaco. One in Midland, too far. He bookmarked the first two. Then he searched "violin teacher Perth adults" and bookmarked the results without calling. Not yet. He was not ready. He needed the instrument to have a voice before he could have a teacher.

He closed the laptop. He leaned back in his chair and looked at the ceiling and thought: *I need to get it fixed. I need to learn to play again. I need to find a teacher.*

The simplicity of the thoughts -- after the labyrinthine complexity of everything else in his life, the Ponzi's tangled accounts, the marriage's negotiated silences, the years of fabricated returns and managed appearances -- was like stepping from a crowded room into cold air. The fund required constant, exhausting deception. The marriage required constant, exhausting performance. The violin required only work. Real work. The kind that uses your hands and makes you better.

---

That night, after Jeannie had ordered Thai delivery and the family had eaten in front of the television -- the domestic entropy of the first week back, everyone too jet-lagged to cook, too scattered to sit at the table -- Bob went to his study. He closed the door. He called the luthier in Fremantle.

"I've got an old violin. European. In poor condition -- no strings, no bridge, cracks in the top plate. But the wood..." He trailed off. He did not have the vocabulary for what the wood was. "Could you take a look?"

"Bring it in," the man said. "Tuesday?"

"Tuesday."

He hung up. He sat at his desk in the lamplight and opened the case and looked at the violin. The broken body, the clouded varnish, the scroll with its precise and asymmetric volute catching the light from the desk lamp the way it had caught the light in Franz's workshop three weeks and ten thousand kilometres ago. He did not take it out. He just looked.

The house was quiet around him. Jeannie's television murmured through the wall. The ocean was audible if you listened for it -- a low continuous presence, the white noise of Cottesloe at night, the sound that had accompanied every sleepless hour in this house, every 3 AM calculation of the fund's shortfall, every predawn walk across Marine Parade to stand on the terraced grass and not-think.

He closed the case. He turned off the lamp. He went to the back door and stepped into the garden.

The Indian Ocean in the dark. You could hear it, always, but tonight Bob listened to it the way he had listened to the concert in Vienna, the way he had listened to the violin in Franz's workshop. As sound. As the sound a thing makes when it is being itself without effort. The waves did not perform for anyone. The waves did not require an audience. They arrived and withdrew and arrived again with a rhythm so old it preceded everything -- the house, the city, the money, the fraud, the marriage, the man standing in his garden in his socks at eleven o'clock on a Monday night thinking thoughts so simple they might have belonged to a child.

*I need to get it fixed.*

*I need to learn to play.*

*I need to find a teacher.*

The house behind him: lit windows, Jeannie's silhouette moving in the bedroom, the shapes of the life. He looked at it and felt, for the first time, the vertigo of a man who could see two lives simultaneously -- the one he was living and the one he had found in a village in the Alps -- and who understood, in a way that was not yet a plan and not yet a decision but was already the beginning of both, that the two could not coexist. That one would eventually consume the other. That the only question was which.

He went inside. He locked the study door. He went to bed.

He did not sleep. His left hand, resting on the sheet beside him in the dark, curled and uncurled around the shape of a neck that was not there, the fingers finding positions on strings that did not exist, the muscle memory running its programme in the quiet of a house where everyone else was sleeping and no one knew that behind the locked door of the study, wrapped in a cloth that smelled of spruce and Alpine air, a broken violin was waiting for the man who had carried it across the world because he could not bear to leave it behind.
