# Chapter 28: The Gossip

Jeannie held her glass at the angle she had perfected over twenty years of holding glasses at parties -- the stem between her second and third fingers, the bowl tilted slightly toward her body, the wine catching the light in a way that looked effortless and was not. It was her third glass. She had counted the first two and had stopped counting at the third because counting was what women who had a problem did, and Jeannie did not have a problem. She had a husband who was standing on the terrace playing a violin at his own birthday party in front of eighty people, which was a problem, but it was his problem, not hers, and she would deal with it later, the way she dealt with everything Bob did that embarrassed her, which was privately, precisely, and without raising her voice, because Jeannie Wallace -- she still thought of herself as Wallace in moments of crisis, reaching back past twenty-one years of marriage to the name that was hers alone, the name that meant something before she attached it to a man who turned out to be less than advertised -- Jeannie Wallace did not make scenes. She managed them. She had been managing scenes since Year 8 at PLC, when Sienna Carmody told everyone at lunch that Jeannie's father's money was new money, and Jeannie had smiled and said nothing and by the end of the week Sienna Carmody was eating lunch alone.

The music was doing something to the party. She could feel it -- the way the conversations had stopped, the way the crowd had turned toward Bob with the dumb, open-mouthed attentiveness of people watching a sunset or a car accident, something they could not look away from even though looking was costing them something. Karen Henderson was standing near the marquee with her hand over her mouth. Paul Beaumont, who ran a mining services company and whose idea of culture was a corporate box at the WACA, was motionless, his beer halfway to his lips, his face carrying an expression Jeannie had never seen on it, something that in a more articulate man might have been called wonder. Even the caterers had stopped. A girl in a black apron stood by the drinks table holding a bottle of sparkling water and not pouring it, her eyes on Bob, and the water was dripping slowly from the bottle's lip onto the white tablecloth, and no one noticed.

Jeannie noticed. Jeannie noticed everything. It was her gift and her punishment, the hyper-vigilance of a woman who had built her life on the management of surfaces and who could read a room the way a pilot reads instruments -- constantly, automatically, scanning for the deviation that presaged a loss of altitude. She saw who was watching Bob and who was watching her watching Bob. She saw the tilt of heads, the softening of faces, the slow surrender of a crowd to something it had not expected and did not know how to resist. And she saw Katarina.

The woman was standing near the low wall at the edge of the garden, apart from the other guests, the way she had been apart all evening -- arriving alone, in dark jeans and a blouse, no jewellery, no effort, the kind of woman who could walk into a room full of women who had spent three hours getting ready and make them all feel like they were trying too hard. Jeannie had noted her arrival with the precision of a customs officer: who she was, what she was wearing, the way she and Bob had exchanged a look -- just a look, nothing dramatic, nothing that anyone else would have caught, but Jeannie caught it because Jeannie caught everything -- a look that contained familiarity and understanding and something private, something that belonged to a conversation Jeannie was not part of.

And now the woman had her phone out. She was recording. Recording Bob. The violet light of the sunset was on her face, and her expression was -- Jeannie searched for the word and the word was *tender*. The woman was looking at Bob with tenderness, and the tenderness was worse than desire, worse than lust, worse than anything Jeannie had prepared herself to see, because tenderness implied knowledge. This woman knew something about Bob that Jeannie did not. This woman had access to a room in Bob's life that Jeannie had never entered, had perhaps never been invited to enter, and the realisation landed in her chest like a swallowed stone.

She catalogued the woman the way she catalogued all women, the assessment instant and comprehensive and performed below the level of conscious thought, the way breathing is performed: Tall. European -- the cheekbones, the colouring, the angular face that was striking rather than pretty. Late thirties. No wedding ring. The dark hair pulled back in a way that suggested function rather than style. The blouse was quality -- Jeannie could read fabric at thirty paces -- but it was worn without awareness of its quality, the way a musician wears clothes, as a necessity rather than a statement. The flat shoes. The absence of makeup. The total, composed indifference to the game that Jeannie had spent her life playing, and the indifference was the most threatening thing of all, because it meant the woman was not competing. She was not even in the arena. She was in some other place entirely, some place where the currency was not appearance but something else, something Jeannie could not name, and she was there with Bob.

The music continued. The D major section -- Jeannie did not know it was D major, did not know what a key change was, did not possess any of the vocabulary that would have allowed her to understand what was happening in the sound -- but she could feel the shift. She could feel the crowd softening, surrendering, the eighty people on her terrace falling under the spell of something she had not arranged and could not control. She saw Jim weeping. She saw Chase standing with his arms at his sides and his face open in a way she had never seen it open. She saw Shanel, her careful daughter, her miniature, standing at the edge of the terrace with wet eyes and a face that was doing something Jeannie had not taught it to do, something that looked like the beginning of a mutiny against the composed, curated, defended self that Jeannie had spent nineteen years installing.

She was losing the room. The phrase arrived in the language of her expertise -- the social strategist's nightmare, the hostess's ultimate failure. She was losing the room, and the person taking it from her was her husband, and the instrument of the theft was a piece of music she could not hear, played on a violin she did not understand, witnessed by a woman whose face was doing the one thing Jeannie's face could never do.

The rage arrived. Not as heat -- Jeannie's rage was never hot. It was cold, mineral, the slow crystallisation of a substance that was always present in her chemistry, the way iron is present in blood, invisible until the conditions are right and then suddenly, structurally, there. The rage was not about the music. The rage was about the exclusion. She was standing at the window of a room she had never entered and she was watching her husband inside it with another person, and the room was the only room in their marriage she had never controlled, and the realisation that it existed -- that it had been there, behind the bland face and the accommodating silences and the suit she chose and the shoes she vetted and the life she managed -- was intolerable.

She turned from the terrace. She walked toward the glass doors, toward the kitchen, toward the light and the surfaces and the order that was hers.

---

The kitchen received her the way it always received her: with marble and light and the faint mechanical hum of appliances that cost more than some people's cars. The Calacatta island bench gleamed under the downlights. The caterer's trays were arrayed along the countertop -- the desserts waiting for service, the champagne in its bucket, the order and precision of a production she had designed. This was her room. This was her domain. The terrace and whatever was happening on it -- the music, the crowd, the violin, the woman -- was outside, separated by glass, muffled to an ambient murmur, and in here the world was controllable.

She poured a glass of wine. Her hand was steady. She drank half of it in one swallow, the sauvignon blanc cold and sharp and medicinal, the familiar chemical partnership of alcohol and sertraline doing its work, levelling her, or what she understood as levelling: a smooth, bright numbness that felt, to Jeannie, like clarity. She was clear. She was in control. She was assessing the situation the way she assessed all situations -- strategically, from the outside, calculating the angles and the exposures and the damage already done.

Sandra Kemp came through the glass doors.

Sandra, who had been Jeannie's friend for twelve years and whose friendship operated on the same principles as a mutual defence pact -- loyalty contingent on the continued usefulness of the alliance, betrayal always available as an option. Sandra, in the silk dress that cost too much, the one she had bought at a boutique in Double Bay on a trip to Sydney that she mentioned every time she wore it, the dress that was her flag, her claim, her announcement that she belonged to the world of women who spent four figures on a single garment and did not think it remarkable. Sandra's face, entering the kitchen, was arranged in an expression Jeannie knew as well as she knew her own: the expression of a woman carrying information she intended to deliver with the appearance of reluctance and the reality of relish. The concerned friend. The bearer of difficult news. The woman who was about to do damage and who needed, first, to establish that the damage was not her intention.

"Jeannie." The voice was low, intimate, the voice reserved for confidences. Sandra set her champagne on the island bench and leaned against it, closing the distance between them, creating the spatial contract of a private conversation in a room that was not private but that they were, by mutual agreement, pretending was. "Are you all right?"

"I'm fine." Jeannie's voice was even. Bright. The party voice, the voice that performed wellbeing the way the house performed abundance: automatically, convincingly, without reference to the thing it concealed.

"That was something." Sandra nodded toward the terrace, toward the glass doors through which the applause was audible, muffled and distant, the sound of the crowd responding to the end of the performance. "I had no idea Bob could play."

"He's been taking lessons." Jeannie heard herself say it with the lightness of a woman discussing a husband's hobby, the way she would discuss his gym membership or his Saturday golf: mildly, indulgently, the tone of a wife who permits her husband his small diversions because they do not threaten the structure of the life she has built.

Sandra paused. The pause was calculated -- Jeannie could see the calculation, because she would have made the same calculation in Sandra's position, the fraction of a second in which the speaker decides whether to proceed and then proceeds, having already decided before the pause began.

"Do you know about the violin teacher?"

Jeannie's face did not change. The face was architecture. The face was the structure she had been building since she was twelve years old, the face that PLC had taught her and that twenty-one years of marriage to a man she did not love had reinforced: the face that absorbed impact without registering it, that received information the way the limestone walls of this house received the afternoon heat -- silently, completely, holding it inside where it could do its slow, structural damage unseen.

"What about her," Jeannie said. Not a question. A door held open a crack.

"She comes to his office. Every week. Has done for over a year. Stays for an hour, sometimes more." Sandra's voice was delicate, solicitous, the voice of a woman performing concern while delivering a weapon. "Marcus Whitfield mentioned it to Lisa. Apparently everyone on that floor knows." A beat. "I'm sure it's just the lessons. But people are talking, Jeannie. And with the way she was looking at him tonight --"

She let the sentence trail off. The trailing off was the cruelest part -- the implication left hanging, the conclusion delegated to Jeannie's imagination, where it would do more damage than any explicit accusation could. Sandra had delivered the information and now she was stepping back, hands clean, face arranged in sympathy, the position of a friend who has done her duty and who is available for further consultation should Jeannie require it.

Jeannie drank the rest of her wine. She set the glass down on the island bench. The sound of glass on marble was precise, a small click, the sound of something being placed rather than set, the distinction between control and its absence measured in decibels.

"Thank you, Sandra." Her voice was level. Warm, even. The warmth was the most controlled thing about it -- manufactured, deployed, the warmth of a woman who is managing a situation and who will not, under any circumstances, let the situation manage her.

Sandra nodded. She picked up her champagne. She moved toward the glass doors, toward the terrace, toward the crowd that was still buzzing with the aftermath of the performance, and she paused at the doors and turned back and said, "There's one other thing."

Jeannie waited.

"Richard Ballantyne tried to redeem from the fund three months ago. Bob wouldn't return his calls." Sandra's face was performing compassion now, the final act, the coup de grace. "And Lisa's husband heard that ASIC have been making inquiries. About the fund." A pause. "I thought you should know. Before someone else tells you tonight."

The second piece of information landed on top of the first the way a second stone lands on a first in a cairn: with precision, with weight, each one supporting the other, the structure suddenly stable in its devastation. The affair. The fund. The two catastrophes arriving together, in the same kitchen, in the same minute, delivered by the same woman in the same silk dress, and Jeannie stood at the island bench and received them both and her face did not move.

Sandra left. The glass doors opened and closed. The kitchen was quiet.

---

Jeannie stood alone in the kitchen of her house on Marine Parade and she processed what she had been told the way she processed all information: from the outside in, the surface first, the depths later, or never. The surface was this: her husband was having an affair with his violin teacher, and her husband's fund was collapsing, and the two facts were connected in ways she did not yet understand but that she could feel, the way you feel the shape of something in the dark by running your hands along its edges.

The affair -- she was calling it that now, internally, the word *affair* having replaced the word *lessons* with the speed and finality of a light being switched off. The affair meant that Bob had been lying to her. Not about the fund -- she did not yet understand the fund, could not yet process the fund, the fund was too large and too abstract and too remote from the architecture of her daily concerns to land with its full weight. But the affair she understood. The affair was a language she spoke fluently, the language of betrayal and property and possession, the language in which Jeannie's deepest fears were written. He had been seeing a woman. In his office. Every week. For a year. The lessons were a cover. The violin was a cover. The soundproofed room on St George's Terrace -- she had never been to the office, had never wanted to, had considered Bob's working life a boring necessity that funded her interesting one -- the soundproofed room was where it happened, and the sound that came out of the room was the sound she had just heard on the terrace, and the sound was not music but evidence.

She was wrong. The lessons were real. The violin was real. The relationship between Bob and Katarina was not sexual and never had been -- it was the relationship between a teacher and a student who shared a profound experience of music, a bond that had no name in Jeannie's vocabulary because Jeannie's vocabulary did not include a word for what happens when two people are connected by the sound a wooden box makes when horsehair crosses a string. But Jeannie could not know this. She could not know it because she had never experienced anything like it, had never been connected to another person through something that was not transactional, had never shared with anyone the kind of wordless, non-sexual, non-economic intimacy that Bob and Katarina shared in the soundproofed office every Tuesday afternoon. The concept was outside her architecture. It did not exist in the building she had built, and so she reached for the concept that did exist, the concept that her building could accommodate: he was sleeping with her. He was sleeping with the tall European woman with the tender face and the phone and the flat shoes, and the sleeping explained everything -- the lessons, the practice, the mark on his jaw, the late nights in the study, the way he had stood on the terrace and played with an intensity he had never shown in twenty-one years of marriage, an intensity he had never shown in bed, in conversation, in any moment of their life together. He had been giving it to her. The intensity that should have been Jeannie's -- that was Jeannie's by right, by contract, by the unspoken terms of the marriage that Jeannie had designed and maintained and that Bob had violated -- he had been giving it to someone else. In a soundproofed room. Behind a closed door. For a year.

And the fund. The fund was worse, because the fund was the floor. The fund was the thing beneath everything -- the house, the boat, the car, the school fees, the boutique, the holidays, the Instagram, the life. If the fund was collapsing, the floor was collapsing, and Jeannie was standing on it, and the drop beneath it was not a metaphor but a reality: the reality of a woman whose house was bought with money that might be stolen, whose boat was financed by returns that might be fabricated, whose entire material existence was built on a structure that was, if Sandra was right, if ASIC was involved, if the word *fraud* was in the air -- a structure that was not merely failing but criminal. She would become the wife of a criminal. She would become a woman people pitied. And pity -- pity was the detonator. Pity was the one thing the architecture could not absorb.

She stood in the kitchen and the two terrors converged. The affair and the fund. The personal and the financial. The image crime and the property crime. They met in the centre of her chest and the meeting produced not grief, not despair, not the vulnerable, human response that might have led to a conversation, a confrontation, a reckoning that could have saved something. What the meeting produced was rage. Cold rage. Clear rage. The rage of a woman who has been publicly humiliated and publicly impoverished in the same hour, at her own party, in front of everyone who mattered, and whose response to the humiliation is not to collapse but to harden, because hardening is the only thing she knows, because the softness she might have felt was walled off decades ago, bricked up behind the composure and the performance and the Botox and the wine, and the walls were too thick now, too old, too structural to take down, even if she had wanted to take them down, which she did not, because taking them down would mean feeling the thing beneath them, and the thing beneath them was a void, and the void was the truth of her life, and the truth was that there was nothing underneath.

Her face did not change. Something behind her eyes shifted. A door closed. A lock turned. The Jeannie who walked back out toward the terrace -- the Jeannie who would emerge through the glass doors into the blue dusk where her husband stood with Jim's hand on his arm and the applause fading and the violin lying on a caterer's napkin on a trestle table -- that Jeannie was not the same Jeannie who had walked into the kitchen three minutes earlier. That Jeannie had been angry. This Jeannie was something else. This Jeannie was clear. This Jeannie was finished.

She pushed open the glass doors. The evening air hit her face -- warm, salt-tinged, carrying the faint residue of the music that had just ended and the sound of voices that were rising now, the party reconstituting itself around the extraordinary thing that had happened, guests moving and talking and reaching for their phones, the social machinery grinding back into gear after the interruption of beauty.

She walked onto the terrace. She walked through the crowd. People tried to speak to her -- a hand on her arm, a face leaning in, the beginning of a sentence she did not hear. She did not stop. Her eyes were on Bob. Her eyes were on the table beside Bob. Her eyes were on the violin.
