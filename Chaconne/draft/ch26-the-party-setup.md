# Chapter 26: The Party (Setup)

The email arrived at eleven forty-seven the previous night, while Jeannie slept and the white noise machine hummed behind her closed door and the house held its silence like a held breath. Bob had been sitting in the study -- the small room at the end of the hall, the room that had been a sewing room and then a wardrobe overflow and was now, by the slow accretion of his retreat from the rest of the house, his room -- and his phone had lit up on the desk beside the violin case, and the subject line read: *Final Assessment Report -- Instrument Ref. ABS-2023-0914*, and he had known before he opened it what it would say, the way you know the shape of a wave before it breaks.

The language was cautious. Hedged with the qualifications that experts used when they knew their words would have financial consequences, each sentence buttressed by conditional clauses and references to methodology. But the conclusion was unmistakable. The dendrochronology was consistent. The spruce of the top plate had been felled between 1695 and 1710. The arching profile, the geometry of the f-holes, the scroll carving, and the varnish composition were consistent with the highest tier of Alpine school construction, specifically the workshop tradition of Jakob Stainer of Absam, or -- less probably but not excludably -- the early Cremonese school. The instrument's condition, provenance gap notwithstanding, placed it in the uppermost category of authenticated seventeenth- to early eighteenth-century instruments. Conservative valuation: fifteen to twenty million US dollars.

Bob read the email three times. He set the phone down. He looked at the violin case on the desk -- the battered black case with its broken latch that he had carried home from Absam two years ago, the case Franz Kaltenbrunner had given him along with the instrument because the old case, Franz said, had been with the violin when it came to him and should stay with it. Inside the case was an object worth more than every other thing Bob owned combined. The house on Marine Parade, the boat, the cars, the paintings Jeannie had bought. More than all of it. More to the point: worth more than the fund's total deficit. If he sold it -- if he declared it, if he put it through the proper channels, if he sat down with a lawyer and an accountant and said, *Here is an asset I purchased with fund money, and here is its value, and here is what I owe* -- the fund would be made whole. Every investor repaid. No prosecution, no gaol, no disgrace. The instrument that Franz had sold him for ten thousand euros, out of grace, out of some recognition of a drowning man reaching for something real -- that instrument could save him.

But. The violin had been purchased with fund money. Ten thousand euros diverted from the operational account during a trip to Austria that Jeannie had organised and that Bob had used, without telling her, as a cover for a detour to a village in Tyrol where a luthier he had found online might have an instrument worth playing. Declaring the violin meant declaring the purchase. Declaring the purchase meant admitting the fund was already insolvent when he spent the money. The instrument that could redeem him was also the instrument that convicted him. The paradox sat in his chest like a stone, and it had been sitting there for two years, and tonight it was heavier, because now the stone had a price, and the price was the price of his freedom, and the freedom was unreachable.

He did not think about the money for long. He thought about the Chaconne.

---

He woke at four and did not try to go back to sleep. The house was dark. Through the study window, the Indian Ocean was a black sheet under a sky that was just beginning to lighten along its eastern edge, the first thin line of grey that preceded the dawn by an hour. He rose from the narrow bed he had made of the study's daybed -- Jeannie had not commented on the nights he spent here; she had, he suspected, not noticed, or had noticed and preferred the arrangement -- and he stood at the window and watched the ocean lighten from black to charcoal to the pewter colour it turned in the minutes before the sun cleared the rooftops behind the house.

He opened the violin case. The instrument lay in its blue velvet lining, the varnish catching the first grey light -- a deep reddish-orange, the colour of autumn leaves, the colour of something that had been alive and was now something better than alive: preserved, transformed, carrying within its grain the accumulated resonance of three centuries of music. He touched the strings. They were cold. He rosined the bow -- three long strokes, the familiar gesture, the rosin's faint sweet smell rising in the dark room.

He picked up the violin. He tucked it under his chin. The chinrest pressed into the hollow on his jaw -- the fiddler's neck, the permanent mark that Jeannie had noticed once and that he had explained as a rash, and she had not asked again. He adjusted the shoulder rest. Drew a breath.

He played the opening chord of the Chaconne.

D, A, D, F-sharp. Quietly. The sound filled the small room and went out through the window into the morning. The four notes breaking across the strings, the bow rolling from bass to treble, and the sound was not a chord in any ordinary sense. It was a door opening. The door that had been sealed shut for twenty-seven years and that he had broken open with his hands and his hours and his love, the door behind which the only true thing in his life had been waiting.

He played through the piece. Not performing -- practising. Running the passages that still troubled him: the high-position work in the first section where his intonation wavered, the arpeggios at the first climax where his bow arm tended to tighten, the transition into D major at bar one hundred and thirty-three. He played the D major section slowly, listening to the sound the Stradivarius made in the small room at dawn -- the warmth of it, the way the instrument seemed to amplify not just volume but intention, so that the simple hymn-like melody sang with a richness that exceeded his technique, that came from somewhere his technique could not reach. Katarina had told him last week that this section was where his playing was strongest. *The simplicity suits you, Robert. Your tone here has a quality I cannot teach, because it comes from somewhere I cannot reach.* She had said this without sentiment, as a technical observation, and it was the highest praise she had ever given him, and he had carried it the way a man carries a talisman -- close, hidden, a small warmth against the cold.

He played the bariolage. His bow arm ached. He played the final bars. The last chord. Silence.

He stood in the study as the sky turned from grey to pink. Through the window, the Indian Ocean was changing colour -- the pewter surface warming to silver, then to the first pale gold as the sun cleared the rooftops and threw its light across the water. The Norfolk pines along Marine Parade stood black against the brightening sky, their strange columnar shapes like columns built to hold up nothing. The grass below the house -- the terraced grass that stepped down from the street to the esplanade and then to the beach -- was still in shadow, damp with dew, and Bob's eyes travelled down it to the water's edge, to the sand, to the ocean, the way his eyes always travelled when he stood at this window. It was a reflex. Not a plan. Not yet.

Today he would play this piece for his family. For his wife. For his children. For the people who had known him for decades and had never known him at all. He would stand in front of them and play the only true thing he had ever done, on an instrument that might be three hundred years old, and they would hear him -- really hear him -- for the first time.

This was what he told himself. Whether it was courage or delusion, the morning did not say.

---

Jeannie was in her element. She had been planning this party for two months, and the planning had been the kind of work that engaged the deepest competencies of her personality -- the management of logistics, the curation of experience, the creation of a social environment so precisely calibrated that every guest would feel, simultaneously, welcomed and impressed and slightly inadequate. This was her art form. She had no other.

The caterers arrived at eight -- a team from a firm in Subiaco she had used three times before, carrying trestle tables, glassware, crates of wine in wooden boxes. Margaret River, naturally: Leeuwin Estate chardonnay, Vasse Felix cabernet, Cullen sauvignon blanc. Two cases of Veuve Clicquot for the toast. A bartender set up on the terrace overlooking the lawn -- a young man with a beard and an apron who moved with the quiet efficiency Jeannie demanded and who would, over the course of the evening, pour approximately three hundred drinks and clear four hundred glasses and remember none of the conversations he overheard, which was the point: the staff were infrastructure, not participants.

A florist arranged native arrangements in galvanised tubs along the terraced grass -- banksias, kangaroo paw, proteas, the burnt orange and deep red of the West Australian bush rendered decorative, tamed, positioned at intervals that Jeannie had specified with a diagram she had drawn on the back of a menu from Wildflower. The aesthetic she had described to the florist was "effortlessly coastal," a phrase that Jeannie used without irony and that had taken forty hours of research, mood boards, and WhatsApp exchanges with three of the gaggle to curate. The marquee -- white, open-sided, positioned on the upper terrace so that the view of the ocean was framed but not obstructed -- had been erected the day before by a hire company, and Chase and two of his mates had carried the remaining furniture out that morning, the trestle tables and the stackable chairs and the wooden bar that would serve as the drinks station.

Jeannie moved through the preparations with the focused composure of a woman who had organised a hundred events and considered each one a test she intended to pass with full marks. She was wearing linen pants and a silk blouse -- not the party outfit yet, that would come later -- and her hair was pulled back and her face was bare, and in the morning light she looked, genuinely, beautiful. The novel notes this without irony. Jeannie was beautiful. The bones of her face were fine and precise, the product of genetics and maintenance -- the Botox, the filler, the disciplined thinness that required effort and white wine and the daily negotiation between appetite and image. Her beauty was real. It was the one thing about her that was not performed, and it was the thing she trusted least, because beauty without an audience was beauty wasted, and Jeannie could not conceive of a self that existed outside the gaze of others.

She checked the seating. There was no formal seating -- "It's not that kind of party, darling" -- but she had positioned the bar, the food stations, and the conversation clusters with the strategic precision of a woman who understood that the architecture of a social space determined the flow of its interactions. The bar was elevated, on the terrace, forcing guests to move upward from the garden and congregate where the view was best and the lighting, as the sun dropped, would be most flattering. The food stations were distributed -- one near the marquee, one by the garden gate, one on the lower terrace -- to prevent crowding and encourage circulation. The DJ would set up near the glass doors, facing outward, so the music would flow from the house through the party and out toward the ocean, a sonic gradient from domestic to wild.

She checked the guest list on her phone: eighty-three confirmed. Work colleagues of Bob's, the gaggle and their husbands, neighbours from Marine Parade and the surrounding streets, a few old boys from Scotch College who had stayed in touch, some of Chase's friends, a handful of Shanel's university acquaintances who would arrive late and leave early. Bob's father, Jim, and his mother, Wendy, driving down from Mandurah, where they had moved after selling the house on Pearse Street. Jeannie's parents, Geoff and Patricia, who would arrive precisely on time and leave precisely at nine because Patricia's circulation did not tolerate late evenings.

She paused. She had not included Katarina Nemcova on the guest list. She had heard the name once, months ago, when Bob mentioned he had a violin teacher, and she had filed it in the category of things Bob did that did not concern her, alongside his gym membership and his habit of watching YouTube late at night with headphones. But Bob had asked, yesterday, that she be invited. "She's my violin teacher. I'd like her to be there." Jeannie's expression at this information had been the expression she deployed when something was not worth fighting about: a micro-tightening of the jaw, a fractional narrowing of the eyes, then a smile. "Fine. One more won't matter." But the request had stayed with her. It had the quality of requests that mattered -- the slight hesitation before he said it, the way his eyes had not met hers, the defensive casualness of a man who knows the thing he is asking for will be scrutinised and is pre-emptively minimising its importance.

There had been a rumour. One of the gaggle -- Sandra Kemp, whose currency was other people's information -- had mentioned it three weeks ago, over lunch at Wildflower, in the oblique, deniable way that Sandra delivered intelligence: leaning in, lowering her voice, beginning with "I'm sure it's nothing, but." *Someone said Bob's been seeing someone in his office. A woman. Every week. For over a year.* The word *seeing*. The word *someone*. Jeannie had laughed. "That's his violin teacher." She had said it lightly, the way you dismiss a rumour you intend to bury. But the words had stayed. They had settled into the substrate of her awareness like a seed in soil, and the soil was the accumulated anxiety of a woman who had built her life on the management of appearances and who could feel, in ways she could not articulate and refused to examine, that the appearances were becoming harder to manage.

She went upstairs to shower and dress. The party outfit: a cream midi-dress, cut on the bias, from a label she had discovered in the Marais on the Europe trip last year. Gold earrings. Tan heels from a shop in Claremont. She stood before the full-length mirror in her retreat -- the bedroom she had colonised three years ago, with its white linen and its blackout curtains and its white noise machine on the nightstand -- and she looked at herself and she looked magnificent. She knew this. The knowledge was not vanity; it was assessment, the cool appraisal of a product she had been refining for forty-five years and that was, today, performing at its peak. She took a photograph on her phone. Considered it. Deleted it. You did not post before the party. You posted during, when the light was golden and the composition included other people admiring you.

---

Chase arrived at four, coming from his flat in Subiaco in the ute he shared with a mate, pulling into the drive behind Jeannie's white Range Rover and Bob's grey Audi. He was wearing what Cottesloe boys wore to family events: chinos, a white linen shirt, RM Williams boots, no socks. His girlfriend of three months -- a girl called Maddie or Mollie, with a ponytail and an easy laugh, pleasant and interchangeable with the last girlfriend and the one before that -- was with him. Chase greeted his mother with a kiss on the cheek and his father with a handshake and a clap on the shoulder. "Fifty, old man. How's it feel?"

Bob smiled. "Like forty-nine with worse knees."

Chase moved through the house with the ease of someone who had grown up in it, who knew where the beer was kept and which floorboards creaked and exactly how far his mother's authority extended. He opened a bottle from the fridge. The caterer's assistant -- a girl in a black apron who had been briefed by Jeannie that no drinks were to be served before the champagne toast -- looked at him and then looked away. Chase drank. This small defiance was Chase in miniature: the assertion of autonomy within a structure he did not question, the rebellion of a man who will inherit the system he pretends to resist.

Shanel was already there. She had been in her room since morning, emerging at five in a pale blue dress that Jeannie had chosen for her -- simple, appropriate, the kind of dress that on Jeannie would have been a statement and that on Shanel was a concession. Her hair was down. She looked beautiful and uncomfortable. She was nineteen and still living under the aesthetic regime of her mother, wearing the clothes Jeannie selected, attending the events Jeannie arranged, performing the version of herself that the household required. The dress was perfect. It was not hers.

She came downstairs and moved through the living room toward the garden, and as she passed the dining table she saw the violin case. It sat on the polished surface like an object from another world -- black, battered, its latches worn, the leather scraped and scarred in a way that no object in Jeannie's house was permitted to be scraped and scarred. Shanel knew about her father's playing. Not the details, not the obsession, not the eighteen months of weekly lessons in the soundproofed office on St George's Terrace. She knew the general fact -- that he had taken it up again, that he practised in the study late at night, that the violin was the reason for the small, permanent mark on his jaw that he thought no one had noticed. She had heard him once, through a wall, the sound muffled but audible -- a slow melody, simple, repeated, the patient work of a man teaching his hands to do something they had forgotten. She had stood on the other side of the wall and listened and had not mentioned it, because mentioning it would mean acknowledging a part of her father that the family's grammar did not include.

She touched the case with one finger. Ran it along the edge, from the handle to the latch. The leather was warm. She moved on.

---

Jim and Wendy Harrington arrived at five. Bob saw the car from the upstairs window -- the old Camry, spotless, Jim's car, driven now by Wendy because Jim's reflexes had slowed and his night vision had gone and the act of driving, which had once been the expression of his autonomy, had been quietly transferred to his wife the way a baton is passed in a relay, without ceremony, the new hand closing around it before the old hand has fully released.

Jim got out of the passenger side slowly. The deliberate, careful movements of a man for whom every physical act now required negotiation -- the body consulted, the joints assessed, the ground evaluated for its willingness to support his weight. He was wearing a suit. Bob recognised it: the charcoal two-piece from the tailor in the Terrace Arcade that Jim had patronised for forty years. The suit had fitted him five years ago. It hung on him now the way a flag hangs on a windless day, the shoulders sloping, the collar standing away from a neck that had thinned and corded with age. He had lost weight -- the incremental diminishment that age imposed on a body that had once been substantial, the flesh falling away from the bones like topsoil in a drought, leaving the essential structure visible: the jaw, the cheekbones, the heavy brow that Bob had inherited and that looked, on Jim's diminished face, like architecture that had outlasted the building it supported.

Jim gripped a walking stick in his right hand. He had started using it six months ago and he hated it with a quiet, stubborn fury -- the fury of a man who had walked to the Cottesloe pylon and back every morning before breakfast for thirty years and who now could not cross a car park without the stick and without the fear that his knees would betray him. Wendy came around the car and took his arm. They walked to the front door together, and Bob came down the stairs and opened it, and Jim looked at his son and Bob looked at his father and for a moment neither of them spoke, because the thing that was between them -- the love and the distance and the years and the regret -- was too large for the doorway to contain.

Jim took Bob's hand. The grip was too tight and held too long. The old man's hand was knotted with veins and age spots, the skin thin and loose over the tendons, and the trembling was there -- the low-grade tremor that was not pathological but merely the body running down, the machine wearing out. "Happy birthday, son." His voice carried something it did not usually carry -- a tenderness that embarrassed them both, the emotion of a man who understood, without being able to say it, that each meeting with his son might be the last, and that the things unsaid between them were accumulating faster than the remaining time could accommodate.

"Thanks, Dad."

Wendy hugged Bob and whispered something -- the novel did not quite catch it, the words lost in the embrace, but the tone was warm and worried and maternal, the tone of a woman who had been watching her son's face for forty-nine years and who could read in its current arrangement something that frightened her, though she could not have said what.

---

Guests began arriving at five-thirty. The scene became panoramic: cars filling the street, the Range Rovers and the Audis and the Mercedes lining both sides of Marine Parade, the sound of heels on the limestone path, the clink of glasses, the murmur of greetings. The DJ started at six -- Fleetwood Mac, yacht rock, the Bee Gees, the soundtrack of western suburbs parties since 1987 -- and the music filled the gaps between conversations the way Jeannie intended it to: not competing, not intruding, simply providing the ambient texture against which the performance of the evening could proceed. The gaggle arrived together, four women in their mid-forties, dressed with competitive precision -- the silk, the gold, the careful balance between showing too much and not enough -- and they descended on Jeannie with the choreographed enthusiasm of a formation Jeannie had designed and they had learned: cheek-kisses, compliments on the dress, the house, the flowers, the view, the everything, the performance of admiration that was also an audit, each woman cataloguing the details of the evening and filing them for later comparison with her own entertaining.

The terrace filled. The sun dropped toward the Indian Ocean. The sky shifted from blue to gold, and the gold intensified, and the light that fell across the party was the particular light of Perth in late afternoon -- warm, slanting, forgiving, the light that made everything look as if it had been photographed and filtered and approved. The terraced grass below the house was dotted with guests holding champagne flutes, their figures silhouetted against the ocean, and the ocean was a sheet of hammered bronze, the swell invisible from this elevation, just the slow vast breathing of the water.

Katarina arrived alone. She came on foot from wherever she had parked -- not up the limestone path but from the side, through the garden gate, a woman accustomed to peripheral entrances. She was wearing dark jeans and a blouse of muted blue, no jewellery, flat shoes. She did not look like the other women at this party. She looked like what she was: a European musician at an Australian garden party, slightly out of place and entirely self-possessed, carrying herself with the understated gravity of a person whose life was organised around sound rather than appearance. She found Bob near the edge of the terrace, where he was standing with a beer he had not drunk, looking at the ocean. They exchanged a look. She nodded. He nodded. Nothing was said that anyone could have interpreted. But the quality of the exchange -- its brevity, its weight, the way it communicated something that existed between them and between no one else at this party -- was visible to anyone watching.

Someone was watching. One of the gaggle. She stood near the marquee with her champagne, and her eyes tracked the look between Bob and the woman in the dark jeans, and she noted it, and she filed it, and she would deliver it later, at the right moment, in the right ear, because this was what the gaggle did. This was their function. They were the information network of the western suburbs, the neural pathways through which secrets travelled and were transformed into currency, and a look exchanged between a married man and an unfamiliar woman at his birthday party was currency of the highest denomination.

---

The sun was fifteen minutes from the horizon. The sky was copper and gold. The party was at full pitch -- eighty people, drinks flowing, the noise level rising, the DJ playing something the crowd recognised, and the recognition produced the collective hum of people performing enjoyment, the sound of a machine working as designed. Jeannie stood near the glass doors with her third glass of sauvignon blanc and surveyed her creation and found it good.

Bob excused himself. He went inside. He walked through the living room to the dining table. He opened the violin case. The instrument lay in its velvet -- the spruce top glowing in the last daylight coming through the west-facing windows, the varnish that deep reddish-orange that seemed to glow from within, as if the wood remembered the workshop and the hands and the sun of whatever country had made it. He picked it up. He rosined the bow. He tuned by ear -- the A first, plucked against the D, listening for the fifth, the two notes aligning with the precision that two years of training had made instinctive.

He caught his reflection in the glass doors: a man in a good suit -- the navy Italian, the one Jeannie had chosen -- holding a violin, backlit by a sunset. He did not recognise himself. Or he recognised, for the first time, the person he had been supposed to be.

Katarina appeared in the doorway. She had followed him. She said nothing for a moment. She took out her phone. She opened the camera. She pressed record.

"You don't need to do that," Bob said.

"Yes I do." And then, quieter: "Play it the way you played it this morning. Do not perform. Just play."

He nodded. He walked back out onto the terrace, carrying the violin in his left hand and the bow in his right, holding them the way she had taught him -- lightly, the scroll pointing down, the bow loose in his fingers. The guests nearest the doors noticed first. A man with a violin was unusual but not alarming. Conversations thinned. Faces turned. Jeannie saw him from across the terrace and the smile on her face -- the warm, proprietary smile of a woman surveying her production -- flickered and went still, replaced by something the people nearest her could see but could not interpret: confusion shading toward apprehension, the expression of a woman watching a variable she had not accounted for enter her controlled environment.

Bob stood at the edge of the terrace. The ocean was behind him. The sun was going down into it like a coal sinking into ash. The violin was in his hand. The crowd was before him. Eighty faces, turning toward him one by one, like a wave.

The moment before.
