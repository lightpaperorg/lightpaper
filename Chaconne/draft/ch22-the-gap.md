# Chapter 22: The Gap

She had told him to play the D major section, and he had played it -- bars 133 through 208, the middle movement of the Chaconne, the passage where the music lifts from D minor into D major and the entire emotional landscape shifts, the way weather shifts when a cloud bank moves and the sun finds the ground. He had played it from memory, which was itself new -- until a month ago he had needed the score in front of him, the notation a safety net he clung to even when his fingers knew the way, afraid that without the printed page the music would abandon him midphrase and leave him standing in silence with the bow raised and nothing to say. Katarina had taken the score away. She had closed it and placed it on the windowsill and said, "The music is in you. Stop reading it and start hearing it." He had been terrified. He had played the first eight bars from memory, stumbled on a chord in bar 141, and stopped.

"Again," she said.

He had played it again. The same eight bars, and then further -- bar 141, the chord clean this time, the voices separating, the soprano floating above the bass the way Katarina had demonstrated week after week, "hear the upper voice as a singer, a woman's voice, and the bass as the ground she walks on." He played through bar 150, bar 160, the variations growing in complexity, the double stops climbing, and somewhere -- bar 168, or bar 172, he could not later say exactly where -- something happened.

The music breathed.

Not the way it had breathed before, which was the breathing of effort, of a man managing the mechanics of bow and string and left hand and intonation while trying to remember the next note and the shape of the phrase and the shape of the whole fifteen-minute arc. That was the breathing of a swimmer counting strokes. This was different. This was the breathing of a man who had stopped counting. For six bars -- maybe eight, he could not say, because the quality of the experience was precisely that he was not counting -- the violin sang and Bob disappeared into it the way he used to disappear as a boy in Mrs Albrecht's studio, the way he had disappeared in the car on Rokeby Road the first time he heard Milstein play the Chaconne, the way he disappeared into the ocean when he swam at dawn off Cottesloe in the winter, when the water was so cold it erased everything except the body's animal insistence on being alive.

What happened in those bars was not a technical event. It was a perceptual one. For eighteen months, he had listened to himself playing the Chaconne -- monitoring, evaluating, comparing the sound he produced against the recordings in his memory, measuring the gap and finding himself, always, on the wrong side of it. That listening was the listening of self-consciousness. It was the way he listened to himself speak at investor lunches, choosing words, managing tone, projecting the competence he did not feel. It was the listening of a man who lived inside the gap between who he was and who he pretended to be.

Now the gap closed. Not permanently -- he would feel it open again in seconds. But for these bars, there was no Bob Harrington playing the Chaconne. There was only the Chaconne, sounding in a room, through an instrument, via a body that had ceased to obstruct it. The concentrating self -- the self that monitored and managed and corrected and judged, the self that stood beside the music and said faster, slower, wrong note, flat, sharp, fix it -- that self released its grip. What remained was the body. The hands. The bow arm. The instrument.

His bow pressure lightened of its own accord. His vibrato widened, slowed, became the gentle pulsing that the Italians call the vibrato del cuore -- an undulation of pitch so subtle it does not change the note but warms it, the way candlelight warms a face. The double stops separated into their constituent voices and the soprano floated above the bass and the two lines sang independently, a conversation between high and low, between grief and its transfiguration, between the thing that was lost and the thing that remained after the losing.

The melody climbed. The arpeggio figures in bars 145 through 152 cascaded upward, the tone brightening, and his bow arm drew the sound with a freedom it had never had -- the elbow swinging, the wrist flexible, the whole mechanism of bowing functioning, for the first time, as a unified system. The bow did not press the string. It breathed across it. The string did not resist. It sang. His fingers found the positions and the positions were correct, not because he was placing them correctly but because the music wanted to go there and his hands followed, the intonation governed not by the conscious placement of the fingertip but by the body's deep agreement with the harmonic series, the mathematical relationships between frequencies that the inner ear recognises the way it recognises gravity -- without thought, without effort, without the intervention of the managing self.

He came back. The bow reached the end of bar 176, or bar 178, and the spell broke -- his third finger missed a shift, the E was flat, and he was suddenly a man playing a violin again, aware of the room, the acoustic foam, the mirror, his own reflection with its furrowed brow and the red mark on his jaw and the sweat on his upper lip. He lowered the instrument.

Katarina was silent. She stood by the window, her own violin hanging at her side, and she did not speak for a long time. Long enough that the silence became its own presence in the room, the way a rest in music is not the absence of sound but its frame, the thing that gives the notes around it their meaning.

"You stopped trying," she said.

He looked at her.

"You let the music play you." She paused. Her English was precise, accented, the syntax occasionally inverted in a way that gave her statements the quality of pronouncements. "That's what I hear in the great recordings -- Milstein, Szeryng, the very best. Not a person playing the Chaconne. The Chaconne playing through a person. You did that. For six bars, maybe eight. You did that."

Bob sat down. He did not decide to sit -- his legs folded, the chair catching him. His hands were trembling. Not from exertion -- from the aftermath of something he did not have language for, the same wordless trembling he had felt in the car on Rokeby Road, the same trembling that had seized him in Franz's workshop in Absam when he first drew the bow across the strings of this violin and the sound that came back was not his but the instrument's, three hundred years of resonance released into the air by the imperfect, desperate, fully human pressure of his hand.

"Can I do it again?" he said.

Katarina smiled. It was not the polished smile of a teacher praising a student. It was something rarer -- the smile of a musician who has witnessed something she had begun to think she would never see. Not from this man. Not from any of her students -- the twelve-year-olds with their Suzuki books, the Scotch College boys doing their AMEB exams, the retired professionals learning folk songs. She had heard good playing. She had heard talented playing. She had not, in twelve years in Perth, heard a moment where the music itself seemed to take over, where the player became the instrument and the instrument became the voice and the voice said something that was true.

"You can't make it happen," she said. "That's the whole point. You can only stop preventing it."

---

He raised the violin. He placed the bow on the string and began the D major section again, bar 133, and this time the concentrating self was fully awake -- monitoring, managing -- and the music that emerged was correct and controlled and dead. The transparency was gone. The vibrato was tight. The double stops were clean but undifferentiated, the voices merged into a texture rather than separated into a conversation. He played through to bar 160 and stopped.

"Gone," he said.

"It will come back. Not because you chase it. Because you've been there now. Your body knows the way. Your body will take you there again, when your mind stops standing at the door."

She picked up her own violin and played the D major section -- not the pedagogical playing she normally did but the way she would play it for herself, alone, at performance tempo, with the tone and phrasing she had spent twenty-eight years developing, the sound of a conservatory-trained violinist who had placed fourth in a national competition and had not placed first and who carried that distance -- between fourth and first, between very good and great -- the way Bob carried his own gap, as a wound that was also the source of everything she understood about music. She played bars 133 through 180. It was expert playing. Beautiful playing. It was not, quite, what Bob had done for those six bars.

She lowered her violin.

"I've been playing the Chaconne for twenty years," she said. "I have performed it twice. Both times, I played it carefully. And carefully --" She stopped. She looked out the window, where rain was falling on St George's Terrace, the drops visible against the glass, the city grey and wet, the river invisible behind cloud. "Carefully is death on the concert stage. That's what my professor told me. Kovar. He was a bastard, but he was right. The great performers are not careful. They are reckless. They put everything at risk -- intonation, tone, the whole structure of the piece -- in order to find something true. And you --" She turned back to him. "You are not reckless, Bob. You are the most careful man I have ever met. But for eight bars, you forgot to be careful. And what came out was more honest than anything I have heard in that piece in twelve years."

She began packing her case. The Prokop went into its velvet. The bow, loosened, alongside.

"Next week, we'll work on the bariolage. Bars 89 to 120. The hardest passage technically. I want you to play it badly."

"I play everything badly."

"No. You play everything carefully. There's a difference. I want you to play the bariolage and not care where the notes land. Just move the bow. Crossing, crossing, crossing -- the right arm does the work. Let the left hand find the notes on its own. Don't place them. Let them arrive."

She stood at the door.

"And Bob. What happened just now -- it will happen again. You won't know when. You can't schedule it. But when it comes, don't grab for it. Don't try to hold it. Let it pass through you. The moment you reach for it, it's gone."

She left. He heard the outer door close. The lift. Then nothing. The building was quiet. The rain tapped on the window behind the acoustic curtains, a soft percussion, barely audible through the foam.

---

He did not play again that afternoon. He sat in the chair in the centre of the soundproofed room with the violin in his lap and the bow resting against his knee and he sat in the silence and felt the residue of those six bars in his body, the way you feel the residue of a wave after it has broken over you -- the salt on your skin, the chill, the buoyancy still in your limbs.

He had done something he could not do. The paradox was simple enough that a child could have understood it: the music was better when he stopped trying to play it. The careful, managing, correcting self -- the self that had gotten him through forty-eight years of life, through a commerce degree he didn't want and a career he hadn't chosen and a marriage he couldn't leave and a fraud he couldn't stop -- that self was the obstacle. It was the wall between him and the music. It was the soundproofing, not of the room, but of the soul.

He lifted the violin. He did not play the D major section. He played a single open D -- the first note he had played in this room, the note he played every morning, the drone, the tonic, the ground beneath everything. The note rang in the foam-deadened air. He held it. The vibration travelled through the bridge, through the body of the instrument, through his collarbone and his jaw, into the bone of his skull, into the place where sound becomes thought becomes feeling becomes the thing that has no name, the thing that music is for.

He held the note until the bow reached the tip. Then he sat in the silence that followed.

---

That evening, he drove home along the Mitchell Freeway in the rain. The wipers beat a rhythm on the windscreen. The traffic was slow -- the Friday afternoon exodus, headlights on wet tarmac, the freeway narrowing at the Loftus Street exit, the long curve past the university and down into the western suburbs.

His phone buzzed on the passenger seat. A voicemail from Henderson, one of the fund's investors, a man who had put in $1.2 million in 2014 and had been asking pointed questions at the last quarterly meeting, questions Bob had answered with the smooth, reassuring fluency that was the most polished of his lies. Henderson wanted to talk. Henderson's tone had the quality of a man who has spoken to his accountant and his lawyer and is now making the call he has been advised to make.

Bob did not return the call. He turned off the phone.

The rain eased as he crossed the bridge at Dalkeith. By the time he reached Marine Parade, the sky was clearing from the west, the clouds breaking to reveal a strip of pale gold above the Indian Ocean. The Norfolk pines along the esplanade were black against the gold. The ocean was grey and churning, the swell pushed up by the front, the waves breaking white against the groyne.

He parked in the garage and sat with the engine off. The violin case on the seat beside him. The phone dark in his pocket. He thought about what Katarina had said. You stopped trying. You let the music play you.

He thought about his wife inside the house, pouring a glass of wine, scrolling the feed that confirmed, image by image, the life she believed she was living. He thought about Chase in his flat in Subiaco, eating takeaway in front of the football, his commerce degree half-finished, his father's trajectory ahead of him like train tracks. He thought about Shanel in her room, the door closed, the made bed, the morning runs, the part of her that was not her mother and did not yet know it.

He got out of the car. He walked through the garage and into the house, where the lights were on and the kitchen smelled of something reheated and his wife stood at the island bench with a glass of wine and her phone and the practised composure of a woman who had not, in twenty years of marriage, heard a single true thing from the man she had married.

"You're late," she said.

"Meeting ran over," he said.

He put the violin case in the study and closed the door. He ate dinner. He watched television without seeing it. He went to bed. He lay in the dark and the D major melody played itself behind his eyelids, the soprano voice climbing, the bass descending, and the six bars where everything had opened and the gap had closed played themselves again and again like a phrase that the mind cannot release because the mind has been changed by it, and the change is permanent, and the old shape no longer fits.

---

Saturday evening. The Harrington house on Marine Parade. Bob was in the shower, the water hot enough to loosen the ache in his left forearm, the steam filling the bathroom, his reflection erased from the mirror. Downstairs, Jeannie stood at the kitchen island with a glass of sauvignon blanc -- the third of the evening, though she had told herself it was the second -- and she was scrolling through a caterer's Instagram page when Bob's phone buzzed on the benchtop.

She looked at it. She did not mean to look at it. She was reaching for her own phone and his was there, face down, and the buzz was the buzz of a text message, and her hand moved before her mind authorised it, the way a hand moves toward a door when you hear something on the other side that you were not supposed to hear.

The lock screen showed a text from "K Nemcova": *Thursday confirmed. Bring the Kreutzer -- we'll work on the 32nd notes before the Chaconne passage.*

Jeannie read this. She did not know what a Kreutzer was. She did not know what the Chaconne passage meant. She did not know the name Nemcova, had never encountered it, could not place its nationality -- Czech, she would discover later, when she found the website, the photograph, the face of a tall woman with dark hair and angular cheekbones and the kind of serious European beauty that Jeannie could not compete with because it was not competing, it simply was.

What she knew was: a woman's name. A weekly appointment. A private language she was excluded from.

She put the phone down. Bob's footsteps on the stairs -- the creak of the third step, the one that had needed fixing for two years and that Bob had said he would organise and had not. She moved to the sink. She rinsed a glass that did not need rinsing. He came into the kitchen in a blue linen shirt, his hair still damp, smelling of the sandalwood soap she had put in the bathroom because Aesop was having supply issues and she had been forced to settle.

"Ready?" she said. They were meeting friends at a restaurant in Claremont -- another couple from the yacht club, the Wilsons, Graham and Di, reliable company, no surprises. The kind of dinner where the men talked about the market and the women talked about renovations and everyone drank too much and drove home pretending they hadn't.

"Ready," Bob said.

She said nothing about the text. They drove to Claremont in a silence that Bob mistook for the ordinary silence of their marriage and that Jeannie filled with a calculation so rapid and so practised it was indistinguishable from instinct. She needed more information. She would not ask Bob, because asking Bob would reveal that she had looked at his phone, and revealing that she had looked at his phone would put her on the defensive, and Jeannie was never on the defensive. She would find out another way.

Dinner was unremarkable. Bob was quiet but present. Jeannie was funny, sharp, the version of herself she deployed at restaurants -- the woman who made Di Wilson laugh and Graham Wilson glance at her with the faint, appreciative attention of a married man who would never act on it. They ate. They drank. They talked about the boat, the children, the renovation the Wilsons were doing to their pool house. Two hours of the performance that constituted social life in the western suburbs, the exchange of curated information between people who knew each other well enough to have dinner and not well enough to say anything true.

After dinner, Jeannie dropped Bob at home and drove to meet two friends at a wine bar on Bayview Terrace in Claremont. She parked on the street and checked her face in the rearview mirror and walked in and found them at a table near the back -- Rachel and Simone, the inner circle, the two women in the gaggle she trusted not because she trusted anyone but because they owed her favours she kept careful track of and because they were, in different ways, afraid of her.

She ordered a glass of Cloudy Bay and told them about the text message. She did not say "I think he's having an affair." That would have been too direct, too vulnerable, too close to an admission that her marriage was something other than the curated performance she presented on Instagram and at dinner parties and in the careful, immaculate staging of the house on Marine Parade. Instead she said: "There's this violin teacher. He sees her every week. In his office. Thursday mornings."

Rachel and Simone exchanged a look -- the quick, lateral glance of women who have spent decades reading subtext, a glance that assessed and categorised and rendered judgment in the time it took to lift a wine glass.

"What does she look like?" Rachel said.

Jeannie said she didn't know.

"Find out," Rachel said.

Simone leaned forward. "Has he been different lately? Distracted? Working late?" The questions were procedural, diagnostic, the checklist of infidelity that these women could recite the way a mechanic recites the symptoms of engine failure, because they had seen it before -- in their own marriages, in the marriages of friends, in the endless recycling of betrayal and discovery and recrimination that constituted the social weather of their world.

"He's been coming home late," Jeannie said. "He's been -- I don't know. Somewhere else." She drank. "There's a mark on his neck. Like a bruise. It's been there for months."

The fiddler's neck. The callused hollow beneath the jaw that forms where the chinrest presses, the physical evidence of hours of practice that Jeannie had noticed and misread, interpreting the evidence according to the only framework she possessed: another woman's mouth.

She drove home. She had drunk most of a bottle at dinner and two glasses at the wine bar. She was not drunk -- she was never drunk, by her own definition, which was a definition that had expanded steadily over fifteen years to accommodate the steady increase in her consumption, the way a river widens to accommodate a rising flood -- but she was something. She parked in the garage and sat in the car for a long time, the engine off, the garage dark, the faint hum of the house's machinery audible through the walls.

She was thinking about the text message. Not the woman -- the language. *The Chaconne passage.* It was a phrase from a world she did not belong to, a world Bob apparently inhabited without her, a world with its own vocabulary and its own rituals and its own intimacy. She had been excluded from something, and she did not know what it was, and the not-knowing was worse than an affair would have been, because an affair she understood. An affair had scripts, responses, outcomes. An affair was a crisis she could manage -- the confrontation, the tears, the ultimatum, the restructuring of the marriage around the wound. This -- whatever this was -- was a language she could not speak, a room she could not enter, a part of her husband's life that had nothing to do with her and needed nothing from her and existed entirely outside the architecture of control she had spent twenty years constructing around him.

She went inside. She locked the car. She walked through the kitchen, where Bob's phone sat on the bench, dark now, and up the stairs to her room, and she closed the door and lay on the bed in her clothes and the wine hummed in her blood and the white noise machine breathed its artificial ocean and she stared at the ceiling and thought: Who is K Nemcova? And what is the Chaconne?

---

Sunday lunch. The family assembled, which was increasingly rare -- Chase had his own flat in Subiaco now, a small one-bedroom above a kebab shop on Rokeby Road that he paid for with his part-time work at the property firm, and Shanel was often out with friends or at the library or doing whatever it was that second-year arts students did on Sundays. But Jeannie had summoned them, and when Jeannie summoned, the family appeared, because Jeannie's summons carried the implicit weight of consequences that none of them had ever tested.

The table was set with the good plates. A chicken from the Claremont deli, reheated, carved, laid on a platter with roasted vegetables that Jeannie had transferred from the aluminium tray to the ceramic dish because aluminium trays were for people who didn't care about presentation. Bread from the bakery. A salad of the kind that required five ingredients and no cooking and that Jeannie described as "thrown together" in a tone that suggested effortless competence rather than the absence of effort. The ocean was visible through the floor-to-ceiling windows -- silver-grey today, the winter light flat and cool, the horizon a ruled line between water and sky. From the outside, a photograph of this lunch would look like the cover of a real estate brochure: the perfect family, the perfect house, the perfect view.

Bob sat at the head of the table because that was where Jeannie directed him and because he had never questioned the arrangement, as he had never questioned any arrangement Jeannie made, absorbing her instructions the way furniture absorbs the weight of the people who sit on it -- silently, structurally, without complaint. He carved the chicken. He poured water. He asked Shanel about her semester -- "Going well, yeah, the Woolf tutorial is really good actually" -- and she looked pleased that he remembered, because he always remembered, because remembering was the quiet currency of his love for his children, the coin he spent without expecting change.

Chase ate with the appetite of a twenty-one-year-old who viewed Sunday lunch as fuel rather than occasion. He loaded his plate, ate quickly, scrolled his phone under the table with the thumb of his left hand while the right hand operated the fork, a coordination that Jeannie found infuriating and that represented, to Chase, nothing more than the efficient management of two simultaneous data streams. The conversation moved across the surface of their lives like a water bug across a pond -- skimming, never breaking through. Chase's uni work: coasting. Shanel's semester: fine. The boat: needed its annual service, the diesels had been rough on the last crossing to Rotto. The birthday party: Jeannie had started planning. "April isn't far away," she said. "We need to book caterers. I've been looking at the woman who did the Wilsons' anniversary."

Bob nodded. He said the right things. He occupied his chair and his role and his portion of the Sunday performance with the ease of long practice, the way an actor in a long-running play can deliver his lines while thinking about something else entirely. He was thinking about the D major section. About the six bars. About the gap closing and opening and the possibility that it might close again.

Then Chase looked up from his phone.

"Dad," he said. "One of my finance lecturers was talking about managed investment schemes last week. He said most boutique funds in WA don't actually have proper independent audits. Is that true?"

The table did not go silent. There was no dramatic pause, no cinematic freeze. Jeannie was checking her own phone, her thumb moving through Instagram with the automatic rhythm of a pulse. Shanel was looking out the window at the ocean, her chin resting on her hand, her gaze somewhere between the glass and the horizon. No one in the room understood the significance of what Chase had just said except the man at the head of the table, who understood it the way a man standing on a fault line understands an earthquake: not as theory but as the ground moving beneath his feet.

Bob's face changed. The colour left it -- not dramatically, not the theatrical draining of blood that happens in films, but a subtle withdrawal, a thinning of the complexion that turned his tanned face a shade closer to grey, as though the skin had tightened over the bones beneath. His hand, holding a glass of water, gripped. The knuckles whitened.

"That's not quite right," he said. His voice was smooth. The smooth, reassuring voice of Robert Harrington, fund manager -- the voice that had kept the Ponzi alive for three years, that had deflected questions from investors and associates and the occasional journalist and, once, an ASIC inquiry officer who had called with what he described as "routine questions" and whom Bob had handled with such fluent competence that the officer had thanked him and hung up and not called back for four months. "We use an external auditor. Baker Tilly. All our returns are independently verified."

This was a lie. The fund's auditor had been a one-man accounting firm in Osborne Park that Bob had chosen because the man was overworked, underpaid, and disinclined to ask questions, and who had signed the annual returns with the perfunctory attention of a man rubber-stamping documents he did not have time to read. Baker Tilly had never been near the fund. The name dropped into the Sunday lunch like a counterfeit coin -- the right weight, the right sound, accepted without inspection.

Chase nodded. "Yeah, that's what I thought," he said. He returned to his phone. The moment passed. The water bug moved on.

But Shanel had seen it.

She had been looking out the window, or she appeared to have been looking out the window, but the glass was tinted against the western sun and it held reflections, and in the reflection she had seen her father's face at the moment Chase asked the question. She had seen the colour change. She had seen the grip on the glass. She had seen something cross his features that she had no name for but that her body recognised the way the body recognises the smell of smoke before the mind identifies fire -- a primal, pre-verbal signal that something was wrong, deeply wrong, in a way that had nothing to do with the Sunday lunch or the chicken or the conversation about caterers and boat engines.

She did not say anything. She filed it away in the part of her mind that registered discrepancies without naming them, the part that had been accumulating small observations about her father for months -- the late nights, the closed study door, the mark on his jaw, the way he sometimes stood at the kitchen window looking at nothing, his hands hanging at his sides, his face wearing an expression she had never seen on him before, an expression she would later, years later, recognise as the face of a man standing between two lives and knowing that he could not occupy both for much longer.

After lunch, Bob was at the kitchen sink doing the dishes -- he always did the dishes, one of his accommodations, his way of being useful, of occupying a role that justified his presence in a house where his wife tolerated him and his children took him for granted. He stood with his hands in the warm water, the window above the sink framing the ocean, and he scrubbed a plate with a methodical attention that was the closest he came, outside the soundproofed room, to meditation.

Chase came in. He leaned against the counter, long-limbed, casual, a glass of water in his hand, his phone finally in his back pocket. He stood there for a moment without speaking, which was unusual for Chase, who filled silences the way he filled plates -- quickly, generously, without pausing to consider whether the silence might have been serving a purpose.

"Dad," he said. "You look tired. Are you okay?"

Bob turned from the sink. His son's face -- the dark hair, the strong jaw, the eyes that were Jeannie's green but held something warmer, something that was Bob's, something that had survived the transmission from father to son despite everything -- his son's face was looking at him with an expression that Bob had not seen before. Not concern exactly. Something more tentative, more uncertain. The expression of a young man who has never asked his father a personal question and does not know the protocol, does not know whether this is something men in his family do, and is asking anyway because something in the Sunday lunch -- the colour change, the grip on the glass, or just the accumulated weight of months of watching his father age in ways that could not be explained by time alone -- had pushed him past the barrier.

"Fine, mate," Bob said. "Just busy."

Chase nodded. He drank his water. He put the glass on the counter and walked out, and Bob watched him go -- the broad shoulders, the easy stride, the body of a twenty-one-year-old who had never been afraid of a room in his life -- and thought: He is studying commerce. The same degree. The same university. The same trajectory, laid out before him like train tracks, leading to the same destination. And for the first time, the thought did not fill him with pride or resignation but with a specific, aching dread: What if he ends up like me?

Not the fund. Not the fraud. The accommodation. The careful, corrosive accommodation that had consumed Bob's life -- the endless adjustment to other people's expectations, the surrender of desire to obligation, the slow burial of whatever he might have been beneath the weight of whatever he was supposed to be. Chase had his mother's confidence and his father's warmth and neither parent's self-knowledge, and he was twenty-one, and the world was offering him the same bargain it had offered Bob -- comfort in exchange for compliance, money in exchange for silence, the appearance of a life in exchange for the life itself -- and he did not know enough to refuse it.

Bob dried his hands. He folded the tea towel. He walked to the study and closed the door and sat in the chair and did not play the violin because it was a Sunday and Jeannie was in the house and the study was not soundproofed and the music -- the real music, the D major section, the six bars, the gap and its closing -- could not be heard by anyone in this house. Not yet. Not until the birthday. Not until he stood up in front of them and played the most honest thing he had ever done, and they heard him for the first time, and then they would know -- Chase would know, Shanel would know -- that their father was not just the man at the head of the table carving the chicken and saying the right things. He was something else. Something buried. Something that had been trying to get out for thirty years and had finally, in a soundproofed room on St George's Terrace, found its voice.

He sat in the dark study and the D major melody played itself in his mind, the soprano climbing, the bass descending, and outside the window the winter ocean moved against the shore, and the Norfolk pines stood black against the grey sky, and the house was quiet around him, and the gap between who he was and who they thought he was had never been wider, and the music had never been more true.
