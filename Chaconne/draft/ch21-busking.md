# Chapter 21: Busking

The hoodie was from Vinnies -- the St Vincent de Paul on Stirling Highway in Claremont, two suburbs from his own, chosen specifically because no one he knew would be caught dead in a Vinnies. It was grey, size large, shapeless in the way of charity-shop clothes that have been washed and donated and washed again until all memory of their original owner has been laundered out of them. The jeans were from the same rack -- dark blue, stiff, sitting too high on his waist in a style no one under sixty would choose deliberately. The baseball cap was plain black, no logo, pulled low. The sunglasses were aviators from a chemist in Northbridge, the lenses dark enough to hide his eyes and cheap enough that the frames pinched the bridge of his nose with a low, persistent ache he would carry for the next two hours without complaint, the way he carried everything.

He had dressed in the office bathroom, folding his linen shirt and chinos into the briefcase, standing for a moment in front of the mirror above the basin in clothes that cost eleven dollars and made him look like a man who might sleep in a park, or wait in line at Centrelink, or do any of the things that Bob Harrington had spent his entire adult life arranging not to do. The transformation was not just visual. Something shifted in his posture when the Vinnies hoodie settled over his shoulders -- a loosening, a surrender of the careful uprightness that Jeannie had trained into him over twenty years of public meals and charity functions and the relentless surveillance of women who noticed a slouch the way a mechanic noticed engine knock. In these clothes he could slouch. He could disappear. He could become nobody, which was the closest he had ever come to becoming himself.

He did not recognise the man in the mirror. That was the point.

---

It was a Thursday, just past noon. The lunch crowd on Murray Street Mall was at its densest -- office workers streaming out of the glass towers on St George's Terrace and the Hay Street arcades, crossing into the pedestrian zone with its plane trees and its public art and its buskers spaced at intervals along the paving, each occupying a patch of territory staked out by long custom and unspoken agreement. A man with a guitar at the Hay Street end, playing something by Fleetwood Mac with the practised ease of a performer who had long ago stopped hearing the songs and now simply delivered them, the way a postman delivers letters. A woman with a cello near Forrest Place, her case open, a hand-lettered sign propped against the lid reading BACH & BAROSSA: TIPS APPRECIATED. Bob had studied the mall's geography over three lunchtime reconnaissance trips, watching from a bench with a takeaway coffee, noting the foot traffic patterns, the sight lines, the spots where the acoustics bounced off the shopfronts and carried versus the spots where the sound bled into the open air and died.

He had chosen his position: midway along the mall, between the entrance to the Carillon City arcade and the old post office, where a slight indentation in the building facade created a shallow alcove that caught the sound and projected it outward into the pedestrian flow. He set the case on the ground -- not the good case, the blue velvet-lined case that cradled the violin like an organ in a body, but a battered black case he had bought second-hand from the Gumtree ads, scuffed and anonymous, the kind of case a music student would carry. He opened the lid and laid it back. He placed a single gold coin in the case -- a two-dollar piece, for suggestion, the way Katarina had told him buskers did it in Prague, a seed to show that payment was welcome and its denomination did not matter.

He lifted the violin. His hands were shaking, but not from tendonitis -- that had subsided after the two-week rest, the stretches, the ice, the reduced practice regime that Katarina had imposed and that he had followed with the desperate obedience of a man who had learned what it meant to lose the thing he loved. The shaking was something else. Stage fright. The terror of exposure that had stopped him performing at fifteen, that had kept the violin in its case for twenty-seven years after that single school concert where the lights had found him and the audience had watched and the experience of being seen while making music had been so unbearable that he had sealed the instrument away rather than endure it again. It was the terror that had driven him to build a soundproofed room, to dress in another man's clothes, to hide behind sunglasses and a cap -- because even now, even in disguise, even on a street where no one would look twice at another busker, the act of playing for someone other than Katarina felt like standing naked in a crowd and asking to be judged.

He did not play the Chaconne. Not yet. He started with the Gavotte en Rondeau from the Third Partita -- an approachable piece, dance-like, major key, the kind of Bach that smiled. A warm-up for both himself and the street. He placed the bow on the string and drew the opening phrase, and the sound that came out was thin and uncertain, the outdoor acoustics stripping away the warmth the soundproofed room had given him, exposing every flaw in his intonation, every unevenness in his bow pressure, the way strong light exposes the lines in a face that looked fine in a bathroom mirror. The sound floated over the heads of the lunch crowd and no one stopped. A woman in a charcoal suit walked past so close her handbag brushed the open case. A man in high-vis workwear glanced in his direction, registered nothing, looked away.

Bob played the Gavotte through to the end. Then a pause. A breath. The specific silence that separates one piece from another in a performer's mind, the moment where the body releases the ghost of what it has just played and prepares for what comes next.

He began the Chaconne.

The opening chord in the open air -- four notes, broken from bass to treble, the D minor that Bach had written three hundred years ago for a single player in an empty room, now sounding in the open of a Perth shopping precinct on a Thursday lunchtime among a thousand people who had never heard of the Chaconne and did not look up from their phones. The chord rose and dispersed into the February heat, the sound scattering against shopfronts and plane trees and the bodies of pedestrians who moved through it without knowing it was there, the way pedestrians move through the air displaced by a passing bus -- felt only as a faint disturbance, registered by the body but not by the mind.

No one stopped. The Fleetwood Mac guitarist down the mall was drawing a small crowd, three or four people nodding along to "The Chain," because that was how street music worked -- you played what people knew, what people could hum, what people could drop a coin for without breaking stride. You did not play the most demanding piece in the solo violin repertoire to a crowd that wanted classic rock and a flat white. The disjunction between the music and the setting was total. The Chaconne required silence, concentration, the willingness to surrender fifteen minutes of your life to a single sustained musical argument. Murray Street Mall at noon offered none of these things. It offered heat, noise, takeaway bags, the beeping of pedestrian crossings, and the implacable indifference of a city at lunch.

Bob played on. He played the opening theme -- the four bars, the descending bass, the first variation -- with his eyes closed. He was not playing for the street. He was playing for the music. The technical imperfections were there -- the chord in bar 25 that lost its bass voice, the shift to fifth position in bar 40 that landed a fraction sharp, the arpeggiated passage in bars 50 through 60 that he navigated with more determination than grace. But the phrasing was his own now, shaped by fourteen months of listening and practice and injury and recovery, and the emotional line -- the grief, the weight, the slow accumulation of variations building on a single foundation -- was genuine.

He was thinking about Joshua Bell. The Washington Post experiment, 2007 -- the greatest violinist alive, playing the greatest music ever written, on a three-and-a-half-million-dollar Stradivarius, in a Metro station at morning rush hour. Forty-three minutes. One thousand and ninety-seven people walked past. Twenty-seven gave money. Seven stopped to listen. The rest -- the overwhelming, annihilating rest -- heard nothing. They walked through the sound the way they walked through the air, the sound registering only as a minor disturbance in the acoustic field of their commute, drowned out by earbuds and phone calls and the thick, impermeable self-absorption of people going somewhere.

Bob was not Joshua Bell. His instrument might or might not be a Stradivarius. And he was playing not to prove a point about aesthetics but because he was drowning in his double life and the only honest thing he did was play, and he could not bear to do it only in a sealed room any longer. The soundproofed office had been a womb. It had protected him and nurtured him and given the music a place to grow. But a womb was also a sealed chamber, and what grew in sealed chambers eventually needed air, needed light, needed the world. He needed to know whether the music communicated when stripped of context -- no concert hall, no programme notes, no reputation, no acoustic foam. Just a man, a violin, and whoever happened to walk past.

One person stopped. A woman -- middle-aged, in her fifties perhaps, carrying a Coles bag in one hand and a water bottle in the other, her face partially shaded by a wide-brimmed sun hat. She stopped about ten metres away, off to the side where the alcove ended and the pavement widened, and she stood very still and listened. She did not approach. She did not put money in the case. She did not take out her phone. She stood with the shopping bag resting against her calf and the water bottle forgotten in her hand and she listened, and Bob did not see her because his eyes were closed.

He was in the D minor variations, the dense middle of the first section where the writing thickened and the chords stacked and the bass voice drove relentlessly downward while the upper voice struggled against it, and the sound that came off the instrument was rough and imperfect but it was also, unmistakably, the sound of a human being telling the truth. Not a polished truth. Not a professional truth. The truth of a man who could not do the thing he was doing and was doing it anyway, the way a man who cannot swim throws himself into the ocean because the alternative is to remain on the shore forever.

The woman listened for three minutes. She heard the end of the first section and the beginning of the D major shift -- bar 133, the note that changed everything, the C-sharp rising to C-natural, D minor becoming D major, and the entire emotional landscape opening the way weather opens when a cloud bank moves and the sun finds the ground. Something happened to her face. A softening. Not tears, but an opening -- the almost imperceptible relaxation of the muscles around the eyes and mouth that occurs when a person encounters something real and does not need to defend against it. She listened for another minute, standing in the heat, the shopping bag heavy against her leg. Then she walked away. She did not look back. She did not put money in the case. She had heard something, and whatever it was, it was enough.

Bob played on. Through the D major section, through the return to D minor, through the bariolage passage in bars 201 through 208 -- the shimmering, obsessive string crossing that sounded, when played well, like a single voice singing and screaming at once, and that sounded, when he played it, like a man trying very hard to do something impossible and almost succeeding. A young man stopped briefly, filmed on his phone for twenty seconds, then moved on. Otherwise: the lunch crowd of Murray Street Mall, a thousand-odd people in the space of fifteen minutes, streaming past in both directions, their paths bending around him the way water bends around a stone in a riverbed, the stone present but irrelevant, an obstacle to be navigated, not a thing to be noticed.

He finished. The final chord -- the great D minor resolution, the four voices stacking and then releasing to the single open D, the tonic, home -- hung in the afternoon air for a moment and then was gone, not absorbed by acoustic foam but simply released, dispersed into the sky above Murray Street the way all sounds are eventually dispersed, into the atmosphere, into nothing. He opened his eyes. The case had four dollars and fifty cents in it: the two-dollar coin he had seeded, plus a two-dollar coin and some silver from someone he had not seen.

He stood on Murray Street Mall with the violin at his side and the sun on his face and felt something that made no sense: joy. Pure, absurd, irrational joy. He had just played the most difficult piece in the solo violin repertoire, imperfectly, on a street corner, for almost no one, and earned four fifty in tips, and it was the most honest thing he had done in twenty years. More honest than the fund reports. More honest than the quarterly letters. More honest than the smile he gave Jeannie when she asked how his day had been and he said fine, everything's fine, which was the lie he told with the fluency of a native speaker because it was the language of his entire adult life. On Murray Street Mall, in a hoodie from Vinnies, with his eyes closed and the sun on his face, he had told the truth for fifteen minutes and the truth had cost him nothing and earned him four dollars and fifty cents and that was exactly right, that was the correct exchange rate between honesty and the world's attention.

---

The following Thursday he came back. Same clothes, same spot, same battered case. He did not play the Gavotte this time. He placed the violin under his chin, drew a breath, and began the Chaconne.

He was three bars into the opening when he saw a face in the crowd that stopped his breath.

Richard Keane. A man Bob had known for fifteen years. A client of the fund -- one of the original investors, a mechanical engineer who had sold his drilling equipment company for eleven million and entrusted three of it to Harrington Capital in 2012. A man who had been trying to redeem his investment for four months, who had received Bob's smoothly evasive emails about quarterly processing windows and ninety-day notice periods and the temporary illiquidity of certain positions, and who had, according to the associate's increasingly frantic messages, retained a lawyer.

Richard was walking toward him. Twenty metres away. Phone to his ear, suit jacket over his arm despite the heat, the thick-necked, weather-beaten solidity of a man who had spent thirty years on mine sites before the money arrived. He was walking at the pace of a man with somewhere to be, the phone conversation absorbing most of his attention, but his eyes were scanning the pedestrian flow in the automatic way of a person who navigates crowds by watching rather than yielding.

Fifteen metres. Ten.

Bob played on. He had no choice -- stopping would be more conspicuous than continuing. He dipped his head so the cap brim covered his face. He played the opening variations with his heart hammering against the Vinnies hoodie, the bow arm shaking, the intonation dissolving into something that would have made Katarina wince. The chords thickened. His fingers found the notes but the notes were dead, drained of everything except the mechanical accuracy of a body following instructions while the mind screamed.

Richard passed within five metres. The reflexive glance -- the brief, assessing flicker that a city-dweller gives a street performer, the glance that categorises and dismisses in a single beat: busker, male, violin, not relevant. His eyes did not linger. He walked on, still talking, the phone pressed to his ear, the suit jacket swinging from the crook of his arm, and in three strides he was past and in ten he was a back in the crowd and in twenty he was gone, swallowed by the lunch-hour current flowing toward Forrest Place.

He had not recognised him. Why would he? The frame was wrong. Bob Harrington was a fund manager in a linen shirt and R.M. Williams boots who sat behind a desk on St George's Terrace and spoke with the calm authority of a man who could be trusted with other people's money. He was not a busker in a grey hoodie on Murray Street. The brain processes faces in context -- this was a fact Bob knew from somewhere, a documentary or an article, the cognitive science of recognition -- and Richard's brain had processed the busker and found no match because the category was wrong. A face in a boardroom is a different face on a street corner. The features are the same. The person is invisible.

Bob played on. The shaking subsided. The music returned, slowly, the way colour returns to a face after the blood has drained from it -- not all at once but in stages, the intonation steadying, the bow pressure finding its weight, the variations resuming their argument. But the near-miss had deposited something in his chest -- a cold, dense weight that sat beside the joy and would not be moved. These worlds were not as separate as he pretended. The man who played Bach and the man who ran the Ponzi walked the same streets, breathed the same air, occupied the same body. They could not be kept apart by acoustic foam and charity-shop clothes. Eventually they would collide.

He finished the Chaconne. The final chord rose and dispersed. He opened his eyes. The case had three dollars in it -- less than last time. He packed the violin, closed the battered case, and walked back to the parking garage on Murray Street where his car sat in the dim concrete light beside the BMWs and the Audis, the car of a man who earned in a month what a busker earned in a year, the car he drove to the office where he committed fraud and to the home where he committed silence and to this street where, for fifteen minutes, wearing another man's clothes, he committed the only honest act of his week.

He sat in the car. He removed the sunglasses. He took off the cap. In the rearview mirror his face looked back at him -- flushed from the sun, the red mark on his jaw from the chinrest, the eyes of a man who had just done something exhilarating and something terrifying in the space of twenty minutes and could not tell which was which.

He drove back to the office. He changed in the bathroom. The linen shirt, the chinos, the R.M. Williams boots. The hoodie and the jeans went into a plastic bag that he kept in the bottom drawer of his desk, beneath the compliance folders, beneath the fabricated quarterly reports, beneath the architecture of his professional life. He walked into the outer office. Nine messages on the phone. He did not play them. He sat at his desk and looked at the Bloomberg terminal and the blinking red light and the photograph of Jeannie and the children at Rottnest, and the four dollars and fifty cents from last week was still in his pocket, mixed with the three dollars from today, seven fifty total, the accumulated earnings of a man who had played the greatest piece of music ever written for solo violin, twice, on a street corner, in disguise, and had been heard -- truly heard -- by one woman with a shopping bag who had walked away without a word.

He put his hand in his pocket and felt the coins. They were warm from his body. They were the most honest money he had ever earned.
