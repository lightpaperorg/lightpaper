# Chapter 5: Rottnest

The swell hit them side-on coming out past the groyne and the Riviera pitched hard to port and Macca's beer went flying off the dash and Chase laughed and opened the throttle and felt the twin Cats dig in, thirteen hundred horsepower of diesel churning the water white behind them, and the bow rose and steadied and the boat found her line and they were out, properly out, the river and the traffic bridge and Fremantle falling away behind them and nothing ahead but eighteen kilometres of open Indian Ocean and Rottnest sitting low and scrubby on the horizon like something that couldn't be bothered to be impressive.

This was the thing. This was the actual thing, and Chase did not have words for it and did not need them. The throttle under his palm, vibrating. The spray coming over the bow in sheets when they punched through a swell. The sound of the diesels, not a roar but a sustained deep growl that came up through the deck and through his legs and settled in his chest like a second heartbeat. He was twenty-one years old and he was driving a forty-eight-foot motor yacht across open ocean on a Friday afternoon in late autumn and his two best mates were below deck loading slabs of Emu Export into the fridge and arguing about the Dockers and the sky ahead was enormous and blue and the water was that dark, almost navy blue it went when the continental shelf dropped away, and he was happy in the uncomplicated way that he was only ever happy on this boat, which was the one place in his life where his body and his mind were doing the same thing.

The Riviera 48 was called *Chardonnay*. His mother's choice. Chase had wanted *Full Send* or *Rip Tide* or something his mates would find funny, but the boat was not his -- it was his father's, technically, paid for in 2015 when Chase was eleven, though by now Chase had driven it more hours than Bob had and knew the crossing by feel: the swell pattern south of the groyne, the wind shift at the halfway point where the Fremantle Doctor met the Southern Ocean rollers and the chop turned ugly for ten minutes, the moment when Rottnest resolved from a smudge into an actual island with that insane turquoise water ringing it like something out of a travel ad. Forty-eight feet of fibreglass and teak, twin Caterpillar C-12s, three cabins, a flybridge, a tender on the swim platform. She was not new and she was not flash -- the blokes with real money had sixty-footers, Sunseekers and Princesses with crew -- but she was solid and she was fast enough and she was his, in every way that mattered.

His dad was up on the flybridge. Chase had registered this without really looking: Bob sitting in the skipper's chair with a coffee, staring at the ocean with that blank look he got sometimes, like he was tuned to a radio station no one else could hear. Dad did this. He went somewhere inside his own head and you could talk to him and he'd nod and say "yeah mate" but he wasn't there, not really, and Chase had learned a long time ago not to examine his parents too closely because examination led to questions and questions led to the kind of territory that his family did not visit. His mum had a word for it -- she said Bob was "in one of his moods" -- and Chase had adopted the phrase without investigating what it meant, the way you adopt a local expression when you move to a new country: you use it, it works, you don't ask about the etymology.

---

Macca came up the companionway holding two Export Golds and handed one across. "Your old man not drinking?"

Chase took the beer. It was ten-thirty in the morning and the can was cold and wet and he cracked it and drank and the taste was the taste of every good day he could remember. "He's got a coffee."

"A coffee," Macca said, like it was a foreign word. He leaned against the helm station, blocking the wind with his body. Macca was six-two, played second row for the Cottesloe rugby club, had the neck of a man who settled arguments by existing near them. He'd been Chase's mate since Year 8, since the afternoon they'd both been sent to the deputy principal's office for throwing bread rolls in the refectory and had bonded over the shared injustice of a two-hour detention. Macca's dad was in mining -- not exploration, proper mining, Pilbara iron ore, the kind of money that didn't need a boat to prove itself but had several anyway.

"He's all right," Chase said, and turned the wheel a degree to port, reading the swell. The next set was coming in long and rolling from the southwest and he wanted to take them at an angle, not beam-on. The Riviera was a heavy boat but she didn't like a side-on swell at speed. "He just likes the quiet."

"Quiet," Macca said, drinking, watching the ocean. "Yeah, nah. Fair enough."

Deano was still below, on his phone, texting someone. Deano was the quieter one -- not quiet by any normal standard, but quieter than Macca, which was like saying a car alarm was quieter than a fire engine. He worked for his brother's property development outfit in Subiaco, wore RM's even to the beach, and believed with the unshakeable conviction of a man who had never tested it that he would be rich by thirty. Chase liked him. Chase liked most people. It was easy, liking people, when you didn't look at them too closely.

The swell shifted. They were at the halfway point now, the chop picking up as the Southern Ocean rollers met the coastal current, and the water went from rolling to confused, the Riviera shouldering through it with a rhythm that was half punch, half slide. Chase widened his stance. He liked this part. He liked the way the boat demanded your attention, the way the sea stopped being a backdrop and became a partner you had to negotiate with. Some of the blokes he knew drove their boats the way they drove their cars -- arrogantly, all throttle, as if the ocean owed them passage. Chase didn't. The ocean didn't owe you anything. You read it, you adjusted, you found the line, and if you got it right there was a moment when the hull and the swell locked into the same frequency and the boat seemed to lift out of the water and fly, and that moment was better than anything -- better than girls, better than footy, better than the look on his mother's face when he showed up in a clean shirt.

He thought about his mother sometimes, out here. Not often. Not in any way he'd call thinking. More like a pressure, a weather system sitting on the edge of the horizon. She hadn't come today. She rarely came anymore. The boat had been hers once -- *Chardonnay*, her name, her choice, her domain -- but somewhere in the last few years she'd stopped. Said she didn't like the swell. Said she got headaches. Said the sun was bad for her skin. All of which might have been true but none of which was the reason, and Chase knew the reason without knowing he knew it, which was that the boat was the one place where Bob was in charge and Jeannie could not control the narrative, and rather than exist in a space she did not curate, she chose not to exist in it at all.

But Chase didn't think about this. He drove the boat.

---

Thomson Bay opened up ahead of them like a postcard someone had oversaturated: the turquoise water so bright it looked artificial, the white sand curve of the bay, the low settlement of white buildings and Norfolk pines, and thirty-odd boats on the moorings, rafted up in twos and threes, their masts and flybridge antennas catching the morning sun. Chase eased back the throttle and the Riviera settled into her displacement hull, the bow dropping, the wake flattening behind them. He idled through the mooring field, reading the numbers, looking for their assigned buoy.

"Starboard, mate," Macca said, pointing. "Number forty-seven."

Chase brought her around. The approach to a mooring was the part that separated the boys from the men, and Chase did it with the ease of someone who'd done it a hundred times: a slow turn to windward, the boat gliding forward under her own momentum, the engines in neutral, Macca on the bow with the boat hook reaching for the mooring line. The line came aboard. Chase killed the engines. And then the silence -- the sudden, total silence when thirteen hundred horsepower of diesel stopped and the world became wind and water and birds and the slap of small waves against the hull.

He stood at the helm for a moment, breathing. The air was different out here. It was the same ocean, the same air mass, but on the island it was cleaner, emptier, as though the thirty-kilometre crossing had stripped something away. The smell was salt and seagrass and the faint rotting-kelp smell that gave the island its name -- *Rottnest*, rotten nest, what the Dutch had called it when they landed in the seventeenth century and mistook the quokkas for giant rats. Chase had learned this in Year 7 and thought it was funny and still thought it was funny, which was perhaps the most useful thing his education had given him.

Deano emerged from below, blinking in the light. "That was rough."

"That was nothing," Chase said. "You should see it in winter."

"I don't want to see it in winter. I want to see the pub."

They inflated the tender, loaded the cooler, and motored to shore. The settlement was small enough to walk across in ten minutes: the general store where you could buy sunscreen and ice cream and overpriced fishing tackle, the bakery that made pies you queued twenty minutes for, the bike hire where tourists picked up their island transport and wobbled off into the scrub to be photographed with quokkas. And at the centre of it, like the sun around which everything orbited, the Rottnest Hotel -- the pub, the beer garden, the social engine of every Rotto weekend Chase had ever known.

They were early enough to get a table in the garden. Mid-strength -- the island's rule, which Chase observed with the amused contempt of someone carrying a cooler full of full-strength Exports on the tender. He bought the first round. He always bought the first round. This was not generosity, or not only generosity. It was position. The boy who bought the round was the boy with the resources, and the boy with the resources was the boy others oriented toward, and the orientation was the point.

The pub filled. It filled the way it always filled on a Rotto Saturday -- the boat crowd, the ferry crowd, the overnighters from the cabins. The boys from Scotch and Aquinas and Hale, the girls from PLC and Iona and St Hilda's, the whole intricate social map of Perth's western suburbs reassembled on a limestone island eighteen kilometres offshore. Everyone knew everyone. Everyone's parents knew everyone's parents. You could draw a line from any face in this beer garden to any other face through a chain of no more than two connections -- school, yacht club, parents' dinner party, property transaction, charitable board. This was Perth. It was small. It was closed. And Chase existed at the centre of it with the unexamined comfort of a man who had never stood outside.

He saw the girl. She was with her friends at a table near the wall, one of the PLC group, dark hair, a tan that came from actual sun and not a bottle, wearing a linen shirt over her bikini top with one shoulder slipping. She looked at Chase and then looked away, and the looking away was the thing, because the looking away was an invitation disguised as indifference, and Chase understood this language because it was the language his mother spoke, the language of power through withholding, though if anyone had pointed this out to him he would have denied it with the full force of his considerable self-certainty.

He did not go to her. He stayed where he was, telling a story about a swell they'd hit on a crossing last month, making Macca laugh, buying another round, being the centre. She would come to him, or a friend would manufacture the encounter, or they would circle each other for the rest of the afternoon until the alcohol and the proximity and the logic of Saturday made the outcome inevitable. He was in no rush. Rushing was for blokes who weren't sure. Chase was always sure.

They swam. The jetty at Thomson Bay was high enough to jump off -- four metres, maybe five -- and they did, one after another, Macca executing a bomb that sprayed a family of tourists, Deano entering with a pencil dive that barely rippled the surface, Chase with a running jump that was all power and no form, the water hitting him like a cold hand across the whole of his body and then the silence underneath, the blue-green silence, the white sand bottom visible below, the shadow of the jetty above, and the feeling -- the clean, wordless, animal feeling -- of being exactly where he was supposed to be.

They surfaced. They floated. The water was cold -- late autumn, maybe eighteen degrees -- but after the first shock it was perfect, the kind of cold that makes every nerve ending declare itself. They trod water and talked rubbish and Deano tried to splash Macca and Macca put him in a headlock and Chase floated on his back and looked at the sky, which was enormous and cloudless and so blue it made his chest ache, though he would not have called it an ache and would not have known what to do with the word.

---

He came back to the boat at dusk. The tender puttered across the mooring field, the outboard coughing in the way it always coughed when it had been sitting in the sun, and the *Chardonnay* was where he'd left her, white and solid on the mooring, the lights not yet on, the flybridge a dark silhouette against the sunset.

His mates went below. Deano was on his phone, scrolling. Macca was making a sandwich from the supplies they'd brought -- bread, ham, cheese, the kind of food that tasted better on a boat than it had any right to. Chase should have gone down too. He was hungry, and tired in the good way, the way you're tired when your body has been in the water and the sun all day and everything is loose and heavy and right.

But he went up.

He climbed the flybridge ladder and found his father sitting there. Bob was in the skipper's chair, a beer beside him -- the same beer from this morning, Chase thought, or maybe a fresh one that he'd barely touched. He was looking west. The sun was almost gone, sitting on the horizon like a coal, the sky around it running through the whole spectrum -- copper, amber, rose, violet, deepening toward blue overhead -- and the water had gone from turquoise to silver to something darker, something that held the light and let it go at the same time.

"Hey," Chase said.

"Hey, mate."

Chase sat in the passenger chair and put his feet up on the dash. They sat there. The silence between them was the comfortable silence of men who share a space without needing to fill it, and Chase appreciated this about his father, or rather: he accepted it without appreciation, the way you accept air, because it had always been this way.

"Port engine was running a bit rough on the way over," Chase said.

"Injectors, probably."

"Yeah. Might need to pull them."

"I'll call the mechanic Monday."

They talked about the boat for a while. The engine, the anodes, whether the tender's outboard needed a service. This was the language they shared -- mechanical, practical, the language of things that could be fixed with tools and parts and money. It was not a limited language. It was rich, in its way. It was how they said the things they could not say in other ways: *I am here. I see you. I care about this thing we share.*

Chase talked about the Dockers' season. About a game he'd been to last weekend, the crowd, a mark someone took in the third quarter that was the best thing he'd seen all year. Bob listened, nodded, offered a comment. Chase talked about Deano's property deal, a development in Scarborough, the margins, the risk. Bob asked a question that was smarter than Chase expected -- something about the financing structure -- and Chase answered and for a moment they were two men having a conversation about money, which was the family's native tongue, the language Jim had spoken and Bob had inherited and Chase was learning, the lineage of numbers passed from father to son like an accent.

Then Bob went quiet.

Chase was still talking -- something about the development timeline, about Deano's brother, about the permits -- when he realised his father had stopped responding. He looked over. Bob was staring at the ocean. Not at the sunset, which was nearly gone now, but at the water itself, the dark surface of the Indian Ocean stretching west toward a horizon that was dissolving into night. And his face --

Chase had seen his father's faces. He'd seen the charm face, the one Bob wore at yacht club dinners and client lunches, the warm and open face that made people feel their money was safe. He'd seen the tired face, the one he wore at the end of a long day, the one that sagged around the jaw and showed his age. He'd seen the angry face, which was rare -- Bob didn't do anger, or didn't show it, which might not be the same thing. He'd seen the Dad face, the one that appeared when Chase did something right on the boat, the one that was pride without words.

This was not any of those faces.

This was his father with no face at all. Or -- not no face, but a face that had been stripped of everything Chase knew how to read. It was open in a way that was almost frightening, the features slack, the eyes seeing something that was not the ocean, or was the ocean but was also something else, something behind the ocean or beneath it or beyond it. It was the face of a man measuring a distance -- not a physical distance but a distance between things Chase could not identify, between where Bob was and where some other version of Bob existed, some version Chase had never met and could not imagine.

He felt something cold in his stomach. Not fear, exactly. Something adjacent to fear. The feeling you get when a wave you thought was small lifts the boat higher than you expected and for a moment the deck tilts and you can't tell which way the water wants to take you.

"You right, Dad?"

Bob turned. And the face disappeared. It folded away so quickly that Chase almost doubted he'd seen it -- the features rearranging, the eyes refocusing, the performance clicking back into place like a mask that had slipped and been caught.

"Yeah, mate." A pause. "Good sunset."

Chase looked at the sunset. It was almost over. The last band of copper along the horizon, the sky above it darkening toward purple, the first stars beginning to show. It was a good sunset. He'd seen a thousand of them from this boat.

"Yeah," he said. "It was."

They sat there for a while longer. The lights of the other boats came on around the mooring field, one by one, reflecting in the black water. From below came the sound of Macca and Deano arguing about something -- music, or food, or whose turn it was to use the head -- the ordinary sounds of his ordinary life. Chase let the moment go. He let it go because he was twenty-one and did not have the language for what he'd just seen, because his family had trained him to let things go, because the alternative was to ask a question he was not equipped to understand the answer to.

He filed it away. The place where he filed it was the same place his father filed things -- the sealed room, the closed door, the place below language and thought where the unspeakable waited. He did not know he had such a place. He did not know his father had one too. He knew only that his dad was in one of his moods, and that the sunset was good, and that the beers below were cold, and that tomorrow they would make the crossing back and the world would be what it always was.

"Want a beer?" Chase asked.

"Yeah," Bob said. "Why not."

Chase climbed down the ladder. He did not look back.
