# Chapter 3: The Gaggle

She arrived second, which was correct. Not first -- first was eager, first was the new girl, first was the woman who had nowhere else to be -- and not last, because last was Margot's privilege and Margot was never late so much as delayed, which was a different thing, a thing that communicated that one's time was valuable and one's entrance was worth waiting for. Jeannie parked the Range Rover on Bay View Terrace -- the full-size, not the Evoque; she had lobbied for the upgrade and Bob had signed the lease without discussion, which was how most things in her life arrived -- and checked her face in the rear-view mirror. This was not vanity. Or it was vanity, but it was also something else: a diagnostic, a systems check, the way a pilot scans instruments before takeoff. Hair: pulled back, the colour still good, the grey managed by Dario at his salon in Subiaco every five weeks. Face: the Botox was holding; the filler along the jawline was subtle enough that no one had mentioned it, which meant it was either invisible or so common among her friends that it no longer registered as a procedure. Eyes: green, clear, no puffiness, the concealer doing its work. She was forty-five years old and she looked like herself at thirty-eight, which was not the same as looking young but was the goal -- not the reversal of time but its arrest, the preservation of a particular vintage, like a wine held at the right temperature in the right cellar.

The restaurant was one of the places on Bay View Terrace that had survived by understanding precisely what Claremont women wanted, which was good food served in a room that looked expensive without being ostentatious, with French doors open to the pavement so that arriving and departing could be witnessed by passersby. The table was in the window. Jeannie took the seat facing the door -- a habit so ingrained she no longer noticed it, though it served the same purpose as the rear-view mirror: a position from which to see everything and be seen seeing it.

Karen was already there, which made Karen first, which was fine because Karen was always first and everyone had stopped reading anything into it years ago. Karen had the look of a woman who had been pretty once in a way that suggested health -- good skin, wide smile, the kind of blonde that came from the sun and not from Dario -- and was now handsome in a way that suggested maintenance. Her husband sold industrial pumps to mining companies and drank too much at dinner parties, a fact the group acknowledged through a shared vocabulary of euphemism: Colin was "tired," Colin had "a big week," Colin was "letting off steam." Karen bore this with a composure that Jeannie simultaneously admired and found repulsive, because the composure was real -- Karen genuinely did not seem to mind -- and Jeannie could not understand a woman who did not mind. Not minding was, to Jeannie, a failure of standards.

They ordered. Jeannie asked for a sauvignon blanc -- it was twelve-fifteen, this was the first glass, the legitimate glass, the glass that did not count because everyone was having one and the sun was out and it was a Wednesday and lunch without wine was not lunch but a meeting. The wine arrived and she took the first sip and felt the familiar softening, the slight recession of the sharp thing that lived behind her sternum, the thing she had no name for and medicated with fifty milligrams of sertraline every morning and an undisclosed number of glasses of Doyen Bay every afternoon. The first sip was always the best. The first sip was the moment the day became manageable.

Amanda arrived next, then Pip, then the new woman -- what was her name, Renee, or Rachael, something with an R -- and the table filled and the noise rose and the performance began in earnest. The conversation moved with the practised rhythm of women who had been having this lunch, at this table or one like it, every second Wednesday for six years: children first (achievements, anxieties, the inexhaustible subject of school politics), then holidays (who had been where, who was going, the careful calibration of destinations to signal wealth without vulgarity), then real estate, then bodies, and then, always, inevitably, the main event: other people.

---

The gossip was the point. It circulated like a currency, and like a currency it had rules of exchange that were never written down but universally understood. You paid with information and you received social standing. The more exclusive the information -- the more private, the more damaging, the more delicious in its implications -- the higher the price it fetched. Jeannie was the central bank. She received the most and distributed the least, which was how power worked in this economy as in every other: the person who controls the supply sets the value, and the value of Jeannie's attention was set by the scarcity of her disclosures.

The conversation flowed. Pip's daughter had been accepted into medicine at UWA, which Pip delivered with the calculated modesty of a woman laying down an ace -- "We're just relieved she got through the interview" -- and which the table received with the correct admixture of congratulation and concealed assessment: where did this place Pip's daughter relative to their own daughters, and did it change the rankings? Amanda's husband had been promoted to partner at his law firm, which Amanda mentioned as if it had happened to someone else, a thing she had observed rather than benefitted from, because to appear too pleased was to acknowledge that the promotion mattered, and acknowledging that something mattered was a vulnerability the table did not reward. The new woman -- it was Renee, Jeannie decided, though she had not yet confirmed this and would not ask because asking was an admission that the name had not been considered worth remembering -- was too eager. She laughed too quickly. She offered information without being asked for it, which was the social equivalent of handing someone your wallet on the street: technically generous, practically suicidal. Jeannie watched her with the assessing patience of a woman who had been deciding who was in and who was out since she was twelve years old at PLC and the architecture of inclusion and exclusion was as legible to her as a balance sheet was to Bob.

The second glass arrived without Jeannie ordering it. The waitress knew. The waitress had been serving this table, or tables like it, for long enough to know that a pause in Jeannie's conversation, a glance toward the bar, a particular rearrangement of her posture that indicated the first glass was approaching empty, were signals as clear as a raised hand. The wine appeared. Jeannie lifted it. "Just a splash more," she said to no one, though the glass was full, and the phrase was not addressed to the table or to the waitress but to an internal auditor whose standards were slipping.

Beneath the performance, the calculations ran. Jeannie was assessing Renee (acceptable but eager; would need to learn restraint before being admitted to the inner layer of information, if she was admitted at all). She was monitoring Amanda's reaction to the promotion news (Amanda was performing indifference too hard, which meant the promotion was a bigger deal than she was letting on, which meant Amanda's husband was making real money now, which changed the relative positions at the table in ways Jeannie would need to recalibrate). She was tracking Karen's drinking -- Karen was on water, which was unusual, which could mean anything from a new diet to a pregnancy scare to something Colin had done that required Karen's full sobriety to manage. And she was watching Pip, who was the one Jeannie watched most carefully, because Pip was the one who always knew things.

Pip was slender, dark-haired, with the kind of face that rested naturally in an expression of sympathetic interest, a face that made people tell her things they had not intended to tell anyone. Her husband was in property -- not development, not construction, but the quieter and more lucrative business of commercial real estate, warehouses and office parks, the buildings you drove past without seeing that made their owners very rich. Pip sat on the committee of two charities and attended every school function and knew, at any given moment, the state of every significant marriage in the western suburbs the way a meteorologist knew the state of every significant weather system: not from malice but from professional attention, the gathering and processing of data that was, for Pip, a vocation. She was not the cruelest member of the gaggle. She was the most informed, which was worse.

"Have you heard about the Marcos?" Pip said, leaning in.

The table tilted toward her. This was the main event. Chris Marco -- the name had been in the papers for months, the trial, the $250 million, the investors who had lost everything, the accountant who had testified, the wife who had stood outside the courthouse in sunglasses and said nothing. It was the biggest financial scandal Perth had produced in a decade, and the table discussed it with the delicious horror of people who believed, with a certainty that was also a prayer, that it could never happen to them.

"She must have known," Amanda said. "You don't live in that house and not know."

"She says she didn't," Karen said, and there was something in her voice -- not defence exactly, but an awareness that a woman could live inside a lie and not know it was a lie, because the lie was the shape of the life and the life was the only shape she had.

"Bob always says," Jeannie heard herself say, and the words arrived with the confident, automatic rhythm of a woman quoting a husband she trusted, "if the returns look too good, they are." She lifted her wine glass. She believed this. She believed it the way she believed in the solidity of the floor beneath her chair, which is to say: completely, without examination, because examining it would require looking down, and Jeannie did not look down.

The third glass was ordered as accompaniment to the main course, which was a salad that cost thirty-two dollars and that Jeannie ate half of and pushed aside with the gesture of a woman who was satisfied rather than a woman who was monitoring, though both were true. The wine was doing its work. Two and a half glasses in, the sharp corners of her anxiety -- the thing behind the sternum, the sertraline hum, the low vibration of something she refused to examine -- softened. The laugh came more easily. The warmth was more generous. She touched arms. She leaned in. She was funny -- genuinely funny, with a timing and an edge that surprised people who had decided she was merely cruel, because cruelty and wit grew from the same root, which was observation, and Jeannie observed everything. She was, in this window, in this hour, in this particular chemical state of wine meeting medication meeting sunlight, the version of herself she wished she were all the time. The version that could enjoy a Wednesday. The version that did not need the performance because she was, for a moment, the thing the performance imitated.

Karen said something. It was a half-sentence, a fragment, offered into a gap in the conversation with the involuntary honesty of a woman who had been sitting on something all morning and could not hold it any longer. "Colin didn't come home Tuesday night." She said it to her wine glass. She said it as if she were telling the glass a secret, and the glass, being wine and being Karen's third, had lowered the drawbridge between what she held inside and what she released to the world.

The table went quiet. Not hostile quiet -- the other kind, the kind that descends when something real has been said in a room that operates on the principle that nothing real is said. Amanda looked at her plate. Pip looked at Karen with professional attention. Renee looked frightened, the way a new employee looks frightened when they stumble into a meeting they were not supposed to attend. Jeannie looked at Karen's hand, which was resting on the tablecloth beside her glass, and the hand was trembling, not much, just a tremor at the fingers, the vibration of a life under pressure that had found, in this moment, a crack through which to escape.

Jeannie put her hand on Karen's hand. The gesture was not performed. It was not calculated. It was the other thing, the thing that existed beneath the performance and that surprised everyone, including Jeannie, when it surfaced: genuine kindness. The kind that arrives without instruction, without agenda, from a place in Jeannie that the wine and the medication and the twenty years of competitive social survival had not quite managed to kill. She did not say anything wise. She said: "I know." She did not know what she knew. She did not know if she was talking about Colin or about the thing behind her own sternum or about the general condition of women who sit at tables like this and perform happiness while their lives rearrange themselves into shapes they did not choose. She said "I know" and she meant it and the moment passed.

The conversation resumed. Karen straightened. She took a sip of water. She said something about a renovation she was planning, and the table received this the way a body receives stitches: with relief, with the understanding that the wound had been acknowledged and could now be covered, and the surface of things restored.

Napoleon Street in the afternoon. Jeannie walked from where she had left the Range Rover -- two blocks away, on Eric Street, because the parking on Napoleon was impossible and because the walk gave her a reason to be seen walking, which was, on Napoleon Street in Cottesloe, a performance of belonging. The street stretched for two hundred and fifty metres from the highway to the ocean, and it held, in those two hundred and fifty metres, the essence of a particular kind of Perth life. FLANNEL, with its bias-cut slip dresses in the window, five hundred dollars apiece, the kind of dress that required a body maintained by Pilates and a life maintained by someone else's money. Picnic, the local label that had survived thirty years by understanding that Cottesloe women wanted clothes that looked like they had been thrown on and cost what they had been selected with. The cafes -- Vans with its pavement tables, Cimbalino with its Italian coffee and its perpetual queue of women in activewear carrying yoga mats and takeaway lattes and the particular air of women who had done something improving with their morning and deserved the rest of the day. The street had a quality Jeannie could have identified but never named: expensive without trying, beachy without being casual, a place where a two-hundred-dollar linen shirt sat in a window next to a candle made from surfboard wax and both seemed natural, because the naturalness was the performance, and the performance was so thorough that it had become the thing itself.

She walked past the shopfront where JEANNIE had been.

It was a wellness studio now, or it was becoming one -- the fitout was in progress, the windows papered over, a sign that read COMING SOON in the sans-serif typeface that every new business in Cottesloe used because the typeface said modern and clean and here, which was what every new business in Cottesloe needed to say. Jeannie walked past it. She did not slow down. She did not look at the window. But her body knew, the way Bob's hand knew the shape of a violin neck, the way the body always knows what the mind has decided to forget. The shopfront was where she had been herself, or the closest she had come to being herself, for eight years.

The boutique had opened in 2009. European labels -- Italian silks, French linens, a Danish brand of minimalist jewellery that Jeannie had discovered at a trade show in Sydney and introduced to Perth. The fitout had cost a hundred and eighty thousand dollars, which Bob paid without complaint, because Bob paid for things without complaint, which was his function and his failing. The shop was beautiful. The racks were spaced with the generosity of a gallery. The lighting was warm. The change rooms had good mirrors and fresh flowers and the scent diffuser that Jeannie had chosen -- fig and bergamot, a smell that she associated with Europe and that she wanted her customers to associate with Jeannie, both the shop and the woman, which were, for those eight years, the same thing.

It never turned a profit. In its best year it lost forty thousand dollars, which Jeannie described to Bob as "basically breaking even" and which Bob accepted because Bob accepted things, and because the shop made Jeannie bearable in a way that nothing else did, and the forty thousand was cheap at the price if it bought him a wife who came home in the evening not angry but merely tired, not cutting but merely distracted, a wife who had spent the day exercising her genuine talent -- and it was genuine, Jeannie had an eye, a real eye, for fabric and form and the alchemy of how a garment changed when a body inhabited it -- in a space that was hers.

It closed in 2017. Jeannie attributed this to the retail downturn. This was true enough -- retail was dying, and Napoleon Street had lost three shops that year, and the women who might have bought Italian silk in Cottesloe were buying it online from Net-a-Porter or driving to Claremont Quarter where the selection was larger and the parking was easier. But the deeper reason, which Jeannie knew and refused to know, was that she had opened a luxury boutique in a suburb where luxury was ambient, where it was in the air and the water and the sandstone and the Norfolk pines, and you could not sell it as a novelty because it was already the medium in which everyone lived. The shop was a redundancy. It sold people what they already were.

Jeannie stopped at a cafe. Not Vans -- too crowded, too seen -- but a smaller place, quieter, where she could sit with her back to the wall and her phone in her hand and the transition from wine to coffee could be managed without witnesses. She ordered a long black. The afternoon pivot, the recalibration. The coffee arrived and she wrapped her hands around it and felt the heat through the ceramic and opened Instagram.

Four thousand one hundred and eighty-seven followers. She had checked this morning. She had checked yesterday. She checked the way she checked her face in the rear-view mirror: compulsively, diagnostically, reading the number not as a number but as a verdict. It had been four thousand two hundred on Monday. Thirteen people, in the course of a week, had decided that Jeannie Harrington's curated life was no longer worth watching. She did not know who they were. She wanted to know who they were.

She scrolled through her recent posts. The boat at sunset, the water gold and violet behind it, the angle chosen to include the name on the stern -- *Chardonnay* -- without appearing to feature it. The garden, a corner of it, the frangipani in bloom against the limestone wall, shot in the morning light that made everything look both abundant and effortless. A flat lay of her coffee and a novel on the marble kitchen island, the novel chosen not for its content but for its cover, a pale blue that complemented the marble and the light and the aesthetic of a life that was, in every visible dimension, exactly what it appeared to be. She checked the likes. The coffee-and-novel post had ninety-three likes, which was down from her average of a hundred and ten, and the decline produced in her the same small, cold sensation that the follower drop had produced: not panic but something adjacent, a vibration of unease, the feeling that the surface she maintained with such precision was developing, somewhere she could not see, cracks she could not reach.

She posted a photograph of her coffee. Just the coffee, the white cup on the zinc table, the afternoon light falling across it. Caption: *Afternoons like this.* She watched the first likes arrive -- a pulse, a notification, a tiny chemical event in her brain that bore no relation to happiness but occupied the space where happiness might have been -- and she put the phone down and looked at the street and felt the weariness settle over her like a garment she could not take off.

She was tired. Not sleepy-tired. The other kind -- the kind that has nothing to do with sleep and everything to do with the accumulation of days that require effort and produce nothing except the maintenance of a surface that requires more effort every year. The lunch was work. The walking past the shop was work. The Instagram was work. The coffee was work, because even the coffee was a choice, a positioning, a statement about who she was and where she sat and how her afternoon was going. Everything was work, and the work produced nothing except itself, a perpetual motion machine of performance that ran on wine and sertraline and the diminishing returns of other people's attention.

The sertraline. Fifty milligrams, every morning, prescribed by Dr Chen at a practice in Subiaco that Jeannie had chosen specifically because none of her friends went there. The prescription was a secret in the way that things are secrets in the western suburbs: not hidden, exactly, but unmentioned, which amounted to the same thing in a social ecosystem where what was not said did not exist. She took the pill with water and a piece of toast at seven every morning. It arrived in her bloodstream at approximately the time she arrived at the day, and together -- the sertraline and the day -- they formed a partnership that produced a sensation Jeannie understood as normal, which was: flat, grey, managed, the emotional equivalent of a room with the curtains drawn and the thermostat set to a temperature that was neither warm nor cold but merely controlled.

She did not think this explicitly. Jeannie did not do explicit self-reflection, which was both her tragedy and her defence. She did not sit in cafes and think: I am performing a life I no longer enjoy. She did not think: the wine is not a pleasure but an anaesthetic. She did not think: I am forty-five years old and the things I maintain -- the face, the house, the marriage, the Instagram, the lunches, the Range Rover, the body that is thin enough and the hair that is young enough and the wardrobe that is right enough -- these things are not a life but a substitute for a life, and the substitute is all I have, and I cannot imagine replacing it because imagining a replacement would require imagining what I want, and I have not wanted anything for myself since the shopfront on Napoleon Street had my name on it and the door was open and the light came through the windows and for a few hours every day I was not Jeannie Harrington but JEANNIE, which is a different thing, a thing with its own space and its own name, and that thing is gone.

She did not think this. She drank her coffee. She checked her phone. She drove home.

---

The kitchen was lit by the late afternoon sun that came through the west-facing window above the sink, turning the marble island gold and throwing the shadow of the window frame across the floor in a long, clean cross. Shanel was at the island. Laptop open, a textbook beside it, a mug of tea held in both hands. She was reading. The screen showed something -- Jeannie glimpsed it as she came through the door, a page of text, dense, academic, not the kind of thing Jeannie would read -- and Shanel's face, bent over the screen, had a quality Jeannie did not recognise: absorption. Not the glazed absorption of scrolling, not the performative absorption of being seen reading, but the real thing, the kind of attention that forgets it is being observed because it has genuinely forgotten that the world outside the text exists.

Jeannie set her bag on the counter. Keys, phone, sunglasses. The ritual of return, the divesting of the public self, which for Jeannie was not a divesting but a recostuming, because her private self wore a costume too, a more comfortable costume, the costume of mother, of domestic authority, of the woman whose home was her stage and whose family was her production.

"You look tired, darling," Jeannie said.

Shanel looked up. Her face rearranged itself -- not dramatically, not with effort, but with the smooth, practised efficiency of a girl who had been adjusting her expression for her mother since she was old enough to understand that her mother's gaze was not neutral. "I'm fine," Shanel said. "Just reading."

"What are you reading?"

Shanel angled the laptop slightly. Not closing it, not hiding it, just tilting the screen a degree or two away from Jeannie's sightline, a gesture so small and so automatic that she did not know she was making it. The self-censoring. The pre-emptive withdrawal of the real thing before it could be assessed and found wanting, or worse, found irrelevant. "Just something for my media unit."

"Did you have lunch?"

"A wrap. At the ref."

"With Olivia?"

"With Sophie. And Maya."

The questions were directives disguised as curiosity. Jeannie asked about lunch to monitor food. She asked about companions to monitor the social map, to track the alliances and the shifts, because Shanel's social life was, for Jeannie, an extension of her own, a satellite operation whose status required constant updating. Shanel answered with the precision of a press secretary briefing a demanding editor: enough information to satisfy, not enough to invite follow-up, the minimum viable disclosure.

Jeannie poured herself a glass of water. The wine fridge was to her left, at eye level, the white side glowing faintly, the bottles arranged by label and by date, the way she arranged everything. She did not reach for it. Not yet. The pivot from lunch wine to afternoon coffee to evening wine required a gap, a demilitarised zone, a period of sobriety that was functional rather than principled and that Jeannie maintained the way she maintained everything: through discipline and timing. She drank the water. She stood at the counter and looked at her daughter.

Shanel was nineteen. She had Jeannie's bone structure -- the high cheekbones, the fine jaw -- and Bob's colouring, a warmth in the skin and the hair that softened Jeannie's angularity. She was beautiful in the way that nineteen-year-old girls are beautiful: unconsciously, wastefully, with a carelessness about the beauty that Jeannie found both enchanting and enraging, because to be careless about beauty was to be careless about the thing that mattered most, and to be nineteen and beautiful and careless was to possess a currency you did not know the value of, and Jeannie wanted to tell her, wanted to grab her shoulders and say: this does not last. Use it. It does not last.

She sat down next to Shanel at the island. She was still softened from the lunch, from the wine, from the genuine moment with Karen, from the afternoon walk that had tired her body if not her mind. She touched Shanel's hair. The gesture was tender. It was real. Jeannie was capable of this -- of tenderness, of the impulse that arrives before the performance catches up with it and converts it to something instrumental. She touched her daughter's hair and said: "You have my bone structure."

Shanel smiled. The smile was correct: pleased, acknowledging, the smile of a daughter receiving a compliment from a mother who is also paying herself one. Beneath the smile, something else -- not rebellion, not the dramatic resistance that Jeannie would have known how to manage, but a fainter thing, an unease, the sensation of being looked at and not seen, of being claimed rather than known, of being a reflection rather than a person. She did not have a word for this feeling. She was nineteen. She filed it in the place where she filed the things she could not say, which was growing fuller every year.

"I was reading about --" Shanel began. She had turned the laptop back toward Jeannie, tentatively, the way you hold out a hand to an animal you are not sure of. The screen showed a text. "It's about how people construct identities that --"

Jeannie was already moving. Not physically -- she was still beside Shanel, still close, still touching distance -- but her attention had shifted, the way attention shifts in people for whom other people's inner lives are interesting only as they relate to their own. She was reaching for the wine fridge. The movement was automatic, unremarkable, the gesture of a woman at home at the end of an afternoon doing what she did at the end of every afternoon: opening the fridge, selecting a bottle, the Doyen Bay sauvignon blanc that she bought by the case from a wine shop in Claremont, pulling the cork with the corkscrew that lived in the third drawer, the one gesture in her daily repertoire that required no performance because it was witnessed only by her daughter, who did not count, or who counted differently, who counted as an extension of self rather than an audience.

"Hmm?" Jeannie said. She was pouring. "Oh, that sounds interesting. Is it for your media thing?"

The misunderstanding was total and mutual. Shanel could not explain -- could not say that the text was not about her media thing but about something larger, something about the way people build selves out of surfaces and performances and the gap between the constructed self and whatever exists beneath it, which was, had either of them been able to see it, a description of their family so precise it could have been written by someone watching them through the kitchen window. And Jeannie could not hear -- could not hear the half-sentence as an offering, a daughter reaching across the space between them with something she had found in a book that felt important, that felt like the beginning of an understanding she could not yet articulate. Jeannie heard "media thing" and filed it under coursework, and Shanel saw the filing happen and withdrew the offering and returned to her textbook and the moment closed the way all their moments closed: quietly, without drama, with both of them pretending it had not been a moment at all.

"Have you decided what you're wearing to the Hendersons'?" Jeannie said.

"Not yet," Shanel said.

Jeannie took her wine to the living room. The west-facing windows were full of light -- the Indian Ocean was out there, beyond the road and the grass and the pines, catching the afternoon sun and throwing it back in a sheet of gold that filled the room with a warmth that was physical and impersonal and beautiful and had nothing to do with the people who lived inside it. She sat on the sofa. She checked her phone. The coffee post had forty-seven likes. She scrolled. She sipped. The sertraline hummed its flat grey hum. The wine softened the edges the sertraline had not reached. The afternoon became the evening, and the evening became the night, and the night became the closed door of her retreat, the white linen, the Diptyque candle, the phone glowing in the dark, the scroll, the search for something she could not name in a screen that held everything except the thing she was looking for.

In the kitchen, Shanel closed her laptop. She rinsed her mug. She stood at the sink and looked through the window at the Norfolk pines and the sea beyond them and thought about the passage she had been reading, about constructed identities, about surfaces and depths, about the gap between who you appear to be and who you are, and she thought about her mother at the wine fridge and her father on the lawn at seven in the morning and the house that was always quiet in a way that had nothing to do with sound, and she did not think these thoughts to a conclusion because she was nineteen and the conclusions were still forming, still gathering in the place below language where the things you will eventually understand wait for you to be ready.

She turned off the kitchen light. She went to her room. She closed the door.
