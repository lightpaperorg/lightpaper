# Chapter 29: The Smashing

The last note of the Chaconne hung in the air the way a bell's sound hangs after the clapper has stilled -- not fading so much as thinning, spreading outward until it became indistinguishable from the silence, until the silence itself seemed to vibrate with the memory of what had just passed through it. The open D string. The final chord resolved to a single voice, the lowest voice, the bass that had been walking beneath the music for fifteen minutes like a heartbeat beneath a body's noise, and now the heartbeat was the only sound, and then it too was gone, and the silence that replaced it was unlike any silence the terrace on Marine Parade had ever held.

It was a silence with weight. It pressed down on the eighty-odd people standing in the blue dusk with their glasses and their plates and their half-formed thoughts, pressed them into a kind of paralysis that was not discomfort but something in the neighbourhood of awe -- something that made the body quiet and the mind stop and the breath hold and the ordinary machinery of social performance go still. No one clapped. No one moved. The fairy lights strung along the terrace railing swayed in the sea breeze and threw shifting patterns across the limestone floor, and the sky beyond the Norfolk pines was the colour of a bruise -- deep blue shading to violet over the ocean, the last copper gone from the horizon, the first stars appearing in the east like punctuation marks in a sentence no one could read.

Jim Harrington was weeping. He stood near the edge of the marquee in his oversized suit, the collar standing away from his diminished neck, and the tears ran freely down the vertical creases of his face, and he made no effort to stop them or hide them. He was eighty years old. He had spent his entire adult life practising restraint, and the restraint had broken -- not from weakness but from the force of what he had just witnessed. His son, standing on a terrace with a violin, playing music that Jim did not understand and did not need to understand because understanding was not the point. The point was that the music was beautiful and his son had made it and Jim had never known this was inside him, had never asked, had never thought to ask. He reached for Bob now, moving forward through the still crowd with the careful steps of an old man on uneven ground, one hand extended, the other gripping the arm of the woman beside him -- a cousin, a neighbour, someone whose name did not matter. "Robert," he said. Just the name. His voice cracked on the second syllable. He said nothing else.

Chase stood ten feet from his father. He had not moved for fifteen minutes. The two beers he had been holding were both on the ground now, set down at some point during the performance he could not remember. His girlfriend had said something to him and he had not heard it. His face was open -- open in the way a door is open when something has forced it from the inside, not gently, not by invitation. He was looking at his father and he was rewriting the man in real time, the spreadsheet of assumptions being overwritten by something that had no column header, and the process was not finished, would perhaps never be finished, but it had begun, and beginning was the thing.

Shanel was at the far edge of the terrace, her arms wrapped around herself, her eyes wet but her face still. She was not looking at her father. She was looking at the ocean beyond him, the last light dying on the water, and she was feeling -- not thinking, feeling -- that the sound her father had just made was the same thing as the photograph she had deleted from her phone three months ago, the one that was beautiful and that she had erased because it did not match her feed. It was the same thing as the passage in Virginia Woolf that had lit her up in a tutorial and that she had not mentioned to anyone. It was the thing she had been editing out. And the editing was the lie.

Katarina lowered her phone. She slid it into her pocket. Her hand was steady. Her face was not. She looked at Bob across the terrace -- he was standing with the violin in one hand and the bow in the other, his arms at his sides, his face carrying the expression of a man who has been somewhere very far away and has not yet fully returned -- and she said, quietly, so that only he could hear: "That was the best you have ever played." She meant the notes, the intonation, the phrasing. She also meant the other thing, the thing that had no technical name. He had played it as though his life depended on it. Because it did.

The crowd began to stir. A few uncertain claps, the tentative percussion of people who did not know whether applause was appropriate, whether this had been a performance in the category they understood -- the kind that ended and was followed by clapping, the social contract of appreciation -- or something else, something that applause would diminish. The clapping spread unevenly, like rain beginning. Jim reached Bob and took his hand in both of his, the grip surprisingly strong, and Bob looked down at his father's wet face and felt the recognition land -- his father seeing him, perhaps for the first time -- and it nearly broke him. Nearly. He held.

Guests approached. Handshakes, shoulder-clasps, the clumsy verbal responses of people who had been moved and did not know how to say so. "That was incredible, mate." "I had no idea." "How long have you been doing that?" Bob answered on autopilot. He was still inside the piece. The Chaconne was still resonating in his nervous system -- the sustained concentration, the feeling of having been somewhere else for fifteen minutes and not yet having fully returned.

He looked for Jeannie. He looked for her face in the crowd the way he had always looked for her face, with the automatic, undiminished hope of a man who has loved a woman for twenty years and has never stopped believing that the next time she looks at him, something will be different.

She was not on the terrace.

He set the violin down. On the table beside the caterer's trays, on a white cloth napkin, beside a tray of uneaten canapes -- the Stradivarius, the instrument whose authentication report had arrived in his email that morning, whose value exceeded the price of every house on this street combined, resting on a napkin on a trestle table in a garden in Cottesloe. The bow beside it. Unprotected. The casualness was not carelessness. It was the gesture of a man who had just given everything he had and had nothing left to protect.

He was still looking toward the glass doors of the house when Jeannie came through them.

---

She walked the way she had always walked into rooms: straight-backed, chin level, her body communicating control even when the thing inside her body was beyond it. She had been in the kitchen for three minutes. Long enough for Sandra Kemp to lean close a second time and say the other thing, the worse thing, the thing about the fund and ASIC and the word *investigation*, and Jeannie had listened the way the limestone walls of her house absorbed the afternoon heat -- silently, completely, holding it inside where it could do its slow, structural damage unseen. She had set down her glass of wine. No. She had finished it first. Then she had set it down, on the marble counter, precisely, the base aligned with the edge, because Jeannie did not slam things down or leave them at angles, because the maintenance of surfaces was the last discipline available to her and she would not relinquish it, not yet, not until she was through the glass doors and onto the terrace where the man who had done this to her was standing in the applause of eighty people who did not know what she now knew.

She came through the doors.

The crowd was between her and Bob. People tried to speak to her -- a hand on her arm, a smile, someone saying her name -- and she did not hear them. She walked through them. They parted. Not consciously; they felt her coming. The energy she carried cleared a path, the way a current in the ocean clears a channel through sand -- not violently, but with a force that could not be resisted because it was not a force that asked permission.

She reached Bob.

He turned. He saw her face.

Her face was the face he had seen a thousand times -- the composed, beautiful, carefully maintained face of a woman who had spent forty-five years learning to show nothing -- but something had come through. Something had cracked the architecture. Her jaw was set in a way that pulled the tendons of her neck taut, and her eyes, which were green and clear and usually carried the cool surveillance of a woman scanning for threats, were bright with something that was not tears. It was the brightness of a furnace door opened. It was heat.

"How could you?"

The words were not shouted. They were spoken at conversational volume, which was worse. The conversations nearest them stopped. The words -- *how could you* -- were heard by everyone within ten metres, which was everyone.

Bob's face shifted. Confusion. The honest, unguarded confusion of a man who has just played the most important performance of his life and is still half-inside the music and cannot understand why his wife is looking at him as though he has committed a crime. He thought she meant the performance. He thought she was angry about the violin -- the midlife hobby displayed in public, the embarrassment of a man in a suit playing an instrument at his own birthday party. He started to speak.

"Jeannie--"

"How could you do this to me?" Louder now. The pretence of conversational volume abandoned. "In front of everyone? In front of *me*?"

He reached for her arm. She stepped back. The step was small and absolute, a border drawn.

"I don't--"

"You've been lying to me. For *years*." Her voice climbed. Not to a shout -- Jeannie did not shout; shouting was what women who had lost control did, and Jeannie had not lost control, she had arrived at a place beyond control where every word came out with the serrated precision of something that had been sharpened by twenty years of swallowed fury. "The fund. The teacher. All of it."

Bob's face changed.

The confusion became understanding became terror. Not fear of Jeannie -- he had never been afraid of Jeannie; you do not fear the person you love, you grieve them -- but fear of exposure. The fund. She knew about the fund. Someone had told her. The room tilted. The fairy lights blurred. He felt the limestone under his feet become uncertain, as though the terrace itself were shifting, and he understood, in the space between one heartbeat and the next, that the two structures he had built his life upon -- the fund and the marriage, the public lie and the private one -- were collapsing simultaneously, and that there was no sequence of words in any language that could stop them.

"Jeannie, please. Not here. Can we--"

"Not here?" The volume rose. Her face flushed, the colour climbing her neck and spreading across her cheekbones, breaking through the foundation and the concealer and the carefully managed surface like something geological, something that had been building pressure for years. "*NOT HERE?*" Several guests stepped back. A woman near the marquee put her hand over her mouth. "Where, then? In your office? With *her*?"

She turned. She looked at the table. She looked at the violin.

---

The moment lasted perhaps two seconds. Later, people would describe it as longer -- would say they saw it coming, that they could have stopped her, that there was time. There was no time. Two seconds is the duration of a breath, or a chord, or the space between lightning and thunder when the storm is directly overhead.

Jeannie picked up the violin.

She picked it up by the neck, the way you would pick up a bottle. Not the way a musician holds an instrument -- cradled, supported, the weight distributed between the chin and the collarbone and the left hand. She gripped it with her right hand around the neck, the scroll hanging down, the body swinging, and the gesture was so fundamentally wrong, so violently careless, that Katarina, standing near the garden wall, made a sound -- a small, involuntary intake of breath, the sound a mother makes when a child reaches for a flame.

Bob saw what she was doing. His face -- the novel has described this face many ways across twenty-eight chapters: the bland handsomeness, the accommodating smile, the concentration of playing, the incandescence of the D major section. What crossed it now was none of these. It was the face of a man watching his child step into traffic. Not anger. Not even fear. Something beyond both: the total, paralysing helplessness of witnessing the destruction of the thing you love most and being unable to reach it in time.

"Jeannie. No. Please. You don't understand -- that instrument--"

She saw his face. She saw that he was more afraid for the violin than for their marriage. And that confirmed everything. The violin was the other woman. The violin was where he went when he left her. The violin was the thing he loved, and she was not, and the knowledge landed in her body like a blade, and the blade became the hand that lifted the instrument above her head, and the hand became the act.

She brought it down against the limestone edge of the terrace railing.

The neck snapped first. The sound was a crack -- not the sound of wood breaking but the sound of a bone breaking, a wet, structural failure, the fibres of the maple separating along a line that had held for three centuries. The body hit the stone and the top plate split. The spruce -- the three-hundred-year-old spruce, cut from a tree that had grown in the Alpine cold during the Maunder Minimum, shaped by hands in Cremona or Absam, the grain so fine that under magnification the growth rings were nearly invisible -- split along the grain with a sound that was not a sound but an exhalation, as if something alive had been killed. The ribs splintered. The back plate cracked but held, a single fracture running from the chin rest to the lower bout like a fault line. The strings unwound and coiled like severed nerves. The bridge snapped. The sound post -- a tiny dowel of spruce no bigger than a pencil, the post that transmitted vibration from the belly to the back, the single small piece of wood upon which the instrument's voice depended -- rolled free across the limestone and came to rest against Bob's shoe.

The violin lay on the terrace in pieces.

The scroll -- the carved head, still attached to the pegbox and a few inches of splintered neck -- had broken free on impact and rolled across the stone, the volute turning slowly, the spiral that the luthier had carved by hand three centuries ago turning end over end like a head severed from a body.

The silence that followed was deeper than the silence after the Chaconne. It was the silence of eighty people who had just witnessed an act of violence and were calculating whether it was over, the silence of a crowd that has been shoved past the boundary of the social and into the territory of the real, where no one knows the protocol because no protocol exists.

---

Bob did not move to stop her. He could not have -- the interval between her picking up the instrument and the instrument hitting the stone was two seconds, perhaps three, and in those seconds his body did not obey him, did not respond, did not do any of the things a body should do when the thing it loves is being destroyed. He stood. He watched. And something left his face.

Every expression. Every animation. The warmth, the confusion, the fear, the openness that had been there during the performance and that was still there when he turned to meet Jeannie -- all of it went. What remained was a surface. Smooth, empty. The face of a man who has been erased.

He made a sound. It was not a scream and it was not a sob. It came from a place in his body that had no name -- below the diaphragm, below the lungs, from the place where the violin's vibrations had lived for two years, the place that had held the Chaconne five minutes ago and now held nothing. The sound left his mouth and the guests nearest him stepped back from it, physically, as if the sound were a heat that could burn them. It was a single syllable. It was not a word. It lasted less than a second and then it stopped and the silence returned, and the silence was worse.

He knelt. Not gracefully, not with control -- his knees simply gave way, the way a structure gives way when the load-bearing element is removed, and he was on the limestone, on his knees, in his good suit, beside the wreckage of the instrument. His hands hovered over the fragments. He did not touch them. His hands moved above the pieces of the violin the way a person's hands move above the body of someone who has just died -- wanting to touch, afraid to touch, knowing that touching will confirm the irreversibility of what has happened.

The sound post lay against his shoe. He looked at it. A cylinder of spruce, five millimetres in diameter, sixty millimetres long. The soul of the instrument. *L'ame*, the French call it. The soul.

Jeannie stood with the broken neck in her hand. She was still holding it. Her fingers were white around the maple, the knuckles bloodless, and she looked down at the wreckage on the terrace floor and then at the piece of wood in her hand and she did not understand what she had done. She knew she had broken a violin. She did not know she had broken a Stradivarius. She did not know she had destroyed an object worth more than the house they were standing in, an object that was the fund's only remaining asset of genuine value, an object that could have -- had it survived this evening -- been sold to pay back every dollar Bob owed and kept him from prison. She knew only that she had broken the thing he loved. It was the most honest act of her life. It did not make her feel better. It made her feel nothing.

She opened her hand. The broken neck fell to the stone with a clatter that was obscene in the silence.

Chase had moved forward. He was standing three feet from his mother, his face white, his hands at his sides. He said: "Mum." Just the word. She did not hear him.

Shanel had turned away. She could not watch. Her hand was over her mouth and she was shaking, the fine tremor running through her shoulders and her arms, and she would remember this -- would carry the image of the violin against the stone for the rest of her life -- and the image would become inseparable from the sound of the Chaconne, the two things fused together, the creation and the destruction, the most beautiful thing she had ever heard followed by the most terrible thing she had ever seen.

Jim had closed his eyes. His hand still extended, reaching for the son who was no longer standing.

Katarina was motionless. Her hand was in her pocket, on her phone. The phone that contained the recording. She did not move toward the fragments. She understood, with the instant clarity of a musician, that the instrument was gone. A violin is not a thing that can be repaired after this kind of destruction. The top plate was split. The ribs were splintered. The geometry that produced the sound -- the precise relationship of arching and thickness and wood density and air volume that the maker had calibrated three centuries ago -- was destroyed. The sound was dead. What she had on her phone was all that remained.

Several guests had reached for their phones. Not to help. To text.

Someone -- a man, a work colleague, someone with the practical disposition of a person who processes catastrophe by naming it -- said: "Jesus Christ."

The words hung in the air. No one answered them.

---

Bob picked up the scroll.

He reached for it the way a man reaches for something in a dream -- slowly, deliberately, his hand closing around the carved head with a gentleness that was almost unbearable to watch, because the gentleness was for the instrument, for the maker, for the three hundred years of music that the scroll represented and that the terrace floor had just ended. The volute fit his palm. The wood was smooth and warm. The spiral had no beginning and no end.

He stood. He rose from his knees in a single motion, the scroll in his right hand, and he was steady. That was the thing the guests would remember afterward -- the steadiness. A man who had just watched his life's work destroyed, who had knelt on stone beside the wreckage, who had made a sound that no one in that garden would ever forget, standing up and being steady. Not the steadiness of control. The steadiness of a man who has passed through something and come out the other side, where the air is different and the rules no longer apply.

He looked at Jeannie.

She looked at him.

What passed between them in that look was not anger and not forgiveness and not grief. It was recognition. He saw her -- perhaps for the first time in twenty years, he saw her clearly: the beautiful, miserable, terrified woman who had married a man she did not love and spent two decades punishing him for it, who had built a life out of surfaces and maintained it with wine and pills and the relentless management of appearances, who had just destroyed the most valuable object any of them would ever touch because she could not bear to see him love something that was not her, and who did not know -- would perhaps never know -- that she had been loved. That she had always been loved. That the first thing he had said before he played the Chaconne was *I love my wife*.

She saw him. She saw a stranger. The man on the terrace was not the man she had married, not the bland provider, not the passive accommodator, not the soft and reliable disappointment she had shaped her contempt around for twenty years. The man on the terrace was someone she did not recognise, and the recognition of her own failure to recognise him -- the understanding that she had lived with a person for two decades and never once looked at him -- crossed her face like a shadow and was gone.

The look lasted three seconds. Four. Then Bob turned.

He walked across the terrace. Past the caterer's tables. Past the marquee. Past the guests who stood in their clusters and stared and did not speak and did not move, because they were too shocked to move and too fascinated not to watch. He walked with the measured pace of a man who has somewhere to be, or nowhere, and the two are the same. The scroll was in his right hand. His shoes sounded on the limestone -- the good shoes, the Italian leather -- and then on the first step down to the grass, and then the sound changed, became softer, the shoes on the grass, and he descended the first terrace.

"Bob." Jeannie's voice. Not a shout. A sound pulled from her throat against her will, the voice of a woman who has just understood, too late, that something is happening that cannot be undone. "BOB."

He did not turn.

Chase started to follow. He took two steps and Shanel grabbed his arm. Her fingers closed on his forearm and she said, "Don't." The word came out flat, certain, with a conviction that surprised them both. Chase stopped. He looked at his sister. She shook her head. He did not know why he listened. She did not know why she had said it. Both of them would spend years wondering.

Bob descended the second terrace. The fairy lights no longer reached him. His white shirt caught the last of the sky's light -- the deep blue above the ocean, the first stars -- and then the Norfolk pines took him, their shadows swallowing his shape, and the grass absorbed his footsteps, and the party on the terrace above him was a blur of light and sound that grew fainter with each step, the way a radio signal fades as you drive away from the tower, the voices thinning, the music gone, until all that remained was the sound of the ocean ahead of him, which had been there all along, beneath everything, patient and vast and entirely indifferent to the things that happened on its shore.

He walked toward it.

No one followed.
