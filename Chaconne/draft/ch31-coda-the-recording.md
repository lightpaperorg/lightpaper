# Chapter 31 (Coda): The Recording

The last student left at four. A ten-year-old named Mia who was working her way through Suzuki Book 3 and who had, this afternoon, for the first time, held the bow with a looseness in the wrist that Katarina had been asking for since September -- a small thing, a millimetre of relaxation in the joint, but it changed the sound the way opening a window changes a room, the air suddenly moving, the stuffiness gone. Katarina had heard it and said nothing. She had nodded, once, and Mia had played the passage again, and the looseness had held, and Katarina had nodded again and said, "Good. That is how it feels. Remember how it feels." She did not say *well done* or *beautiful* or any of the words that parents wanted to hear through the studio door. She said *remember how it feels*, because feeling was the instrument's real teacher, not the woman holding the metronome, and if Mia could remember the sensation in her wrist -- the absence of effort, the letting-go that paradoxically produced more control -- then the lesson would survive the week between now and the next one, which was all a teacher could hope for: that something planted on a Tuesday afternoon would still be alive the following Tuesday, a small green thing pushing through the soil of a child's crowded and inattentive life.

Mia's mother collected her at the door. The usual exchange -- the tight smile, the question about progress that was really a question about value, about whether the $90 per hour was being converted into something the mother could measure and report to other mothers. Katarina answered the way she always answered: briefly, honestly, without the reassurance the mother was looking for. "She is listening better," Katarina said. The mother waited for more. There was no more. The door closed. The footsteps went down the stairs and out to the street, and the car door opened and closed, and the engine started, and the sound faded, and the studio was quiet.

The particular quiet of a room that has held music and is now holding its absence. The way a glass holds the shape of the water after the water has been poured out. Katarina sat in the straight-backed chair beside the music stand and let the quiet settle. She did this every day after the last student -- five minutes, sometimes ten, sitting in the room without playing, without listening, without doing anything except being present in the space where sound had been. It was not meditation. She would not have called it that. It was closer to what a carpenter does when he runs his hand along a surface he has just planed: checking for imperfections, feeling for the grain, confirming that the work is sound. The studio was her workshop. The quiet after teaching was her hand on the wood.

The room was small. A converted space above a shop on Rokeby Road -- not a shop anymore, a physiotherapy practice, and the faint chemical smell of massage oil sometimes rose through the floor on warm afternoons. The studio held a music stand, a straight-backed chair, a piano against the far wall that she used for accompaniment and that was tuned every six months by a man from Nedlands who talked too much and tuned well. A shelf of scores arranged by period and difficulty -- Suzuki at one end, the Bach Sonatas and Partitas at the other, the progression of a violinist's education laid out in spine widths. A mirror on the wall opposite the window, for checking students' posture. The window itself faced west, and the light that came through it now was the low gold light of a Perth autumn afternoon -- the light that arrived at this hour and this angle for a few weeks in April and May and that filled the room with a warmth that was not heat but colour, as if the air itself had been dipped in amber.

The rosin dust on the floor caught it. A fine powder, almost invisible, that accumulated over weeks and that she swept up on Sundays and that was already, three days after sweeping, beginning to reappear. The residue of horsehair on gut. The physical trace of the friction that produced sound. Evidence that work had been done here -- that a bow had been drawn across strings and the air had moved and something that did not exist before had briefly existed and was gone.

She sat. Her violin was on her lap -- the Czech instrument, the one she had played since she was seventeen, the one Kovar had helped her choose from a maker in Brno who worked in a converted garage and whose instruments had a darkness of tone that Kovar admired and that Katarina had come to love the way you love a voice you have heard every day for twenty years: not because it is the most beautiful voice, but because it is yours. She rested her hand on the instrument's body and felt the varnish under her fingers, cool and smooth, and beneath the varnish, beneath the wood, the faintest vibration -- residual, imagined, the ghost of the last note Mia had played still living in the spruce.

Her phone was on the music stand.

---

Three weeks had passed. Or four. She had not counted precisely, and the imprecision was deliberate -- a refusal to mark the days since the evening on the terrace, to give the calendar authority over something that existed outside of time, the way music exists outside of time, the way grief does. She knew the facts. She had read the newspaper reports. She had seen the headlines -- *Perth fund manager missing after Cottesloe beach incident*, and then, two days later, the other headlines, the ones about the fund, about ASIC, about the numbers that did not add up. She had read these with the detached attention of a person reading about a country she has visited but does not live in. The fund was not her Bob. The headlines were not her Bob. Her Bob was the man in the soundproofed office on St George's Terrace, drawing a bow across the strings of a violin that sang under his hands with a warmth that made her breath catch, the man whose intonation slipped on the high positions and whose vibrato was too wide and whose musicality, in the moments when he forgot to be afraid, was as honest as anything she had heard in twenty-five years of playing and listening and teaching.

She had not attended the memorial. There may not have been one -- she did not know, had not asked. She had not spoken to Jeannie, or Chase, or Shanel, or Jim, or anyone who had been on the terrace that evening. Journalists had called. She had not answered. A detective had come to the studio -- a woman, polite, thorough, asking questions about Bob's state of mind, about the lessons, about the violin. Katarina had answered carefully and completely and had said nothing that was not true and nothing that was private, because the private things -- the sound of his playing, the look on his face when a passage opened up, the afternoon he played the D major section for the first time and neither of them spoke for a full minute afterward -- were not for a detective's notebook. They were not for anyone. They were hers.

The word she used, when she thought of him, was *gone*. Not dead. The body had not been recovered, and the word *dead* implied a certainty that the ocean had not provided. *Gone* was accurate. *Gone* held the ambiguity -- the not-knowing, the incompleteness, the open question of a man who had walked into the water and not walked out, and whom the water had taken or not taken, and whom the sharks had found or not found, and who was now somewhere -- in the ocean, in the sand, in the vast and indifferent dark of the continental shelf -- or nowhere, which was the same thing, because the ocean made no distinction between a body and the absence of a body, between a man and the water that replaced him.

She picked up the phone. She found the recording. It was not hard to find -- it was the last video in her camera roll, the file that sat at the end of a sequence of photographs of students' hand positions and close-ups of bowing technique and a single image of a cat on Rokeby Road that she had taken three months ago and kept because the cat's expression -- imperious, self-contained, utterly uninterested in the photographer -- reminded her of Kovar.

The thumbnail showed a blur of sunset. Overexposed, the phone's camera overwhelmed by the light behind Bob, so that his figure was a dark shape against a white-gold wash. A silhouette. A man made of shadow holding a violin.

Fourteen minutes and forty-seven seconds.

She pressed play.

---

The sound came through the phone's speaker and filled the studio the way water fills a crack in stone -- finding every space, every corner, every silence.

His voice first. Thin, compressed by the phone's microphone, stripped of the warmth it had carried across the terrace that evening. But recognisable. His voice.

"Thank you all for coming. I love my wife. I love my children. I want to play you something."

Then the opening chord.

D, A, D, F-sharp. Through the tiny speaker, the chord was a ghost of itself -- the resonance flattened, the overtones lost, the great instrument's voice reduced to a frequency range barely wider than a telephone call. But the chord was there. The slight unevenness of the bow roll that was not a flaw but a signature, the way a voice has a grain that is not an imperfection but an identity. She closed her eyes.

The Chaconne played.

She did not listen to it the way she had listened on the terrace, standing in the garden with her phone held up and her heart in her throat. She listened to it the way a musician listens to a recording: with attention divided between the sound and the memory of the sound, between what the phone's microphone had captured and what had existed in the air that evening and was now gone, irretrievable, surviving only in the nervous systems of the eighty people who had been present and in this small, compressed, imperfect file.

She could hear things she had not heard that night. The ambient sound -- the sea breeze, a glass set down on a table, the shuffle of someone shifting weight. A cough in the crowd during the seventh variation, quickly stifled. The distant sound of the ocean beneath everything, a low wash that the microphone had picked up and that served, in the recording, as a kind of ground bass beneath Bob's ground bass, the ocean's voice beneath the violin's voice, the oldest sound beneath the newest.

She could hear his bow. The contact with the string, the slight scrape at the beginning of each stroke that a professional would have eliminated but that Bob had never fully eliminated and that gave his playing a quality of effort, of physical reality, that cleaner technique would have erased. She could hear his breathing -- or thought she could -- in the pauses between phrases, the quick intake of air that betrayed the concentration, the sustained demand of playing a fifteen-minute unaccompanied work from memory on a terrace in Cottesloe while the sun went down and your wife was in the kitchen and your life was ending.

The first section passed. The variations built. She heard the passage in the ninth variation where his intonation slipped -- a flattened C-sharp, quickly corrected, the kind of error that a listener without training would not notice and that a listener with training would forgive, because the phrase that followed was clean and the recovery was part of the music, the way a stumble is part of a walk, the way imperfection is part of any honest act.

Then the key changed.

The D minor broke open into D major, and even through the phone speaker, even compressed and distorted and stripped of everything the instrument had given it that evening, the shift was audible. The darkness lifting. The light entering. She had heard this section live, standing in the garden, and it had made her press the phone more firmly against her palm, as if the device could absorb what the air was carrying. She had heard Perlman play this section in a recording from 1990, and Hahn in a recording from 2018, and Kavakos in a concert in Prague that she had attended when she was twenty-three and that had changed her understanding of what the piece could be. None of them sounded like this. None of them sounded like a man who had nothing left to lose.

The D major section played through the small speaker and filled the small studio, and the quality of the light in the room seemed to change -- or she imagined it changed -- the gold deepening, the amber warming, as if the sound and the light were the same substance and the phone was a window into an evening three weeks gone, an evening that existed now only in this file and in the memories of the people who had been there, memories that would degrade and shift and contradict each other over time until the file was the only reliable witness, the only thing that would not change, the only record that Robert James Harrington had played the Chaconne on the evening of his fiftieth birthday on a terrace on Marine Parade while the sun went down behind him and the Indian Ocean glittered and his father wept and his son stood still and his daughter recognised something she had been suppressing and his wife was in the kitchen being told that everything was over.

The bariolage. She heard it -- raw, effortful, the repeated string crossings that required a bow arm of steel and a wrist of silk and that Bob had practised ten thousand times in the soundproofed office and that he played now, on the recording, with an intensity that was not technique but conviction, the fierce, uneven, truthful sound of a man who knew he was not good enough and refused to stop.

The return to D minor. The final variations. The architecture closing, the theme returning, the bass descending one last time.

The final chord. D, A, D, F-sharp. The same chord as the beginning. The circle completed.

Silence.

Then, on the recording, the sound of the crowd -- a rustling, a stirring, the uncertain beginning of applause, scattered, tentative. And then a voice, distant but clear, cutting through the applause with the precision of a knife through cloth:

"How could you?"

The recording ended. She had stopped it there, that evening, her thumb pressing the screen at the sound of Jeannie's voice. The file preserved exactly fourteen minutes and forty-seven seconds. The Chaconne, complete. Everything that followed -- the confrontation, the smashing, the walk, the beach -- existed outside the recording, in the world of the unrecorded, where most of life takes place.

She put the phone down on the music stand. The screen went dark. The studio was quiet. The gold light from the window had not changed -- or had changed, had shifted by a degree, the sun moving, the shadow of the building opposite advancing imperceptibly across the floor.

---

She picked up her violin. She rosined the bow -- three long strokes, the familiar gesture, the rosin's faint sweet smell rising from the friction, the amber dust settling on the horsehair. She stood. She needed to stand for this. Needed the full body, the breath from the diaphragm, the feet on the floor.

She placed the instrument under her chin. The chinrest found its mark -- the small depression in her jaw that matched the one that had formed on Bob's jaw, the callus that all violinists carry, the brand of the instrument, the mark that says *I am played* and *I am a player* and that Jeannie had noticed on Bob's jaw and had asked about once and never asked about again.

She drew a breath. She placed the bow on the string.

She played the opening chord of the Chaconne.

Her chord was clean. The bow roll smooth, the intonation precise, the four notes ringing with the clarity of a musician who had spent twenty-five years training her hands to do exactly this. The sound filled the studio -- rounder than Bob's sound, more controlled, more beautiful in the way that a polished stone is more beautiful than a broken one. And less true. She knew this. She had known it on the terrace, watching him play, hearing the imperfections that she would have corrected in a student and that she would not have corrected in him, because the imperfections were where the truth lived -- in the gap between what his hands could do and what his heart demanded, in the reaching past capability, in the falling short and the reaching again.

She played the descending line. She played the first variation, and the second, and the third. The sound of her violin -- the Czech instrument, darker than the Stradivarius, smaller in the room, more intimate -- filled the studio and pressed against the walls and the window and the mirror and the shelf of scores and was absorbed, the way all sound is absorbed, by the surfaces that receive it and hold it for a fraction of a second before letting it go.

She played through the first section. Her technique was clean. Her shifts were smooth. Her vibrato was controlled -- the narrow, focused vibrato that Kovar had insisted on and that she had adopted as her own, the vibrato that warmed the note without distorting it, that added colour without adding weight. She played well. She played the way she had always played: with discipline, with intelligence, with the precision of a woman who had understood at twenty-five that she would never be a soloist and had spent the next fifteen years learning to be something better, which was a musician who could play for the music's sake and not for the audience's and not for her own.

But she played it now with something she had not had before.

She played it with the knowledge of what this piece costs.

The D major arrived. The key changed. She felt it in her body before she felt it in her hands -- the lifting, the opening, the moment when the darkness breaks and the light enters and the music steps from one world into another. She had played this section a hundred times. She had played it in Prague and in Brno and in the studio on Rokeby Road, alone, for herself, in the evenings after teaching, when the room was quiet and the light was low and there was no one to hear her except the walls. She had never played it like this.

The tears came without sound. They ran down her face -- from the corners of her eyes, along the line of her cheekbones, down to her jaw, where they met the chinrest and pooled and slid onto the wood of the instrument. She did not stop playing. The bow arm continued. The left hand found the positions. The tears blurred her vision but she did not need to see -- the music was in her hands and in her memory and in the body that had been playing this piece for twenty years, and the body did not need eyes, and the body did not stop for grief. The Chaconne does not permit you to stop. It asks you to carry everything -- the technique and the feeling and the memory and the loss -- simultaneously, and to keep going. Bach did not stop. Bach composed the Chaconne and buried his wife and married again and raised his children and wrote the Mass in B minor and went blind and died. He did not stop. The music does not stop. You keep going because the music keeps going, and the music keeps going because that is what music does, it does not pause for the musician's grief, it insists, it demands, it carries you forward through the darkness and into the light and back into the darkness, and you play, and you weep, and you do not stop.

She played the bariolage. The repeated string crossings, the fierce alternation between the high voice and the low, the passage that had cost Bob months of practice and that she played cleanly, precisely, without the raw effort that had made his version of it so unbearable and so true. She played it well. She knew it was less.

The return to D minor. The final variations. The architecture closing in, the theme returning for the last time, the bass descending, the music narrowing like a corridor toward a door.

The final chord.

She held it. The bow pressed against the strings, the four notes ringing, the D and the A and the D and the F-sharp sustaining in the small room, and she held the chord longer than she needed to, longer than the score demanded, held it because releasing it would mean the end and the end would mean the silence and the silence would mean that the piece was over and the piece being over would mean that the last thing connecting her to the evening on the terrace -- the last living thread between the recording on her phone and the sound in this room -- would be gone.

She lifted the bow.

---

The studio was dim. The sun had gone behind the building opposite, and the gold had left the room, and the light that remained was the grey-blue light of a Perth autumn evening -- the light that comes after the gold, the light that is not dark but is no longer bright, the light in which everything looks both clear and far away.

She stood in the silence after the last note. Her violin at her side. The bow in her right hand, the horsehair slack. Her face wet. The rosin dust on the floor, catching the last of the light.

The phone on the music stand, its screen dark. The recording inside it. Fourteen minutes and forty-seven seconds. The only recording. The only proof that Robert James Harrington had played the Chaconne on the evening of his fiftieth birthday and that the playing had been imperfect and true and that it had mattered. She was the keeper. She had not asked to be the keeper. The role had arrived the way the recording had arrived -- not through intention but through presence, through the simple fact of being there, of holding up a phone, of pressing a button, of being the one person in the garden who understood what was happening and who had the instinct to preserve it. She was the keeper of the recording, and of the memory, and of the knowledge that a man who had spent his life being someone else had, in his final years, become himself, and that the becoming had been done through music, through the specific music of a piece composed in 1720 by a man whose wife had died while he was away, a piece that contained grief and transcendence and return, a piece that asked everything of the player and gave nothing back except the playing itself, which was enough. Which was everything.

The silence held.

Outside the window: Rokeby Road. A car passing. A bicycle bell, clear and small. A magpie's call, the last of the day, liquid and descending, the sound that was to Perth what the blackbird's song was to London or the muezzin's call was to Istanbul -- the sound of the city's particular evening, its signature in the air. And beneath it, distant, constant, faintly: the ocean. The Indian Ocean, two kilometres to the west, audible in the stillness of the evening through the open window, a low, continuous wash that was not a sound so much as a presence, the way a heartbeat is a presence -- felt rather than heard, always there, beneath everything, beneath the traffic and the birds and the bicycle bells and the silence of a studio where a woman had just played the Chaconne and wept.

She did not move. The silence continued. The room held her the way a room holds anyone who has played music in it -- with the residue of sound, with the memory of vibration, with the faint, invisible, indelible trace of what had passed through the air and was gone.

The light faded. The evening came. The city outside the window went on with its ordinary business -- the traffic, the shops closing on Rokeby Road, the families sitting down to dinner in the limestone houses of Subiaco, the lives lived at the level of the daily and the practical and the small. The ocean went on. The stars appeared. The recording waited on the phone in the dark studio, patient, complete, containing everything and nothing, the way all recordings do -- the sound preserved, the meaning left to whoever listens.

The music was over. The music was not over. The music was never over.
