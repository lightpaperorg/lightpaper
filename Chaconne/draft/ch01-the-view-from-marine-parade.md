# Chapter 1: The View from Marine Parade

The grass was cold under his feet. It was the cold of late autumn in a place that did not really have seasons, a mild affront, barely noticed, the dew soaking through the soles of his feet and into the soil beneath, as if the earth were drawing something out of him. Bob stood on the first terrace of the stepped lawn that descended from Marine Parade toward the beach, a coffee in his right hand, wearing yesterday's chinos and a linen shirt he had slept in, and he looked at the ocean.

It was early -- seven, or just past it -- and the light had that quality it only had at this hour: pale gold, provisional, as though the day had not yet committed to itself. The Norfolk Island pines threw long shadows westward across the grass, their strange columnar shapes dark against the brightening sky, like columns built to hold up nothing. Below and to the south, the Indiana sat closed and white on the beachfront, its colonial facade catching the first horizontal light. The esplanade was empty. A magpie picked at the edge of the path. The ocean beyond the sand was flat, glassy, a pewter colour that would burn off into blue within the hour, and it stretched -- as it always stretched, as it had stretched for every one of the fifteen years he had stood on this grass with a coffee in his hand -- unbroken to Africa, or to nothing, or to whatever existed on the far side of eleven thousand kilometres of Indian Ocean. The sky above it was enormous. That was always the thing you forgot about Perth until you stood outside before the day started and looked up: the sky was enormous, and the silence was almost total, and for a few minutes you could believe you were the last man alive, or the first, and that nothing had gone wrong yet.

This was his ritual on the mornings when he could not sleep, which was most mornings now. He would come out the back gate and cross Marine Parade -- the street empty, the houses on either side still shuttered, the Range Rover and the Mercedes parked nose-to-tail in the drive -- and he would stand on the grass and drink his coffee and do the thing he had done out here for years, which was not-thinking. Letting the view fill the space where thought would be. The view had cost him four and a half million dollars. That was what he had paid for the house in 2010, at the peak of the mining boom, stretching further than he should have because Jeannie wanted Marine Parade and Marine Parade was what Jeannie got. He did not think about this. He did not think about the fund, or about the phone call he needed to return, or about the quarterly reports that were due on Friday and contained numbers that did not correspond to anything real. He looked at the ocean and he was no one for a while, and the no one felt like the truest thing about him.

He was forty-nine. Sandy hair going grey at the temples. Six foot, with an extra ten kilos that had settled around his waist and softened the line of his jaw, so that his face, which had once been handsome in the way that suggested competence and trustworthiness, had gone bland -- a face for a bank advertisement, a face for the back page of a financial newsletter, a face that made people feel their money was safe. He was wearing RM Williams boots when he dressed, but he had come out barefoot, and the grass was cold, and his hands were large and well-kept, the hands of a man who had never built anything with them.

His left hand -- the one not holding the coffee -- curled slightly at his side. The fingers closed, not into a fist but around something that was not there, the thumb bracing against an invisible neck, the fingertips pressing against nothing. It was a gesture so small and so unconscious that Bob himself did not notice it, and it lasted only a moment before his hand relaxed and hung at his side again. But it was there. The hand remembered what the mind had locked away.

He turned and looked back at the house. Five bedrooms. Limestone. Ocean views from the upper floor. A garden Jeannie had landscaped three times. It sat above him on the rise of Marine Parade, lit by the early sun along its eastern wall, and from here it looked like what it was supposed to look like: the house of a man who had done well. Jeannie's bedroom curtain -- her retreat, as she called it, the second bedroom she had colonised three years ago -- was still drawn. The house was quiet. The house was always quiet in a way that had nothing to do with sound.

---

He crossed Marine Parade and came through the back gate and into the kitchen, and the kitchen received him the way it always received him: with marble and light and the faint mechanical hum of appliances that cost more than his first car. The island bench -- Calacatta marble, Jeannie's third choice after Carrara and Statuario, settled upon after a dispute with the stonemason that Bob remembered only as a series of phone calls he was not meant to hear -- gleamed under the east-facing window. The Miele coffee machine, the dual-zone wine fridge (one side for white, one for red, though the red side was mostly empty now because Jeannie drank only white and Bob drank beer and nobody in this house drank red wine but the fridge hummed on regardless, cooling nothing, maintaining the correct temperature for an absence). He rinsed his coffee cup and set it on the drying rack and stood at the sink and looked at the garden through the window and felt the house settle around him like a suit he could not take off.

From upstairs: Jeannie's voice. Not the words -- the walls and the distance reduced language to melody -- but the tone. The bright, performative register she used with her friends, pitched ten degrees warmer than the voice she used with him, rising and falling with the cadences of someone organising, arranging, making things happen. She was on the phone. He could hear the particular rhythm of a woman who was in her element: quick, decisive, the laugh arriving at the right moments, the pauses that invited the other person to feel included. He had lived with this woman for twenty-one years. He could map her emotional state by the pitch of her phone voice the way a sailor could read weather by the shape of clouds. This morning she was organising something. A lunch. A charity thing. An event. Something that required her particular genius, which was the management of people and occasions, the choreography of social life, and which she performed with an energy and competence she never directed at him.

He moved through the house. The living room received him next, and here too was Jeannie's hand -- not on him, never on him anymore, but on every surface, every object, every arrangement of light and fabric and space. The white walls. The Italian floor tiles, large format, a pale grey that Jeannie said was taupe and Bob saw as the colour of nothing. The furniture from a shop in Claremont that had closed two years ago, its pieces now irreplaceable, which Jeannie mentioned when guests admired the sofa the way a collector mentions provenance. The photographs on the console table: the family in Bali, 2019, all of them brown and smiling on a villa terrace; the family at Rottnest, 2022, the boat behind them, the water that impossible turquoise; the family at a charity gala, 2023, Jeannie in a gown that cost what some people paid in rent, Bob in a dinner jacket, the children flanking them like columns. In every photograph they were smiling. In every photograph Bob was standing slightly apart from the others, not dramatically -- a few inches, a half-step -- as if he had wandered into someone else's portrait and was waiting for the right moment to leave.

The children's rooms were upstairs. Chase's door was closed, which meant he was in there, which meant he was asleep, because Chase at twenty-one slept the way he did everything -- fully, without reservation, without the low-grade insomnia that kept his father standing on wet grass at seven in the morning. Chase slept until noon on Sundays. Chase slept like a man with nothing on his conscience, which he was, and the simplicity of his son's sleep was something Bob envied with a tenderness that frightened him when he allowed himself to feel it, which was almost never.

Shanel's door was open. The bed was made. She was already out -- running, a habit she had picked up from a university friend, one of the few things she did that Jeannie had not initiated or approved. She ran along the beachfront in the early morning, before the heat and the families, and she came back flushed and quiet and disappeared into the shower and emerged into her day as if she had completed some necessary private negotiation with herself. Bob noticed the made bed, the neatness. The books on the desk, stacked with a precision that reminded him of no one in his family. She is more disciplined than any of us, he thought, and the thought arrived with something that might have been pride and might have been sorrow, because discipline in this family was not rewarded -- it was merely survived.

He heard Jeannie's footsteps on the stairs. Not the phone voice now -- the voice had stopped, the call concluded, the warmth switched off like a lamp -- but the footsteps, which had their own vocabulary: brisk, precise, the heel striking each step with the certainty of a woman who knew where she was going and expected the house to arrange itself around her arrival.

She came into the kitchen. She was already dressed: white linen pants, pressed, a silk top in a colour Bob could not have named but which he understood was expensive, her hair pulled back in the style that made her look like a photograph of herself. She looked at him -- standing at the sink in yesterday's clothes, barefoot, his hair pushed sideways by the pillow and the wind, the linen shirt creased across the back where he had slept on it -- and her mouth moved in a way that was not quite a smile and not quite a wince but something between the two, a calibration, an assessment, a verdict delivered before the defendant had spoken.

"You look like you slept on the lawn," she said.

It was not loud. It was not angry. It was the tone of a woman commenting on the weather, or on a stain on a tablecloth, or on something mildly distasteful that would need to be dealt with before the day could properly begin. Bob absorbed it. He did not respond. He turned back to the kettle and began making more coffee, and the kitchen held the remark the way the house held everything -- silently, in its clean white walls and its expensive surfaces, absorbing the impact without registering a mark.

This was the dynamic. She cut; he absorbed. It had been this way for so long that neither of them registered it as abnormal. It was simply the weather of their marriage, the prevailing conditions, as constant and as unremarkable as the Fremantle Doctor that blew in every summer afternoon and rearranged the surface of things without touching anything beneath.

"The Hendersons are already across," Jeannie said, looking at her phone. "They left at six. We'll miss the good mooring."

They were taking the Riviera to Rottnest. This had been arranged -- by Jeannie, because all social logistics were arranged by Jeannie -- for a week. Chase was bringing friends. Jeannie had packed hampers the night before, wicker and linen, the contents organised with the precision of a military operation: cheeses from a deli in Subiaco, bread from the bakery on Napoleon Street, two bottles of Leeuwin Estate chardonnay nestled in ice packs, a fruit platter for the photographs. The boat was at RFBYC -- the Royal Freshwater Bay Yacht Club in Peppermint Grove, the most exclusive yacht club in Perth, where membership required a proposer and seconder of five or more years and the waiting list was a polite fiction that concealed an architecture of social vetting so thorough it would have impressed a security agency. Bob had joined because Jim was a member. Jeannie had joined because it mattered.

"There's always a mooring," Bob said.

Jeannie looked up from her phone. The look was brief and total. "There's always a mooring in Thomson Bay for people who don't care where they end up."

This was not about moorings. Bob understood this. He had understood this kind of exchange for twenty-one years. The mooring was a metaphor, and the metaphor was a weapon, and the weapon was aimed at the same target it was always aimed at: his insufficiency, his willingness to settle, the fundamental passivity that Jeannie had identified early in their marriage and had been punishing ever since. He poured his coffee. He said nothing. He went upstairs to shower, to dress, to put on the clothes Jeannie had not laid out for him but whose selection she would assess the moment he reappeared, and the morning advanced along the rails that Jeannie had laid for it, toward the yacht club, toward the boat, toward the sea.

---

The Riviera 48 was called *Chardonnay*. Jeannie's choice. Bob had found it funny once -- the naked admission of it, the announcement of a preference that was also a personality -- and now found it merely accurate. The boat sat in its berth at RFBYC, forty-eight feet of fibreglass and teak, twin Caterpillar diesels, three cabins, a flybridge, the swim platform at the stern where a tender hung in its davits. She was not new. She was not the biggest boat at the club -- the real mining money had sixty-footers, Sunseekers and Princesses with professional crew, boats that made the *Chardonnay* look modest, which was the point, Jeannie would have said, had anyone asked, which they did not, because at RFBYC the coded language of restraint was as important as the thing being restrained. The boat had cost four hundred and twenty thousand dollars second-hand in 2015 and ran forty thousand a year in maintenance, berthing, and insurance. Bob had not thought about whether he could afford it in years. This was because he could not, and not-thinking was what Bob did.

He was on the jetty, prepping the boat, when Chase arrived. The morning had sharpened. The Swan River was silver and flat, the jetty timbers warm already under the early sun, the yacht club behind them -- its low colonial buildings, the flagpole, the lawn where old men in blazers drank gin on summer Saturdays -- quiet at this hour. Bob was checking the engines, running through the pre-departure that he had performed a hundred times: fuel, oil, bilge pumps, safety gear. This was the one domain where he was competent in a way Jeannie could not diminish. He knew engines. He knew tides. He knew the crossing to Rottnest the way he knew the walk from Marine Parade to the grass: by feel, by repetition, by the body's accumulation of a knowledge that did not require thought. Out here, on the water, with his hands on the boat, Bob was a version of himself that almost worked.

Chase came down the jetty with two mates trailing him. He was physical, loud, taking up space the way he always took up space -- six-one, broad, dark-haired, Jeannie's jawline set in a face that was harder and more certain than Bob's had ever been. He wore board shorts and a Patagonia fleece and thongs that slapped the jetty planks with each step, and he greeted Bob with a half-hug, one arm, the briefest compression, the physical vocabulary of affection between Australian men, and then he was past him, stepping aboard, taking over the helm prep with the ease of a son who had been driving this boat since sixteen. He had his Recreational Skipper's Ticket. He had taken the *Chardonnay* to Rottnest more times than Bob had in the last two years. The handover from father to son at the wheel was a ritual they both enjoyed without examining, one of the few transactions in this family that did not carry a hidden cost.

The mates -- two boys, interchangeable in the way of twenty-one-year-old western suburbs boys, both in board shorts, both in Patagonia, both carrying slabs of Emu Export that they loaded into the fridge below with the practised efficiency of young men who had performed this operation many times -- settled into the boat's rhythms with the confidence of guests who knew they were welcome. They called Chase "Hazza" and Bob "Mr H" and they laughed too loud and took up too much room and Bob liked them for it, liked the noise of them, the uncomplicated volume of young men for whom the day was still a thing to be filled rather than endured.

Jeannie arrived last. She descended the jetty in white linen and a wide-brimmed hat and sunglasses that covered half her face, carrying a tote bag from a brand Bob did not recognise, and she boarded the *Chardonnay* the way she entered every room -- as if she were completing it. She went straight to the flybridge. She had a glass of something in her hand before they cleared the river mouth, and Bob, who was at the helm for the river section, navigating the channel markers past Freshwater Bay and the millionaires' row of Mosman Park, did not look at it and did not count. It was before noon. She was performing moderation. The glass was sauvignon blanc.

The crossing: Bob handed the helm to Chase at the river mouth, where the Swan opened out past the Fremantle traffic bridge and the breakwater and the fishing boats tied up at the mole, and the swell hit them. The Indian Ocean was not the Pacific, not the gentle, island-dotted sea of postcards. Here, where the continental shelf dropped away and the Southern Ocean swells rolled in uninterrupted from Antarctica, the water was serious. The *Chardonnay* lifted and slammed in a rhythm that was either exhilarating or nauseating, and Bob loved it. He had always loved the crossing. He stood on the flybridge and held the rail and watched Chase at the helm below, his son's hands on the wheel, the concentration on his face, the way he read the swell and adjusted the trim without thinking, and he felt something that had no agenda and no cost: love. Simple, physical, uncomplicated love for his son's competence, his son's ease, his son's hands on the wheel of a boat that cut through open water toward an island that appeared on the horizon like a promise.

Chase was good at this. Bob watched him and thought: this is the version of me that worked. The physical, confident, outward version. The one that does not need a sealed room, does not need silence, does not carry a weight in his chest that no one can see. The version that lives in the body and in the moment and does not know what he is missing, because you cannot miss what you never had, or what you had once and put away so long ago that the having feels like something you imagined.

The swell settled as they approached the island. Rottnest resolved from a grey smudge into its true self: low, scrubby, the bush-covered hills barely hills at all, the coastline scalloped into bays where the water changed from deep navy to the turquoise that visitors photographed and showed to people who had never been and who did not believe the colour was real. It was real. It was the reef shelf rising, the white sand below turning the water luminous, and as Chase brought the *Chardonnay* around into Thomson Bay, throttling back, the diesels dropping from a growl to a murmur, Bob felt what he always felt at this moment: the coast falling away behind, the constructed world receding, and the open water receiving him with an indifference that felt, to a man who was needed by everyone and known by no one, like mercy.

Jeannie was on the flybridge, taking photographs. The wind in her hair, the ocean behind her, the hat tilted at the angle that said effortless. Content for the feed. She held her phone in front of her and smiled the smile she had refined over ten thousand posts -- the one that was warm without being eager, beautiful without being vain, the smile of a woman for whom this moment was spontaneous and not the product of twenty minutes of positioning. She did not look at Bob. She looked at her screen. She looked at herself looking at the ocean, and the looking was the experience, and the experience was the content, and the content was the life.

---

Thomson Bay in the late afternoon held a stillness that made the rest of the world seem like a rumour. The boat was moored. Thirty-odd vessels sat on the water around them, rafted in twos and threes, their hulls ticking gently against fenders in the barely perceptible swell. The water was absurdly clear -- four metres down, the sandy bottom was visible, the shadow of the *Chardonnay* printed on it like a dark thought, and a school of herring drifted beneath the hull in a loose formation that tightened and relaxed and tightened again, going nowhere, as if they too were waiting for something.

Chase and his mates had taken the tender to shore. Bob could hear them, faintly, across the water -- or he imagined he could: the pub, the beer garden of the Rottnest Hotel, the noise of young men in the particular key of a Saturday afternoon, mid-strength beer and football talk and the performative volume of boys who were happy in the only way they knew how to be. The settlement was visible from the mooring: low white buildings, the general store, the bakery with its queue that started at ten and did not end until closing, the bike hire shed. The afternoon light was slanting in from the west, turning the white walls gold, turning the water gold, turning everything into the version of itself that people remembered when they said they loved Rottnest.

Jeannie was below. She was napping, or she was resting, which was the word she used for the state that followed three glasses of sauvignon blanc and two hours of sun and the particular exhaustion of performing leisure. The cabin door was closed. He could hear, faintly, the sounds of her occupancy: a tap running, the click of a glass set down on the bedside shelf, the small mechanical sounds of a woman maintaining herself in a small space. She would sleep for an hour. She would emerge showered and changed and ready for the evening as if the afternoon had not happened, as if the wine had not happened, as if the nap were a lifestyle choice and not a necessity, and the performance would resume.

Bob was on the flybridge. His feet were up on the rail. He had a beer in one hand but he was not drinking it. The tin was warm. He was looking at the water and he was, for once, not not-thinking. He was thinking. Or rather: something was thinking him, pushing up from beneath like water through sand, a restlessness he could not name, a feeling like the moment before you remember a word that is on the tip of your tongue. He had been feeling this more often lately -- in the car on the drive home, in the shower, in the moments between sleep and waking when the mind's defences were at their thinnest and things that had been sealed away leaked through the cracks. Something knocking at a door he had closed a long time ago. Something persistent and patient and unwilling to accept that the door was locked.

The sound came from across the water. He did not identify it immediately -- it arrived as part of the afternoon's ambient texture, mixed with the wind and the lapping of the hull and the distant noise from shore. A violin. Or what might have been a violin. A few bars of something, carried on the breeze, distorted by salt air and distance into something approximate, something that could have been a child practising on another boat, or a busker at the pub playing for tourist coins, or a radio left on, or -- nothing. The wind shifted. The sound was gone. It may never have been there.

Bob went still. The kind of still that has nothing to do with relaxation and everything to do with recognition -- the stillness of an animal that has heard a sound it knows, a sound from before language, before thought, from the body's oldest archive. His left hand, the one holding the beer, did not move. But the other hand -- his left hand, resting on his thigh -- curled. The fingers closed around a neck that was not there. The thumb braced. The fingertips found positions on invisible strings. The gesture was the same as the one on the grass that morning, but longer this time, more deliberate, as if the hand were reaching for something it had almost found and could not quite grasp. His breath changed. Something in his chest opened, or tried to open -- a door swollen shut from years of disuse, the hinges rusted, the wood warped, and behind it a room he had not entered since he was seventeen years old, a room where a boy stood with a violin under his chin and played a Handel sonata and the world, for the duration of a phrase, made sense.

The music stopped. Or the wind shifted. Or it was never there. The water lapped. The herring schooled. The sun moved. Bob sat on the flybridge of a boat named *Chardonnay* in Thomson Bay, Rottnest, and he held a beer he was not drinking and he looked at the water and he felt -- for the first time in years, or perhaps for the first time ever with this specificity, this precision, this unbearable clarity -- the grief of a man who has lost something he never quite had. Not a person. Not a place. Something closer to a dimension of himself that had existed briefly in a bedroom on Pearse Street, Cottesloe, behind a closed door, in the vibration of a string under his finger, and that had been sealed away so thoroughly and for so long that he had forgotten it was there until this moment, on this water, in this light, when a sound that might have been a violin reminded his body of what his mind had spent thirty years refusing to know.

The sun dropped. The sky turned copper. The copper turned rose and the rose turned violet and the violet turned to the deep, depthless blue of an ocean at dusk, and the lights of the other boats came on, one by one, warm and yellow against the darkening water. The stars appeared. They were extraordinary out here, away from the city's glow, away from the light pollution of a million houses and a million cars and the ceaseless illumination of a life that ran on electricity and performance. The Milky Way was visible. The Southern Cross hung above the horizon like something that had always been there and would always be there and did not care whether anyone looked at it or not.

He heard the tender before he saw it. The outboard motor's whine, then the thud against the swim platform, then Chase's voice -- loud, happy, the particular volume of a young man who has had enough beer to be generous -- and the scramble of bodies boarding, his mates laughing, someone dropping something, the easy chaos of return. Chase came up the stairs to the flybridge. He was slightly drunk. His face was open and flushed and his dark hair was pushed back from his forehead and he looked, in the fading light, so young that Bob felt something turn over in his chest.

"You right, Dad?"

Bob looked at his son. The stars were behind Chase's head, and the lights of the other boats were reflected in the water below, and the night was soft and enormous and held them both -- this man and this boy, this father and this son, on this boat, on this water, in this silence that was not silence but the sound of everything they could not say to each other.

"Yeah, mate. Good."

Chase nodded. He dropped into the chair beside Bob and put his feet on the rail and looked at the stars and said nothing, because nothing was the language they shared, the vocabulary of their love, and it was sufficient and it was not enough and it was all they had.

Whatever had opened in Bob did not close entirely. But he could not speak of it, and Chase would not have known how to hear it, and the night settled over Thomson Bay and the boats rocked gently on the water and the stars turned overhead and somewhere, on the wind or in memory or in the chamber of a brain that had been sealed shut for thirty years, a violin played a phrase and stopped, and played it again, and stopped, and the silence after the music was the loudest thing in the world.
