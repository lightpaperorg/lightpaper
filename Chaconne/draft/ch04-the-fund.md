# Chapter 4: The Fund

The key card beeped and the light went green and Bob pushed through the glass door into the lobby of the building on St George's Terrace with the same movement he had made every weekday morning for eleven years, a movement so habitual it had ceased to be an action and become a reflex, like breathing, like lying. The lobby was marble and steel, the kind of corporate entrance that exists to communicate seriousness -- the security desk, the turnstiles, the lift bank with its brushed aluminium doors reflecting the shapes of people who did not look at each other. He pressed the button for level twelve. The lift rose. A woman from one of the law firms on fourteen stood beside him holding a takeaway coffee and looking at her phone, and they rode in the particular silence of people who share a building and nothing else, and the doors opened and she continued upward and Bob stepped out into the corridor that led to the frosted glass door with HARRINGTON CAPITAL in matte grey lettering, and he felt what he always felt at this moment, which was nothing. A nothing so practised, so thorough, so carefully maintained that it had become its own kind of expertise.

The office was small. That was the first thing a visitor would notice, if visitors came, which they rarely did anymore -- Bob preferred to meet clients at restaurants, at the yacht club, in environments he controlled and that communicated the right things. The office itself communicated very little. A reception area with a desk that was unmanned three days out of five. A meeting room with a view of the Terrace below. Bob's office beyond that: a window looking east toward the park at the top of the city, two screens on the desk, a bookshelf containing financial texts he had read once and regulatory documents he consulted when he needed the precise language of compliance. The carpet was grey. The walls were pale. There was a print of something -- a nautical scene, boats in a harbour -- that Jeannie had chosen when the office was fitted out in 2012. It was the most personal object in the room.

He sat down. He turned on the screens. The left screen showed the ASX -- tickers scrolling, the small red and green movements of a market that had nothing to do with him and everything to do with the performance he maintained. The right screen showed his email. There were forty-two unread messages. He scanned the subject lines the way a man scans a crowd for the face he is afraid to see: quickly, peripherally, looking not at the messages themselves but for the particular words that signalled danger. *Redemption. Statement. Query. Meeting request.* He found three redemption requests. One was small -- fifty thousand, manageable, he could meet it from the operating account. One was two hundred thousand, which would require liquidating a real position, which would widen the gap between what the fund owned and what the fund owed. The third was from Richard, requesting five hundred thousand, politely, with a note about a property purchase, and Bob read it twice and then minimised the window and looked at the wall.

The wall held no answers. It never had. But Bob looked at it the way he looked at the ocean from the grass on Marine Parade -- as a surface onto which he could project nothing, a blankness that asked nothing of him, a space in which he could practise the only skill he had truly mastered, which was the postponement of the unbearable. The fund's assets under management had been, at their peak in 2011, approximately eighty-five million dollars. In the quarterly reports that went to investors -- the beautifully formatted documents that Sandra printed and posted in cream envelopes with the Harrington Capital logo -- the figure was seventy-four million, reflecting a managed and dignified decline that suggested prudent rebalancing in uncertain markets. In reality -- in the set of numbers that existed only in Bob's head, because to put them on paper would be to make them evidence -- the fund held approximately thirty million in actual assets backing seventy-four million in reported obligations. The gap was forty-four million dollars. The gap was the lie. The gap was the distance between the man Bob pretended to be and the man he was, expressed in a currency everyone understood.

He opened the email from Richard and began composing a reply. The words came easily. They always came easily. That was the terrible thing.

---

The mechanics were banal. That was the thing about fraud that no film or headline ever captured: the sheer, grinding ordinariness of it. It was not a heist. It was not a scheme hatched in a darkened room with diagrams on a whiteboard and a cast of co-conspirators. It was emails. It was spreadsheets. It was a man at a desk in a grey office on the twelfth floor of a building that nobody looked at twice, adjusting numbers the way an accountant adjusts numbers, with a cup of coffee at his elbow and the hum of the air conditioning overhead and the ASX scrolling on a screen he was not watching.

Bob opened the fund's administration portal. Sandra logged in on Tuesdays and Thursdays and processed the paperwork -- the trade confirmations, the bank reconciliations, the correspondence -- with the quiet competence of a woman in her mid-fifties who had worked in financial administration for thirty years and asked no questions. She asked no questions because the questions were not hers to ask. She processed what Bob gave her. She printed what Bob approved. She posted the quarterly reports in their cream envelopes and filed the copies in the filing cabinet and went home at four-thirty and thought about her grandchildren. Sandra was not complicit. Sandra was efficient, which in this context amounted to the same thing.

The fifty-thousand-dollar redemption he could handle. The money was in the operating account, which held the management fees -- the two per cent that the fund charged on reported assets under management, calculated on the seventy-four million the investors believed existed rather than the thirty million that actually did. Two per cent of seventy-four million was one point four eight million a year, which paid for the office lease, Sandra's wages, the car, the insurance, and the flow of money that sustained the Harrington life. It was the cleanest part of the fraud, the part that looked most like legitimate business, because it was legitimate business -- a management fee, charged at the industry standard rate, on a figure that happened to be fiction.

The two-hundred-thousand-dollar redemption was harder. To meet it, Bob would need to sell a real position -- one of the listed equities the fund actually held, a parcel of BHP or Woodside or one of the mid-cap miners that constituted the fund's genuine portfolio. The genuine portfolio was worth approximately thirty million and was managed with a competence that would have surprised anyone who knew the truth, because Bob was not a bad investor. He was a reasonable investor who had made a catastrophic mistake and then compounded it, daily, for a decade, until the compounding became the structure and the structure became the life and the life could not be unwound without destroying everything it contained.

Selling two hundred thousand in real positions would reduce the fund's actual assets to twenty-nine point eight million while leaving the reported obligations unchanged at seventy-four million. The gap would widen from forty-four million to forty-four point two million. The numbers were absurd. They had been absurd for years. But absurdity, when it accumulates slowly enough, stops registering as absurdity and becomes merely the landscape in which you live, the permanent weather of your days, no more remarkable than the view from your window or the sound of your own breathing.

The five hundred thousand from Richard was the problem. Richard Kemp -- late fifties, silver-haired, a retired mining executive who had made his real money in the real economy, iron ore and nickel and the honest extraction of metals from the ground, and who had entrusted a portion of it to Bob because they were members of the same yacht club and their wives sat on the same charity committee and Bob's face, at a barbecue at RFBYC in 2009, had told Richard everything Richard needed to hear: competence, warmth, reliability, the face of a man you could trust with your money because it was the face of a man who looked like he had money of his own and did not need yours. Bob could not meet the five hundred thousand. Not from the operating account, not from selling positions, not without triggering a cascade of liquidity problems that would bring the fund's actual state into contact with its reported state, which was the one thing that could never happen, because the space between those two states was not a problem to be solved but an environment to be maintained, a sealed room in which the lie lived and breathed and required only that no one open the door.

He composed the email to Richard. Dear Richard. Thank you for your note. I wanted to touch base regarding the timing of your request -- Bob paused. He reread the sentence. Touch base. The phrase was a weapon, deployed with the precision of a man who understood that language in finance was not communication but camouflage, a system of phrases that conveyed warmth while containing nothing: touch base, circle back, move the needle, position for growth, long-term horizon. Every phrase was a small coffin in which the truth was buried.

He deleted the draft. He would call Richard. He would suggest lunch. In person was always better, because in person Bob could deploy the full apparatus -- the face, the voice, the eye contact, the wine, the hand on the shoulder, the slow and patient explanation that what might appear to be a delay was actually a strategy, and that the strategy was working, and that the returns would justify the patience, and that the money was there, it had always been there, it would always be there. The lie was fluent because it was the only fluent thing about him. In every other area of his life -- with Jeannie, with his children, with his father, with himself -- Bob was halting, inarticulate, a man who could not say what he meant because he did not know what he meant. But here, in the language of the fund, in the performance of the competent manager, the words came like water. They came without effort. They came with a sincerity that was itself the cruelty, because the sincerity was real -- Bob genuinely wanted to reassure, genuinely wanted Richard to feel safe, genuinely wished the money were there -- and the realness of the wanting made the lie indistinguishable from the truth.

---

The restaurant was on the Terrace, two blocks west of the office, a business-lunch venue with leather banquettes and a wine list organized by region and a room that was dim enough to suggest discretion and bright enough to read a menu. Bob arrived first. He always arrived first for client meetings, because arriving first meant controlling the table, the wine, the tempo -- the whole performance set up before the audience arrived.

He ordered a bottle. Not the most expensive on the list -- the second most expensive, a Margaret River chardonnay, which signalled confidence without extravagance and which cost what it cost, which was ninety dollars, which was the fund's money, which was, in the most literal sense, Richard's money being used to buy wine for a lunch whose purpose was to prevent Richard from retrieving his money. Bob did not think about this. He had stopped thinking about the ironies years ago. The ironies were infinite and they were useless, and thinking about them was a luxury he could not afford.

Richard arrived. He came through the door with the unhurried gait of a man who had spent thirty years walking into meetings where the power was his, and even now, retired, the gait had not changed -- it was calibrated, authoritative, the walk of a person who expected rooms to adjust to his presence rather than the reverse. He was taller than Bob. Broader. His hair was silver and cut close, the kind of hair that announced executive competence in a language older than words. He wore a jacket without a tie, the Perth business-casual that meant he took the meeting seriously but not anxiously. He shook Bob's hand. The handshake was firm. The eye contact was direct. Richard Kemp was not a man who worried. He was a man who expected answers, and the expectation was, in this room, the only power that mattered.

"Bob." The name delivered like a fact. "Good to see you."

"Richard. How are you? How are the grandkids?"

The grandkids. Bob remembered their names -- Maisie and Tom -- because Bob remembered everyone's names, everyone's children's names, everyone's anniversaries and interests and concerns, the way a good bartender remembers orders and a good priest remembers sins. It was the raw material of his deception, this social memory, this warmth, this ability to make people feel that they existed in his mind as full human beings rather than as line items in a portfolio. Richard's face softened. He talked about Maisie's swimming carnival, about Tom's first day at kindy. Bob listened. He asked follow-up questions. He laughed at the right moments. He poured the wine.

Twenty minutes passed before the redemption came up. This was Bob's doing -- the twenty minutes of rapport, of personal connection, of the slow, patient construction of an atmosphere in which two friends were having lunch rather than a client confronting a manager. The atmosphere was the anaesthetic. By the time Richard said, casually, over the second glass, "Now, about the withdrawal --" the emotional ground had been prepared, the relationship reaffirmed, the context established: this was not an interrogation. This was a conversation between men who trusted each other.

"Of course," Bob said. He set down his glass. He leaned slightly forward -- a posture that said: you have my full attention, and my full attention is worth having. "Tell me about the property. Sounds like a good opportunity."

Richard described it. A block in Dalkeith -- one of the big blocks, eight hundred square metres, near the river. His daughter and her husband wanted to build. The money was not urgent, exactly, but the block would not wait -- there were other buyers, the market was moving -- and Richard needed the five hundred thousand within the month.

Bob nodded. He listened. He did the thing he was best at, which was holding the silence after someone has spoken, the silence that communicates not hesitation but consideration, the silence of a man who takes your concerns seriously enough to think before he responds.

"Here's where we are," Bob said. And then: the lie.

The fund's current portfolio, he explained, was weighted toward positions with a twelve-to-eighteen-month investment horizon. Resource equities, some private placements, a couple of unlisted ventures that were approaching liquidity events. Liquidating now would crystallise losses on positions that were structured for medium-term growth. The timing was unfortunate. If Richard could hold until Q2 next year -- April, May -- the fund would be in a significantly stronger position to process the withdrawal, and Richard's overall return would benefit from the patience.

He said this with calm. He said it with eye contact. He said it with the measured, unhurried cadence of a man who lived and breathed this, who woke up thinking about portfolios and went to bed thinking about positions, a man whose expertise was so thoroughly integrated into his being that the information emerged not as a prepared speech but as a natural expression of understanding. The projections were in his head -- not on paper, never on paper -- and he shared them the way you share an opinion, casually, with the authority of lived experience. The projections were invented. The positions they described did not exist in the amounts he implied, or existed only as a fraction of what they needed to be, and the liquidity events were not approaching but receding, and the fund would not be in a stronger position in Q2 because the fund would never be in a stronger position again, because the gap was structural and permanent and growing and the only thing that could close it was an inflow of new money that would itself become part of the lie.

But the delivery was flawless. Bob's voice was calm. His eye contact was steady. His body was open -- no crossed arms, no tightened jaw, no micro-expressions of anxiety that the human animal is wired to detect. He was not sweating. He was not nervous. He was doing the thing he was best at, the thing he was better at than anything else in his life, better than fathering, better than marriage, better than the market itself: making people feel that everything was fine.

Richard was quiet for a moment. He turned the wine glass by its stem. The restaurant hummed around them -- other lunches, other conversations, the soft percussion of cutlery on plates, the restrained laughter of people who were also performing something.

"I'm not worried, Bob," Richard said. "I just need to know it's there."

"It's there, Richard." Bob did not hesitate. The words arrived with the force of a physical law, a statement so simple and so certain that doubt could not adhere to it. "It's always been there."

Richard nodded. He lifted his glass. They talked about the cricket -- the second Test, the pitch at the WACA, the fast bowler from Queensland who was making the selectors look smart. They talked about the yacht club, about the commodore's dinner next month, about whether Chase was still bringing the boat across on weekends. They shook hands at the door. Richard walked away. His gait was the same as when he arrived -- measured, confident, the walk of a man who has been reassured by a man he trusts.

Bob walked back to the office. The Terrace at one in the afternoon: the suits flowing in both directions, the construction site on the corner with its crane rotating slowly against the blue, the coffee shop on the ground floor of his building with its queue of people looking at their phones. He took the lift to twelve. He sat at his desk. He looked at the wall.

He looked at it for a long time.

The nautical print. The boats in the harbour. The water that Jeannie had chosen because it was calming, or because it matched the carpet, or because it was the kind of art that communicated nothing and therefore offended no one, which was the operating principle of this office and of the man who sat in it. Bob looked at the boats and thought about Richard's grandchildren. Maisie, who swam. Tom, who had just started kindy. He thought about the five hundred thousand that did not exist and the property in Dalkeith that Richard's daughter would not buy, or not with Bob's money, because Bob's money was not money but a number on a screen that corresponded to nothing in the physical world.

He did not feel guilt. This was the thing he had learned about himself, the thing that frightened him more than the fraud itself: he did not feel guilt in the conventional sense, the sharp and localised pain of a man who has done a specific wrong. He felt something else. Something larger and more diffuse, something that had no edges and no centre, something that sat in his chest like a change in atmospheric pressure -- a heaviness, a tightness, a low-frequency vibration of dread that never fully resolved and never fully surfaced. He felt vertigo. The perpetual, low-grade dizziness of a man standing on a surface he knows is not solid, who has learned to walk on it by refusing to look down. He compartmentalised. He was the fund manager in this room. He was the husband at Marine Parade. He was the father on the boat. The three rooms did not connect. They could not connect, because the connecting corridor was the truth, and the truth would collapse all three rooms simultaneously, and the collapse would not be a reckoning but an annihilation, the destruction of every structure that held his life in place.

---

The freeway at five in the afternoon. The Mitchell, heading west, the city skyline receding in the mirror, the late light falling across Kings Park on the left -- the escarpment of bushland that rose above the road, the eucalyptus trees catching the sun along their trunks, the magpies circling. Perth traffic was not Sydney traffic. It was not a siege. But the bottleneck at the Narrows Bridge -- where the freeway funnelled from four lanes to two and every car in the western suburbs seemed to converge at the same moment -- was real, and Bob sat in it every afternoon in the sealed cabin of his Mercedes, alone with the engine ticking and the air conditioning humming and the fund.

The car was a Mercedes E-Class, not the newest model, not the AMG. Jeannie had wanted a Porsche. Bob had chosen the Mercedes because his father had driven Volvos and a Mercedes felt like an appropriate escalation: substantial, reliable, a car that said money without shouting it. The leather was cool. The cabin was quiet. The world outside -- the traffic, the heat, the radio towers on the escarpment, the other drivers in their other sealed cabins, each alone with their own version of the drive home -- was muted, separated by glass and engineering and the comfortable isolation that German automotive design sold as a feature.

The phone rang. Not the mobile in the cradle but the car's Bluetooth, the sound filling the cabin with the particular insistence of a call that could not be ignored. The screen on the dashboard showed: DAD.

Bob pressed the button on the steering wheel.

"Bobby." Jim's voice, thinned by age and by the speaker system but still recognisable, still carrying the cadences of a man who had once run his own book on the Terrace and who spoke, even now, in the language of markets and outcomes. "How's the afternoon treating you?"

"Good, Dad. Just heading home. How are you?"

"Oh, you know. Same same. Watched the market close. BHP had a good day."

"Yeah, I saw."

Jim called twice a week. The calls followed a pattern so fixed that Bob could have scripted both sides: weather first (Jim would mention the temperature and whether the garden needed water), then cricket (the current Test, or the shield match, or the reminiscence of a match from 1987 that Jim remembered with more precision than he remembered what he had eaten for lunch), then the market. Jim still read the Australian Financial Review. He read it at the kitchen table on Pearse Street, at the same seat where he had read it for forty years, with a cup of Nescafe and a red pen for marking stocks that interested him. He still had opinions. He still believed in the language he had taught Bob -- steady hands, the long view, don't panic, the market rewards patience -- and the opinions arrived in Bob's ear with the authority of a man who had once been right about things and who had not yet discovered that his rightness had been circumstantial, a product of timing and a rising tide rather than of wisdom.

"How's the book looking?" Jim asked. The book -- the old broker's term for a portfolio, for the accumulated positions and obligations that constituted a fund manager's professional life. Jim used it the way he used all the old terms: with affection, with ownership, with the unconscious assertion that the language he had given his son was still the operating language, still the code that unlocked the world.

"Book's good, Dad. We're positioned well for the next quarter."

"Markets are choppy, though. Must be keeping you busy."

"It does. Keeps you on your toes."

"That's the game, Bobby. That's the game."

The lie. Bob delivered it in the language Jim had taught him, in the vocabulary Jim had invented, using the cadences Jim had modelled every evening at the dinner table on Pearse Street when he came home from Patersons and Wendy put the chops on the table and Jim said the market was up or the market was down and the world was ordered by these simple facts, these clean binaries, and the son sat and listened and learned the music of confidence, of competence, the particular rhythm of a man who knew what he was doing and could explain it to anyone who asked.

"Your mother would have loved to see what you've done with the place," Jim said. He said this sometimes. He said it without sentimentality, in the matter-of-fact tone of a man reporting a truth he considered self-evident: Wendy would have been proud. Wendy, who had signed Bob up for violin because it would round him out. Wendy, who had kept the garden and played bridge and loved her son in the way she loved everything -- quietly, completely, without expecting anything in return. Wendy, who had died three years ago in the same house on Pearse Street, in the bedroom she had shared with Jim for fifty-two years, of a cancer that moved quickly and painlessly, as if it understood that Wendy was not a woman who wanted a fuss. Bob's grief for his mother had been the most authentic emotion he had allowed himself in years. It had arrived and stayed and he had not tried to manage it or perform it or convert it into something useful. He had simply missed her, and the missing was clean and real and it was the one thing in his interior life that was not contaminated by the lie.

"She would, Dad," Bob said. His hands tightened on the steering wheel. Not the violin grip -- not the curl of the left hand around an invisible neck, the ghost of music -- but a fist. Both hands. Gripping the leather-wrapped wheel with a pressure that turned his knuckles white and that he would not notice until later, when the impression of the stitching was visible on his palms. He was gripping the wheel because the alternative was to grip nothing, and gripping nothing meant releasing, and releasing meant feeling, and feeling meant the specific, localized, unbearable pain of a man who is lying to his father in his father's own language, using his father's own values as the instrument of deception, and whose father's pride -- the pride in Bobby, in the book, in the fund, in the life -- is the cruelest punishment because it is undeserved and cannot be corrected without destroying the man who holds it.

Jim talked about the old days. A client at Patersons who had panicked during the '87 crash and sold everything at the bottom. "Sold the lot, Bobby. Wouldn't listen. I said, 'Hold your nerve, the market will come back,' and he sold the lot. Lost two hundred thousand. Two hundred thousand in '87 dollars." The moral of the story was about steady hands. About not panicking. About the long view and the patience of a man who trusts the system. Jim was describing, without knowing it, the opposite of what Bob had done. Bob had not panicked and sold. He had panicked and lied, which was worse, infinitely worse, because selling at the bottom was a failure of nerve that cost only money, and lying was a failure of character that cost everything and was still costing, every day, in every conversation, in every email and every phone call and every carefully formatted quarterly report that went out in cream envelopes to people who trusted the face on the letterhead.

"You've got a good head, Bobby. Always have."

"Thanks, Dad."

The freeway cleared. The Narrows Bridge opened into Kwinana, and Kwinana became Stirling Highway, and the highway unwound through the suburbs -- Nedlands, where Mrs Albrecht had lived, where the studio with the frosted glass and the metronome and the poster of Menuhin had been; Claremont, where Jeannie bought her clothes and her wine and her life; and then Cottesloe, where Marine Parade climbed the ridge above the beach and the Norfolk pines stood like sentinels and the house waited. Bob turned off the highway. He wound up the hill. The ocean appeared on his right, enormous, pewter in the late light, the same ocean he looked at every morning from the grass. He turned into the drive.

He sat in the car after turning off the engine. The garage was quiet. The engine ticked as it cooled, a metallic countdown to nothing. Above him, through the ceiling of the garage and the floor of the house, he could sense the building: its weight, its presence, its five bedrooms and its limestone walls and its ocean views and its four-and-a-half-million-dollar assertion that the man who owned it was a man who had done well. Jeannie's silhouette passed an upstairs window -- she was moving, attending to something, performing the evening rituals of a woman in her house, and from down here, from the sealed cabin of the car in the garage, she looked like a figure in a window in a painting, a woman seen from a distance, composed and unreachable.

Bob closed his eyes. The garage was dark. The car was quiet. His hands rested on his thighs, and his left hand -- the hand that curled around invisible things, the hand that the steering wheel had punished and the violin had once blessed -- was still. It did not curl. It did not reach. It lay on his thigh like something defeated, like a hand that had tried to hold too many things and had dropped them all and was now merely a hand, attached to a man, sitting in a dark garage, in a house he could not afford, in a life he could not sustain, in a silence so total it was not the absence of sound but the presence of everything he could not say.

He opened his eyes. He got out of the car. He went inside. Jeannie's voice from upstairs, on the phone -- the warm voice, the bright voice, the voice for other people. The house received him. The white walls. The Italian tiles. The photographs of a family that smiled. The fund on the Terrace. The father on Pearse Street. The ocean on Marine Parade. The lie in every direction, and no door that opened into a room where he could be real.

Not yet. But the door was there, somewhere in the house, somewhere in the city, somewhere in the algorithm that was learning him better than he had learned himself, and it was swelling in its frame, and the hinges were loosening, and the wood was beginning to give.
