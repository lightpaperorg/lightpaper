# Chapter 12: Vienna

The Musikverein rose from the Ringstrasse like a golden reliquary, its façade lit by the evening sun in a way that made the stone seem to generate its own warmth. Bob stood on the pavement and looked at it. He had never seen a building that looked like music sounded -- that had the proportions and the colour and the contained intensity of something you were meant to hear rather than see. Jeannie had booked the tickets because the building photographed well. She had declined to attend because two hours of classical music was, in her formulation, "not my idea of a holiday." Chase had laughed. Shanel had hesitated -- he had seen it, the brief flicker of interest before she read her mother's face and sided with the majority. Bob had taken the single ticket and walked here alone, with the violin case in his left hand and the lightness of a man who has been released, for a few hours, from the obligation to be witnessed.

The violin case went everywhere with him now. He had transferred the instrument from the cardboard box into a cheap hard-shell case he had bought at a music shop in Innsbruck the morning after Absam -- black, anonymous, the kind of case a student would carry, nothing that would attract attention. Jeannie had watched him pack the broken violin into it with the expression she reserved for his more baffling behaviours, somewhere between indulgence and contempt. "You're carrying that around?" she had said. "It doesn't even have strings." He had said something about not wanting to leave it in the hotel room where housekeeping might disturb it. She had shrugged. A broken violin in a cheap case was beneath her threshold of interest, which was the threshold below which she stored most of what Bob did.

He carried it now up the steps and through the gilded foyer and into the Goldener Saal, and the hall received him.

He had read about it on his phone -- everyone read about it on their phones, the world having been reduced to a thing you looked at before you looked at the thing you had come to look at -- but the reading had not prepared him. The hall was smaller than he expected, smaller than the Perth Convention Centre, smaller than the ballroom of the Royal Freshwater Bay Yacht Club where he and Jeannie had attended a hundred charity dinners. But every surface was gold. Gold caryatids holding up the balcony, their faces serene, their bodies impossibly graceful in the way that European sculpture was graceful -- with the weight and the presence of actual bodies rather than the idealized thinness that passed for beauty in Perth. Gold rosettes in the ceiling. Gold mouldings framing painted panels of clouds and cherubs and a sky that was not blue but a kind of luminous cream, as though the painters had understood that the sky above music is not the sky above the world. The seats were red velvet, worn to a nap by a century of audiences. The stage was bare except for a single music stand.

No piano. No orchestra. No amplification. No screen, no projection, no microphone. Just a wooden stage in a golden room, and the air had a quality Bob could feel against his skin -- a density, a readiness, as though the room itself were an instrument waiting to be played. He had been in expensive rooms. Boardrooms panelled in Tasmanian oak. The fit-out at the yacht club that Jeannie had overseen for the renovation committee. But those rooms were designed to contain people. This room was designed to contain sound. Every surface -- the gold, the wood, the velvet, the plaster -- existed not for decoration but for acoustics, calibrated by architects and engineers and a hundred and fifty years of performances to do one thing: to take the sound that a single instrument produced and make it larger, richer, more present, more itself.

He found his seat. Third row from the back, left side -- the seat he had chosen online because it was far enough to be invisible and close enough to hear. Around him the audience settled: the rustle of programmes, the murmur of German and Japanese and English, the particular collective hush of a room full of people who have come to listen. He had never been in a room that hushed like this. At the yacht club, at dinner parties, at investor events, silence was a failure -- a gap that someone needed to fill, a moment of social dead air that signalled boredom or disagreement or the unforgivable absence of charm. Here, the silence was the point. The silence was what they had come for. The music would interrupt it, and then the silence would return, changed by what had passed through it, and the changed silence would be the thing they carried home.

He set the violin case on the floor between his feet. He rested his hands on his knees. He waited.

---

The violinist walked onstage. A woman, young -- late twenties, perhaps thirty -- in a black dress that left her arms bare. She carried her violin in her left hand and her bow in her right and she walked to the centre of the stage with the unhurried certainty of a person who has been going there her whole life. She did not smile. She did not acknowledge the audience. She stood, lifted the instrument to her shoulder, and waited.

The room went silent with a silence Bob had never experienced -- not the absence of sound but the presence of attention, six hundred people breathing together, opening together to receive whatever was about to come. He could feel it -- the collective intake, the held breath, the room contracting around the woman on the stage the way a lens contracts around a point of light. She raised the bow. She held it above the string for a moment that lasted longer than a moment, a held breath, a suspension -- and then she drew it across and the first note entered the room and Bob's body responded before his mind could name what it was hearing.

The sound filled the Goldener Saal the way light fills a church through stained glass -- not all at once but in layers, the fundamental tone and then the overtones blooming above it, each one finding a surface in the golden room and reflecting back, so that the sound was not coming from the stage but from everywhere, from the walls and the ceiling and the caryatids and the three centuries of music that had seeped into the plaster and the gilt and the velvet and the wood. One woman. One instrument. No safety net. The sound she produced was simultaneously the most vulnerable and the most powerful thing Bob had ever heard -- vulnerable because there was nowhere to hide, because every imperfection, every breath, every shift of weight would be audible in this room, and powerful because the vulnerability was the source of the power, the way an open wound is more affecting than a closed fist.

She played. He did not know what she was playing. He did not know the name of the piece or the composer or the period or the form. He knew nothing. He was the most ignorant person in the room, a man who had played Grade 4 violin at a school in Perth thirty years ago and had not listened to classical music with any seriousness since, and his ignorance was, for once, an advantage, because it meant that the music reached him without mediation, without the screen of knowledge that tells you what you are hearing before you hear it, without the programme notes that explain the architecture so you can admire the architecture instead of living inside it. He did not admire. He did not analyse. He sat in his seat with his hands on his knees and the violin case between his feet and the music entered him through his chest, through his hands, through the base of his skull, through the soles of his feet where they pressed against the floor, and it reached a place in him that nothing else had reached -- not the fund's terrors, not Jeannie's contempt, not twenty years of living inside a lie. The music walked through every door he had ever closed and stood in the room behind them and said, simply: *here*.

He wept. The tears came without permission, without sound, running down his cheeks and into the corners of his mouth, and he did not wipe them away because wiping them would have meant moving his hand and he could not move. He was held by the sound the way a river holds a stone -- not by force but by surrounding it, by occupying every surface, by being so completely present that resistance was not possible and not desired. He had not cried in years. Not at his father's funeral, where he had stood in the Perth sun in a dark suit and shaken hands and accepted condolences and felt nothing because the mechanism that converted feeling into expression had been disabled so long ago he could not remember it working. Not during the worst nights of the fund, when he lay awake at three in the morning calculating the gap between what the statements said and what the accounts held and the gap was a chasm and the chasm was growing and the only thing between him and the bottom was the next deposit from the next investor, the next lie. Not in the marriage, not in the moments when Jeannie's contempt cut so close to the bone that the bone should have bled. He had not cried because crying required access to the sealed room, and the room had been sealed, and the key had been lost, and he had accepted that loss as the cost of the life he was living.

He cried now because the sound had found the room. The sound had walked through every lock and every wall and every year of careful, practised numbness, and it had opened the door, and behind the door was not grief, exactly, or joy, exactly, but the thing that contains both, the thing that grief and joy are merely the upper and lower registers of: the full range of a life felt at its full intensity. He had not felt this since he was fourteen years old, standing on a stage at Scotch College with Mrs Albrecht's violin under his chin and the lights in his eyes and the sound of his own playing filling his ears and the world, for the duration of the piece, making sense.

The music changed. The piece -- whatever it was -- moved from darkness to something else, not lightness exactly but the memory of lightness, the way a person who has been ill remembers health, and the shift was so unexpected and so right that a sound escaped Bob's throat, a small sound, not a word, and the woman beside him glanced at him with the discreet concern of a stranger assessing whether the man next to her was in distress or merely moved, and decided on the latter, and returned her attention to the stage.

The violinist played on. She was very good. Even Bob, who had no framework for judgment, who could not distinguish between technical brilliance and emotional depth, who did not know the difference between a Stradivarius and a student instrument except that he had recently bought something that might be one and had no way to tell, even Bob could hear that this woman was doing something extraordinary. She was alone on a stage in one of the most famous concert halls in the world, with nothing but a wooden box and four strings and a horsehair bow, and she was producing a sound that made six hundred people stop breathing. The courage of it. The absolute, terrifying, magnificent courage of standing alone and making something beautiful and offering it to a room full of strangers who might or might not receive it, who might be bored, or distracted, or thinking about dinner, or checking their phones, and you stood there anyway, and you played, and you gave them everything, because that was what the music demanded, and the music did not care whether they were ready.

Bob sat in his seat and wept without wiping his face and listened. The violin case between his feet contained a broken instrument that could not make a sound. The woman on the stage held a functioning instrument that was making the most profound sound he had ever heard. Between the two -- between the silence and the voice, between the broken and the whole -- was the distance he would need to travel, and it was enormous, and it was impossible, and he did not care.

---

The concert ended. Applause -- the particular applause of a classical audience, which begins tentatively, as though the audience needs a moment to return to the world, and then builds, the polite appreciation thickening into something genuine, hands striking harder, a few bravos from the standing rows. Bob clapped. His hands moved mechanically, the palms meeting and parting, the sound lost in the general sound. His face was wet. He did not care about that either.

The audience rose, gathered bags and programmes, filed toward the exits. The murmur of conversation returning -- German, English, Japanese, the post-concert analysis that was itself a kind of applause, the audience reassembling their critical faculties after having them dismantled. Bob stood. He picked up the violin case. He joined the flow toward the door and passed through the gilded foyer and down the steps and out into the Vienna evening.

The Ringstrasse. The streetcar bells. The warm summer air. The sky above the city was the colour of bruised apricots, the sun having set behind the buildings to the west, and the first lights were appearing in the windows of the apartment blocks across the boulevard. Bob walked. He was in no hurry. The hotel was twenty minutes away, through the First District, past the Opera, through streets he did not know and did not need to know. The case in his left hand. The sound still in his body, the way a bell's vibration remains in the metal after the bell has stopped ringing, a resonance that was not audible but physical, a hum in his bones.

He passed a cellist on the Kärntnerstrasse. A young man, seated on a folding stool, playing Bach -- Bob knew it was Bach without knowing how he knew, the formal architecture of the phrase, the implied voices beneath the melody, the particular quality of music that is simultaneously mathematical and human. The cellist played with his eyes closed and his body swaying and the sound was deep and warm in the warm night air, a sound that belonged to the street and the city and the centuries of musicians who had played on these streets, in these buildings, in this air. Bob stopped. He listened. He dropped a note into the open case. The cellist nodded without opening his eyes, without stopping, and Bob walked on, and the sound of the cello followed him for half a block before the city's own sound absorbed it.

He arrived at the hotel. The lobby was quiet. The concierge nodded. Bob took the lift to the fourth floor and walked the carpeted corridor to the suite and opened the door and the room was dark. Jeannie was in bed, her back to the door, the duvet pulled up to her shoulders. Asleep or performing sleep -- it did not matter; the effect was the same. She had opted out. She had opted out of the concert, opted out of the evening, opted out of whatever her husband had experienced in the Goldener Saal, and the opt-out was so habitual, so deeply built into the structure of their marriage, that it no longer registered as a choice. It was simply the way they lived. He went to places alone. She was not there when he returned. The space between them was not a gap but a country, and neither of them had a passport.

The children's rooms were dark. Chase and Shanel, behind their own doors, in their own silences. The family, scattered through a hotel suite in Vienna, each one sealed in their separate compartment, the evening spent in four different ways that would never be compared or discussed or shared.

Bob sat on the edge of the bed. He did not turn on the light. He set the violin case on the floor and rested his hands on his knees and sat in the dark with the sound of the concert still in his body and his wife's back turned to him and the broken violin at his feet, and the silence of the room was not the silence of the Musikverein -- not the charged, anticipatory silence of a room waiting for music -- but the other kind, the kind that comes after, the kind that knows what has passed through it and is changed by the knowing.

He sat for a long time. Then he lay down, on his side of the bed, on top of the covers, still dressed, and closed his eyes. The sound was still there. The violinist on the stage, the bow on the string, the first note entering the golden room. The tears he had cried were dry on his face and he could feel their tracks, the slight tightness of the salt, and he did not wash them off. They were evidence. Evidence that something had happened, that something had reached him, that the sealed room was open and the thing inside it was alive.

---

The next morning Jeannie posted a photograph of the Sacher Hotel's breakfast -- the silver coffee pot, the white tablecloth, the Sachertorte she had ordered because the hotel was famous for it, the caption "When in Vienna" with a musical note emoji. She had 4,200 followers and the post would receive 187 likes, which was above her average, which would sustain her through the morning and into the afternoon, which was all she needed.

Bob ate breakfast and said nothing about the concert. Jeannie did not ask. The children ate and scrolled and the family occupied the same table without occupying the same morning, each of them in a private relationship with their phone or their plate or the view from the window that showed the Ringstrasse and the opera house and the city that had been making music for three hundred years and that had given Bob, the night before, something he could not name and would not attempt to name and would carry home across twelve thousand kilometres in the cargo hold of a Qantas 787, wrapped in cotton cloth, in a cheap black case, silent and broken and waiting.

They did Schönbrunn that afternoon. Jeannie photographed the gardens. Shanel posed in front of the palace with the practised half-smile she had learned from her mother's Instagram tutorials. Chase had his hands in his pockets and a face like a closed door. Bob trailed behind them, fifteen metres, twenty, the violin case in his left hand, and watched his family from a distance and saw them as a stranger might: a handsome woman and a pretty girl posing in front of a Habsburg palace, a tall young man who did not want to be there, and a middle-aged man with a black case who did not seem to belong to any of them.

He belonged to the case. He understood this now. The case and its contents -- the broken violin, the reddish-amber varnish, the scroll with its impossible precision, the *Seele* that Franz said was waiting to be heard -- this was what he belonged to. Not the family. Not the fund. Not the life that had brought him to this city and this palace and this particular Tuesday afternoon in July. He belonged to the thing in the case, or the thing in the case belonged to him, or -- and this was closer to the truth -- they belonged to each other, the broken instrument and the broken man, and the belonging had begun in a workshop in Absam and had deepened in the Goldener Saal and would deepen further, in ways he could not yet imagine, in a study in Perth and a soundproofed office and a beach at dawn.

He sat on a bench in the palace gardens while Jeannie took Shanel to the gift shop. The case on the bench beside him. He took out his phone. He searched "violin restoration Perth." He found three names. He bookmarked them. He searched "violin teacher Perth adults." More names. He bookmarked those too. Franz's words: *Find someone who listens.* He would find someone. He would have the violin restored. He would learn to play it. He would learn to play the piece he had heard at the Musikverein -- or not that piece, but whatever piece the violin wanted him to play, whatever piece was waiting in the broken body of the instrument the way the music had been waiting in the golden room, patient and present and ready.

At the airport the next day, he checked the violin case through as oversized luggage. He watched it go on the belt -- the cheap black case with its precious, invisible cargo disappearing into the airport's digestive system, carried away on rubber rollers into the fluorescent depths where luggage goes -- and he felt an anxiety he had never felt for a suitcase, a clenching in his chest that was not the fund's dread but something older and more tender, the feeling of watching something you love being carried away by forces you cannot control.

On the plane, window seat, eyes closed: the engines roaring through the fuselage, the cabin dimming, Jeannie already asleep with her eye mask on and her earplugs in, Chase watching something on his phone, Shanel reading. Below them, in the cargo hold, in the dark, the violin. Wrapped in Franz's cloth. Silent. Broken. Possibly extraordinary. Going home to a city it had never been, to a continent the maker had never imagined, to a man who did not yet know what he had bought or what it would cost him or what it would give him.

Bob kept his eyes closed. The plane climbed. Vienna fell away below them, the Danube a dark ribbon, the city a grid of lights, the Musikverein somewhere in that grid, the golden room dark now, empty, waiting for the next sound that would fill it. He thought of the violinist -- her bare arms, her steady bow, the first note that had entered the room and entered him and opened the door. He thought of Franz -- the checked shirt, the pale blue eyes, the word *Seele* spoken as fact. He thought of the instrument in the hold beneath his feet, the reddish-amber varnish, the silent f-holes, the fallen soundpost lying in the dark interior like a heart that has stopped and is waiting to be started again.

His left hand curled on the armrest. He let it.

In twelve hours he would be in Perth. The fund would be waiting. The marriage would be waiting. The life would be waiting -- the limestone house on Marine Parade, the ocean, the office on the Terrace, the clients and the lies and the spreadsheets and the slow, grinding machinery of a fraud that was running out of time. All of it would be there, exactly as he had left it, unchanged, unaware that the man returning to it was not the man who had left.

He slept. If he dreamed, the dreams were of sound.
