# Chapter 7: What Jeannie Doesn't Know

The room was hers. That was the point of it, and Jeannie was conscious of this every time she closed the door -- not locked it, never locked, locking would be a declaration and declarations were not how Jeannie operated; she closed it, which was a suggestion, a boundary stated in the passive voice. She had taken the second bedroom three years ago, moving her things across the hall in stages -- first the reading lamp, then the cashmere throw, then the pillows (her pillows, the European goose-down ones from The White Company that Bob's neck could not tolerate and that she refused to replace with anything from a department store), then the phone charger, and finally, one Tuesday evening without announcement or discussion, herself. Bob had said nothing. This was his principal talent: saying nothing when something was happening, absorbing the event like water absorbing a stone, registering no disturbance on the surface. She had expected a question. She had prepared an answer -- something about sleeping better alone, something about his snoring, something plausible and face-saving that they could both pretend was the reason. He did not ask. She did not explain. The marriage rearranged itself around the new configuration the way a family rearranges itself around a death: silently, practically, with everyone pretending the empty chair has always been empty.

The room, then. It was ten o'clock on a Thursday night. Jeannie was in bed -- her bed, queen-size, white linen, the Diptyque candle on the nightstand still burning because she liked the smell and because the small orange flame gave the room a quality she thought of as European, though she had never articulated this even to herself. On the nightstand beside the candle: her phone, a glass of sauvignon blanc that was not her first, the prescription bottle of sertraline from the GP in Subiaco. She had taken the pill an hour ago. The pill and the wine had met in her bloodstream and were performing their nightly collaboration, a chemical detente that lowered the volume on everything -- the anxiety, the restlessness, the low hum of something she might have called despair if she were the kind of woman who used that word, which she was not. She was the kind of woman who called it being tired.

She was on Instagram. The screen glowed against her face in the dim room, and she scrolled with the practised, frictionless movement of a thumb that had performed this gesture ten thousand times, past the photographs of other women's lives -- the interiors, the holidays, the children in school uniforms, the flat lays of coffee cups and novels and cashmere socks arranged on white marble with the artful carelessness of a production that had taken twenty minutes. She was looking for something. She did not know what she was looking for. She was always looking for something on this screen and she never found it and she never stopped looking, and this was the rhythm of her evenings: the wine, the pill, the screen, the scroll, the thin blue light on her face in the dark, and the feeling -- beneath the medication, beneath the alcohol, beneath the performance of a woman at rest in a beautiful room -- of a tide going out and leaving behind it not sand but something harder and more desolate, the exposed and ugly architecture of a life that looked, from the outside, like everything anyone could want.

Her follower count was 4,187. It had been 4,200 on Monday. She had noticed the drop the way you notice a hairline crack in a wall -- not alarming in itself, but suggestive, evidence of some structural shift you cannot see and do not want to investigate. Thirteen people had decided, over the course of a week, that Jeannie Harrington's curated life was no longer worth watching. She did not know who they were. She wanted to know who they were.

---

The app offered her a memory. Instagram's "on this day" function, surfacing a photograph from 2016 with the algorithmic cheerfulness of a technology that does not understand cruelty. The photograph: Jeannie behind the counter at JEANNIE, her boutique on Napoleon Street, Cottesloe. She was wearing a silk blouse -- ivory, with a soft collar, from a European label she had stocked because the cut was beautiful and the price point was aspirational and the name was unpronounceable, which meant it signalled exactly the kind of knowledge Jeannie wanted her customers to possess. She was holding a champagne flute. Behind her, the shop: the racks of carefully curated pieces, the pale timber shelves, the vase of white peonies she had replenished every Monday from the florist on Napoleon Street at a cost of sixty-five dollars a week, fifty-two weeks a year, three thousand three hundred and eighty dollars a year in flowers for a shop that had not turned a profit in any of its eight years of operation.

She swiped past it. She swiped the way you swipe at a wasp -- quickly, reflexively, not because you are afraid but because the contact is intolerable. The shop. The shop that had been hers. The only thing in her life that bore her name and belonged to her and was not an extension of Bob's money or Bob's career or the identity that being Mrs Robert Harrington conferred. JEANNIE: the letters in a serif font on the shopfront, designed by a graphic designer in Fremantle who had charged her $3,200 for a logo she loved with a ferocity she did not love anything else. The shop that had opened in 2009, in the middle of a global financial crisis, because Jeannie did not read economic indicators, she read rooms, and the room she read was one in which women like her needed beautiful things and would pay for them if the experience was right.

The experience was right. The shop was beautiful. The champagne evenings were beautiful. The labels were perfect and the fitout was stunning and the part-time girls were lovely and the customers -- the friends, the gaggle, the women from school and yacht club and charity board -- came and browsed and tried things on and drank the champagne and sometimes bought a dress or a scarf or a pair of earrings, and then went to Claremont to do their actual shopping, because Claremont had the range and the convenience and the parking, and Napoleon Street in Cottesloe, for all its charm, was not where women who could afford these prices actually bought their clothes.

The shop lost money every year. In its best year -- 2014, a year when the mining money was still sloshing through the western suburbs and women were buying without thought -- it lost only $40,000, which Jeannie described to Bob as "basically breaking even" and to her friends as "doing really well," both of which were lies, though lies of different magnitudes, and Jeannie did not think of them as lies because thinking of them as lies would have required a self-awareness she had spent her adult life avoiding. The shop closed in 2017. Jeannie attributed this to the retail downturn, to online shopping, to the changing nature of brick-and-mortar commerce, to everything except the truth, which was that she had opened a luxury boutique in a suburb that did not need one, stocked it with product her friends admired but did not buy, and sustained it for eight years with Bob's money because the alternative -- admitting it had failed -- was a kind of death she was not prepared to die.

She had not spoken about the shop in two years. She had not walked past the shopfront on Napoleon Street -- it was a wellness studio now, something to do with infrared saunas and adaptogens and the particular vocabulary of health that had replaced the vocabulary of fashion among the women she knew -- in at least six months. The shop existed only here, in the algorithm's memory, surfacing unbidden, a ghost in the machine.

She finished her wine. She poured another from the bottle she had brought upstairs -- not a full pour, a pour that looked moderate, a pour she could describe as "just a little more" if anyone were watching, though no one was watching, no one was ever watching in this room, and the pour was the pour of a woman who has stopped performing for an audience and is now performing only for herself, which is the loneliest kind of performance there is.

---

She heard Bob downstairs. Not specific sounds -- not footsteps, not a door, not the television -- but the general atmospheric shift of a house that contains another person, the subtle change in pressure that distinguishes a house with one person in it from a house with two. He had been home for an hour. He had come in, said hello -- "Hi" was the word, not "hello," the monosyllable of a man who has reduced marital communication to its smallest possible unit -- and gone to the living room, and she had gone upstairs, and the house had divided itself between them the way it divided itself every evening, the downstairs belonging to Bob and the upstairs belonging to Jeannie and the hallway and the staircase belonging to no one, a demilitarised zone connecting two countries that shared a border and nothing else.

She checked the time. Ten-forty. Late enough that going downstairs would require a reason -- a glass of water, a question about the morning, something logistical. She did not want a reason. She wanted -- and the wanting surprised her, because she had spent years not wanting anything from Bob except his money and his compliance -- to know what he was doing. There was a quality to his silence tonight that was different from his usual silence. His usual silence was the silence of absence, the silence of a man who was not there even when he was there. Tonight his silence had a texture. It was the silence of a man who was present, who was doing something, who was engaged with something she could not see and did not understand.

She got up. She put on her robe -- silk, pale grey, the kind of robe that exists to be beautiful rather than warm, though it was warm enough for a house heated to twenty-two degrees by a ducted system that cost them $800 a quarter. She went downstairs.

Bob was in the living room. The television was on, muted, flickering -- a financial channel, the usual wash of numbers and talking heads that Bob kept on like a nightlight, the ambient glow of a world he was supposed to belong to. He was on the couch. He was on his phone. And his face --

Jeannie stopped at the bottom of the stairs. She did not enter the room. She stood in the doorway, one hand on the frame, the robe falling open slightly at the throat, and she looked at her husband, and what she saw was a face she did not recognise.

Not unfamiliar in its features -- she knew the features, knew them better than she knew her own, had watched them age and soften over twenty-one years, had catalogued their decline with the dispassionate eye of a woman who assessed faces the way a jeweller assessed stones. She knew the forehead, the jaw, the mouth. But the expression on the face was not one she had seen before. It was -- she searched for the word and could not find it, because her vocabulary for Bob's interior life was limited to the terms she had assigned him: tired, moody, checked-out, boring. This was none of those. This was a man looking at something with a concentration so total that it altered his features, tightened them, made them younger, made them strange. His lips were slightly parted. His eyes were fixed on the screen with an intensity that Jeannie had never seen directed at anything -- not at her, not at the children, not at the fund, not at the ocean he stared at every morning. He was looking at his phone the way people in paintings look at miracles.

She did not see what was on the screen. She did not need to. Whatever it was, it was something Bob cared about, and the caring was alarming, because Bob was not supposed to care about things. Bob was supposed to be the flat, accommodating surface against which she arranged her life. Bob caring about something was a disruption to the architecture, a structural anomaly, a crack in the wall she did not want to investigate.

She made a sound -- not deliberate, just the shift of her weight on the floorboard, the creak of the house's old bones -- and Bob's face changed. It folded. The expression disappeared so completely and so quickly that it was as if she had imagined it. He looked up.

"Hey."

"What are you watching?"

"Nothing. News." He turned the phone face-down on the couch. The gesture was casual. It was also the gesture of a person hiding something, and Jeannie, whose entire social intelligence was built on the detection of concealment, registered it without comment.

She went to the kitchen. She poured a glass of water she did not want. She stood at the island with her back to the living room and felt, in the pit of her stomach, a sensation she could not name but that a more self-aware woman might have identified as the first stirring of fear: the fear that comes not from knowing a thing but from suspecting one, from the dawning recognition that the ground you are standing on is not the ground you thought it was.

---

Later -- much later, three in the morning, the hour when the wine has metabolised and the sertraline's grey hum has thinned and the anxiety that both substances were hired to suppress comes roaring back like a creditor who knows you're home -- Jeannie could not sleep.

This was not unusual. The 3am insomnia was her most reliable companion, more faithful than Bob, more consistent than the friends who surrounded her at lunch and disappeared at midnight. It arrived every night, or nearly every night, a cold and lucid wakefulness that turned her mind into a searchlight, sweeping the dark for things to worry about. The house. The children. The money -- always the money, though she did not worry about it in the way that people who understand money worry about it; she worried about it the way you worry about weather, as a force that determined the conditions of life and that she could influence only through the man who controlled it.

She got up. She went to find a blanket -- the cashmere one, the good one, which she thought was in the hallway cupboard, the deep cupboard above the linen press where they kept things that did not have a place: spare doonas, Christmas decorations, old sports equipment Chase had outgrown, the accumulated sediment of twenty years in one house.

She opened the cupboard. She reached for the blanket and her hand touched something else. A case. A hard case, rectangular, covered in black canvas that had gone dusty and slightly sticky with age. She pulled it down. It was lighter than she expected and heavier than it should have been for its size, and the weight was distributed unevenly, the centre of gravity shifting as she tilted it, something inside moving slightly, something loose.

She knew what it was. She had seen it once before -- years ago, when they moved to Marine Parade, when the removalists had carried everything in and Bob had carried this one thing himself, walking it up the stairs and placing it on the top shelf of this cupboard with a care that she had noticed and not thought about, because Jeannie did not think about things that did not concern her, and this case, whatever it contained, did not concern her.

She opened it. The clasps were stiff -- old, cheap brass clasps that resisted and then gave way with a click that sounded, in the 3am silence of the house, like a small bone breaking. Inside, on crushed velvet the colour of old blood, lay a violin.

It was not a beautiful instrument. Even Jeannie, who knew nothing about violins, could see that. It was small and plain and the wood had a dull, unpolished look, the colour of old furniture in a charity shop. The strings were loose, hanging off the bridge at angles that suggested years of neglect. The bow lay beside it, the horsehair yellowed and slack, a few strands broken and curling. The case smelled of rosin -- she did not know the word, but she knew the smell, a sweet, resinous, faintly medicinal smell that was oddly specific, oddly present, as though the instrument had been sealed in its case and had been quietly decomposing in its own atmosphere for decades.

She looked at it. She tried to feel something appropriate -- nostalgia, tenderness, curiosity -- and felt instead the thing she always felt when confronted with evidence of Bob's interiority: irritation. The violin was pathetic. It was a cheap, neglected instrument in a battered case, hidden in a cupboard for twenty years, and its very hiddenness was an accusation, because hidden things are things you care about, and Bob was not supposed to care about things that Jeannie did not know about. His caring was her territory. His inner life was her jurisdiction. And here was evidence that he had a province she had not known existed, a sealed room she had not been invited into, and the discovery did not make her curious. It made her angry, in the low, controlled way that Jeannie was always angry -- not a blaze but a chill, a withdrawal, a recalibration of the terms.

She closed the case. She put it back. She did not find the blanket. She went back to bed and lay in the dark and the anger settled into its usual position, behind her sternum, alongside the anxiety and the exhaustion and the unnamed thing she medicated every morning and every night, and she waited for sleep, and sleep did not come, and the house was silent around her.

---

In the morning, she said it.

She said it over coffee, in the kitchen, the way she said all her cruelest things: casually, in passing, without emphasis, as though the remark were an observation about the weather rather than a precision-guided weapon aimed at the softest part of a man she had been studying for twenty-one years. Bob was at the island, standing, drinking his coffee, wearing yesterday's shirt -- he had slept in it again, which was a habit she found revolting and which she had stopped commenting on because the commenting had no effect and the lack of effect was itself a humiliation.

"I found your fiddle," she said.

Bob's cup stopped. Not dramatically -- he did not drop it, did not spill it, did not freeze in any way that a casual observer would notice. But Jeannie was not a casual observer. Jeannie was the most attentive observer of Bob's micro-expressions on the planet, because twenty-one years of marriage to a man who did not speak his feelings had made her an expert in the language of the involuntary -- the tightening of the jaw, the fractional widening of the eyes, the stillness that descends on a body when it hears something it was not prepared for. She saw all of it. She catalogued it. She filed it away.

"In the cupboard upstairs," she continued, sipping her coffee. "I didn't know you played the violin. How very Year Seven."

"It's nothing." His voice was steady. "Just kids' stuff."

"Shall I put it in the charity bin? It's not exactly concert quality."

"Leave it."

The words came out differently from the ones before. *It's nothing* had been flat, deflective, the Bob she knew -- the man who absorbed and accommodated and gave nothing back. *Leave it* was something else. There was a tension in it, a wire drawn tight, a vibration that Jeannie heard the way she heard everything -- instinctively, bodily, without analysis. *Leave it* was the voice of a man protecting something by pretending it did not matter.

She looked at him. He was not looking at her. He was looking at his coffee, at the surface of the liquid in the cup, at nothing. And his face -- there it was again, the thing she had seen last night in the living room, the expression she could not name, the flicker of something alive behind the blandness, something that contracted around his eyes the way his face had contracted once before, years ago, when Chase had fallen off the boat as a toddler and Bob had gone in after him without hesitation, without thought, the automatic response of a body protecting something it loved.

*Fear*, she thought. Not fear of her. Fear for something. Fear for the violin -- the cheap, pathetic, charity-bin violin in the cupboard upstairs, the instrument a boy had played and a man had hidden and a wife had just found, the thing that should not have mattered and clearly, transparently, devastatingly did.

She let it go. She let it go the way she let everything go in this marriage -- not with grace but with strategy, filing it away in the archive of observations she maintained about her husband, the file of leverage and intelligence and the small, sharp instruments of domestic power. She did not understand what the violin meant. She did not need to understand. She needed only to know that it existed, and that it mattered, and that Bob's face when she mentioned it was the face of a man with a secret.

"Fine," she said. "Leave it."

She poured more coffee. Bob left the kitchen. She heard his footsteps in the hallway, the front door, the car. The house was quiet again. She stood at the island and drank her coffee and thought about the violin and about the expression on Bob's face and about the thirteen followers she had lost this week and about the boutique that had closed and about the woman she was at thirty-eight and the woman she was at forty-five and about the distance between the two, which was not measured in years but in something harder, something she had no word for, something that the wine and the pills and the candle and the white linen and the ocean views and the four-and-a-half-million-dollar house could not, for all their beauty, disguise.
