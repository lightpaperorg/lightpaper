# Chapter 8: The Trip

Perth Airport's international terminal had a particular quality of light that Bob had never been able to name -- not quite fluorescent and not quite natural, a compromised brightness that flattened everything, so that the families in the departure lounge all looked like versions of the same family: sunburned, slightly overdressed, clutching passports and boarding passes with the anxious reverence of people who do not travel often enough for the ritual to become routine. The Harringtons were not that family. Jeannie had organised them into a tableau of practised ease: matching Rimowa luggage arranged in a formation that was not accidental, she and Shanel in linen that would crease before Singapore but looked immaculate now, Chase in the one good shirt Jeannie had laid out on his bed that morning with a note that said *Wear this.* They looked like a family in a magazine. They looked like what they were supposed to be.

Bob sat slightly apart. Not dramatically apart -- not across the lounge, not making a statement -- but one seat removed from the cluster of his family, holding his boarding pass in his left hand with a looseness that suggested calm and concealed something else. He was watching the planes through the floor-to-ceiling windows. The Indian Ocean was visible beyond the runways, the same ocean he looked at every morning from the grass on Marine Parade, the same pewter expanse that had been the backdrop of his entire life, and in a few hours he would be above it, crossing it, leaving it behind. Jeannie was photographing the luggage arrangement. Chase was on his phone, body angled away from his mother in the universal posture of a twenty-year-old who has not yet forgiven his parents for existing in public. Shanel was reading a novel -- Bob could not see the cover from this angle, but she was absorbed in it, her body curled into the plastic airport chair with a self-containment that reminded him, with a tenderness so sudden it startled him, of his own mother reading in the garden on Pearse Street.

He felt something. It was not a large feeling and it did not arrive with drama. It arrived the way a change in air pressure arrives: subtly, without announcement, altering the quality of everything without changing its appearance. He felt light. Or lighter. The fund was nine thousand miles behind him, or would be by tonight. Sandra had the office. The quarterly was filed. The machine would run without him for three weeks, the way machines run when no one is watching -- which is to say, it would run exactly the same, because the machine was automatic now, self-sustaining, requiring only his face and his voice to maintain the fiction, and his face and his voice would be in Europe, where no one knew him, where no one needed anything from him, where he could be -- and here the feeling sharpened, became almost painful in its specificity -- no one.

In his search history, undeleted, was the word *Absam*.

He did not know what he would find there. He knew only that he would go.

---

Two years earlier, or almost two. The planning had begun in winter, in the Marine Parade kitchen, on an evening when the rain was doing the thing Perth rain did in July -- arriving suddenly, in sheets, hammering the windows with a violence that lasted twenty minutes and then stopping as though it had made its point. Jeannie had been at the island with her laptop, the spreadsheet open, the Google doc colour-coded, the hotels bookmarked in four separate browser tabs, each tab a city, each city a performance venue for the version of the Harrington family she intended to project across an entire continent.

"Three weeks," she had said, not looking up. "London, Paris, then we go south or we go east. I'm thinking east. Austria. Everyone says Vienna."

Bob had been at the kitchen table, eating leftover pasta, drinking a beer he did not particularly want. The children were elsewhere -- Chase in his room, Shanel at a friend's. The house was doing the thing it always did in winter: closing in, the walls seeming to press inward, the ocean invisible behind the rain, the rooms smaller and darker and more honest than they were in summer, when the light poured through the windows and made everything look like a property listing.

"Sounds great," he had said.

"The Hotel Sacher. Have you seen it? Margot went last year. The interiors are incredible." She turned her laptop toward him. Photographs: a grand European hotel, red velvet, gold fixtures, the particular opulence of old-world money that Jeannie found intoxicating because it was the antithesis of Perth -- layered, historical, heavy with meaning, everything that a mining city built on sand and ambition was not. "And the Opera. We could do the Opera. Shanel would love it."

"Shanel would love it," Bob agreed, because agreeing was what he did, because agreeing was the currency of their marriage -- his compliance purchased her tolerance, and her tolerance was the closest thing to peace their household achieved.

But when she had said *Austria*, something moved. Not in the room -- in his body. A sensation in his chest, below the sternum, a contraction or an opening, he could not tell which, and the word -- Austria -- sat in the air between them like a struck note, reverberating, and he did not know why.

He knew now. Or he knew the shape of the why, if not its content. He had been on YouTube. He had been reading. He had typed things into search bars at midnight that he would not have typed in daylight: *violin making Austria. Inn Valley luthier. Absam Stainer.* He had read about Jakob Stainer, born 1617, died 1683, the greatest violin maker outside Cremona, whose instruments had been preferred to Stradivari's for a century after his death. He had read about the village -- three thousand people, a parish church, a museum, workshops that still operated, the tradition continuing four hundred years later in a valley beneath the Alps. He had read about the spruce. The Alpine spruce, grown at altitude, tight-grained, resonant, cut in winter when the sap was down, seasoned for years in the mountain air. He had read about the varnish. He had read about the scroll. He had read about the f-holes and the bass bar and the sound post, the tiny wooden dowel that transmitted vibrations from the top plate to the back, and that luthiers called, in several languages, the *soul*.

He had not told Jeannie. He had not told anyone. The research existed in the same space as his midnight YouTube sessions -- a private province, a sealed room within the sealed room, a place where he could approach the thing he had lost without the risk of exposure. Jeannie's itinerary included Innsbruck. Innsbruck was fifteen kilometres from Absam. The proximity was coincidental, or it was providential, or it was the kind of alignment that happens when a man who cannot act is acted upon by the geometry of his wife's Instagram feed. Jeannie wanted Vienna because Margot had been to Vienna. Innsbruck was a stop on the way. Absam was a village no one in the Harrington family had heard of and only one member would remember. And Bob -- who could not bring himself to search for a violin teacher, who could not bring himself to play the Hilary Hahn video, who could not bring himself to open the case in the hallway cupboard -- would walk into a violin shop in the Inn Valley because Jeannie wanted to photograph herself in front of the Hotel Sacher.

This is how it would happen. Not through courage but through accident. Not through decision but through the slow convergence of an algorithm and a wife's ambition and a man's inability to act on his own behalf. The universe would drag Bob to his destiny because Bob would not walk there himself.

---

Jeannie packed the way Jeannie did everything: with the meticulous, exhausting precision of a woman who understood that appearance was not separate from reality but constitutive of it. The packing began two weeks before departure. It occupied the master bedroom, which had become hers in all but name -- the bed covered in outfits laid flat, photographed on her phone for reference, each outfit assigned to a city and a day and a probable activity. London: the tailored coat, the ankle boots, the scarf from a brand Jeannie could pronounce correctly and her friends could not. Paris: the linen dress, the sandals, the straw bag that looked effortless and had cost four hundred dollars. Vienna: something darker, more structured, something that said *I understand this city's history*, though Jeannie's understanding of Vienna's history extended to the Sacher Hotel and a film she had once half-watched about the Habsburgs.

She had a content strategy. She did not call it a content strategy -- she called it "capturing memories" or "documenting the trip" -- but it was a content strategy, as rigorous and as calculated as any brand's quarterly plan. She had identified the locations that would photograph well. She had planned the angles. She had researched the hashtags -- not the popular ones, which would drown her posts in a sea of identical content, but the mid-range ones, the niche ones, the ones that signalled taste rather than tourism. #ViennaLife. #EuropeanSummer. #SlowTravel. She had even, though she would deny this, planned her captions in advance, drafting them in the Notes app on her phone with the careful attention to voice and tone that a more self-aware person might have directed toward actual writing.

The children received their instructions. Chase was informed that he would be coming for the first week -- London, possibly Paris -- after which he could fly home, because Chase in a museum was a liability and Chase at a dinner table in Vienna was unthinkable. Chase received this with the performative indifference of a nineteen-year-old who has been given permission to do exactly what he wanted: spend a week in London drinking with his mates' connections and then fly home to the boat and the sun and the world he understood. "Sounds cool," he had said, which was Chase's response to everything from a holiday to a house fire.

Shanel, at sixteen, was more engaged. She wanted to see Paris. She wanted to see Vienna, genuinely -- she had read something, or seen something, a Klimt exhibition mentioned in a magazine at the school library, or a documentary about the Secession, and the images had produced in her the particular excitement of a young person encountering beauty that has not been pre-digested by her mother's aesthetic program. She asked questions. "Can we go to the Belvedere?" "Is there a Klimt museum?" Jeannie approved: "We'll do the Belvedere, darling. You'll love it." The approval was warm, was genuine in its way, and was also a claim of ownership -- Shanel's curiosity was acceptable because it aligned with Jeannie's vision of what a cultured family did on a European holiday. If Shanel had asked about, say, a punk rock club in Berlin, the approval would not have been forthcoming.

Bob said yes to everything. He said yes to the hotels and yes to the restaurants and yes to the itinerary and yes to the Rimowa luggage set that Jeannie ordered from David Jones at a cost he did not ask about. He said yes because saying yes was the path of least resistance and the path of least resistance was the only path Bob knew how to walk. And beneath the yes, beneath the compliance and the accommodation, a different itinerary was forming -- not in a spreadsheet, not in a Google doc, not in any medium that could be observed or questioned, but in the private province of his mind, the place where the YouTube sessions and the midnight research and the aching left hand existed, the place where a man who could not act was, despite himself, preparing to act.

He had googled "violin workshops Absam." He had found a website -- rudimentary, in German, with a few pages translated into halting English -- for a workshop on Stainerstrasse. The photographs showed a small room, benches, tools, instruments hanging from the ceiling. A man with white hair and large hands, holding a violin by the scroll, examining it in natural light. Bob had looked at the photographs for a long time. He had not bookmarked the page. He had not written down the address. But he had memorised it -- Stainerstrasse 12, Absam, Tirol -- the way you memorise the address of a place you are going to go whether you admit it or not.

---

The morning of departure was controlled chaos. Not real chaos -- real chaos was spontaneous, disordered, human -- but Jeannie's version of it, which was chaos stage-managed to produce the appearance of a family in motion while actually executing a protocol she had rehearsed in her mind for weeks. The Rimowa cases were by the front door at 6am, arranged by size and colour. Jeannie had checked the passports three times. She had checked the boarding passes twice. She had checked the house -- windows, doors, the alarm, the tap in the laundry that sometimes dripped -- with the thoroughness of a woman who believed that vigilance was the price of perfection and that the universe punished those who left taps running.

Chase arrived at the last possible moment. He had slept at a mate's -- Macca's, or Deano's, or someone's -- and he came through the front door at six-forty wearing the good shirt Jeannie had left on his bed, though he had paired it with boardshorts and thongs, a combination that Jeannie addressed with a single look that contained, in its compressed fury, the full history of every sartorial argument they had ever had. Chase changed into jeans. He did this without comment, because the path of least resistance ran in the family like a genetic trait.

Shanel was ready. She had been ready since five-thirty, sitting on her suitcase in the hallway, reading on her phone, self-contained in the way she always was -- a girl who had learned early that the safest position in her family was one of composed readiness, prepared for anything, surprised by nothing, requiring no one's attention and therefore receiving it only when she chose.

Bob did something before they left. He went to his home office and closed the door. Not the soundproofed office it would become -- that was two years away, a renovation he could not yet imagine. Just a room: a desk, a computer, a filing cabinet, a window looking east toward the neighbour's jacaranda tree, which was in flower, the purple blossoms covering the lawn beneath it in a carpet of colour that Jeannie found beautiful and Bob found melancholy, though he could not have said why. He stood in the room. He stood in it the way you stand in a place you are about to leave, not with the intention of memorising it but with the sense that something about you, in this room, is about to change, and you want to feel the before of it, the last moment of the person you are, before you become the person you will be.

He locked the door. He left the house.

Jim called while they were in the car. The driver -- a hire car, because Jeannie did not park her Range Rover at the airport long-term and Bob did not argue -- answered the Bluetooth, and Jim's voice filled the car with the particular warmth of an eighty-year-old man who has learned to speak louder because he can no longer trust his ears.

"Have a wonderful trip, son. You deserve it."

"Thanks, Dad."

"Bring Shanel to the Opera. She'll remember it for the rest of her life."

"We will."

"And don't let Jeannie spend all your money." This was a joke. Jim always made this joke. It landed in the car with the dull thud of a line that had been repeated so often it had lost its edges and become smooth, a conversational pebble, harmless and familiar.

"No promises," Bob said, and Jeannie smiled -- the automatic smile, the smile she gave Jim because Jim was Bob's father and therefore deserved the performance of warmth even when the warmth was not felt -- and Chase was on his phone and Shanel was looking out the window and Jim said "Love you, mate" in the gruff, embarrassed way that Harrington men said the word *love*, as though it were a foreign currency they had to declare at customs, and Bob said "Love you too, Dad" and the line went dead.

*You deserve it.* The word *deserve* sat in Bob's chest like a stone. He did not deserve it. He did not deserve any of it -- not the trip, not the hire car, not the Rimowa luggage, not the family arranged around him in their good clothes, not the fund that paid for all of it, not the life that the fund maintained. He deserved whatever Peter Grainger and Richard Lacey and the other investors deserved to do with him when they discovered the truth. He deserved the Chris Marco headlines and the courtroom and the sentence. He deserved what he had built, which was nothing.

But the stone in his chest was accompanied by something else. Something lighter, something that existed alongside the guilt and the dread and the familiar grey weight of the fraud, occupying the same space but vibrating at a different frequency. He was going to Europe. He was going to Austria. He was going to the Inn Valley. And in the Inn Valley, in a village he had never visited, on a street named after a man who had died three hundred and forty years ago, there was a workshop, and in the workshop there were violins, and the violins were the only honest things in his life, the only things that existed outside the fraud and the marriage and the performance, and he was going to them the way a man goes to water when he is dying of thirst -- not because he has decided to, but because his body has overruled his will.

---

Perth Airport. Terminal 1, International Departures. The particular purgatory of a space designed to process humans: the queues, the screens, the announcement system with its recorded voice telling you things you already knew in a tone that suggested you were too stupid to know them. The Harringtons moved through it with the efficiency of a family that had done this before -- not often enough to be blasé, but often enough to know the choreography. Jeannie managed the logistics. She managed them the way she managed everything: completely, competently, with a control that left no space for anyone else to contribute and that she experienced not as control but as care. She checked them in. She negotiated the luggage. She steered them through security with the quiet authority of a woman who understood that chaos was a personal failing and that airports were tests of character.

The departure lounge was half-full. Families with children, couples, a group of young men in matching polo shirts who were probably heading to Bali. The Harringtons found seats near the window. Jeannie arranged the carry-on bags. She took a photograph -- the passports fanned on her lap, the boarding passes, a coffee cup, the corner of a Rimowa case visible at the edge of the frame. She posted it. Caption: *Adventure begins.* She watched the first likes arrive and felt the small, reliable pulse of validation that had replaced, over the past decade, every other source of feeling in her life.

Chase was asleep within ten minutes. He had the gift of unconsciousness -- the ability to fall asleep in any position, in any location, at any time -- which Jeannie attributed to a lack of inner life and Bob attributed to the profound and enviable peace of a man who had never lied about anything that mattered. He slept with his mouth open, his body slung across two plastic chairs, his good shirt already creased, and he looked, in sleep, younger than his nineteen years, and softer, and more like the boy he had been before Jeannie's criticism and the world's expectations had hardened him into the performance of manhood.

Shanel read. She was deep in a novel -- Bob still could not see the cover, but she had not looked up in twenty minutes, and her face had the particular quality of absorption that he recognised, with a pang, as his own. The quality of disappearing into something. The quality of being somewhere else while sitting in a plastic chair. She had it. She had the thing he had -- the capacity to leave the world through a door that other people could not see -- and she did not know she had it, and he did not know how to tell her, and the distance between his knowledge and his silence was one more measure of the failure of his life.

Jeannie was on her phone. She was always on her phone. She was texting someone -- the gaggle, or a friend, or the woman at the hotel in London confirming something Jeannie had already confirmed twice. Her fingers moved across the screen with the fluid, unconscious speed of a person who communicates primarily through text, who has offloaded most of her emotional life onto a device, who is more articulate in 280 characters than in the actual presence of the people she lives with.

Bob sat slightly apart and watched his family and felt the feeling again -- the lightness, the strange, painful, specific lightness of a man about to be no one. The fund was behind him. Sandra had the office. The emails would arrive and he would answer them from hotel rooms and airport lounges, typing the same reassuring phrases into the same small screen, maintaining the machine from nine thousand miles away. But the machine would not need him. That was the truth of it. The machine did not need Bob. It needed only the fiction of Bob, the voice and the face and the quarterly reports, and these could be deployed from anywhere. The actual man -- the man sitting in this plastic chair, watching his daughter read, watching his wife post, watching his son sleep -- was unnecessary. He was a redundancy in his own life.

Except. Except for the word in his search history. Except for Stainerstrasse 12. Except for the pull in his chest that had been there for months now, growing, becoming harder to ignore, the vibration of a string that someone had plucked a long time ago and that had never quite stopped resonating. Except for the Alps. Except for the spruce forests. Except for the workshop with the white-haired man and the violins hanging from the ceiling. Except for all of that, Bob was no one.

But all of that was enough. All of that was everything.

The boarding call came. They gathered their things -- Jeannie directing, Chase stumbling upright, Shanel marking her page. They joined the queue. They shuffled forward in the universal posture of humans about to be processed -- passports open, boarding passes ready, faces arranged into the particular blankness of people submitting to a system.

Bob looked back, once, through the floor-to-ceiling windows. The Indian Ocean was there, beyond the runways, beyond the terminal, beyond the car parks and the access roads and the freight hangars -- the same ocean, the same flat pewter expanse, the same unbroken line to Africa or to nothing or to whatever existed on the far side of eleven thousand kilometres of water. He had looked at it every morning of his life. He had stood on the grass on Marine Parade and let it fill the space where thought would be. He had looked at it from the flybridge of the *Chardonnay* and from the beach at Cottesloe and from the windows of the house that had cost four and a half million dollars. It was the backdrop of his existence. It was the surface against which his surface life was arranged.

He was crossing it. He was going to the other side.

The queue moved. Jeannie said something -- "Bob, passports" -- and he handed them over, and she took them with the automatic efficiency of a woman who had long ago assumed control of all administrative functions in the marriage, and they moved forward, and the gate swallowed them, and Perth fell away behind them.

---

They boarded. They settled. The plane taxied and turned and the engines rose from idle to roar and the wheels left the ground and Perth tilted beneath them -- the river, silver in the morning light, winding through suburbs laid out in the neat, optimistic grid of a city that believed in order. The coast. The beaches -- Cottesloe, Swanbourne, Scarborough -- white curves against the blue. And then the ocean, filling the window, filling everything, the Indian Ocean spreading out below them, and Bob, in his window seat, with Jeannie adjusting her neck pillow beside him and Chase already unconscious and Shanel already reading, looked down at the water and felt it.

Not happiness. He did not trust the word, and he would not have used it. Something more provisional, more fragile, more conditional. Something that existed alongside the guilt and the dread and the fraud and the knowledge that he was a man who had destroyed everything he was supposed to protect. Something that had been dormant for decades -- since a Tuesday evening in a bedroom on Pearse Street, since the Handel sonata, since the door had opened and then closed and then been sealed shut by his father's expectations and his wife's contempt and his own catastrophic inability to want things on his own behalf.

The feeling. The thing that was not happiness and not hope and not courage but was related to all three, the way a seed is related to the tree it might become. The feeling of a man who has lived his entire life in a house and is, for the first time, stepping outside, not because he has decided to but because the walls have thinned and the door is ajar and the wind from outside is carrying a sound he has not heard in thirty years -- the sound of a violin, or the memory of a violin, or the promise of a violin, the single sustained note of a life that might, even now, even this late, even after everything, still be lived.

Below them, the ocean. Ahead of them, Europe. Ahead of them, London and Paris and Vienna and a village in the Inn Valley where a white-haired man was, at this very moment, holding a violin up to the light in a workshop on Stainerstrasse, examining the grain of the spruce, checking the seam where the top plate met the ribs, and feeling, in the wood and in the varnish and in the four hundred years of tradition that had made the instrument possible, the thing he called *Seele* -- soul -- which was not a metaphor but a fact, a presence, a quality that the finest violins possessed and that could be heard only by those who were ready to hear it.

Bob was not ready. He did not know he was on his way. He knew only that the ocean was behind him and something was ahead, and that his left hand, resting on the armrest, was curling slightly, the fingers closing on nothing, the thumb bracing against a neck that was not there.

Not yet.
