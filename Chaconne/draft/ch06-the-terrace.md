# Chapter 6: The Terrace

The phone rang at nine-fourteen on a Monday morning in November, and Bob knew. He did not know in the way that a person knows a fact -- with certainty, with evidence, with the architecture of reason supporting the conclusion -- but in the way a body knows: the phone vibrating against the desk surface, the sound of it thin and industrial in the quiet office, and something in his chest cavity dropping, a physical descent, as though a trapdoor had opened between his ribs and his organs had shifted downward by a centimetre. He looked at the screen. The number was not in his contacts, which meant it was either a cold call or it was the other thing, the thing he had been waiting for without admitting he was waiting for it, because admitting you are waiting for something means admitting it exists, and Bob's entire life was built on the premise that certain things could be kept from existing by the simple act of not looking at them.

He let it ring. Once, twice, three times. The office was empty -- Sandra came in on Tuesdays and Thursdays, and on the other days the reception desk sat unmanned and the meeting room sat unused and Harrington Capital consisted of one man in a room on the twelfth floor of a building on St George's Terrace, looking at a phone he did not want to answer. The morning light came through the east-facing window and fell across the desk in a long rectangle, illuminating the phone, the two screens (both dark -- he had not yet turned them on), a pen, a glass of water, the ordinary objects of a working life arranged with the neatness of a man who did not have enough real work to create disorder.

He picked up. He did this not because he wanted to but because not picking up was worse. Not picking up meant the call would come again, and then again, and then it would come as a letter, and then it would come as something else entirely, something with a letterhead and a reference number, and by then the architecture of not-looking would have collapsed under its own weight. Better to answer. Better to perform.

"Bob Harrington."

The voice on the other end was male, measured, and precise in the way that professional voices are precise when they are building toward something. Not angry. Worse than angry: informed. The man identified himself -- an investor, not Richard, someone else, someone whose name Bob recognised from a redemption request he had deferred three months ago with the twelve-month-horizon speech. The speech had worked at the time. Speeches like that always worked, because they were constructed from the same materials that Bob used in every client interaction -- warmth, authority, the reassuring cadence of a man who lives and breathes this -- and because people did not want to believe that the person managing their money was lying to them any more than Bob wanted to believe he was the person lying.

But this man had done something. Bob could hear it in the voice before the words confirmed it: the particular tone of a person who has been thinking, who has compared notes, who has spoken to his accountant or his lawyer or, worst of all, to another investor. The questions were specific. Technical. They arrived in a sequence that suggested preparation -- not hostility, not yet, but the careful architecture of a man who wants to give you the opportunity to explain before he escalates. "What's the fund's current NAV, Bob?" "Can I get the most recent audited statements?" "Who do you use for the external audit?"

Bob answered. The answers were fluent, practised, technically accurate in ways that obscured the truth. He had delivered them before, to other callers, in other years. The machine ran.

---

But the machine stuttered.

The man -- Grainger, that was his name, Peter Grainger, a semi-retired cardiologist who had invested $1.2 million with Harrington Capital in 2016 on the recommendation of a colleague at Sir Charles Gairdner Hospital -- did not follow the script. The script said that the investor asked questions, Bob answered them, the answers were reassuring, the investor thanked him and hung up and the architecture held for another quarter. Grainger did not thank him and hang up. Grainger paused. The pause was long enough for Bob to hear the air conditioning in whatever room Grainger was sitting in, long enough for the morning light to shift fractionally across the desk, long enough for the silence to become a pressure.

"Bob, I'm going to ask you something, and I'd appreciate a straight answer."

"Of course."

"Is the fund solvent?"

The word -- *solvent* -- sat in the air like a stone dropped into still water. Bob could feel the ripples spreading outward: through his chest, through his hands, through the walls of the office, through the twelve floors below him to the street, outward into the city, toward every person whose money he held and whose trust he carried and whose life he had made a little worse without their knowing. Solvent. The word was clinical. It was the word a cardiologist would use, a man accustomed to asking bodies whether they were still functioning. It demanded a clinical answer.

Bob said: "The fund is performing within its mandate. I can send you the latest quarterly."

"I've seen the quarterly."

"Then you'll know we're tracking well against the benchmark--"

"I've also spoken to Richard Lacey."

The second silence was different from the first. The first had been the silence of a question being loaded. This was the silence of a detonation, small and contained, felt only by the two men on the line. Richard. Richard had spoken to Grainger, or Grainger had spoken to Richard, and they had compared notes, and comparing notes was how Ponzis died -- not through investigation, not through regulation, not through the sudden and dramatic intervention of authorities, but through the slow and devastating act of ordinary people talking to each other.

"Richard's a good client," Bob said, and the words came automatically, the way breathing comes automatically, the way lying comes automatically when lying is the medium you have lived in for a decade. "I've spoken to him about his redemption timeline and we're--"

"He can't get his money out either, Bob."

The word *either* was the sharpest thing in the conversation. It contained the history of Grainger's own redemption request -- filed three months ago, deferred with the twelve-month-horizon speech, accepted at the time, now reexamined in the light of Richard's identical experience. *Either* meant pattern. *Either* meant that two data points had become a line, and a line was a trajectory, and a trajectory pointed somewhere.

"I understand the concern," Bob said. He heard his own voice as though from a distance, marvelling at its steadiness, at the warmth in it, at the complete absence of the terror that was filling his chest like cold water. "These positions are structured with a redemption horizon that--"

"I'd like to see the audited financials. The full audit, not the quarterly summary."

"I can certainly arrange that. Let me speak to our auditor and--"

"Who is your auditor?"

Bob named a firm. The firm was real. They had performed the fund's initial audit in 2010. They had not performed one since 2017. The gap between 2017 and now -- eight years without an independent audit -- was the gap in which the fraud lived, like a creature that survives in darkness and dies in light.

"I'll follow up with them directly, if that's all right."

"Of course," Bob said. "I'll let them know to expect your call."

The line went dead. Bob sat at his desk. His hands were shaking -- a fine tremor, visible only to himself, the kind of tremor that might be caffeine or might be age or might be the specific physiological response of a body that has just felt the ground shift beneath it. He placed his palms flat on the desk. He breathed. He looked at the rectangle of light on the desk surface and counted the seconds until the tremor stopped.

It took forty seconds. He counted every one.

---

He opened the spreadsheet. Not a real spreadsheet -- not a file on a computer, not something that could be discovered or subpoenaed or entered into evidence. The spreadsheet existed only in his head, a set of numbers he ran every morning and every night, the way a man with a chronic illness checks his symptoms, not to cure them but to measure the rate of deterioration.

Assets: approximately $29 million. This was the real number -- the sum of the fund's actual holdings, the listed equities that had survived the mining bust and the subsequent years of creative rebalancing, the few property interests that still held value, the cash in the operating account. Twenty-nine million dollars, sitting behind the facade of a fund that reported $74 million to its investors.

Liabilities: the $74 million. This was the number on the quarterly reports, the number in the cream envelopes Sandra printed and posted with the Harrington Capital logo, the number that Bob's investors believed they owned. Seventy-four million. Some of it was real. Most of it was not. The gap -- $45 million, give or take, depending on the day's market movements and which positions Bob chose to value optimistically -- was the size of the lie.

Three new redemption requests had come in this week. One was $80,000 -- small, manageable, he could meet it. One was $350,000 -- he would need to liquidate, which would widen the gap, which would make the next redemption harder. The third was $600,000, from a family trust, and this one he could not meet without new money, and new money meant new clients, and new clients meant new lies, new performances, new lunches at restaurants on the Terrace where he ordered the second-most-expensive bottle and looked people in the eye and told them their money was safe.

The machine was speeding up. This was the arithmetic of a Ponzi: the faster the money went out, the faster the money had to come in, and the faster it came in, the more obligations it created, and the more obligations it created, the faster the money went out. It was a wheel, and it was accelerating, and the only thing keeping it turning was Bob's face and Bob's voice and the trust that both inspired, and one day -- not today, not next week, but one day that was closer now than it had been yesterday -- the wheel would stop. It had to. It was a mathematical certainty. The gap was a one-way function. It only widened.

Bob closed the mental spreadsheet. He turned on his screens. The left screen showed the ASX: tickers scrolling, green and red, the small movements of a market that had nothing to do with him and everything to do with the performance he maintained. The right screen showed his email. Forty-seven unread messages. He began to work through them with the methodical diligence of a man maintaining a machine he knows is broken, because the alternative -- stopping -- was not an option he could see.

---

Jim arrived at eleven-thirty. Bob heard the lobby door and knew it was his father before he saw him, because Jim still opened doors with the particular firmness of a man who had spent forty years entering offices he owned, even though he had not owned this one and did not own any. The sound carried: the beep of the key card (Bob had given him a spare, years ago, a gesture of filial generosity that was also a measure of how rarely anyone else visited), the slow footsteps across the reception area, the slight wheeze that had been there for two years now and that Jim attributed to the weather and Bob attributed to eighty years of living.

"Robert."

Jim stood in the doorway. He was smaller than Bob remembered him being, though Bob saw him every week and the shrinking was so gradual it should not have registered. He was thinner, too -- the frame that had once filled a suit now occupied it loosely, the collar of his shirt gapping at the neck, the trousers a little wide at the waist. He wore a tie. He always wore a tie to the office, even though it was no longer his office and the tie was an anachronism and the building's other tenants -- the lawyers, the accountants, the mining consultants -- wore open collars and chinos. Jim's tie was striped. His shoes were polished. His hair, what remained of it, was combed.

"Dad." Bob stood. "Good to see you."

They shook hands. They had always shaken hands in the office, even when Bob was twenty-three and working under Jim at Patersons, even when they saw each other every day. The office demanded formality. The office was where men were professional, where the relationship between father and son was transmuted into the relationship between mentor and protege, between founder and successor, between the man who built the thing and the man who was supposed to maintain it.

Jim settled into the visitor's chair -- the chair where clients sat, the chair where Richard had sat two months ago asking about his $500,000. Jim sat in it with the ease of a man who had once sat across desks from ministers and CEOs and who still believed, with the simple faith of the generation that built Perth's financial infrastructure, that a desk and a handshake and an honest word were the foundations of commerce.

"How's the book looking?"

"Solid," Bob said. The word came out of him the way air comes out of a punctured tyre: automatically, under pressure, without decision. "Markets are choppy but we're well-positioned."

"That's what I like to hear." Jim looked around the office with the approving gaze of a man surveying what his son had built. He took in the screens, the desk, the credenza with its neatly arranged files, the print of boats in a harbour that Jeannie had chosen in 2012 -- a nautical scene that communicated solidity and calm, the visual language of a man who knew about tides and weather and had the patience to navigate both. Jim saw what the office was designed to show: competence, order, a steady hand.

He did not see what was actually there, which was nothing. An empty room on the twelfth floor of a building on St George's Terrace, maintained at $85,000 a year in rent and overheads, staffed two days a week by a woman who processed the paperwork and asked no questions, containing no real business activity, no genuine investment process, no research, no analysis -- nothing except a man and his lie and the machinery required to sustain it.

"Markets are choppy," Jim repeated, tasting the phrase. "Iron ore's down."

"Sixty-two a tonne this morning."

"I remember sixty-two. That was the price in 2003. Before the boom." Jim shook his head, the way old men shake their heads at prices, at markets, at the passage of time measured in commodity cycles. "Your mother and I bought the house for $280,000 in 1974. Do you know what they're asking for places on Pearse Street now?"

"I'd rather not think about it."

"Two-eight. Two million eight." Jim pronounced this with the awed disgust of a man who understood money in one generation's terms and could not recalibrate for another's. "For a four-bedroom limestone. Your mother would have laughed."

Bob smiled. The smile was genuine -- or as genuine as anything was, in this room, in this conversation, in this life. His mother. Wendy. Dead three years now, a stroke in the garden she had maintained for forty years, the paramedics finding her on the lawn beneath the roses she had pruned that morning. Bob's grief had been quiet and real and was the last entirely honest emotion he had permitted himself. He missed her. He missed her garden. He missed the way she had looked at him when he was eleven, at the school gate, the violin case over his shoulder, and said nothing, because Wendy had the gift of recognising things she did not need to name.

Jim reached for the quarterly report on the credenza. "Mind if I have a look?"

Bob's heart rate spiked. A single hard contraction, like a fist clenching inside his chest, and then the settling -- the learned calm of a man who has been flinching at the same danger for a decade and has trained himself to flinch invisibly. "Of course."

Jim opened it. The quarterly: twelve pages, laser-printed, professionally formatted. A cover page with the Harrington Capital logo. A portfolio summary. A performance table showing twelve percent annualised returns over the trailing year. An asset allocation chart -- equities, fixed income, alternatives -- rendered in the colours of a financial advisory firm that inspired confidence: navy, gold, a tasteful grey. The document was a work of fiction. It was the most carefully crafted document Bob had ever produced, more thoughtful than any essay he'd written at university, more considered than any email he'd sent to a client. Every number in it had been chosen not for its truth but for its plausibility, calibrated to be impressive without being incredible, strong without being suspicious, the performance of a fund that was doing well in the way that funds ought to do well -- steadily, reliably, without the kind of returns that made accountants ask questions.

Jim read it. He read it the way he had once read the financial pages of the *West Australian* -- carefully, with attention, with the respect of a man who understood that numbers were a language and that the ability to read them was a kind of literacy. He nodded as he turned the pages. His lips moved slightly, the way old people's lips move when they are processing numbers they find satisfying.

"Twelve percent annualised?" He looked up. "In this market? That's strong, son."

"We've had a good run."

The lie. The generational lie, delivered in the vocabulary of the man who had taught him to speak. Jim heard it and was reassured, because the lie was fluent, because the lie used the right words in the right tone with the right cadence, because the lie was constructed from the same materials Jim had used to build his own career -- confidence, competence, the steady hand -- and because a father does not want to believe his son is lying any more than a son wants to believe he is the kind of man who lies to his father.

Jim told a story. It was a story Bob had heard before -- about the '87 crash, about a client at Patersons who had panicked and sold everything at the bottom, BHP at four dollars, CRA at six, the lot, and had spent the next decade watching the market recover without him. "The point is," Jim said, leaning forward in the visitor's chair with the earnestness of a man delivering a lesson he believed in with his whole being, "you never panic. Steady hands. That's what your clients need. That's what you give them."

Bob nodded. He listened. He understood that his father was describing, without knowing it, the opposite of what Bob had done. Jim's client had panicked and acted. Bob had panicked and lied. The client had lost money through fear. Bob's clients were losing money through trust. The moral was the same -- steady hands, patience, the long view -- but the application was inverted, and the inversion was so complete, so perfectly awful, that Bob felt, beneath the performance of attentive son, a grief so deep and so specific that he could not look at it directly. He had taken the life his father gave him. He had taken the values, the language, the path. He had taken it all and used it to build a fraud, and his father's pride -- sitting across the desk in a striped tie, reading a fabricated quarterly and nodding with satisfaction -- was the cruelest punishment, because it was undeserved.

---

He drove Jim home. The route along Stirling Highway was familiar enough to be involuntary -- the avenue of peppermint trees, the speed camera at Claremont, the turn past the university, the limestone walls of the old colleges. Jim talked. He talked about the garden -- the roses were struggling, the reticulation needed replacing, the lawn service was costing him a hundred and twenty a week and doing a job that Wendy had done for free. He talked about the neighbours -- the Hendersons' boy had moved to Sydney, the property on the corner had sold for something unconscionable. He talked about cricket. He did not talk about his health, which was a topic he treated with the same resolute avoidance his son applied to the fund's balance sheet: if you did not name it, it could not advance.

Pearse Street. The limestone cottage. Four bedrooms, a garden, a carport where a Volvo had once sat and where a Toyota Camry now sat, the automotive equivalent of a man diminishing. The garden was sparser than Bob remembered -- not neglected, exactly, but thinned, as though Wendy's death had drained something from the soil itself. The roses were still there, climbing the limestone wall, but they looked tired, and the natives she had planted along the front path had gone leggy, reaching for light that the grown-up trees were now blocking.

Bob walked Jim to the door. The door was the original -- solid jarrah, the brass handle worn smooth by fifty years of hands. Jim fumbled briefly with the key, then got it, and the door opened onto the hallway that Bob had walked through every day of his childhood: the polished floorboards, the hat stand (still there, still holding Jim's one remaining hat), the smell of the house, which was the smell of old wood and closed windows and something else, something beneath, the accumulated scent of two lives lived in one place for half a century. Wendy's ghost was everywhere. The kitchen she had kept. The lounge where she had read. The garden visible through the back window, maintained now by strangers.

"You're doing well, Bob." Jim stood at the threshold, one hand on the doorframe, and looked at his son with an expression that was pride and something else -- something softer, something that might have been gratitude, the gratitude of a father who believed his son had justified the life he had been given. "Your mother would be proud."

Bob said something. He said "Thanks, Dad" or "Means a lot" or whatever the correct response was, the response that sealed the exchange and allowed both men to return to the postures they had maintained for forty years. He said it and he smiled and he touched his father's shoulder -- briefly, the way Harrington men touched each other, which was rarely and with precision -- and he walked back to the car and drove away.

He did not drive to the office. He drove to the office car park and pulled into his reserved space and turned off the engine and sat in the dark.

The parking garage was underground, four levels deep, the concrete stained and cracked, the fluorescent lights humming their flat, institutional hum. Bob's Mercedes occupied its designated space between a column and a wall, and he sat in it the way a man sits in a confessional or a prison cell -- alone, enclosed, the engine off, the silence total. His hands were on the steering wheel. His left hand was tight -- not the violin grip, not the unconscious curl of fingers around an invisible neck, but a fist. A simple, desperate fist, the hand of a man holding on to something that is not there.

He sat for fifteen minutes. He thought about his father. He thought about his mother. He thought about the quarterly report and the twelve percent and the word *proud*, which was the most dangerous word in the English language because it meant someone believed in you and the believing was based on nothing and the nothing was your fault.

---

The afternoon was worse. Not in any dramatic way -- there was no crisis, no phone call, no revelation. The afternoon was worse in the way that Wednesdays are worse than Mondays: the initial energy of coping has dissipated and the weekend is too far away and the grey middle of the week stretches out in all directions and you are simply in it, simply existing in the middle of a situation that is deteriorating at a rate too slow to constitute an emergency and too fast to permit denial.

Bob worked. He returned emails. He processed a redemption -- the small one, $80,000, a straightforward transaction that required only a transfer from the operating account and a polite letter thanking the investor for their continued trust. He drafted a response to the $350,000 request -- a careful response, warm but measured, suggesting a meeting to discuss timing. He did not respond to the $600,000 request. He did not respond to the message from Grainger. He placed both in the category of things he would deal with tomorrow, which was the category where most of the fund's problems lived, deferred and accumulating, like interest on a debt that only compounds.

The Terrace emptied. He watched it through the window -- the suits dispersing toward the parking garages and the train station and the bars on the corner, the particular late-afternoon rhythm of a city that works hard and drinks harder, the shadows lengthening across the glass facades of buildings that existed to contain the ambitions of men like him. The Palace Hotel, across the road, was filling with the after-work crowd: lawyers and bankers and mining executives and the women who worked beside them or waited for them or both, all performing the ritual of the midweek drink, which was the Terrace's sacrament, the thing you did to prove you were still in the game.

Bob did not go to the Palace. He had not been for years. The Terrace's social life required a fluency he once possessed and had lost -- not the fluency of conversation but the fluency of being, the ability to stand in a room with a drink and be at ease, and Bob was no longer at ease anywhere except on the ocean and in the car and, increasingly, in the private hours after midnight when the house was asleep and the phone was silent and he could sit in the dark and do the thing he had always done, which was not-thinking, except that the not-thinking was changing, was becoming something else, was being interrupted more and more by a sound he could almost hear, a vibration at the edge of perception, the ghost of a note played on an instrument he had not touched in decades.

He opened YouTube. He did not intend to. His thumb went to the app the way a hand goes to a wound -- involuntarily, compulsively, drawn by a pain it knows it cannot relieve. The algorithm had been busy. It had been learning from his midnight sessions, from the videos he watched and the videos he watched twice and the videos he hovered over and did not click, and tonight it offered him something it had not offered before.

*Bach: Chaconne from Partita No. 2 in D Minor, BWV 1004 -- Hilary Hahn*

The thumbnail was dark. A concert stage, a single light, a woman standing alone with a violin. The instrument was tucked under her chin and her eyes were closed and the bow was poised above the strings in the moment before contact, the moment of silence that precedes the first note, and even in the stillness of a photograph the image communicated something that Bob felt in his body before his mind could process it: the coiled intensity of a person about to do the most difficult thing they know how to do.

He did not play the video. He looked at the thumbnail. He read the title. He did not know what a chaconne was. He did not know Hilary Hahn. He did not know Bach's Partita No. 2, or BWV 1004, or anything about the piece that would, within two years, become the organising principle of his remaining life. He knew only that the image -- the woman, the violin, the moment before the sound -- produced in him a feeling that was neither pleasure nor pain but something anterior to both, something that existed in his chest and his hands and his jaw, a vibration without a source, a resonance that had been there for thirty-eight years, waiting, patient as stone, waiting for the water.

He closed the app. He closed the laptop. He drove home.

The house was empty. Jeannie was out -- a dinner, an event, something. Chase at a mate's. Shanel at the library, or at a friend's, or somewhere that was not here. Bob stood in the hallway of the house that had cost $4.5 million and contained five bedrooms and a wine fridge and a landscaped garden and Italian tile and ocean views and everything that a successful man was supposed to have, and the house was empty, and the silence was the kind of silence that does not mean the absence of sound but the absence of presence, the silence of a place that has been evacuated, that has been left behind, and he stood in it and felt the specific loneliness of a man who is surrounded by the evidence of a life and knows that none of it is real.

He went upstairs. He went to bed. He lay in the dark, in the king-size bed that he occupied alone, and he thought about the chaconne -- the word, not the music, because he had not heard the music yet. Chaconne. It was a word he did not know. It sat in his mind like a small, hard stone, opaque, resisting interpretation. He did not know that it was a dance form, or that it was a set of variations on a repeating harmonic pattern, or that Bach had written the greatest chaconne ever composed as a lament for his first wife, or that the piece lasted fifteen minutes and contained within it more human feeling than most people experience in a lifetime. He knew only the word, and the image, and the vibration in his hands that would not stop.

He slept. He dreamed of music. In the morning, he did not remember the dream, but his left hand ached, the fingertips sore, as though he had been pressing them against strings all night.
