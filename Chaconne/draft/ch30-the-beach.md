# Chapter 30: The Beach

The grass was cool under his shoes. The good shoes, the Italian leather, the pair Jeannie had chosen for him from a shop in Claremont whose name he had never learned because the names of shops where Jeannie bought things were Jeannie's knowledge, not his, part of the vast and intricate catalogue of the life she had built around them both. The life that was behind him now, up the slope, on the terrace, where the sound of voices had taken on the particular quality of voices processing catastrophe: high, broken, uncertain, the social machinery stripped of its gears and spinning free.

He descended the first terrace. The couch grass was thick here, maintained by the council, and it gave slightly under his weight, the blades springing back behind him as if he had not passed. The fairy lights were above and behind him now, their warm yellow glow reaching only partway down the slope before giving way to the blue dark of the April evening. He could feel the scroll in his right hand -- the carved head of the violin, the volute warm and smooth, the four tuning pegs jutting at angles from the shattered neck. He did not remember putting it there. He did not remember standing up from the terrace floor or walking to the edge or stepping down onto the grass. His body had done these things without consulting him, the way it had done things without consulting him for most of his life -- accommodating, complying, moving through the world on instructions he had never examined, and now moving through the world on no instructions at all, which felt, for the first time, like freedom, or like its opposite, the two being closer than he had understood.

The second terrace. The Norfolk pines rose on either side of him, their trunks straight and dark, their canopies high and still against the sky. The air smelled of pine resin and salt and, faintly, jasmine from a garden to the south -- the night-blooming jasmine that grew along the fences of the Marine Parade houses and that released its scent at dusk as if the darkness were a signal. He breathed it in. His chest expanded and the breath caught on something -- not a sob, not yet, a physical obstruction, as if the sound he had made on the terrace when the violin hit the stone had left a residue in his airways that the breath could not pass through. He breathed out. He kept walking.

He thought of nothing.

No: he thought of the sound. The crack of the spruce plate splitting. The specific, structural sound of three hundred years of resonance ending in a single second on a limestone terrace in Cottesloe. He could not stop hearing it. The sound played on a loop in his inner ear, layered over the ghost of the Chaconne that was still there too, the two sounds existing simultaneously -- the music and its destruction -- and between them was a silence that was not silence but the space where the instrument's voice had lived, a space that was now empty, a room with the furniture removed, the echo of a room.

He reached down and touched the scroll. Felt the spiral of the pegbox, the smooth wood. He thought of Franz in Absam -- the small man with the large hands, the pale blue eyes, the workshop that smelled of spruce shavings and hide glue. *It needs to be played. You will play it.* He had played it. The thing Franz asked for had happened. The Chaconne had passed through the instrument one final time, and the instrument had held, had given everything, and then it had been broken, and the breaking was not Franz's fault and not Bob's fault and not even, in a way he could not yet articulate, Jeannie's fault. The breaking was what happened to things that were beautiful and unprotected in a world that did not understand what it was holding.

The third terrace. The esplanade. The low stone wall that separated the grass from the footpath, and he stepped over it, and the concrete was hard and flat under his shoes after the give of the grass. A streetlight cast a circle of orange on the path. He walked through it and out the other side, into the dark.

The sand began.

---

He stopped at the edge and took off his shoes. He did this carefully, methodically -- loosening the left shoe with the toe of the right, then the right with the toes of the left, stepping out of each one and placing them side by side on the concrete, aligned, the heels touching the low wall. The socks he pulled off and tucked inside the shoes. The gesture was automatic. Bob was a man who had always put things in order -- the desk at the office, the lines on the quarterly reports, the numbers that did not add up and that he arranged in rows anyway, giving them the appearance of order because the appearance was all he had. He had been putting things in order his entire life, and this was the last thing he would put in order, and he did it with the same care he had always done it, because care was what he had, and care was what the world had never asked for, and he gave it anyway, to the shoes, to the placement of the shoes, to the small and final act of a man arranging his effects.

He took off the jacket. The navy Italian jacket, the jacket that matched the trousers, the jacket Jeannie had hung on the back of his chair that morning with a hanger she kept specifically for occasions when his clothes needed to be ready and unwrinkled and correct. He folded it. Not hastily -- he folded it the way Jeannie had taught him: the shoulders together, the sleeves aligned, a single fold at the waist. He placed it on the sand beside the shoes. He took the scroll from his right hand and put it in the jacket's inside pocket, the silk-lined pocket where he kept his wallet, and the scroll fit as though the pocket had been made for it.

The sand was cool and fine under his bare feet. Cottesloe sand -- white, almost powder, the quartz grains ground by the Southern Ocean over millennia into a softness that felt, against the sole of a bare foot, like something between silk and ash. He stood for a moment. The beach was empty. It stretched north and south in a long pale crescent, the sand luminous in the faint light, the darkness beginning at the waterline and extending west to the horizon and beyond the horizon to wherever the Indian Ocean went -- to the Cocos Islands, to Madagascar, to Africa, to the places that existed on the far side of the eleven thousand kilometres of water he had looked at every morning from the grass above this beach for fifteen years without once considering what it would feel like to enter it and not come back.

He was not considering it now. Or he was. The distinction had ceased to matter. He was walking toward the water because the water was ahead of him and the terrace was behind him and between the two there was nothing -- no plan, no intention, no architecture of thought, nothing but a man in a white shirt and dark trousers walking barefoot across a beach at dusk with the Indian Ocean in front of him and the sound of a broken violin in his ears and the knowledge, which was not knowledge but sensation, that the life he had lived was over and the only direction that remained was west.

He reached the waterline. The first thin sheet of water ran up the beach and over his feet and retreated, and it was cold -- colder than the air, the Indian Ocean in April carrying the memory of the deep water beyond the continental shelf, the water that came from the south, from the Southern Ocean, from places where the sea was not a surface but a body, vast and dark and indifferent to the things that lived and died upon its skin.

He walked into it.

The water rose around his ankles, his shins, his knees. The trousers darkened and clung. The fabric billowed around his calves in the small current, and the sensation was of weight being added, the water claiming him by increments, pulling at him gently with the patience of something that had all the time in the world, which it did. He kept walking. The water reached his waist. The cold pressed against his stomach and he drew a breath, sharp, the air tasting of salt, and he could feel his heart adjusting, the rhythm changing, the body's ancient response to immersion -- the crossing of the boundary between the element that sustained it and the element that could take it away.

The water reached his chest. His shirt floated around him, the white cotton spreading on the surface like something spilled, and his arms were in the water now, and his hands, and the sensation of the water on his hands -- the hands that had held the violin, that had drawn the bow across the strings, that had felt the vibration of three-hundred-year-old spruce transmitting the sound of a piece written in 1720 by a man whose wife had died while he was away -- the sensation of the water on those hands was cold and clean and absolute, and something in him, some last tension, some held breath he had been holding for years, for decades, released.

He began to swim.

---

The shore behind him. The lights of Marine Parade, small and warm, the terrace where he had played, where the violin had been smashed, where Jeannie's voice had risen and broken. The lights grew smaller. The houses grew smaller. The Norfolk pines became silhouettes, became shapes, became suggestions.

He swam west. Freestyle, steady, unhurried -- the stroke of a man who had grown up on this coast and had swum in this ocean all his life, the arms reaching and pulling, reaching and pulling. His breathing settled into rhythm. Three strokes, a breath. Three strokes, a breath. The water was dark around him and the sky was dark above him, and the stars were appearing -- not the faint suburban stars of Cottesloe but the real stars, the ones you saw when you got beyond the light, the ones that had been there all along behind the glare of the life you had built.

He thought of the Chaconne.

Not the piece -- the experience. The fifteen minutes on the terrace when he had been, for the first and last time in his life, entirely himself. The opening chord. The weight of the bow on the strings. The moment in the D major where the music had gone somewhere he could not follow and he had followed it anyway, past his technique, past his ability, into a place where the sound was no longer something he was making but something he was inside, the way a swimmer is inside the water, held and carried and moved by something larger than himself. He could not regret it. Whatever else was true -- the fund, the prosecution, the house, the marriage, the wreckage on the terrace floor -- he could not regret the one thing he had done that was true.

He swam.

The shore was distant now. The lights of Marine Parade were a thin line of warmth along the dark coast, and the ocean around him was black, and the sky above him was black, and between the two blacks was the horizon, which was not visible, which had ceased to exist, so that he was swimming in a space that had no edges, no boundaries, no up or down, only the water beneath him and the air above him and the stars, and the sound of his own breathing, and the ocean, which made no sound, which needed no sound, which had been here for four billion years and would be here for four billion more and did not care about funds or marriages or violins or the Chaconne or any of the things that human beings built on its shore and called permanent.

Something moved beneath him.

A shadow. A displacement in the dark water, something large and slow, passing below and to the left. There and then not there. A dolphin, perhaps -- the bottlenose dolphins that rode the swells off Cottesloe, the grey shapes surfers saw in the mornings, rising and turning in the green water inside the break. Or a seal. Or something else. The coast was home to other things -- things that moved in the dark water with a patience that was not patience but appetite, the ancient machinery of a body designed for one purpose in a sea that had been dark since before there was light.

Bob may have seen it. He may not have.

The novel does not say, because the novel is no longer with him.

The point of view has risen. It has lifted like a bird from the shoulder of a man swimming alone in the Indian Ocean at night, and what it sees from this height is only a shape in the water. A shape that is growing smaller. A white shirt in the dark ocean, the arms still reaching, still pulling, still steady. The shape is difficult to distinguish from the dark water and the dark sky and the dark. And the strokes are still steady. Still unhurried. Still reaching.

And then the shape is very small.

And then the shape is not there.

The morning came the way mornings come to coastlines: slowly, then all at once. The ocean was pale blue, glassy, a sheet of hammered silver under a sky that had not yet decided whether to be clear or overcast. The sand was cool. A jogger ran along the waterline, earbuds in, not looking at the water. A woman walked a Labrador that strained toward the shallows and was pulled back. The Norfolk pines cast their long morning shadows east, away from the sea, toward the houses and the gardens and the lives waking behind their limestone walls.

A couple walked their dog along the beach at seven. The woman saw them first -- the shoes on the concrete at the edge of the sand, placed neatly side by side against the low wall, the heels aligned. Italian leather. Expensive. Beside them, on the sand just above the high-tide line, a folded jacket. Navy. She pointed them out to her husband. He looked at the shoes and then at the water and then at his wife, and something crossed his face, and he took out his phone.

By eight the beach was closed. Yellow tape along the esplanade. A lifeguard on a jet ski, scanning the water in slow passes, the engine's whine carrying across the morning calm. Two police officers at the waterline, one talking into a radio, the other looking at the shoes. A helicopter appeared from the south, low, the thudding of its rotors carrying across the water, the downdraft flattening the surface into a disc of chop that spread and dissipated. It circled twice and moved north.

A shark report. Something had been sighted offshore the previous evening -- a recreational fisherman, heading back to the boat ramp at dusk, had seen a large shape in the water off Cottesloe, moving south. He had not thought much of it. He had filed a report that morning when he heard the news.

The police opened the jacket. In the inside pocket, a carved wooden scroll -- the head of a stringed instrument, old, very fine, the spiral of the volute worn smooth. The officers looked at it and then at each other and placed it in an evidence bag with the jacket and the shoes.

The body was not recovered.
