# Chapter 25: Köthen, Autumn 1720

The ink had dried unevenly on the final bar. He noticed this as he noticed everything -- the slight pooling where the nib had rested a fraction too long on the downstroke of the last note, the faint bleed where the iron gall had found a weakness in the paper's sizing. He would need to touch it up. Not now. Later, when the page had set and he could lay a straightedge along the staff line and correct the stem without smudging what was beneath it. These were the concerns that occupied him: the practical, the material, the solvable. A man could lose himself in such concerns, and Bach had been losing himself in them for three months, ever since the afternoon he had walked into his house in Köthen and found it rearranged by grief into a place he did not recognise.

The study was cold. October had come in dry and sharp, the light through the leaded window arriving at a lower angle each morning, throwing the shadow of the mullion across the desk in a slow diagonal that he had learned to read like a clock. He had been here since before dawn, working by candle and then by the thin yellow light that crept over the rooftops of the town and found his window and lay across the ruled paper like a hand. The candle was still burning. He pinched it out between his thumb and forefinger -- the callus there thick enough that he felt only pressure, not heat -- and the smoke rose in a single thread and dissolved.

Before him on the desk, the nearly complete fair copy of the *Sei Solo a Violino senza Basso accompagnato*. The Six Solos for Violin without Bass Accompaniment. He had been transcribing for weeks -- not composing, for the music was written, had been written for months, existed in the working drafts pinned and stacked around the desk in his rapid, forward-leaning hand. This was the scribe's work. The transformation of a draft into a document. Ink, precision, the particular satisfaction of a clean page filling with notes that had already been decided, already been heard, already been tested against the instrument hanging on its peg on the wall above the manuscript shelf. The working violin. Not valuable. Scarred across the upper bout where the chinrest had worn through the varnish years ago, the neck darkened by the oils of his hand, the strings gut and uneven and perfectly serviceable for a man who played to think rather than to perform.

He was on the Partita No. 2 in D minor. The final movement. The Chaconne.

The working draft was pinned open beside the fair copy, the pages held flat by two smooth stones he kept on the desk for this purpose. His quill moved across the staves with the fluency of a hand that had been forming these shapes since boyhood -- the note-heads small and precise, the stems straight, the beaming of the semiquavers regular and clean. Remarkably few corrections. The autograph showed what his manuscripts always showed: a man who knew what he wanted before pen touched paper. The composing had happened elsewhere -- in his head, on the instrument, in the working drafts where an occasional crossed-out bar or rewritten passage recorded the adjustments that had shaped the music into its final form. Here, in the fair copy, there was only the act of setting it down. Making it permanent. Giving it the form in which it would survive.

He did not think of it in these terms. He did not think of survival. He thought of the next bar, the care required to notate the thirty-second notes cleanly in the arpeggiated passages where the writing was dense and the staves crowded. He thought of the spacing. He thought of the quality of the paper, which was good -- better than the Weimar paper, thicker, less inclined to bleed. Prince Leopold's court provided good materials. This was not a small thing. Bad paper ruined manuscripts the way bad foundations ruined buildings, and Bach had spent enough years writing on paper that drank the ink and feathered the lines to appreciate the difference.

The opening section of the Chaconne filled the first pages. The four-bar theme -- D minor, the descending bass, the voice that stated itself with the grave simplicity of a man who has something to say. The variations building above it: the semiquaver runs, the arpeggiated figures, the implied polyphony that was the movement's deepest ambition -- the illusion of four voices produced by a single instrument, four strings, one bow. He had worked this out on the violin, standing in this room, playing and listening, playing and revising, finding the places where the writing could suggest a bass voice and a treble voice and the inner voices between them, the ear supplying what the instrument could not. The technique was not new. The Italians did it -- Corelli, Vivaldi. But they did it for display, for the performer's glory. Bach did it for architecture. He wanted a structure that would stand of its own weight, the way a fugue stands, the way a church stands: every element bearing load, every voice necessary, the whole greater than the sum because the sum was not the point. The point was the whole.

He copied the arpeggiated passage that climbed toward the first climax. His quill moved quickly here -- the notes small and crowded, the beaming intricate, the page requiring his full attention to keep the spacing even. He could hear the music as he wrote it. Not in the way a layman imagines a composer hears -- not as a hallucination, not as sound produced from silence. He heard it the way a man reading a letter hears the voice of the person who wrote it: internally, structurally, as pattern rather than sensation. The climbing arpeggios. The bow crossing and recrossing the strings. The visceral intensity of the bariolage-like passages that he had conceived during the winter months and that, when played, produced an effect he could describe only as *dringend* -- urgent, pressing, the sound of a voice that could not stop speaking because what it had to say exceeded the capacity of its instrument and would be lost if it paused for breath.

He reached bar one hundred and thirty-three.

The key changed. On the page it was a simple matter: the cancellation of the B-flat, the introduction of the B-natural, the shift of the tonal centre from D minor to D major. Three centuries of music theory would describe this modulation in terms of its harmonic function, its structural placement, its relationship to the preceding material. None of this captured what the modulation was. What the modulation was, in the simplest terms Bach would have understood, was a door opening. A passage from one room to another. The room behind the door was filled with light.

He wrote the D major section. The hymn-like melody, simple and singing, sustained above the bass in long notes that breathed with the patience of plainchant. The block chords that rang with a fullness the minor section had denied. He wrote this with the same steady hand, the same even spacing, the same care. The ink did not change. The quill did not hesitate. The manuscript recorded no pause, no alteration of pressure, no physical evidence that the man writing these notes was aware of what they contained.

Whether he was aware is a question the manuscript does not answer and history cannot resolve. The music was composed during a period that included the death of his wife. The fair copy was dated the year of her death. The D major section possesses a luminosity that three centuries of listeners have heard as transcendence, as grief transformed, as the sound of a man who has passed through darkness and found light on the other side. The romantic reading is almost irresistible: the Chaconne as tombeau, as memorial, as love letter written in notes because words were insufficient.

But Bach did not compose autobiographically. His goal was not to expose the hidden and the personal but to replicate the empirical and the universal. The Chaconne was a chaconne -- a baroque dance form, a set of variations on a ground bass, a demonstration of compositional mastery. He had written chaconnes before. He would write music of equal or greater complexity after. He did not sit at this desk in a fever of grief and pour his broken heart onto the page. That was not how he worked. That was not how anyone in 1720 worked.

And yet.

And yet the Chaconne was unlike anything else he wrote. Longer, more emotionally extreme, more structurally ambitious than any other single movement in the solo violin works. The D major section occupies a proportion of the whole that corresponds to the golden ratio, a mathematical relationship that Renaissance architects had used and that the ear recognises without the mind's permission. Whether Bach calculated this or simply heard it -- whether the proportions were the product of design or of an ear so refined that its intuitions produced the same results as mathematics -- is another question the manuscript does not answer.

He wrote the bariolage. The obsessive, sawing passage near the end -- the bow alternating between a stopped note and an open string with metronomic intensity, one voice crying while the other droned, the effect raw and urgent and unlike anything in the preceding variations. The notes were small and dense on the page. He wrote them quickly, the quill scratching across the ruled staves, and the sound of the quill was the only sound in the room.

The final bars. The return to the opening theme -- D minor, the descending bass, the grave statement that had begun the movement now ending it, but changed, transfigured, carrying inside it the memory of the major and the cry of the bariolage and the fifteen minutes of music that lay between the first bar and the last. He wrote the final chord. He drew the double bar-line.

He put down the quill.

He sat in the cold study and looked at the page. The ink was still wet. The morning light, which had been creeping across the desk for hours, had reached the manuscript and lay across the final bars like a hand. Motes of dust turned slowly in the shaft of light. The house was quiet -- the children's voices, which had been audible earlier through the study door, had moved elsewhere, into the courtyard or the street, the sounds of a household that had learned to function around the closed door behind which their father worked.

He flexed his writing hand. The knuckles were thick, the fingers ink-stained, the nails cut short. These were not the hands of an artist as later centuries would imagine an artist's hands -- slender, pale, otherworldly. These were the hands of a working man. Hands that brewed beer, that tuned clavichords, that gripped a pen for ten hours at a stretch. Hands that had held a dying infant. Hands that had reached, three months ago, for a wife who was not there.

---

He took a fresh sheet. Ruled it. Drew the title page.

*Sei Solo.*
*a*
*Violino*
*senza*
*Basso*
*accompagnato.*

The words in his careful Italian hand, centred on the page. *Sei Solo* -- six solos. In Italian the phrase carried a second meaning, one that a man who spoke German and Latin as his primary languages may or may not have intended, may or may not have heard. *Sei Solo.* You are alone.

He wrote his name. He wrote the date: **1720.**

He blew on the ink. He reached for the sand-shaker -- a small brass vessel, perforated at the top -- and dusted the page. The fine sand settled into the wet ink, absorbing the excess moisture, fixing the letters. He tipped the sheet and the loose sand ran off into the tray and the title page was set.

The year sat on the page. 1720. The year his wife died. The year the Chaconne was finished. The two facts side by side, as they would sit side by side for three centuries, and the space between them was a space that no amount of scholarship or speculation or romantic projection could close. He may have felt the weight of the date. He may have simply noted it as a fact -- the year of completion, a cataloguing detail, the kind of information a conscientious craftsman records so that future users of the manuscript will know when the work was done. The manuscript does not say. The face in the Haussmann portrait, painted twenty-six years later, gives nothing away. The eyes are dark and steady. The mouth is closed.

---

He stood. He stretched -- the back stiff from sitting, the shoulder aching where it always ached from years of playing and writing and holding his body in positions that the body was not designed to sustain for decades. He gathered the manuscript -- the entire *Sei Solo*, all six works, the three sonatas and three partitas, the fair copy complete -- and placed it on the shelf beside his other finished scores. It sat between a set of cello suites he had recently completed and a sheaf of orchestral parts for the court ensemble. It was not given special treatment. It was not locked away or displayed or wrapped in protective cloth. It was a manuscript among manuscripts. The output of a working composer who had other work to do and who would, in the coming weeks, begin composing cantatas for a position in Leipzig that he had not yet been offered and did not yet know he would spend the rest of his life regretting accepting. The manuscript sat on the shelf and the October light fell across its edge and began the slow work of drying the ink that would survive the man who wrote it by three hundred years.

He left the study. In the hallway, he passed the hook by the kitchen door. A woman's shawl hung on it -- dark wool, plain, the kind of shawl a practical woman threw over her shoulders against the drafts that came through the leaded windows in autumn. No one had moved it. It had hung there since July, untouched, not because the household treated it as a shrine but because no one could face the act of taking it down. Taking it down would mean deciding what to do with it, and deciding what to do with it would mean she was not coming back to wear it.

Bach's eyes passed over the shawl. The novel will not say what he felt. The novel will only note that he paused, for the duration of a breath, and then continued into the kitchen, where the light was warmer and the air smelled of bread and the domestic sounds of his surviving world were audible through the walls.

Catharina Dorothea was at the table, kneading dough. Twelve years old, flour to her elbows, her sleeves rolled above the wrists, performing a task that had been her mother's and that she had assumed without being asked and without complaint. She was the substitute. She would be the substitute for seventeen more months, until Anna Magdalena arrived and took her place, and the seventeen months would mark her in ways that a twelve-year-old could not foresee and that the father standing in the doorway could not prevent. She looked up. Her face was her mother's face -- the wide-set eyes, the firm mouth, the composure that was not a child's composure but the composure of a person who has learned, too early, that the world does not pause for grief.

"I need flour," she said.

Bach nodded. "I will bring it from the market. I am going to the palace this afternoon -- the Prince requires music for a dinner."

"The bread will be ready by noon."

"Good."

He stood in the doorway of his kitchen and looked at his daughter kneading bread and he did not say the thing that the three centuries between his world and ours would want him to say -- did not tell her he was proud of her, did not tell her he understood what it cost her, did not cross the room and put his arms around her floury shoulders and weep. He was not that kind of man. He was a man who solved problems, and the problem of flour could be solved, and the problem of grief could not, and so he would solve the one and endure the other, and the enduring would not look like enduring. It would look like a man going about his business. It would look like a father asking about bread and leaving for the palace and returning with flour and eating supper and going to bed and rising the next morning to begin the next day's work, because the work did not stop, because the work was the thing that held the world together when everything else had come apart.

He left the kitchen. He gathered his coat and his hat. He went out into the October morning, into the streets of Köthen with their smell of wood-smoke and horse dung and the first chill of winter in the air. Behind him, in the house, the bread was rising. In the study, on a shelf between the cello suites and the orchestral parts, a manuscript was drying in the light. The ink settling into the paper. The notes fixed. The date -- 1720 -- fixed.

He did not know what he had written. He knew it was the last movement of the second partita, that it was two hundred and fifty-six bars long, that it was a chaconne in D minor with a central section in D major, that it was technically demanding and musically complex and that it completed a set of six solo violin works that he considered among the best things he had done. He did not know that it was the greatest single movement ever written for a solo instrument. He did not know that it would be played in concert halls that had not yet been built, in cities that did not yet exist, by performers who would spend their lives learning to play it and would never feel they had fully succeeded. He did not know that a man in a country that did not yet bear its modern name would, three hundred and six years later, stand on a terrace overlooking an ocean he had never heard of and play this music for the first and last time in front of people who had never heard him play, and that the playing would be imperfect and true, and that it would matter.

He knew that he needed to buy flour. He knew that the Prince expected him at two. He knew that his children were fed and that the bread was rising and that the manuscript was finished and that his wife was dead and that the world, for all its reconfiguration, continued.

He walked into the morning. The door of the house closed behind him. The shawl hung on its hook. The bread rose. The ink dried.

The Chaconne waited on its shelf for the world to find it.
