# Chapter 18: Recordings

The headphones were Jeannie's -- Bose, noise-cancelling, bought for a flight to Bali three years ago and abandoned in a drawer because she preferred to sleep on planes rather than listen to anything. Bob had found them while looking for a phone charger. They fit too tightly across the crown of his head and pressed against his temples with a low, constant ache that he would later be unable to separate from the music itself, as though Bach required a certain amount of pain as the price of admission.

It was past midnight. The house on Marine Parade was asleep -- Jeannie in her room with the door closed, Chase somewhere out, Shanel's light off since ten. Bob sat at the desk in the study, which was not really a study anymore but a room where Jeannie stored overflow clothes and Bob kept the computer he used for fund administration and, now, for the only work that mattered. The desk lamp was off. The screen was the only light, its blue glow turning his face into something hollowed and strange, like a man seen underwater. YouTube was open. The search bar read: "Bach Chaconne Milstein 1972."

He pressed play. Not for the first time. Not for the fifth. He had been listening to the Chaconne for eleven days, since the afternoon in Katarina's studio when she had played the opening eight bars and the floor had dropped out from under his life. Eleven days of the same piece, the same recordings, the same fourteen minutes repeating like a loop in his skull, and each time he heard something the previous listening had hidden, as though the music were a building with rooms he had not yet entered, corridors he had not yet walked, and each repetition opened another door.

He had a notebook. A ruled Moleskine he'd bought at the newsagent in Claremont, the kind of thing he had never owned and had no use for in his professional life, where everything was digital, trackable, deletable. In this notebook he wrote in handwriting that had not improved since school:

*2:14 -- chord change, Milstein waits before resolving. Why? The silence before the note is part of the note.*

*3:47 -- bass voice disappears for four bars, then returns. You don't notice it's gone until it comes back. Like breathing.*

*5:22 -- the run in sixteenths. Hahn plays it like a river. Milstein plays it like a man walking through a river. Different thing entirely.*

*9:12 -- THE SHIFT. D major. Bar 133. Every recording, every time, this is the moment. Like stepping from a dark room into sunlight. Milstein plays it quieter than anyone else. This is wrong, it should be louder, but it is right. The light doesn't shout. The light just arrives.*

He was on his seventh recording now. He had started with Milstein because Katarina told him to, and Milstein had broken him open -- the tone autumnal, philosophical, the playing of an old man who had lived with this piece for fifty years and no longer needed to prove anything, who played it the way you tell a story you have told a thousand times, not for the listener but for the story itself, because the story needs to be told. Then Hilary Hahn, and the shock of her: seventeen years old on her debut album, technically flawless, the notes struck with a clarity that was almost violent, as though she were cutting the music into the air with a scalpel. Where Milstein was warm, Hahn was luminous. Where Milstein breathed, Hahn burned.

Then Perlman: the tone like honey poured over wood -- gorgeous, generous, almost too beautiful, so beautiful that Bob found himself listening to the sound rather than the architecture, and he wrote in the notebook: *Perlman makes you love the violin. Milstein makes you love the music. Not the same thing.* Then Heifetz: blazing, relentless, technically terrifying, the Chaconne played the way a general took a city, with total commitment and no mercy. Then Szeryng: the middle ground, balanced, humane.

He turned the page. He wrote: *What am I hearing? Not the notes. The person. Each recording is the same 256 bars and each one is a different life.*

But the listening had opened into something else. The notebook had begun to fill not just with observations about the performances but with facts, dates, names -- the debris of a man who had discovered Google and a subject that consumed him with the same totality that the fund had once consumed his working hours, except that this consumption was not a diminishment but an enlargement, a feeling of growing room inside him where there had previously been only the dense, compressed mass of things he did not want to know.

---

Brahms.

He found the letter on a Wikipedia page and then traced it to its source -- a collection of Brahms's correspondence with Clara Schumann, translated, posted on a university website in Illinois that Bob navigated with the fumbling persistence of a man who had never voluntarily visited an academic site in his life. The letter was dated June 1877. Brahms had been sent a manuscript of the Chaconne -- a piano transcription, arranged for the left hand alone by a mutual friend -- and he had written to Clara:

*On one stave, for a small instrument, the man writes a whole world of the deepest thoughts and most powerful feelings. If I imagined that I could have created, even conceived the piece, I am quite certain that the excess of excitement and earth-shattering experience would have driven me out of my mind.*

Bob read the passage three times. He read it a fourth time, slowly, word by word, as though the sentence were a lock and each word a tumbler falling into place. Then he copied it in his handwriting onto a Post-it note and stuck it to the edge of his computer monitor, where it sat between the flashing notification icon for fourteen unread fund emails and a yellow Post-it from the associate reading "ASIC -- please call." He did not call. He looked at the Brahms quote. *A whole world of the deepest thoughts and most powerful feelings.* Brahms -- who had written four symphonies, the German Requiem, the violin concerto, hundreds of pieces -- said the Chaconne would have driven him out of his mind. Brahms was not given to hyperbole. He was a burly, irascible man who lived alone in Vienna and expressed emotion reluctantly and through music. If Brahms said the piece was earth-shattering, it was because the earth had shattered.

Bob sat in his office on St George's Terrace, the Bluetooth speaker playing the Chaconne at low volume while the fund's voicemail counter climbed from fourteen to sixteen, and he felt something he had never felt in this room: the presence of a conversation that mattered. Brahms talking to Clara about Bach. A triangle of minds, separated by centuries, connected by the same 256 bars. And now Bob, sitting in the fourth point, the unlikely point, the stockbroker in Perth who had no business being in this conversation but who was in it because the music had reached him and he could not pretend it hadn't.

---

He discovered the chaconne as a form. Not the piece -- the form. He fell into the Wikipedia article on the chaconne and then into the citations, and then into a book called *Dance and the Music of J.S. Bach* that he ordered from Amazon and that arrived in two days and that he read at his desk while the fund's voicemail filled up.

The chaconne began in the Americas. In the Spanish colonies of New Spain -- Mexico, Peru, the Caribbean -- sometime in the late sixteenth century. It was a dance. Not a stately, courtly dance but a vulgar one -- fast, physical, the dancers moving their hips in ways that scandalised the colonial authorities. The word itself was disputed: some scholars linked it to a Basque word, *chocuna*; others to an Aztec word, *chacona*. What was not disputed was that the dance was sexual, popular, and banned -- repeatedly banned by the Inquisition, which was always a reliable indicator of popularity. Bob read about the chaconne's journey from the taverns of Mexico City to the courts of Spain to the dance suites of Italy and France, and he thought: a dirty dance from a colonised country becomes the vehicle for the greatest piece of music ever written. The low becomes the high. The body becomes the spirit. The thing that was banned becomes the thing that is sacred.

He did not examine why this resonated. He did not need to. A stockbroker's fraud becomes a musician's devotion. The thing that was hidden becomes the thing that is real. The parallel was there, and he felt it in his chest the way he felt the D major shift, and he did not articulate it because articulation would have made it smaller.

He read about the technical structure. Sixty-four variations built on a four-bar ground bass -- a repeating harmonic pattern, the same chord progression cycling through the entire piece like a heartbeat. The constraint below and the infinite freedom above. Each variation obeyed the same harmonic structure -- D minor, descending bass, four bars -- but what each variation did within that structure was entirely free. The piece was a prison and a universe simultaneously. He bought a score from Henle Verlag, printed on heavy cream paper, and he spread it open on his desk and began to mark it up. Red pencil for the first D minor section, bars 1-132. Blue for D major, bars 133-208. Red again for the return, bars 209-256. He numbered each variation in the margin. He circled the structural joints -- the places where the four-bar cycle restarted and the melody crossed the joint and kept singing. The score began to look like a map of a country he was planning to invade, the red and blue territories marked, the borders identified, the terrain assessed. He did not yet know how to read music with any fluency beyond the simple. He was learning. The Chaconne was teaching him.

---

Maria Barbara.

He found the story in a biography -- *Bach: Music in the Castle of Heaven* by John Eliot Gardiner, a book he would not have been able to name as literature but that he read in three days with the avidity of a man reading a thriller, because the life it described was a life that made sense to him in ways his own never had. Bach at Kothen, 1717-1723. The happiest years. Prince Leopold loved music. The ensemble was excellent. The salary was good. And Maria Barbara was there -- Bach's first wife, the mother of his seven children, four of whom survived. They had married in 1707. She sang. She played keyboard. She was, by all accounts that history has bothered to preserve (which are few, because history does not preserve the accounts of women who stayed home and raised children and sang in the parlour and died), a musician.

In the summer of 1720, Bach travelled with Prince Leopold to the spa at Carlsbad. He was gone for several weeks. The ensemble played. The prince bathed. Bach composed, or revised, or simply played -- the historical record is sparse. What the record shows, with the brute clarity of a parish register, is that Maria Barbara died on July 7, 1720, and was buried the same day or the next. When Bach returned to Kothen, his wife was in the ground. His four surviving children -- the eldest, Catharina Dorothea, was twelve -- had been cared for by servants.

Bob read this and stopped. He pushed the book away from him on the desk. He stared at the wall -- the wall of his office on St George's Terrace, the wall with the Bloomberg terminal and the telephone and the framed photograph of Jeannie and the children at Rottnest -- and he thought about the gap. The gap between the leaving and the knowing. Bach had left his wife alive and come home to find her dead. He had been somewhere else, playing music, living his life, and while he played, Maria Barbara had sickened and died and been lowered into the ground, and the world had not paused to inform him, and the children had stood by the grave without their father, and the house had gone quiet, and when Bach walked through the door -- the specific, physical moment of walking through the door, the threshold between not-knowing and knowing -- everything he thought he was standing on had been gone for days.

Bob thought about Jeannie. Not with love, exactly, or guilt, exactly, but with the sudden vertiginous awareness that you can live beside someone for twenty years and not know them, and they can die, and you can be somewhere else entirely, and the world will not pause to inform you. He thought about the separate bedrooms and the locked door and the sertraline and the sauvignon blanc and the twenty years of performing a marriage that was neither a marriage nor an ending but a holding pattern, a slow orbit around a dead star that neither of them had the courage to name. She could die. He could be at his desk, or in the soundproofed room, or in Katarina's studio, and Jeannie could die, and the gap between the living and the knowing would be the same gap, the same unbridgeable distance, the same door that opens onto emptiness.

He read the memorial theory. The Chaconne as tombeau for Maria Barbara -- a monument in sound, a grief-work composed in the months after her death. He read the evidence: the Partita No. 2 in D minor was almost certainly composed at Kothen, probably in 1720 or shortly after. The Chaconne was its final movement, its longest, its most profound -- a movement that exceeded the rest of the Partita in scope and ambition the way a cathedral exceeds the houses around it. He read Helga Thoene's theory: that the Chaconne encoded fragments of Lutheran funeral chorales in its counterpoint -- *Christ lag in Todesbanden*, *Jesu meine Freude* -- hymns that would have been sung at Maria Barbara's burial. He read the scholars who disagreed. He read the counter-arguments: Bach's generation did not compose autobiographically; the Chaconne's form was predetermined by its placement in a dance suite; there was no documentary evidence linking the piece to Maria Barbara's death.

He did not care. The scholarly question of whether the Chaconne was "about" Maria Barbara was irrelevant to Bob. What mattered was simpler. A man lost his wife. A man wrote this music. The two facts existed in the same life, and that was enough. The music did not need to be about the death to be shaped by it. A man who has buried his wife does not compose the same way he composed before. The grief enters the wood. It enters the horsehair and the rosin and the ink on the page and the shape of the note and the weight of the chord and the length of the silence between phrases. The grief enters everything because grief is not a feeling you have but a substance you carry, and it gets into everything you touch, the way smoke gets into clothes, the way salt gets into timber near the sea.

Bob closed the book. He sat at his desk and he did not look at the Brahms quote or the fund emails or the photographs of the violin he had taken the previous night in the soundproofed room, the raking light across the varnish, the scroll from four angles. He looked at nothing. He was thinking about Maria Barbara, whom he had never heard of until three days ago and who was now as real to him as Jeannie, as real as Katarina, as real as the children. Maria Barbara, dead in summer, buried without her husband. Bach, walking through the door. The sound of a house with a missing voice.

---

He was driving home on the Mitchell Freeway, the river visible through the gaps between the glass towers of South Perth, the sun low and the water turning from grey to copper, and he was thinking about Isaac Stern.

He had found the story on a website dedicated to the history of the Chaconne's performances. November 22, 1963. Dallas, Texas. Isaac Stern -- one of the great violinists of the twentieth century, a man whose tone was as recognisable as a speaking voice -- was in the city when Kennedy was shot. The next evening, in San Antonio, Stern stood before four thousand people. He did not play the scheduled programme. He said he would pray. He would pray by playing the Chaconne.

He wept through the performance. The audience did not know what they were hearing -- most of them, probably, had never heard the piece. What they heard was a man standing alone on a stage with a violin and making a sound that was commensurate with what had happened, a sound that met the unspeakable with the only language adequate to it. When it was over, Stern asked the audience not to applaud. They didn't. Four thousand people sat in silence.

Bob, merging onto the freeway at the Mill Point Road on-ramp, thought about that silence. Four thousand people, in a city near the city where the president had been murdered, sitting in silence after fourteen minutes of music played by a man who was weeping. The Chaconne as prayer. The Chaconne as the only adequate response to the thing that cannot be spoken. He thought about what it would take to stand on a stage, on that stage, on that evening, and play that piece. The courage. The recklessness. The understanding that music is not entertainment but testimony, that the violinist's job is not to play notes but to stand in the presence of something too large for words and translate it into sound.

And then Joshua Bell. January 12, 2007. Washington, D.C. The L'Enfant Plaza Metro station, morning rush hour. Bell -- one of the greatest living violinists, playing a Stradivarius worth three and a half million dollars -- set up as a street busker. He opened his case. He placed it on the ground. He played six pieces in forty-three minutes. The third piece was the Chaconne.

One thousand and ninety-seven people walked past. Seven stopped. He earned thirty-two dollars and seventeen cents.

The experiment had been designed by Gene Weingarten, a journalist at the Washington Post, and it won the Pulitzer Prize. Bob read the article on his phone in the car park at Cottesloe, having pulled off the freeway at the Stirling Highway exit and driven to the beach instead of home, and the article did something to him that he could not name. Not anger. Not sadness. Something else. Recognition. The world walks past the most beautiful thing it has ever heard because the most beautiful thing it has ever heard is not wearing the right clothes, is not in the right room, does not have the right frame around it. Bell in his jeans and baseball cap, playing a three-and-a-half-million-dollar Stradivarius, playing the greatest piece of music ever written for a solo instrument, and a thousand people walked past because they were late for work and because beauty without context is invisible.

Bob sat in the car on Marine Parade, looking at the ocean. The sun was low, the sky turning copper -- the Cottesloe sunset, the daily miracle Perth takes for granted, the light falling on the water the way Milstein's D major section fell on the minor, quiet and unhurried and offering nothing that the world was required to accept.

He thought: I am Joshua Bell in the subway. No -- I am worse. I am the thousand people who walk past. I have been walking past my own life for thirty years.

He sat in the car until it was dark. The ocean turned from copper to grey to black, and the Norfolk pines were silhouettes against the last light, and the evening star appeared above the horizon line where the sky met the water, and Bob Harrington, forty-seven years old, fund manager, fraud, father, husband, Grade 4 violinist, sat in his car and let the night come and did not go inside the house because inside the house was the life he had built and the life he had built was the subway platform and the people walking past and the music that nobody heard.

He would learn, in the weeks that followed, that the Chaconne was many things. A technical impossibility. A structural marvel. A theological statement. A grief-work. A prayer. A mirror. But what he would never forget -- what the recordings and the research and the late nights had deposited in him like sediment, like the minerals that settle into the grain of Alpine spruce over centuries and give it its voice -- was the feeling of hearing it for the first time, in a parked car on Rokeby Road, with cheap earbuds and the neon of a bottle shop and the evening dying around him, and knowing, with a certainty that preceded reason and survived every argument against it, that the sound was the sound of his life, and that the only response to it was to play.
