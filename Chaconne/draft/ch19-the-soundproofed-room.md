# Chapter 19: The Soundproofed Room

At seven-thirty on a Tuesday morning in May, Bob Harrington swiped his key card at the ground-floor entrance of 108 St George's Terrace, walked through the lobby past the security guard who nodded without interest, and took the lift to the fourth floor. The corridor was empty. It was always empty at this hour -- the accountancy firm on level three did not start until nine, and the recruitment agency at the far end had been vacant since February, its frosted glass door dark, a small drift of uncollected mail fanning across the carpet beneath the slot. Bob turned left at the fire-exit sign, unlocked the office, and stepped inside.

The office was two rooms divided by a wall he had paid a contractor to install eighteen months ago, citing the need for a private meeting space. The contractor, a sun-ruined man from Balcatta who chewed gum and did not ask questions, had also installed the acoustic foam -- fifty panels of dark grey polyurethane, glued in staggered rows to the walls and ceiling of the inner room, transforming it from a standard commercial space with a window into something that looked like the inside of a recording studio, or a padded cell, depending on your disposition. The foam absorbed reflections. It ate echoes. It turned the room into a sealed acoustic environment where sound went in and did not come out, and where the outside world -- the traffic on the Terrace, the construction crane on the next block, the phone that rang and rang on the other side of the wall -- could not enter.

Bob set his briefcase on the desk in the outer room. The desk held a Bloomberg terminal, dark. A telephone with a red message light blinking -- nine messages, he knew without checking, because there had been nine yesterday and he had not listened to them and by tomorrow there would be eleven. A stack of compliance documents in a manila folder. A framed photograph of Jeannie and the children at Rottnest that Jeannie had placed there six years ago and that Bob had not moved because moving it would require having a reason, and having a reason would require a conversation he did not want to have. He did not turn on the Bloomberg. He did not check the messages. He opened the inner door and stepped into the soundproofed room.

The transformation of this space had happened gradually, then all at once, in the way of things that are denied and then surrendered to. First the foam. Then the music stand -- a heavy Manhasset, not the flimsy wire kind, positioned at the centre of the room where the acoustic response was most even. Then the metronome, a Wittner mechanical that he wound each morning with a small key, preferring its wooden tick to the sterile pulse of a digital app. Then the mirror -- a full-length mirror propped against the far wall so he could watch his bowing arm, his posture, the angle of his left elbow, all the things Katarina corrected each week and that crept back into error between lessons like weeds reclaiming a garden. Then the notebook, open on the stand beside the score. Then the stretching mat on the floor, because his shoulders had begun to ache after the first hour. Then the ice pack in the small bar fridge he had moved from the outer office, because his forearm had begun to burn after the second.

He stood in the centre of the room. The acoustic foam swallowed the ambient sound of the building -- the air conditioning, the distant lift, the traffic four floors below -- and returned a silence so complete he could hear the blood in his ears. This was his room. Not the outer office with its Bloomberg terminal and its blinking phone and its photographs of a family arranged for public viewing. Not the house on Marine Parade with its ocean views and its separate bedrooms and its kitchen where Jeannie's wine glass left pale rings on the stone. This room. This sealed, foam-padded, hidden room inside a room inside a building on a street in a city that did not know it existed. The architectural expression of everything Bob had become. A space hidden inside a space. A secret inside a secret. The fund was a fraud concealed inside a legitimate business. The violin was a passion concealed inside a career. And somewhere inside the violin, if Franz was right -- if the scroll and the varnish and the wood were what they appeared to be -- was an instrument concealed inside an instrument, a history hiding beneath the surface of a provincial Austrian violin that had cost ten thousand euros and might be worth ten million. Layers within layers. Bob did not think this clearly. He felt it -- the way you feel the weight of water above you when you dive deep, the pressure increasing with each metre of descent, the silence deepening.

He opened the case. The violin lay in its blue velvet -- Franz's violin, the one from Absam, its varnish the colour of dark honey, the scroll carved with a precision that still stopped him each time he looked at it, the volute's spiral tightening into an eye that seemed to watch him as he lifted the instrument free. He rosined the bow. He tightened the hair -- a quarter-turn, the tension something his thumb had learned to measure without thought, the first of the body's small capitulations to the instrument. He placed the violin under his chin. The chinrest pressed the spot on his jaw that was now permanently marked -- a reddish-brown patch of roughened skin that Jeannie had not noticed, or had noticed and not mentioned, or had noticed and filed away with the other accumulating evidence that her husband was becoming someone she did not recognise.

He played a D minor scale. Slowly. Two octaves up, two octaves down. Each note placed with deliberate care, the bow drawing the sound from the string the way a brush draws paint from a palette -- not scraped, not forced, but invited. He listened. The E was slightly sharp. He adjusted. The B-flat had a thinness he did not like, a reedy quality in the upper half of the bow, and he corrected the pressure, adding weight from the index finger, feeling the string resist and then yield. The room absorbed every imperfection and returned nothing but the truth.

---

This was his real work now.

The daily practice had acquired the quality of ritual -- not in the vague, metaphorical sense but in the specific, liturgical sense of actions performed in sequence, at fixed times, with the understanding that the sequence itself was sacred. He arrived at seven-thirty. He changed into practice clothes he kept in the closet: trackpants, a T-shirt, flat shoes that did not elevate his heels and throw off his balance. He stretched -- forearms, shoulders, neck, the sequence Katarina had taught him, each stretch held for thirty seconds, the tendons loosening from the night's contraction. Then the open strings. Long, slow bows on each string, working from the G up to the E, listening for the quality of the contact -- the point where the hair bit the string and the string began to vibrate, the moment when friction became sound. He drew each bow the full length of the stick, from frog to tip and back, the stroke consuming four seconds each way, the arm moving with a deliberation that would have looked absurd to anyone watching and that was, Katarina had told him, the foundation of everything.

"Tone production is not in the left hand," she had said, in one of the early lessons. "The left hand chooses the note. The right hand chooses the sound. Your right arm is the voice. The left hand is the brain. And the voice matters more than the brain, because people hear the voice first, and if the voice is ugly, they stop listening before the brain has anything to say."

After the open strings: scales. D minor, three octaves, slowly, the metronome clicking at sixty beats per minute, each note a full beat. He focused on the upper positions -- fifth, sixth, seventh -- where his hand still cramped and the intonation wavered, the notes spread further apart on the fingerboard and his fingers struggling to find them without the anchoring reference of the open strings below. The seventh position was where the Chaconne climbed in its climactic passages, and each time Bob reached for the high A his hand protested, the tendons of the forearm drawing tight like cables, the fingers splaying across the fingerboard in shapes they had never been asked to hold.

Then double-stop exercises. Thirds first -- two notes a third apart, played simultaneously, the bow resting on two strings, the left hand holding two positions at once. His thirds were still uneven, the intervals occasionally too wide or too narrow, producing a sour quality that made him wince. Sixths next -- harder, the hand spread wider. Then octaves -- the first and fourth fingers stretched to the maximum, the interval pure, unforgiving, every error audible because the octave was the simplest consonance in music and any deviation from it sounded not like a mistake but like a lie. He thought about that sometimes: the octave as truth-teller. Two notes, one vibrating exactly twice as fast as the other, and if the ratio was not exact, the ear knew. The ear always knew. You could deceive a client, a spouse, a regulator. You could not deceive an octave.

After an hour of technical work: the Chaconne itself. Bar by bar. He was working through the first section -- bars 1 through 32, the theme and the first eight variations. His method was Katarina's method, which was Kovar's method, which was the method of every serious musician since the invention of notation: play a four-bar phrase at half tempo with the metronome. Identify the worst moment -- a missed shift, a chord that crunched, a bow change that bumped the line. Isolate it. Extract the two notes on either side of the failure. Play those two notes twenty times. Thirty times. Fifty times. The repetition was not mindless. Each repetition was a question: what went wrong? The answer was always specific, always physical. The third finger landed a millimetre short. The bow pressure increased at the string crossing. The wrist stiffened at the shift. Find the error. Feel the error. Correct the error. Put the phrase back together. Move to the next.

The physical reality of this work was something no one who had not done it could understand. His left fingertips were calloused now -- hard yellow pads that had replaced the soft skin of a stockbroker's hands, the skin that had handled nothing rougher than a telephone receiver and a fountain pen for thirty years. The calluses cracked in the dry office air. He applied superglue to the cracks, a trick Katarina had taught him -- a drop of medical-grade cyanoacrylate on the split, pinched shut for thirty seconds, the glue forming a transparent seal that held the callus together and allowed him to keep playing. His neck had developed the fiddler's mark -- the reddish-brown discoloration on the left jaw where the chinrest pressed, a permanent brand that marked him as a player the way a writer's callus marks a pen-holder, the body's testimony of devotion. His left shoulder ached constantly, a deep, dull ache in the rotator cuff from hours of holding the arm elevated, and he iced it after practice and took ibuprofen before, and the ache did not diminish but it became familiar, a companion, a tax the work charged.

For the first three hours the playing was effortful and frequently ugly. The chords crunched. The shifts landed flat or sharp. The bariolage passages -- which he was attempting even though Katarina said he was not ready -- dissolved into chaotic string-crossings, the bow catching two strings when it should have caught one, the alternation that should have shimmered instead juddering like a machine with a faulty gear. But in the fourth hour, something happened. Something that happened to every musician who practised with this intensity and that Katarina had told him about but that he had not believed until he experienced it: the conscious mind stepped back and the body took over. A passage that had been laboured suddenly flowed. A chord that had been fought suddenly rang. The fingers found their positions without being directed. The bow arm moved without instruction. The music, for bars at a time -- four bars, eight bars, never more than twelve -- played itself through him, the way water flows through a pipe, the way breath flows through a singer, not created but released, not performed but permitted.

These moments lasted seconds. They were the reason Bob practised four hours a day, five hours, six hours. They were the reason his forearm burned and his shoulder ached and his fingertips bled through the superglue. They were a drug more powerful than anything the fund had ever given him -- the money, the status, the adrenaline of a rising market -- because what the drug delivered was not pleasure but truth, the brief, intoxicating experience of being exactly where he was supposed to be, doing exactly what he was supposed to do, the gap between his ear and his hands closing for a few bars and the music emerging clean and whole and his.

---

Katarina arrived on Thursdays at four.

She had been coming to the office for months now, the arrangement Bob had insisted on from the beginning -- his studio, not hers. She carried her own violin in a worn case, a good Czech instrument from her student days, its varnish darker than Franz's violin, its tone warmer in the lower register and less brilliant at the top. She wore her usual uniform: dark jeans, a blouse, flat shoes. She was always exactly on time, which was, Bob had learned, not punctuality but precision -- the same quality she brought to her intonation, her phrasing, her assessment of his playing. Everything Katarina did had the quality of a note placed exactly where it belonged.

She entered the soundproofed room and stood by the music stand and said, as she said every week: "Show me."

Bob played bars 1 through 32 of the Chaconne at roughly sixty per cent of performance tempo. The sound was recognisable now. The chords were mostly clean, the shifts mostly accurate, the bass voice mostly audible beneath the upper lines. But "mostly" was the operative word. Every passage had at least one moment where the technique failed -- a shift that landed a quarter-tone flat, a chord that lost its bottom voice, a bow change that interrupted the legato line. He played through the section without stopping, the way she had taught him -- do not stop for errors, play through them, feel them, let them register in the body so the body knows what to correct -- and when the last chord of bar 32 died into the acoustic foam, he lowered the violin and waited.

Katarina did not speak. She stood by the window -- the window with the acoustic curtain that blocked most of the eleven-thousand-dollar view of the river -- and her eyes were closed, her head tilted slightly, the posture of a musician listening with her whole body, her nervous system still processing the sound the way a palate processes a wine after the glass is empty. When she opened her eyes, she picked up her own violin and played the same passage.

The difference was devastating. Katarina's Chaconne had the technical polish of a conservatory-trained professional: the chords were voiced with each note audible, the shifts were invisible, the bow changes seamless. But more than technique -- her phrasing revealed the polyphonic architecture. Where Bob played notes, Katarina played voices. The bass line sang independently of the soprano. The tenor voice, buried in the middle of the chords, emerged when it should and receded when it should. The music was three-dimensional.

She played the passage once, then set down her violin. "You're playing the right notes," she said. "Now I need you to hear the wrong ones."

Bob looked at her.

"In a four-voice passage on a single-string instrument, the voices that are not sounding are as important as the ones that are. The violinist must imply the absent voices -- through emphasis, through timing, through the colour of the tone. Bach wrote four voices. You can play two at a time. The other two must live in the silence between the notes." She picked up her violin and played the opening chord, slowly, the roll from bass to treble taking a full two beats. "Listen. The D in the bass -- that is the foundation. The A above it -- that is the fifth, the architecture. The upper D -- the octave, the reinforcement. And the F-sharp at the top -- the minor third. The voice that makes the chord sad. Now: when the bow rolls from the lower pair to the upper pair, the D and the A die. They stop sounding. But the ear must still hear them. The chord must linger in the air even after the bow has left. You are playing a melody. Bach wrote a conversation."

Bob absorbed this. He tried again, voicing the opening chord so the bass D rang longer, the bow lingering on the lower strings for an extra fraction of a beat before the roll, letting the foundation establish itself before the soprano entered. Katarina nodded. "Again." He played it again. "Again." Twenty minutes on four bars. This was how lessons worked.

---

The score had been sitting on the music stand for three weeks, open to the first page of the Chaconne. Katarina had placed it there at the end of their last lesson, aligning it carefully, then stepping back and looking at it the way you look at a landscape you are about to enter.

"Not yet," she had said. "Next time."

Bob had not touched the page. He had practised around it -- scales, etudes, the double-stop studies that Katarina had prescribed to strengthen his chord voicing. But each morning, when he entered the soundproofed room and turned on the music stand light, the Chaconne's first page caught the lamp's edge and the four-note chord at the beginning of bar one stared back at him -- four black noteheads stacked vertically, connected by a single stem, an impossible instruction written on a single beat.

D on the G string. A on the D string. D on the A string. F-sharp on the E string.

Four notes. One bow. And the physical fact that a violin bow could only contact two adjacent strings simultaneously. The bridge was curved. The strings were arrayed in an arc. You could press two neighbouring strings at once, but not three, and certainly not four. The chord was a fiction -- a notation that looked like a single vertical event but could only be realised as a horizontal one, a roll, the bow sweeping from the lower pair of strings to the upper pair in a motion that was part pressure, part rotation, part the controlled falling of the arm's weight across the instrument's register from bass to treble. The bottom two notes sounded first. Then the bow rolled, releasing the lower strings and catching the upper two. If the roll was smooth, the ear heard a single chord arriving in stages. If the roll was not smooth, the ear heard a scrape, a crunch, a mechanical failure.

Today was the day.

Katarina arrived at four. She walked into the soundproofed room with the brisk economy of a woman who had work to do, stood by the music stand, and looked at the score. Then she looked at Bob.

"Have you tried it?"

"No. You said not yet."

The corner of her mouth moved -- the ghost of a smile, the private amusement of a teacher encountering obedience she had not expected. "Good. Show me your chord exercises."

He played the exercises she had assigned. Three-note chords on the lower strings, rolling the bow from the bottom pair to the middle pair. The motion was becoming familiar: the slight rotation of the forearm, the arm weight shifting, the moment of transition where all three strings vibrated simultaneously for a fraction of a second before the bow settled. His chords were cleaner than they had been. The roll was audible but controlled -- a sweep rather than a lurch.

Katarina listened with her eyes closed, her head tilted. "Better. But you're still gripping at the transition." She played the same chord on her own violin. Seamless. The three notes arrived as a single event. "The secret is in the index finger. At the moment of the roll, your index finger presses into the stick -- a reflex. But the press stiffens the bow, and the stiffness catches the string. At the moment of the roll, release the index finger. Let the weight fall through the arm."

He tried. The chord crunched. The reflex overrode the instruction.

"Again."

Better -- the transition smoother, but the bottom notes died too quickly, the bass evaporating before the soprano established itself.

"You're rushing the roll. The bottom notes need time to speak. Stay on them. The audience needs to hear the foundation before they hear the house."

He slowed the roll. This time the bottom D and A rang for a full beat, and the transition, when it came, was gentler -- not seamless, but recognisable. Three notes the ear could, with generosity, assemble into a chord.

"Good." She placed both hands on the music stand, on either side of the open score. "Try it."

Bob looked at the page. The four-note chord. D-A-D-F-sharp. He knew exactly what it should sound like. He had listened to Milstein's version and Szeryng's and Hilary Hahn's until the sound of the opening chord had become as familiar as a phrase of speech. He knew the weight of it -- the bass D anchoring the chord to the earth, the open A adding the fifth, the octave D reinforcing the root, and the F-sharp at the top, the minor third, the note that made the chord sad. He could hear it. The problem was not his ear. It had never been his ear.

He placed his fingers. The left hand stretched across the strings -- first finger on D, fourth finger reaching for F-sharp. The stretch was at the limit of his hand span. The fourth finger trembled, the tendon straining, the fingertip pressing the steel at an angle slightly too far toward the nail. He could feel the F-sharp sitting a hair below where it needed to be -- flat, the eternal problem with the fourth finger, the weakest digit.

He placed the bow on the G string, resting against the D string. He drew.

The sound was nothing. A scrape, a whisper, the ghost of a note strangled at birth. The bow had touched the strings too lightly, producing a breathy non-sound that was neither note nor noise.

"More weight. You're afraid of it. Don't be."

He reset. Drew again, pressing in. Too much. The tone cracked -- harsh, forced, the strings vibrating irregularly under excessive pressure.

"Between those two. There's a place between not enough and too much. That's where the sound lives."

He breathed. He placed the bow. This time he felt for the balance point. He drew. The two lower strings spoke -- D and A, the open strings, the simplest sound the violin could make. Rough but present. They rang together, the fifth interval open and resonant.

Now the roll.

He let the arm weight shift. The bow tilted, releasing the G string, and then -- the transition -- the bow moved to the upper pair. A string and E string. The stopped notes. D under the first finger. F-sharp under the fourth.

The F-sharp cracked. His fourth finger, trembling, had shifted a millimetre, and the pitch dropped into the gap between F-sharp and F-natural, a microtonal no-man's-land that sounded not sad but sour. The bow caught the E string at an angle that produced a scratch -- the horsehair engaging unevenly, the sound splintering into two competing frequencies before collapsing into noise.

Bob stopped. He lowered the violin.

"Again," Katarina said.

He raised the instrument. The lower strings spoke. The roll. This time the F-sharp was closer, but the bow had lost contact with the A string during the roll and the middle D dropped out, leaving only the F-sharp thin and exposed, a soprano without her alto.

"You lost the D on the A string. The roll has to keep three strings vibrating at the transition. It's an overlap, not a switch. Watch." She played the chord slowly. Bob watched her bow arm -- a controlled rotation of the forearm, the bow tilting across the strings in an arc. At the transition, the A string was the hinge -- the bow resting on it continuously while the G string was released below and the E string engaged above. The A string sang through the entire chord. "The A is the bridge. It connects the bass to the treble. Keep the A."

He tried again. He focused on the A string. The roll was glacial -- nearly two full beats instead of the half-beat performance demanded. But the A held. The octave D rang through the transition. And the F-sharp, when the bow settled onto the E string, was -- almost. Not clean. Not true. The pitch a hair flat, the tone thin. But the note was there. It occupied its place at the top of the chord and said, however imperfectly: minor. This is D minor.

Bob played the chord again. And again. And again.

The room held the repetitions the way a well holds water -- each iteration sinking into the acoustic foam, absorbed, making space for the next. Katarina did not speak. She corrected when corrections were needed -- "Lighter at the top," "Your fourth finger is collapsing, arch it," "Breathe" -- but mostly she watched, because the corrections were becoming fewer, and the chord was becoming something.

Not beautiful. It was a chord the way a child's first word is a word -- barely recognisable, malformed, thrilling.

On perhaps the thirtieth attempt, something happened. The bow found its weight. The lower strings spoke. The roll began, and the A string held, and the F-sharp landed at the correct pitch, and for half a second -- maybe less, a duration too brief to be measured and too significant to be forgotten -- all four notes were present in the room simultaneously. Not as a clean chord. But as a cluster of related sounds that the ear could assemble into the object Bach had written: D minor. Root, fifth, octave, minor third. The foundation of the Chaconne.

"There," Katarina said. Her voice was quiet. "That was it."

He played it again. It did not work -- the fourth finger slipped. Again. Better, but the bass notes were swallowed. Again. The A string lost contact. Again. Again. The chord rang -- rough, imperfect, alive.

Katarina looked at her watch. They had been working on the opening chord for forty-five minutes. One bar. Four beats.

"Enough for today."

He lowered the violin. His left hand was trembling, the fourth finger throbbing, the tendons of the forearm hot. His right shoulder ached from an hour of continuous repetition.

"Practice the chord. Twenty minutes a day on the chord alone. No more -- your fourth finger needs to build strength gradually. If the tendon aches, stop."

She packed her violin. She paused at the inner door. She looked at the acoustic foam, the mirror, the scores spread across the desk, the ibuprofen bottle beside the music stand, the tube of superglue.

"Bob. I had a student in Brno -- ten years old, preparing for her first recital. A Vivaldi concerto. The opening required a chord. She spent two weeks on that chord. Her mother called me: she's playing the same three notes for an hour every day, is something wrong? I said: nothing is wrong. She is learning what a chord is. She is learning that music is not notes played in sequence but notes played in relationship."

She shifted the case to her other hand.

"You are learning what the Chaconne is. The chord will teach you."

She paused again, and something shifted in her expression -- not the teacher's face but the musician's, the face of a woman who had carried her own version of the gap between ambition and reality for twenty years.

"You know," she said, "in twenty years of teaching, I have never had a student who practises like you. I've had students with more talent. I've never had one with more --" She paused, looking for the English word, her eyes narrowing in the way they did when Czech and English failed to align and she was reaching into the space between them for something precise. "Need."

She left. The soundproofed room was perfectly quiet. The foam absorbed everything -- the building's air conditioning, the distant traffic, the sound of his own breathing. In this room, when nothing was playing, the silence was so complete that he could hear the blood in his ears.

---

He should go home. It was after five-thirty. He should return the calls blinking on the office phone. He should open the compliance folder. He should do any of the things that the other Bob Harrington -- the fund manager, the husband, the man whose name was on the door in brass letters -- was supposed to do.

He picked up the violin.

He played the chord.

D, A, D, F-sharp. The roll from bass to treble. It was not clean. The F-sharp was a hair flat. The roll was too slow. The bass notes died before the treble notes sang.

Again. Better. The F-sharp found its pitch. The A string sustained. The chord hung in the foam-deadened air for a moment before the room swallowed it.

Again. The same four notes. The same gesture of the bow. But this time the notes cohered. They did not merely coexist. They spoke to each other. The bass D grounded the chord, the open A added its resonance, the octave D reinforced the root, and the F-sharp sat at the top of the structure and said: I am the reason this chord is sad. I am the distance between major and minor. I am the half-step that turns a house into a ruin.

He played the opening chord of the Chaconne ten times. Twenty. Each iteration slightly different, the quality fluctuating the way a radio signal fluctuates through terrain -- the music coming in clearly for a moment, dissolving into static, returning. But the chord was there. He was playing the first bar of the piece that Brahms had called a whole world, the piece that professional soloists prepared for months, the piece that his teacher had laughed at when he named it and then agreed to teach because she had seen in his face a need that could not be redirected.

He thought of Brahms. *On one stave, for a small instrument, the man writes a whole world of the deepest thoughts and most powerful feelings.* He was at the bottom of that world. He could see the wall above him -- the 256 bars, the sixty-four variations, the fifteen minutes of unaccompanied music that climbed from this chord through every human emotion and returned to it, transformed. He could not see the top.

But he could see a handhold. The first bar. The four notes. The chord that his hands had learned in one afternoon to almost produce. And he could feel -- in his aching fourth finger, in his burning shoulder, in the red mark on his jaw, in the particular quality of silence that followed each attempt -- that the wall was not smooth. It had texture. It had places where a hand could grip and a foot could find purchase and a body could press itself against the stone and climb, slowly, one bar at a time, one chord at a time, toward something he could not yet see but could hear, distantly, the way you hear the ocean before you see it.

He put the violin in its case. He turned off the light. He stood for a moment in the dark room and felt the vibration still in his fingers and his jaw and his chest, the ghost of the chord, the memory of the sound held not in his mind but in his body, in the muscles and tendons and bones that had spent an afternoon learning a new shape and would spend the night consolidating it, so that tomorrow the chord would be a fraction easier, a fraction closer to the sound he heard when he closed his eyes and listened to the Chaconne playing itself in the concert hall of his imagination, where the acoustics were perfect and the intonation was true and the music said everything he could not say and never would, except with this instrument, in this room, alone.

He walked into the outer office. The fund side. The blinking phone. The compliance folder. He changed back into his work clothes -- the pressed shirt, the chinos, the RM Williams boots. He became the other Bob, the way an actor becomes himself again after the curtain falls, the costume shed, the role released but not forgotten, never forgotten, because the role was the truth and the costume was the lie and the transition between them was the most exhausting thing in his life.

He sat at the desk. He signed three documents the associate had left for him. He returned one phone call -- a longstanding investor, friendly, just checking in. Bob was smooth, reassuring. Returns were strong. The market was favourable. No need to worry. The lie came easily because he had told it a thousand times, and telling it was the thing his mouth did while his mind was elsewhere, in the soundproofed room, in the chord, in the vibration that still hummed in his fingertips like the afterimage of a bright light.

He did not return the other calls. There were nine messages. Three were from investors requesting meetings. One was from the fund's external accountant with a question about a discrepancy in the September quarterly. One was from a compliance officer at ASIC with a "routine inquiry" -- the words printed on the message slip in the associate's careful handwriting, the word "routine" underlined, which meant the associate did not believe it was routine and was leaving the decision to Bob, the way the associate had been leaving decisions to Bob for years, the quiet delegation of a man who had stopped asking questions because the answers might require him to act.

Bob looked at the ASIC message for a long time. He looked at the number on the slip. He looked at the phone. He did not pick it up. He put the message face-down on the desk and stared at the wall, where the edge of the acoustic foam was visible through the connecting door, the grey panels in their staggered rows, the room within the room, the secret within the secret.

He picked up the violin case. He locked the office. He took the lift down to the lobby, where the security guard was watching something on his phone and did not look up. He walked into the evening air of St George's Terrace, where the city smelled of exhaust and eucalyptus and the faint salt breath of the river, and he drove home to Marine Parade, where the house was lit and Jeannie's car was in the garage and the kitchen light spilled through the window onto the driveway like a stage light illuminating the entrance to a set.

In the car, he did not listen to the Chaconne. He listened to nothing. The silence after practice was its own kind of music -- the ghost of what he had been playing, echoing in his body, the chord still present in his fingertips, the D minor still humming in the bones of his jaw where the chinrest had pressed and pressed and pressed until the vibration became indistinguishable from the bone itself, until the music was not something he played but something he carried, the way the violin carried the memory of every note that had ever been drawn from its strings, the way Alpine spruce carried the memory of cold winters and short summers and the slow, dense growth that gave it its voice.

He sat in the driveway. The Indian Ocean was black behind the Norfolk pines. The evening star was visible above the horizon. The house waited, lit and occupied, full of people who did not know what he had done today, what he was becoming, what the sound of a single chord -- D, A, D, F-sharp, rough and imperfect and alive -- meant to a man who had spent thirty years in silence.

He went inside.
