## Chapter 16: "Cremona, 1715"

The hands woke before the man. They always did. In the dark of the bedroom above the workshop, Antonio Stradivari lay on his back and felt his fingers stir against the linen sheet — the knuckles swollen, the joints stiff with the particular cold of late autumn in the Po Valley, where the damp rose from the river and settled into the stone walls and from the stone walls into the bones of anyone old enough to have stopped resisting it. He was seventy-one. He had been making violins for fifty-three years. Every morning now began with this negotiation between his will and his hands, and every morning the hands won eventually, loosening by degrees, the index finger of the left hand uncurling from the permanent crook that decades of holding a chisel against maple had carved into the tendon. He waited. Beside him, Antonia breathed the slow, even breath of a woman twenty years younger who did not need to bargain with her body for the right to begin the day.

He rose without waking her. Down the stairs, the stone cold under his bare feet — a cold he had felt ten thousand times, and which no longer registered as discomfort but simply as the texture of descent, of the passage from sleep to work, from the horizontal life to the vertical one. The workshop occupied the ground floor of the house on Piazza San Domenico, the same room he had worked in since he was a young man copying the arching templates of his teacher Nicola Amati and thinking, with the quiet certainty that was his defining characteristic, that he could do better. He had done better. He had done better for half a century, and he was not finished.

He lit a candle. Then a second, placing it on the far side of the bench so the light fell across the work from two angles, the shadows cast by the instrument's curves doubling and shifting as the flames steadied. The smell of the workshop entered him the way a prayer enters a man who has prayed the same words for fifty years: not through attention but through the body, through the lungs and the skin. Spruce shavings. Hide glue, its faintly animal warmth. The mineral tang of the varnish ingredients in their stoneware jars along the back wall — the dragon's blood, the turpentine, the linseed oil, the colophony, and the others, the ones he did not name even to his sons, the ground coat that penetrated the wood and did something to its voice that he could hear but not explain and would never write down.

He put on the white linen apron. The white cap. He stood before the bench.

On it, clamped in a cradle of his own design — a simple thing, two curved blocks of walnut lined with felt, holding the body without touching the plates — was a violin in the late stages of construction. The top plate of Alpine spruce was fitted and glued, the bass bar set, the purfling inlaid in its narrow channel around the edge, three strips of wood — black, white, black — bent and laid with a precision that would survive three centuries of handling and still be identifiable as his. The back plate was maple, the flame figure running diagonally across the surface in a pattern that caught the candlelight and released it, caught it and released it, as though the wood were breathing. Today he would work on the scroll.

The scroll was the signature. Every maker's hand could be read in the volute's curve, in the depth of the fluting, in the tightness of the final turn — the way every man's character could be read in his handwriting, if you knew how to look. Stradivari's scrolls were deeper-cut than Amati's, more assertive, the chamfers sharper, the eye of the volute slightly larger. He had been carving them for fifty years. Each one was slightly different. Each one was unmistakably his. He did not think of this as artistry. The word would not have occurred to him, and if it had, he would have found it imprecise. He was a maker of instruments. His father had been a maker of instruments. The genius — if it was genius, and he would not have used that word either — was in the hands, in the accumulated knowledge of tools and materials and the ten thousand small decisions that separated a good violin from a great one, and a great one from whatever his instruments were, the ones that left his workshop and went into the world and never came back because no one who owned one would willingly let it go.

He picked up the gouge. He tested the edge against his thumb — a habit so old it preceded thought, the way breathing precedes thought. The steel was sharp. He turned the scroll toward the light, studied the line of the volute where he had stopped yesterday, and began to cut.

The maple parted without sound. The curl fell to the bench in a tight, pale spiral. And Antonio Stradivari, seventy-one years old, in the forty-first year of his workshop on Piazza San Domenico, in the city of Cremona, in the Duchy of Milan, in the early morning of a day in late autumn in the year seventeen hundred and fifteen, did the thing he had done every day of his adult life. He made a violin. He made it a little better than the last one. And then, when this one was finished, he would make the next.

## Chapter 17: "The Cliff Face"

The studio was above a Thai restaurant on Rokeby Road, and the stairwell always smelled of lemongrass and coconut milk, a combination that Bob would associate for the rest of his life with the sound of the violin. He climbed the stairs on a Thursday afternoon in March, six months into his lessons with Katarina, carrying the case in his left hand and a bottle of water in his right, and he was thinking about the Gigue — the passage in the second half where the key shifted and his third finger had to stretch for a note he could not yet reach cleanly — and he was not thinking about anything else. Not the fund. Not the fourteen messages on his phone from the associate, or the three from investors he had not returned, or the compliance document on his desk that needed a signature he kept forgetting to provide. For the forty minutes of the lesson, and the fifteen minutes before it when he warmed up in the car, and the twenty minutes after it when he sat in the car park and replayed what Katarina had told him, the fund did not exist. It was the only hour in his week when he was not afraid.

He opened the door. The studio was a single room with a north-facing window that let in the last of the afternoon light — a warm, golden light in summer that was now, in early autumn, beginning to soften and cool. A music stand. A straight-backed chair. A metronome on the windowsill that Katarina never used because, she said, a metronome teaches you to keep time and she wanted to teach him to keep music, which was not the same thing. The walls were bare except for a framed photograph of Janacek that Bob had learned not to ask about, and a small mirror she sometimes positioned so he could watch his bowing arm. The room smelled of rosin and old carpet and, faintly, of the lemongrass rising through the floor.

Katarina was sitting with her violin on her lap, which was unusual. Normally she stood when he arrived, greeted him, asked about his practice week — a ritual of transition, she called it, the bridge between the world outside and the world in this room. Today she was seated, her instrument across her thighs, her bow resting against the chair leg, and she was looking at him with an expression he could not read. Not the attentive, slightly amused expression of a teacher assessing a student. Something else. Something that had weight.

"Sit down," she said.

He sat. He had not opened his case. He held it on his knees like a man in a waiting room.

She lifted her violin. No preamble. No explanation. She placed it under her chin with the economy of a woman who had done this ten thousand times, and she drew the bow across the strings.

The sound that came out was not like anything he had heard in this room. He had heard her demonstrate passages — a phrase here, a correction there, always in service of his playing, always pedagogical. This was different. She was not demonstrating. She was playing.

Four notes. A chord. D, A, D, F-sharp, struck from the bottom and rolled upward, the bow breaking the chord because a violin cannot sustain four notes at once and must choose, must move, must let the lower voices die so the upper ones can sing. The chord filled the room the way a stone fills a pool — not gradually but all at once, displacing everything, the air, the silence, the lemongrass, the traffic on Rokeby Road, the entire ordinary world pushed to the walls and held there by the sound. Then the descent. The bass voice stepping down — D, C-sharp, B-flat, A — and above it the melody, the upper voice, beginning its long fall into the theme that would, Bob did not yet know, consume the next two years of his life and then end it.

She played eight bars. Two statements of the theme. Then she stopped.

The bow lifted from the string. The last note — a D, the tonic, home — hung in the room for a moment and then was gone, and the silence that followed was not the absence of sound but its ghost, the shape the music had made still pressing against the walls, still displacing the air.

Bob did not speak. His hands, resting on the violin case, were trembling.

"That," Katarina said, "is where we're going. Eventually."

She set her violin down. She looked at him. He understood later that she had chosen this moment deliberately — had waited six months, had assessed his progress, his seriousness, his capacity for what she was about to unleash — but in the moment he understood nothing except that the sound she had made was the sound he had been listening for his entire life without knowing it existed, and that he would do anything, sacrifice anything, to make it himself.

"What is it?" he said. His voice was wrong. Too quiet, too tight. As if the music had compressed something in his chest.

"The Chaconne. Bach. Partita Number Two in D minor. The last movement." She paused. "Fifteen minutes of unaccompanied solo violin. I've been working on it myself for years. I'm not ready to perform it." She looked at the window, at the last of the gold draining from the sky. "No one is ever really ready."

"Play it again," he said.

It was not a question.

She did not play it again. She told him to go home. She told him to find the Milstein recording — the 1972 one, she specified, not the earlier version — and to listen to it alone, at night, with headphones. Then they would talk.

He drove home. He did not remember the drive.

## Chapter 18: "Recordings"

The headphones were Jeannie's — Bose, noise-cancelling, bought for a flight to Bali three years ago and abandoned in a drawer because she preferred to sleep on planes rather than listen to anything. Bob had found them while looking for a phone charger. They fit too tightly across the crown of his head and pressed against his temples with a low, constant ache that he would later be unable to separate from the music itself, as though Bach required a certain amount of pain as the price of admission.

It was past midnight. The house on Marine Parade was asleep — Jeannie in her room with the door closed, Chase somewhere out, Shanel's light off since ten. Bob sat at the desk in the study, which was not really a study anymore but a room where Jeannie stored overflow clothes and Bob kept the computer he used for fund administration and, now, for the only work that mattered. The desk lamp was off. The screen was the only light, its blue glow turning his face into something hollowed and strange, like a man seen underwater. YouTube was open. The search bar read: "Bach Chaconne Milstein 1972."

He pressed play. Not for the first time. Not for the fifth. He had been listening to the Chaconne for eleven days, since the afternoon in Katarina's studio when she had played the opening eight bars and the floor had dropped out from under his life. Eleven days of the same piece, the same recordings, the same fourteen minutes repeating like a loop in his skull, and each time he heard something the previous listening had hidden, as though the music were a building with rooms he had not yet entered, corridors he had not yet walked, and each repetition opened another door.

He had a notebook. A ruled Moleskine he'd bought at the newsagent in Claremont, the kind of thing he had never owned and had no use for in his professional life, where everything was digital, trackable, deletable. In this notebook he wrote in handwriting that had not improved since school:

*2:14 — chord change, Milstein waits before resolving. Why? The silence before the note is part of the note.*

*3:47 — bass voice disappears for four bars, then returns. You don't notice it's gone until it comes back. Like breathing.*

*5:22 — the run in sixteenths. Hahn plays it like a river. Milstein plays it like a man walking through a river. Different thing entirely.*

*9:12 — THE SHIFT. D major. Bar 133. Every recording, every time, this is the moment. Like stepping from a dark room into sunlight. Milstein plays it quieter than anyone else. This is wrong, it should be louder, but it is right. The light doesn't shout. The light just arrives.*

He was on his seventh recording now. He had started with Milstein because Katarina told him to, and Milstein had broken him open — the tone autumnal, philosophical, the playing of an old man who had lived with this piece for fifty years and no longer needed to prove anything, who played it the way you tell a story you have told a thousand times, not for the listener but for the story itself, because the story needs to be told. Then Hilary Hahn, and the shock of her: seventeen years old on her debut album, technically flawless, the notes struck with a clarity that was almost violent, as though she were cutting the music into the air with a scalpel. Where Milstein was warm, Hahn was luminous. Where Milstein breathed, Hahn burned.

Then Perlman. The tone like honey poured over wood — gorgeous, generous, almost too beautiful, so beautiful that Bob found himself listening to the sound rather than the architecture, and he wrote in the notebook: *Perlman makes you love the violin. Milstein makes you love the music. Not the same thing.* Then Heifetz, and the terror of Heifetz: the speed, the ferocity, the sense of a man who played the Chaconne the way a general takes a city, with total commitment and no mercy and no interest in whether the conquered territory loved him for it.

He turned the page. He wrote: *What am I hearing? Not the notes. The person. Each recording is the same 256 bars and each one is a different life.*

## Chapter 19: "The Soundproofed Room"

At seven-thirty on a Tuesday morning in May, Bob Harrington swiped his key card at the ground-floor entrance of 108 St George's Terrace, walked through the lobby past the security guard who nodded without interest, and took the lift to the fourth floor. The corridor was empty. It was always empty at this hour — the accountancy firm on level three did not start until nine, and the recruitment agency at the far end had been vacant since February, its frosted glass door dark, a small drift of uncollected mail fanning across the carpet beneath the slot. Bob turned left at the fire-exit sign, unlocked the office, and stepped inside.

The office was two rooms divided by a wall he had paid a contractor to install eighteen months ago, citing the need for a private meeting space. The contractor, a sun-ruined man from Balcatta who chewed gum and did not ask questions, had also installed the acoustic foam — fifty panels of dark grey polyurethane, glued in staggered rows to the walls and ceiling of the inner room, transforming it from a standard commercial space with a window into something that looked like the inside of a recording studio, or a padded cell, depending on your disposition. The foam absorbed reflections. It ate echoes. It turned the room into a sealed acoustic environment where sound went in and did not come out, and where the outside world — the traffic on the Terrace, the construction crane on the next block, the phone that rang and rang on the other side of the wall — could not enter.

Bob set his briefcase on the desk in the outer room. The desk held a Bloomberg terminal, dark. A telephone with a red message light blinking — nine messages, he knew without checking, because there had been nine yesterday and he had not listened to them and by tomorrow there would be eleven. A stack of compliance documents in a manila folder. A framed photograph of Jeannie and the children at Rottnest that Jeannie had placed there six years ago and that Bob had not moved because moving it would require having a reason, and having a reason would require a conversation he did not want to have. He did not turn on the Bloomberg. He did not check the messages. He opened the inner door and stepped into the soundproofed room.

The transformation of this space had happened gradually, then all at once, in the way of things that are denied and then surrendered to. First the foam. Then the music stand — a heavy Manhasset, not the flimsy wire kind, positioned at the centre of the room where the acoustic response was most even. Then the metronome, a Wittner mechanical that he wound each morning with a small key, preferring its wooden tick to the sterile pulse of a digital app. Then the mirror — a full-length mirror propped against the far wall so he could watch his bowing arm, his posture, the angle of his left elbow, all the things Katarina corrected each week and that crept back into error between lessons like weeds reclaiming a garden. Then the notebook, open on the stand beside the score. Then the stretching mat on the floor, because his shoulders had begun to ache after the first hour. Then the ice pack in the small bar fridge he had moved from the outer office, because his forearm had begun to burn after the second.

He opened the case. The violin lay in its blue velvet — Franz's violin, the one from Absam, its varnish the colour of dark honey, the scroll carved with a precision that still stopped him each time he looked at it, the volute's spiral tightening into an eye that seemed to watch him as he lifted the instrument free. He rosined the bow. He tightened the hair — a quarter-turn, the tension something his thumb had learned to measure without thought, the first of the body's small capitulations to the instrument. He placed the violin under his chin. The chinrest pressed the spot on his jaw that was now permanently marked — a reddish-brown patch of roughened skin that Jeannie had not noticed, or had noticed and not mentioned, or had noticed and filed away with the other accumulating evidence that her husband was becoming someone she did not recognise.

He played a D minor scale. Slowly. Two octaves up, two octaves down. Each note placed with deliberate care, the bow drawing the sound from the string the way a brush draws paint from a palette — not scraped, not forced, but invited. He listened. The E was slightly sharp. He adjusted. The B-flat had a thinness he did not like, a reedy quality in the upper half of the bow, and he corrected the pressure, adding weight from the index finger, feeling the string resist and then yield. The room absorbed every imperfection and returned nothing but the truth.

This was his real work now. The fund — the emails, the calls, the fabricated quarterly report that sat in the outer room like a grenade with the pin half-pulled — was the interruption. The fraud that had grown around him like scar tissue, layer upon layer of lies calcified into a structure that looked from the outside like a business, was the thing that kept him from the only honest labour of his life. In this room, with this instrument, there was no performance. The violin did not care about his net worth or his wife's opinion or the blinking message light. The violin cared only about the truth of the sound, and the sound could not be faked.

## Chapter 20: "Injuries"

He woke at three in the morning with his left forearm on fire.

Not fire — that was wrong, that was the language of metaphor, and the pain was not metaphorical. It was specific, anatomical, a deep burning ache that ran from the inner crease of his elbow along the belly of the forearm to the base of his wrist, following the line of the flexor tendons with a precision that would have impressed the anatomy textbook he had ordered from Amazon and read at his desk between practice sessions, trying to understand what was happening inside his own arm. He knew what this was. He had been reading about it for weeks with the morbid fascination of a man studying a disease he suspects he already has. Tendonitis. Flexor tendinopathy. The violinist's occupational curse — the inflammation of the tendons that connect the forearm muscles to the fingers, caused by repetitive gripping, by hours of pressing steel strings against an ebony fingerboard, by the relentless demand of the left hand to hold shapes it was not designed to hold for durations the body was not built to sustain.

He lay in the dark and tried to straighten his fingers. They would not straighten. The ring finger and the little finger had curled inward during sleep, contracting into a loose fist, and when he attempted to extend them the pain flared from a burn to a shriek — a bright, electric shriek that ran up the tendon and into the muscle belly and stopped his breath. He let them curl back. He lay still. He breathed.

Jeannie was asleep in her room. The door between their bedrooms — the connecting door that had been installed when they renovated in 2016, and that Jeannie kept closed from her side, the lock unnecessary because the closed door was itself the statement — was shut. No light beneath it. No sound. She slept well, Jeannie. She slept the deep, medicated sleep of a woman on sertraline and sauvignon blanc, a chemical partnership that carried her under each night at eleven and released her at seven, refreshed, functional, armoured for the day. Bob envied that sleep. He had not slept through a night in months — not since the practice had intensified, not since the Chaconne had moved from something he was learning to something that was learning him, colonising his hours, reshaping his body, demanding from his muscles and tendons and joints a payment he was discovering he could not afford.

He held his left arm against his chest with his right hand, cradling it the way you cradle a wounded animal, and he thought: *I can't play.*

The two most terrifying words he knew. More terrifying than *ASIC inquiry* or *structural insolvency* or *redemption request*. More terrifying than anything Jeannie had ever said to him, or anything the silence between them had ever meant. He could survive the fund's collapse — he had been surviving it in slow motion for a decade, carrying the knowledge of it like a stone in his chest, and the stone had become so familiar that he sometimes forgot it was there. He could survive Jeannie's contempt, because he had been surviving it for twenty years, and survival was the thing he was best at, the thing his entire life had been organised around: the absorption of damage without visible effect, the quiet endurance that passed for competence, for stability, for the kind of dependable blandness that people trusted with their money and their daughters.

But he could not survive silence. Not this silence — not the silence of the soundproofed room with the violin in its case, unplayed. Not the silence of the Chaconne interrupted at bar ninety-seven, or bar one hundred and forty, or wherever the tendon chose to seize, the music stopping mid-phrase like a sentence cut off before the verb, the meaning left hanging in the acoustic foam with nowhere to go. That silence would kill him. Not literally, not yet, but in the way that matters — the slow death of the only part of himself he had ever found, the part that had been buried for thirty years under chinos and compliance documents and the smooth, practised smile of a man who managed other people's money because he could not manage his own life.

He turned onto his back. The ceiling was dark. Somewhere outside, the Indian Ocean moved against the shore — the low, constant sound of Cottesloe at night, the sound that had accompanied every sleepless hour in this house, every 3 AM calculation of the fund's shortfall, every predawn walk across Marine Parade to stand on the terraced grass and not-think. He listened to the ocean. He held his arm.

Tomorrow he would see the physiotherapist — the one in Subiaco, not the sports medicine clinic in Claremont where the rugby players went and where he might be recognised. He would explain that he played the violin, that he played four to six hours a day, that the pain had been building for three weeks and was now acute. The physiotherapist would tell him to stop. Everyone would tell him to stop. Katarina would tell him to reduce his practice to ninety minutes, with breaks, with stretching, with ice. The internet forums — the violin forums he read late at night, the threads titled "Tendonitis: How long before I can play again?" and "Left arm pain — should I be worried?" — would tell him to rest for two weeks minimum, to do gentle stretches, to apply ice twenty minutes on, twenty minutes off, to take ibuprofen, and above all, above everything, to stop playing until the inflammation subsided.

Two weeks. Fourteen days without the Chaconne. Fourteen days without the room, the stand, the scroll watching him from the case.

He closed his eyes. The pain in his forearm pulsed with his heartbeat, a slow, deep throb that felt like a clock counting down to something he could not name.

## Chapter 21: "Busking"

The hoodie was from Vinnies — the St Vincent de Paul on Stirling Highway in Claremont, two suburbs from his own, chosen specifically because no one he knew would be caught dead in a Vinnies. It was grey, size large, shapeless in the way of charity-shop clothes that have been washed and donated and washed again until all memory of their original owner has been laundered out of them. The jeans were from the same rack — dark blue, stiff, the wrong cut, sitting too high on his waist in a style no one under sixty would choose deliberately. The baseball cap was plain black, no logo, pulled low. The sunglasses were aviators from a chemist, the lenses dark enough to hide his eyes and cheap enough that the frames pinched the bridge of his nose. He had dressed in the office bathroom, folding his linen shirt and chinos into his briefcase and standing for a moment in front of the mirror in clothes that cost eleven dollars and made him look like a man who might sleep in a park, or wait in line at Centrelink, or do any of the things that Bob Harrington had spent his entire adult life arranging not to do.

He did not recognise himself. That was the point.

It was a Thursday, just past noon. The lunch crowd on Murray Street Mall was at its densest — office workers streaming out of the glass towers on St George's Terrace and the Hay Street arcades, crossing into the pedestrian zone with its plane trees and its public art and its buskers spaced at intervals along the paving, each occupying a patch of territory staked out by long custom and unspoken agreement. A man with a guitar at the Hay Street end, playing something by Fleetwood Mac. A woman with a cello near Forrest Place, her case open, a hand-lettered sign reading BACH & BAROSSA: TIPS APPRECIATED. Bob had studied the mall's geography over three lunchtime reconnaissance trips, watching from a bench with a takeaway coffee, noting the foot traffic patterns, the sight lines, the spots where the acoustics bounced off the shopfronts and carried versus the spots where the sound bled into the open air and died.

He had chosen his position: midway along the mall, between the entrance to the Carillon City arcade and the old post office, where a slight indentation in the building facade created a shallow alcove that caught the sound and projected it outward. He set the case on the ground. Open, lid back, the blue velvet lining facing up. He placed a single gold coin in the case — a two-dollar piece, for suggestion, the way Katarina had told him buskers did it in Prague, a seed to show that payment was welcome and its denomination didn't matter.

He lifted the violin. His hands were shaking, but not from tendonitis — that had subsided after the two-week rest, the stretches, the ice, the reduced practice regime that Katarina had imposed and that he had followed with the desperate obedience of a man who had learned what it meant to lose the thing he loved. The shaking was something else. Stage fright. The terror of exposure that had stopped him performing at fifteen, that had kept the violin in its case for twenty-seven years, that had driven him to build a soundproofed room rather than let a single note escape into a world that might judge it.

He raised the bow. He placed it on the D string. He drew the opening chord of the Chaconne.

The sound bounced off the glass shopfront behind him and rolled out into the mall — four notes, broken from bass to treble, the D minor chord that Bach had written three hundred years ago for a single player in an empty room, now sounding in the open air of a Perth shopping precinct on a Thursday lunchtime among a thousand people who had never heard of the Chaconne and did not look up from their phones.

No one stopped. A woman in a charcoal suit walked past so close her handbag brushed the open case. A man in high-vis workwear glanced in his direction, registered nothing, looked away. Two teenagers with frozen yoghurt wandered past, their earbuds in, sealed inside their own music. The Fleetwood Mac guitarist down the mall was getting more attention, which was right, which was the order of things — you play what people know, what people can hum, what people can drop a coin for without breaking stride. You do not play the most demanding piece in the solo violin repertoire to a crowd that wants "Dreams" and a flat white.

Bob played on. He played the opening theme — the four bars, the descending bass, the first variation — and the sound was rough, imperfect, the outdoor acoustics stripping away the warmth that the soundproofed room had given him, exposing every flaw in his intonation, every unevenness in his bow, the way strong light exposes the cracks in a face that looked fine in the bathroom mirror. But he played. He stood in his Vinnies hoodie and his chemist sunglasses and he played Bach on Murray Street Mall, and the anonymity was a liberation he had not expected — no one knew him, no one cared, he was invisible in the way that buskers and homeless people and anyone without economic utility is invisible in a city that measures human value in square footage and school fees.

## Chapter 22: "The Gap"

She had told him to play the D major section, and he had played it — bars 133 through 208, the middle movement of the Chaconne, the passage where the music lifts from D minor into D major and the entire emotional landscape shifts, the way weather shifts when a cloud bank moves and the sun finds the ground. He had played it from memory, which was itself new — until a month ago he had needed the score in front of him, the notation a safety net he clung to even when his fingers knew the way, afraid that without the printed page the music would abandon him midphrase and leave him standing in silence with the bow raised and nothing to say. Katarina had taken the score away. She had closed it and placed it on the windowsill and said, "The music is in you. Stop reading it and start hearing it." He had been terrified. He had played the first eight bars from memory, stumbled on a chord in bar 141, and stopped.

"Again," she said.

He had played it again. The same eight bars, and then further — bar 141, the chord clean this time, the voices separating, the soprano floating above the bass the way Katarina had demonstrated week after week, "hear the upper voice as a singer, a woman's voice, and the bass as the ground she walks on." He played through bar 150, bar 160, the variations growing in complexity, the double stops climbing, and somewhere — bar 168, or bar 172, he could not later say exactly where — something happened.

The music breathed.

Not the way it had breathed before, which was the breathing of effort, of a man managing the mechanics of bow and string and left hand and intonation and dynamics while also trying to remember the next note and the note after that and the shape of the phrase and the shape of the section and the shape of the whole fifteen-minute arc. That breathing was the breathing of a swimmer counting strokes. This was different. This was the breathing of a man who had stopped counting. For six bars — maybe eight, he could not say, because the quality of the experience was precisely that he was not counting, not measuring, not managing — the violin sang and Bob disappeared into it the way he used to disappear as a boy in Mrs Albrecht's studio in the music wing at Scotch, the way he had disappeared in the car on Rokeby Road the first time he heard Milstein play the Chaconne, the way he disappeared into the ocean when he swam at dawn off Cottesloe in the winter, when the water was so cold it erased everything except the body's animal insistence on being alive.

He came back. The bow reached the end of bar 176, or bar 178, and the spell broke — his third finger missed a shift, the E was flat, and he was suddenly a man playing a violin again, aware of the room, the acoustic foam, the mirror, his own reflection with its furrowed brow and the red mark on his jaw and the sweat on his upper lip. He lowered the instrument.

Katarina was silent. She stood by the window, her own violin hanging at her side, and she did not speak for a long time. Long enough that the silence became its own presence in the room, the way a rest in music is not the absence of sound but its frame, the thing that gives the notes around it their meaning.

"You stopped trying," she said.

He looked at her.

"You let the music play you." She paused. "That's what I hear in the great recordings — Milstein, Szeryng, the very best. Not a person playing the Chaconne. The Chaconne playing through a person. You did that. For six bars, maybe eight. You did that."

Bob sat down. His legs were trembling. Not from exertion — from the aftermath of something he did not have language for, the same wordless trembling he had felt in the car on Rokeby Road, the same trembling that had seized him in Franz's workshop in Absam when he first drew the bow across the strings of this violin and the sound that came back was not his but the instrument's, three hundred years of resonance stored in the grain of Alpine spruce and released into the air by the contact of horsehair and rosin and the imperfect, desperate, fully human pressure of his hand.

"Can I do it again?" he said.

Katarina smiled. It was not the polished smile of a teacher praising a student. It was something rarer — the smile of a musician who has witnessed something she had begun to think she would never see.

"You can't make it happen," she said. "That's the whole point. You can only stop preventing it."

## Chapter 23: "The Authentication"

He sat at the desk in the outer office — the fund side, the side with the Bloomberg terminal and the blinking phone and the compliance folders and the photograph of Jeannie and the children at Rottnest — and he composed an email. He had been composing it for three days. Not writing it — composing it, the way a man composes a letter to a lover, or a resignation, or an apology that might save his life: choosing each word, weighing each sentence, reading it back and deleting and starting again, because this email mattered more than any communication he had ever sent, more than the quarterly reports with their fabricated returns, more than the investor letters with their reassuring prose, more than the love note he had written Jeannie on their first anniversary that she had kept in a drawer for a decade and then, during one of her reorganisations, thrown away.

The recipient was a name he had found on a website: Bein & Fushi, Chicago, one of the foremost dealers and authenticators of fine stringed instruments in the world. He had also drafted a version addressed to Peter Biddulph in London, and a third to Tarisio, the auction house that had sold more Stradivari instruments than anyone alive. He would send only one. He did not want the enquiry to circulate, to become gossip in a world that was, he had learned from his reading, surprisingly small — a few hundred people worldwide who could tell a Stradivari from a Guarneri from a Stainer from a good Cremonese copy, and who talked to each other constantly, the way people in any rarefied field talk, trading rumours and sightings and the occasional breathless story of a lost instrument surfacing in an attic or a garage sale or a small shop in a village in the Austrian Alps.

*Dear Mr. Bein,*

*I am writing to request your professional assessment of a violin in my possession. The instrument was purchased in 2023 from a luthier in Absam, Austria, who described it as a fine Tyrolean instrument of uncertain age. Since acquiring it, I have had it examined by a Perth-based luthier who believes it to be of early eighteenth-century Cremonese origin, possibly from the school of Stradivari.*

He stopped. Deleted "possibly from the school of Stradivari." Too much. Too soon. The man would stop reading, or worse, he would laugh — the weary laugh of an expert who receives a hundred emails a year from hopeful amateurs who think their grandfather's violin is a Strad because the label says *Antonius Stradivarius Cremonensis Faciebat Anno 17—* and a date that may or may not be legible. Bob knew the label meant nothing. Millions of violins carried Stradivari labels — they had been pasted into cheap factory instruments since the nineteenth century, a tradition of aspirational forgery so widespread that it was not even considered fraudulent, merely conventional, the violin world's version of a designer knockoff. He was not writing about the label.

He was writing about the wood. The varnish. The scroll.

He opened the photographs on his laptop. He had taken them two nights ago, alone in the soundproofed room after the last practice session, using the desk lamp to create raking light across the surfaces, the kind of oblique illumination that revealed what direct light concealed. The scroll from four angles — front, left, right, and from below, looking up into the pegbox where the chisel marks were visible, the precise cuts of a hand that knew exactly how deep to go and exactly when to stop. The f-holes in close-up, their elegant S-curves slightly asymmetric in the way that all hand-cut f-holes are asymmetric and that the best authenticators can read like a signature. The varnish in raking light, its surface crazed with a network of fine cracks — craquelure, the word was, the pattern of aging that forms over centuries as the organic compounds in the varnish oxidise and contract, a pattern that cannot be faked because it cannot be hurried, because it is the signature not of a maker but of time itself.

The back plate. The maple's flame figure, the tight curls of grain running diagonally across the surface, catching the light and releasing it in waves. The interior, photographed through the left f-hole with a phone torch: the bass bar, the linings, the corner blocks, the subtle graduation marks where the maker had carved the plates to their final thickness, the wood thinner at the edges and thicker at the centre in a pattern that determined the instrument's voice as surely as the shape of a larynx determines a singer's.

He attached the photographs. He resumed writing.

*I attach photographs taken in controlled lighting conditions. I am particularly interested in your assessment of the scroll carving, the arching profile, the varnish composition and craquelure pattern, and the f-hole geometry. I understand that a definitive attribution would require physical examination and dendrochronological analysis, and I am prepared to arrange both.*

He read it back. It was too formal, too stiff, the language of a man who had spent his career writing investor correspondence and could not shed the register. But it was also precise, and precision was what these people respected — not enthusiasm, not hope, but the careful, evidence-based language of someone who understood the difference between asking "Is this a Stradivarius?" and asking "What can you tell me about this instrument?"

He changed the subject line. He had tried *Instrument Assessment Request* and *Authentication Enquiry* and *Violin — Possible Early 18th Century Cremonese*. He settled on: *Request for Assessment — Violin of Unknown Provenance, Possibly Cremonese*.

His finger hovered over the send button. His hand was shaking. Not from tendonitis — that had receded, managed now, a chronic companion rather than an acute crisis. His hand was shaking because this email would begin a process he could not control and could not take back, a process that would end in one of two verdicts: the violin was what he suspected it was, and everything changed, or the violin was not what he suspected, and nothing changed except the loss of a hope he had not admitted to himself he was carrying.

He pressed send.

## Chapter 24: "The Invitation"

The last note — a D, low, open, the final chord of the Chaconne resolved to its single tonic voice — hung in the soundproofed room and then was gone, absorbed by the acoustic foam, eaten by the silence, and Bob stood with the violin at his side and the bow in his right hand and did not move.

He had played the complete Chaconne from memory. All 256 bars. All fifteen minutes. No stops, no restarts, no moments where the music collapsed and he had to find his place in the score and begin the passage again. He had played it from the opening chord — the four-note D minor break that he had spent a year learning to voice, the bottom two notes dying as the bow rolled to the upper two, the sound not clean, never entirely clean, but clear enough now that the chord's architecture was audible, the harmonics ringing in the room like a bell struck underwater — through the thirty-two variations of the first section, the descending bass, the building intensity, the arpeggiated climbs and the thirty-second-note runs that had defeated him for months and that he could now navigate, if not with grace then with conviction. Through the D major section — the passage that still, every time, stopped his breath, the shift from minor to major that Katarina called the most beautiful transition in all of music and that he played now with a tenderness he could not explain and did not need to, because the tenderness was not his but the music's, the quality inherent in the notes that emerged when a player stopped imposing himself on the sound and let the sound impose itself on him. Through the return to D minor, the bariolage — the obsessive string crossing in the final section that sounded, when it was played well, like a single voice screaming and singing at once, and that sounded, when he played it, like a man trying very hard to do something impossible and almost, almost, succeeding. The bariolage was uneven. The high-position passage in variation twenty-two was slightly flat. The final chords, the great stacked harmonies that brought the piece home to its opening key, were rough at the edges, the bow pressure a fraction too heavy, the voicing imperfect.

But he had played it. Beginning to end. Fifteen minutes without a break.

He stood at the window. The soundproofed room had one window, facing west, and through it he could see the Swan River turning gold in the last light, the water shifting from grey to amber as the sun dropped toward the Indian Ocean beyond Fremantle. The Norfolk pines on the South Perth foreshore were silhouettes now, black against the burning sky. A ferry crossed the river, small and white, leaving a wake that caught the light and held it. The city behind him was emptying — the office workers draining out of the glass towers on the Terrace, the traffic thickening on the Mitchell Freeway, the daily evacuation of the CBD that left the buildings dark and the streets quiet and Bob alone on the fourth floor with a violin that might be worth more than the building he was standing in.

He made the decision. Or the decision made itself — rose up from the place where such things form, below thought, below reason, in the region of the body where music lives and where the truth about a person is stored not as proposition but as conviction, as certainty, as the thing you know before you know you know it. He would play this at his birthday. He would play the Chaconne — all of it, all fifteen minutes — in front of Jeannie and Chase and Shanel and the hundred and twenty people who would fill the house and the garden and the marquee overlooking Cottesloe Beach on the evening of his fiftieth birthday, three weeks from now. He would stand up in front of the investors and the school parents and the yacht club members and the gaggle and the caterers and the DJ and the people who thought they knew him, who had known him for years, for decades, who had never known him at all, and he would play the most honest thing he had ever done.

This was insane. He knew it was insane. He could hear Jeannie's voice already — the practiced incredulity, the carefully calibrated contempt. *You want to play your fiddle at your own birthday?* Fiddle. She would say fiddle. She had always said fiddle, the diminutive stripping the instrument of its dignity the way her observations stripped him of his — casually, almost fondly, the way you diminish something you need to keep small so it doesn't threaten the order of the world you've built around its absence.

But the decision was made. It had been made in the car on Rokeby Road nine months ago, or in Katarina's studio when the opening chord filled the room and the floor dropped out, or in Franz's workshop in Absam when the old man handed him a violin that needed to be played and he drew the bow and the sound that came back was not his but older, deeper, a voice from inside the wood. It had been made, perhaps, thirty-two years ago in a school assembly hall where a fourteen-year-old boy played a simplified Bach movement and disappeared into the sound and was pulled back by a world that had no room for what he'd found.

He put the violin in its case. He closed the latches. He turned off the light in the soundproofed room and walked into the outer office, where the phone's message light blinked its silent red accusation and the compliance folder sat unopened and the Bloomberg terminal stared at him with its dark, empty screen, and he picked up his briefcase and his jacket and walked out, locking the door behind him, and took the lift down to the lobby, and stepped into the evening air of St George's Terrace, where the city smelled of exhaust and eucalyptus and the faint salt breath of the river, and he drove home to Marine Parade to tell his wife.
