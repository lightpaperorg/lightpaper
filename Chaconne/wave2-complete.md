## Chapter 1: "The View from Marine Parade"

**POV:** Bob | **Target:** ~700 words

---

The grass was cold under his feet. It was the cold of late autumn in a place that did not really have seasons, a mild affront, barely noticed, the dew soaking through the soles of his feet and into the soil beneath, as if the earth were drawing something out of him. Bob stood on the first terrace of the stepped lawn that descended from Marine Parade toward the beach, a coffee in his right hand, wearing yesterday's chinos and a linen shirt he had slept in, and he looked at the ocean.

It was early -- seven, or just past it -- and the light had that quality it only had at this hour: pale gold, provisional, as though the day had not yet committed to itself. The Norfolk Island pines threw long shadows westward across the grass, their strange columnar shapes dark against the brightening sky, like columns built to hold up nothing. Below and to the south, the Indiana sat closed and white on the beachfront, its colonial facade catching the first horizontal light. The esplanade was empty. A magpie picked at the edge of the path. The ocean beyond the sand was flat, glassy, a pewter colour that would burn off into blue within the hour, and it stretched -- as it always stretched, as it had stretched for every one of the fifteen years he had stood on this grass with a coffee in his hand -- unbroken to Africa, or to nothing, or to whatever existed on the far side of eleven thousand kilometres of Indian Ocean. The sky above it was enormous. That was always the thing you forgot about Perth until you stood outside before the day started and looked up: the sky was enormous, and the silence was almost total, and for a few minutes you could believe you were the last man alive, or the first, and that nothing had gone wrong yet.

This was his ritual on the mornings when he could not sleep, which was most mornings now. He would come out the back gate and cross Marine Parade -- the street empty, the houses on either side still shuttered, the Range Rover and the Mercedes parked nose-to-tail in the drive -- and he would stand on the grass and drink his coffee and do the thing he had done out here for years, which was not-thinking. Letting the view fill the space where thought would be. The view had cost him four and a half million dollars. That was what he had paid for the house in 2010, at the peak of the mining boom, stretching further than he should have because Jeannie wanted Marine Parade and Marine Parade was what Jeannie got. He did not think about this. He did not think about the fund, or about the phone call he needed to return, or about the quarterly reports that were due on Friday and contained numbers that did not correspond to anything real. He looked at the ocean and he was no one for a while, and the no one felt like the truest thing about him.

He was forty-nine. Sandy hair going grey at the temples. Six foot, with an extra ten kilos that had settled around his waist and softened the line of his jaw, so that his face, which had once been handsome in the way that suggested competence and trustworthiness, had gone bland -- a face for a bank advertisement, a face for the back page of a financial newsletter, a face that made people feel their money was safe. He was wearing RM Williams boots when he dressed, but he had come out barefoot, and the grass was cold, and his hands were large and well-kept, the hands of a man who had never built anything with them.

His left hand -- the one not holding the coffee -- curled slightly at his side. The fingers closed, not into a fist but around something that was not there, the thumb bracing against an invisible neck, the fingertips pressing against nothing. It was a gesture so small and so unconscious that Bob himself did not notice it, and it lasted only a moment before his hand relaxed and hung at his side again. But it was there. The hand remembered what the mind had locked away.

He turned and looked back at the house. Five bedrooms. Limestone. Ocean views from the upper floor. A garden Jeannie had landscaped three times. It sat above him on the rise of Marine Parade, lit by the early sun along its eastern wall, and from here it looked like what it was supposed to look like: the house of a man who had done well. Jeannie's bedroom curtain -- her retreat, as she called it, the second bedroom she had colonised three years ago -- was still drawn. The house was quiet. The house was always quiet in a way that had nothing to do with sound.

## Chapter 2: "Nedlands, 1987"

**POV:** Bob | **Target:** ~600 words

---

He found it on a Tuesday night, or a Wednesday -- a nothing evening in late November, the kind of Perth evening where the heat has broken but the air still holds the memory of it, and the house is quiet because Jeannie is upstairs and the children are elsewhere and the television is on but muted, the way Bob always has the television, the images washing over the room like light through water. He was on his phone. He was always on his phone in the evenings now, scrolling with the glazed purposelessness of a man who does not know what he is looking for but cannot stop looking, and the YouTube algorithm -- which had learned things about him he had not learned about himself -- had begun sliding violin performances between the market commentary and the football highlights. Thumbnails of women on stages, instruments tucked beneath their chins, bows poised. He scrolled past them. He scrolled past them every night. But tonight a thumbnail stopped him: a girl, young, twelve or thirteen, playing on a stage that looked like a school hall, and the angle of her left hand on the neck of the instrument -- the wrist flat, the fingers curving down over the strings with that particular concentrated stillness -- sent something through him that was not thought and not memory but something older than both: a sensation in his own hand, a weight beneath his own chin, the pressure of a string against the pad of his index finger, and he was eleven years old and it was 1987 and the bus was grinding up the hill to Scotch College with the sun coming through the windows and his school bag wedged between his knees and inside the bag, in a case that smelled of rosin and old cloth, was a violin.

He did not tap the video. He put the phone face-down on the couch and sat in the flickering light and let the memory arrive, which it did not as narrative but as a series of physical impressions: the weight of the case on his shoulder. The bus jolting over the speed bumps on Stirling Highway. The music room at Scotch -- its particular smell, floor polish and brass cleaner and the dusty warmth of instruments stored in cupboards that were never quite closed. And the teacher, the first teacher, a young woman whose name he could not retrieve -- not Mrs Albrecht, that was later, this was earlier, Year Seven, the beginning -- placing the violin under his chin and adjusting the chin rest with her thumb, and showing him how to draw the bow across the open D string.

The sound had been horrible. A scraping, nasal whine that made the other boys laugh and made his face burn and made the teacher say something encouraging that he did not hear, because beneath the horrible sound, transmitted through the chin rest into his jawbone and from his jawbone into his skull and from his skull into some chamber of his brain that had never been opened, was a vibration. Not a musical vibration -- not yet, not for months -- but a physical one, as if the string and the wood and the bone were all the same material and the bow had set all of it resonating at once. He did not know what to do with this sensation. He was eleven. He stored it in the place where children store things they cannot explain, below language, below thought, in the body itself, where it would remain for thirty-eight years like a seed in dry ground, waiting for water.

The phone buzzed. A notification from the fund's administrator -- something routine, something about quarterly filings. Bob picked it up, read the message, typed a brief response, and the room reassembled itself around him: the muted television, the empty couch, the Marine Parade house with its ocean views and its silence. He put the phone down. He sat in the dark for a long time.

## Chapter 3: "The Gaggle"

**POV:** Jeannie | **Target:** ~700 words

---

She arrived second, which was correct. Not first -- first was eager, first was the new girl, first was the woman who had nowhere else to be -- and not last, because last was Margot's privilege and Margot was never late so much as delayed, which was a different thing, a thing that communicated that one's time was valuable and one's entrance was worth waiting for. Jeannie parked the Range Rover on Bay View Terrace -- the full-size, not the Evoque; she had lobbied for the upgrade and Bob had signed the lease without discussion, which was how most things in her life arrived -- and checked her face in the rear-view mirror. This was not vanity. Or it was vanity, but it was also something else: a diagnostic, a systems check, the way a pilot scans instruments before takeoff. Hair: pulled back, the colour still good, the grey managed by Dario at his salon in Subiaco every five weeks. Face: the Botox was holding; the filler along the jawline was subtle enough that no one had mentioned it, which meant it was either invisible or so common among her friends that it no longer registered as a procedure. Eyes: green, clear, no puffiness, the concealer doing its work. She was forty-five years old and she looked like herself at thirty-eight, which was not the same as looking young but was the goal -- not the reversal of time but its arrest, the preservation of a particular vintage, like a wine held at the right temperature in the right cellar.

The restaurant was one of the places on Bay View Terrace that had survived by understanding precisely what Claremont women wanted, which was good food served in a room that looked expensive without being ostentatious, with French doors open to the pavement so that arriving and departing could be witnessed by passersby. The table was in the window. Jeannie took the seat facing the door -- a habit so ingrained she no longer noticed it, though it served the same purpose as the rear-view mirror: a position from which to see everything and be seen seeing it.

Karen was already there, which made Karen first, which was fine because Karen was always first and everyone had stopped reading anything into it years ago. Karen had the look of a woman who had been pretty once in a way that suggested health -- good skin, wide smile, the kind of blonde that came from the sun and not from Dario -- and was now handsome in a way that suggested maintenance. Her husband sold industrial pumps to mining companies and drank too much at dinner parties, a fact the group acknowledged through a shared vocabulary of euphemism: Colin was "tired," Colin had "a big week," Colin was "letting off steam." Karen bore this with a composure that Jeannie simultaneously admired and found repulsive, because the composure was real -- Karen genuinely did not seem to mind -- and Jeannie could not understand a woman who did not mind. Not minding was, to Jeannie, a failure of standards.

They ordered. Jeannie asked for a sauvignon blanc -- it was twelve-fifteen, this was the first glass, the legitimate glass, the glass that did not count because everyone was having one and the sun was out and it was a Wednesday and lunch without wine was not lunch but a meeting. The wine arrived and she took the first sip and felt the familiar softening, the slight recession of the sharp thing that lived behind her sternum, the thing she had no name for and medicated with fifty milligrams of sertraline every morning and an undisclosed number of glasses of Doyen Bay every afternoon. The first sip was always the best. The first sip was the moment the day became manageable.

Amanda arrived next, then Pip, then the new woman -- what was her name, Renee, or Rachael, something with an R -- and the table filled and the noise rose and the performance began in earnest. The conversation moved with the practised rhythm of women who had been having this lunch, at this table or one like it, every second Wednesday for six years: children first (achievements, anxieties, the inexhaustible subject of school politics), then holidays (who had been where, who was going, the careful calibration of destinations to signal wealth without vulgarity), then real estate, then bodies, and then, always, inevitably, the main event: other people.

## Chapter 4: "The Fund"

**POV:** Bob | **Target:** ~700 words

---

The key card beeped and the light went green and Bob pushed through the glass door into the lobby of the building on St George's Terrace with the same movement he had made every weekday morning for eleven years, a movement so habitual it had ceased to be an action and become a reflex, like breathing, like lying. The lobby was marble and steel, the kind of corporate entrance that exists to communicate seriousness -- the security desk, the turnstiles, the lift bank with its brushed aluminium doors reflecting the shapes of people who did not look at each other. He pressed the button for level twelve. The lift rose. A woman from one of the law firms on fourteen stood beside him holding a takeaway coffee and looking at her phone, and they rode in the particular silence of people who share a building and nothing else, and the doors opened and she continued upward and Bob stepped out into the corridor that led to the frosted glass door with HARRINGTON CAPITAL in matte grey lettering, and he felt what he always felt at this moment, which was nothing. A nothing so practised, so thorough, so carefully maintained that it had become its own kind of expertise.

The office was small. That was the first thing a visitor would notice, if visitors came, which they rarely did anymore -- Bob preferred to meet clients at restaurants, at the yacht club, in environments he controlled and that communicated the right things. The office itself communicated very little. A reception area with a desk that was unmanned three days out of five. A meeting room with a view of the Terrace below. Bob's office beyond that: a window looking east toward the park at the top of the city, two screens on the desk, a bookshelf containing financial texts he had read once and regulatory documents he consulted when he needed the precise language of compliance. The carpet was grey. The walls were pale. There was a print of something -- a nautical scene, boats in a harbour -- that Jeannie had chosen when the office was fitted out in 2012. It was the most personal object in the room.

He sat down. He turned on the screens. The left screen showed the ASX -- tickers scrolling, the small red and green movements of a market that had nothing to do with him and everything to do with the performance he maintained. The right screen showed his email. There were forty-two unread messages. He scanned the subject lines the way a man scans a crowd for the face he is afraid to see: quickly, peripherally, looking not at the messages themselves but for the particular words that signalled danger. *Redemption. Statement. Query. Meeting request.* He found three redemption requests. One was small -- fifty thousand, manageable, he could meet it from the operating account. One was two hundred thousand, which would require liquidating a real position, which would widen the gap between what the fund owned and what the fund owed. The third was from Richard, requesting five hundred thousand, politely, with a note about a property purchase, and Bob read it twice and then minimised the window and looked at the wall.

The wall held no answers. It never had. But Bob looked at it the way he looked at the ocean from the grass on Marine Parade -- as a surface onto which he could project nothing, a blankness that asked nothing of him, a space in which he could practise the only skill he had truly mastered, which was the postponement of the unbearable. The fund's assets under management had been, at their peak in 2011, approximately eighty-five million dollars. In the quarterly reports that went to investors -- the beautifully formatted documents that Sandra printed and posted in cream envelopes with the Harrington Capital logo -- the figure was seventy-four million, reflecting a managed and dignified decline that suggested prudent rebalancing in uncertain markets. In reality -- in the set of numbers that existed only in Bob's head, because to put them on paper would be to make them evidence -- the fund held approximately thirty million in actual assets backing seventy-four million in reported obligations. The gap was forty-four million dollars. The gap was the lie. The gap was the distance between the man Bob pretended to be and the man he was, expressed in a currency everyone understood.

He opened the email from Richard and began composing a reply. The words came easily. They always came easily. That was the terrible thing.

## Chapter 5: "Rottnest"

**POV:** Chase | **Target:** ~600 words

---

The swell hit them side-on coming out past the groyne and the Riviera pitched hard to port and Macca's beer went flying off the dash and Chase laughed and opened the throttle and felt the twin Cats dig in, thirteen hundred horsepower of diesel churning the water white behind them, and the bow rose and steadied and the boat found her line and they were out, properly out, the river and the traffic bridge and Fremantle falling away behind them and nothing ahead but eighteen kilometres of open Indian Ocean and Rottnest sitting low and scrubby on the horizon like something that couldn't be bothered to be impressive.

This was the thing. This was the actual thing, and Chase did not have words for it and did not need them. The throttle under his palm, vibrating. The spray coming over the bow in sheets when they punched through a swell. The sound of the diesels, not a roar but a sustained deep growl that came up through the deck and through his legs and settled in his chest like a second heartbeat. He was twenty-one years old and he was driving a forty-eight-foot motor yacht across open ocean on a Friday afternoon in late autumn and his two best mates were below deck loading slabs of Emu Export into the fridge and arguing about the Dockers and the sky ahead was enormous and blue and the water was that dark, almost navy blue it went when the continental shelf dropped away, and he was happy in the uncomplicated way that he was only ever happy on this boat, which was the one place in his life where his body and his mind were doing the same thing.

The Riviera 48 was called *Chardonnay*. His mother's choice. Chase had wanted *Full Send* or *Rip Tide* or something his mates would find funny, but the boat was not his -- it was his father's, technically, paid for in 2015 when Chase was eleven, though by now Chase had driven it more hours than Bob had and knew the crossing by feel: the swell pattern south of the groyne, the wind shift at the halfway point where the Fremantle Doctor met the Southern Ocean rollers and the chop turned ugly for ten minutes, the moment when Rottnest resolved from a smudge into an actual island with that insane turquoise water ringing it like something out of a travel ad. Forty-eight feet of fibreglass and teak, twin Caterpillar C-12s, three cabins, a flybridge, a tender on the swim platform. She was not new and she was not flash -- the blokes with real money had sixty-footers, Sunseekers and Princesses with crew -- but she was solid and she was fast enough and she was his, in every way that mattered.

His dad was up on the flybridge. Chase had registered this without really looking: Bob sitting in the skipper's chair with a coffee, staring at the ocean with that blank look he got sometimes, like he was tuned to a radio station no one else could hear. Dad did this. He went somewhere inside his own head and you could talk to him and he'd nod and say "yeah mate" but he wasn't there, not really, and Chase had learned a long time ago not to examine his parents too closely because examination led to questions and questions led to the kind of territory that his family did not visit. His mum had a word for it -- she said Bob was "in one of his moods" -- and Chase had adopted the phrase without investigating what it meant, the way you adopt a local expression when you move to a new country: you use it, it works, you don't ask about the etymology.

## Chapter 6: "The Terrace"

**POV:** Bob | **Target:** ~600 words

---

The phone rang at nine-fourteen on a Monday morning in November, and Bob knew. He did not know in the way that a person knows a fact -- with certainty, with evidence, with the architecture of reason supporting the conclusion -- but in the way a body knows: the phone vibrating against the desk surface, the sound of it thin and industrial in the quiet office, and something in his chest cavity dropping, a physical descent, as though a trapdoor had opened between his ribs and his organs had shifted downward by a centimetre. He looked at the screen. The number was not in his contacts, which meant it was either a cold call or it was the other thing, the thing he had been waiting for without admitting he was waiting for it, because admitting you are waiting for something means admitting it exists, and Bob's entire life was built on the premise that certain things could be kept from existing by the simple act of not looking at them.

He let it ring. Once, twice, three times. The office was empty -- Sandra came in on Tuesdays and Thursdays, and on the other days the reception desk sat unmanned and the meeting room sat unused and Harrington Capital consisted of one man in a room on the twelfth floor of a building on St George's Terrace, looking at a phone he did not want to answer. The morning light came through the east-facing window and fell across the desk in a long rectangle, illuminating the phone, the two screens (both dark -- he had not yet turned them on), a pen, a glass of water, the ordinary objects of a working life arranged with the neatness of a man who did not have enough real work to create disorder.

He picked up. He did this not because he wanted to but because not picking up was worse. Not picking up meant the call would come again, and then again, and then it would come as a letter, and then it would come as something else entirely, something with a letterhead and a reference number, and by then the architecture of not-looking would have collapsed under its own weight. Better to answer. Better to perform.

"Bob Harrington."

The voice on the other end was male, measured, and precise in the way that professional voices are precise when they are building toward something. Not angry. Worse than angry: informed. The man identified himself -- an investor, not Richard, someone else, someone whose name Bob recognised from a redemption request he had deferred three months ago with the twelve-month-horizon speech. The speech had worked at the time. Speeches like that always worked, because they were constructed from the same materials that Bob used in every client interaction -- warmth, authority, the reassuring cadence of a man who lives and breathes this -- and because people did not want to believe that the person managing their money was lying to them any more than Bob wanted to believe he was the person lying.

But this man had done something. Bob could hear it in the voice before the words confirmed it: the particular tone of a person who has been thinking, who has compared notes, who has spoken to his accountant or his lawyer or, worst of all, to another investor. The questions were specific. Technical. They arrived in a sequence that suggested preparation -- not hostility, not yet, but the careful architecture of a man who wants to give you the opportunity to explain before he escalates. "What's the fund's current NAV, Bob?" "Can I get the most recent audited statements?" "Who do you use for the external audit?"

Bob answered. The answers were fluent, practised, technically accurate in ways that obscured the truth. He had delivered them before, to other callers, in other years. The machine ran.

## Chapter 7: "What Jeannie Doesn't Know"

**POV:** Jeannie | **Target:** ~600 words

---

The room was hers. That was the point of it, and Jeannie was conscious of this every time she closed the door -- not locked it, never locked, locking would be a declaration and declarations were not how Jeannie operated; she closed it, which was a suggestion, a boundary stated in the passive voice. She had taken the second bedroom three years ago, moving her things across the hall in stages -- first the reading lamp, then the cashmere throw, then the pillows (her pillows, the European goose-down ones from The White Company that Bob's neck could not tolerate and that she refused to replace with anything from a department store), then the phone charger, and finally, one Tuesday evening without announcement or discussion, herself. Bob had said nothing. This was his principal talent: saying nothing when something was happening, absorbing the event like water absorbing a stone, registering no disturbance on the surface. She had expected a question. She had prepared an answer -- something about sleeping better alone, something about his snoring, something plausible and face-saving that they could both pretend was the reason. He did not ask. She did not explain. The marriage rearranged itself around the new configuration the way a family rearranges itself around a death: silently, practically, with everyone pretending the empty chair has always been empty.

The room, then. It was ten o'clock on a Thursday night. Jeannie was in bed -- her bed, queen-size, white linen, the Diptyque candle on the nightstand still burning because she liked the smell and because the small orange flame gave the room a quality she thought of as European, though she had never articulated this even to herself. On the nightstand beside the candle: her phone, a glass of sauvignon blanc that was not her first, the prescription bottle of sertraline from the GP in Subiaco. She had taken the pill an hour ago. The pill and the wine had met in her bloodstream and were performing their nightly collaboration, a chemical détente that lowered the volume on everything -- the anxiety, the restlessness, the low hum of something she might have called despair if she were the kind of woman who used that word, which she was not. She was the kind of woman who called it being tired.

She was on Instagram. The screen glowed against her face in the dim room, and she scrolled with the practised, frictionless movement of a thumb that had performed this gesture ten thousand times, past the photographs of other women's lives -- the interiors, the holidays, the children in school uniforms, the flat lays of coffee cups and novels and cashmere socks arranged on white marble with the artful carelessness of a production that had taken twenty minutes. She was looking for something. She did not know what she was looking for. She was always looking for something on this screen and she never found it and she never stopped looking, and this was the rhythm of her evenings: the wine, the pill, the screen, the scroll, the thin blue light on her face in the dark, and the feeling -- beneath the medication, beneath the alcohol, beneath the performance of a woman at rest in a beautiful room -- of a tide going out and leaving behind it not sand but something harder and more desolate, the exposed and ugly architecture of a life that looked, from the outside, like everything anyone could want.

Her follower count was 4,187. It had been 4,200 on Monday. She had noticed the drop the way you notice a hairline crack in a wall -- not alarming in itself, but suggestive, evidence of some structural shift you cannot see and do not want to investigate. Thirteen people had decided, over the course of a week, that Jeannie Harrington's curated life was no longer worth watching. She did not know who they were. She wanted to know who they were.

## Chapter 8: "The Trip"

**POV:** Bob | **Target:** ~500 words

---

Perth Airport's international terminal had a particular quality of light that Bob had never been able to name -- not quite fluorescent and not quite natural, a compromised brightness that flattened everything, so that the families in the departure lounge all looked like versions of the same family: sunburned, slightly overdressed, clutching passports and boarding passes with the anxious reverence of people who do not travel often enough for the ritual to become routine. The Harringtons were not that family. Jeannie had organised them into a tableau of practised ease: matching Rimowa luggage arranged in a formation that was not accidental, she and Shanel in linen that would crease before Singapore but looked immaculate now, Chase in the one good shirt Jeannie had laid out on his bed that morning with a note that said *Wear this.* They looked like a family in a magazine. They looked like what they were supposed to be.

Bob sat slightly apart. Not dramatically apart -- not across the lounge, not making a statement -- but one seat removed from the cluster of his family, holding his boarding pass in his left hand with a looseness that suggested calm and concealed something else. He was watching the planes through the floor-to-ceiling windows. The Indian Ocean was visible beyond the runways, the same ocean he looked at every morning from the grass on Marine Parade, the same pewter expanse that had been the backdrop of his entire life, and in a few hours he would be above it, crossing it, leaving it behind. Jeannie was photographing the luggage arrangement. Chase was on his phone, body angled away from his mother in the universal posture of a twenty-year-old who has not yet forgiven his parents for existing in public. Shanel was reading a novel -- Bob could not see the cover from this angle, but she was absorbed in it, her body curled into the plastic airport chair with a self-containment that reminded him, with a tenderness so sudden it startled him, of his own mother reading in the garden on Pearse Street.

He felt something. It was not a large feeling and it did not arrive with drama. It arrived the way a change in air pressure arrives: subtly, without announcement, altering the quality of everything without changing its appearance. He felt light. Or lighter. The fund was nine thousand miles behind him, or would be by tonight. Sandra had the office. The quarterly was filed. The machine would run without him for three weeks, the way machines run when no one is watching -- which is to say, it would run exactly the same, because the machine was automatic now, self-sustaining, requiring only his face and his voice to maintain the fiction, and his face and his voice would be in Europe, where no one knew him, where no one needed anything from him, where he could be -- and here the feeling sharpened, became almost painful in its specificity -- no one.

In his search history, undeleted, was the word *Absam*.

He did not know what he would find there. He knew only that he would go.

## Chapter 9: "Kothen, July 1720"

The road from Carlsbad was dust and heat and the smell of horses. Bach sat with his manuscripts on his knees and his wig in his bag and the white cotton cap he preferred for travelling pulled low against the afternoon sun, which came through the carriage window in a solid shaft and heated the leather until it was almost too hot to touch. He had been riding for three days. His stockings were grey with road dust. The coachman, a Saxon with a harelip whose name Bach had forgotten and did not intend to relearn, had been whistling the same four bars of a dance tune since Jena, and Bach had spent the last hour trying to determine whether the man was sharp on the third note or whether the intervals were simply unfamiliar. He concluded it was both. The man was sharp, and the mode was Mixolydian, and the combination produced a sound that was not wrong, exactly, but unresolved -- a phrase that wanted to go somewhere and could not find the door.

This was the quality of Bach's attention. He could not hear sound without hearing structure. The rhythmic clatter of hooves on packed earth laid down a ground bass over which the creaking of the chassis and the driver's tuneless whistle arranged themselves into a kind of rough polyphony. He did not choose to hear this way. It was simply how the world entered him -- not as noise but as pattern, not as chaos but as counterpoint waiting to be recognised.

He thought of Maria Barbara now with the absent, functional gratitude of a man thinking of a floor that holds his weight. She would have the household in order. The children would be fed. He would need to speak with her about Friedemann's shoes -- the boy was growing again -- and the organ at the Jakobskirche wanted tuning. Small things. The things a man thinks about when the large things -- the four-bar descent in D minor that he had been turning over for weeks like a stone in his pocket -- are too vast to hold consciously for long.

The partitas were nearly finished. The fair copy of the Sei Solo lay in the portfolio on his knees. The first four movements of the second partita were complete: Allemande, Courante, Sarabande, Gigue. The fifth -- the final movement, the one that would not behave -- existed in sketches and in the phrase that had lodged in his inner ear sometime during the winter and refused to leave. A chaconne. A ground bass, descending, D minor, four bars over which the violin would build everything. He could hear it: the voices moving against each other, the polyphony implicit in a single line, the moment when the key would lift from minor to major and the darkness would break open into light.

The carriage lurched through a village. A church steeple, a well, a woman hanging linen. Wheat fields beyond, the grain heavy and gold. Germany in high summer: the rich smell of turned earth and horse dung and something sweeter underneath, linden blossom. He thought of supper, of the study in the evening when the children were asleep and the candle burned and the only sound was the scratch of the pen. His fingers moved involuntarily on the portfolio's leather cover, finding positions that were not there.

The carriage turned. Kothen appeared, and Bach leaned forward to look for the smoke from his own chimney. He did not find it. The house had its shutters closed against the heat, which was ordinary, but the front door was open, which was not. Maria Barbara kept the door closed in summer. He had told her this was unnecessary. She had ignored him. The door was always closed.

It was open now. A woman stood in the doorway -- not Maria Barbara. A neighbour, Frau Schreiber. She was wearing black. She was holding a cloth in her hands, wringing it, the way a person wrings a cloth when their hands need something to do because the rest of their body has been told to stand still and wait.

Bach's hand tightened on the portfolio. The carriage had not yet stopped. ## Chapter 11: "Absam"

## Chapter 12: "Vienna"

The Musikverein rose from the Ringstrasse like a golden reliquary, its facade lit by the evening sun in a way that made the stone seem to generate its own warmth. Bob stood on the pavement and looked at it. He had never seen a building that looked like music sounded -- that had the proportions and the colour and the contained intensity of something you were meant to hear rather than see. Jeannie had booked the tickets because the building photographed well. She had declined to attend because two hours of classical music was, in her formulation, "not my idea of a holiday." Chase had laughed. Shanel had hesitated -- he had seen it, the brief flicker of interest before she read her mother's face and sided with the majority. Bob had taken the single ticket and walked here alone, with the violin case in his left hand and the lightness of a man who has been released, for a few hours, from the obligation to be witnessed.

Inside, the Goldener Saal. He had read about it on his phone but the reading had not prepared him. The hall was smaller than he expected, smaller than the Perth Convention Centre, but every surface was gold. Gold caryatids holding up the balcony, gold rosettes in the ceiling, gold mouldings framing painted panels. The seats were red velvet, worn to a nap. The stage was bare except for a music stand. No piano, no orchestra, no amplification. Just a wooden stage in a golden room, and the air had a quality he could feel against his skin -- a density, a readiness, as though the room itself were an instrument waiting to be played.

He found his seat. Third row from the back, left side -- far enough to be invisible, close enough to hear. Around him the audience settled: the rustle of programmes, the murmur of German and Japanese and English, the particular collective hush of a room full of people who have come to listen. He had never been in a room that hushed like this. At the yacht club, at dinner parties, at investor events, silence was a failure -- a gap that someone needed to fill. Here, the silence was the point.

The violinist walked onstage. A woman, young, in a black dress that left her arms bare. She carried her violin in her left hand and her bow in her right and she walked to the centre of the stage with the unhurried certainty of a person who has been going there her whole life. She did not smile. She stood, lifted the instrument to her shoulder, and waited.

The room went silent with a silence Bob had never experienced -- not the absence of sound but the presence of attention, six hundred people breathing together, opening together to receive whatever was about to come. She raised the bow. She held it above the string for a moment that lasted longer than a moment, a held breath, a suspension -- and then she drew it across and the first note entered the room and Bob's body responded before his mind could name what it was hearing.

The sound filled the Goldener Saal the way light fills a church through stained glass -- not all at once but in layers, the fundamental tone and then the overtones blooming above it, each one finding a surface in the golden room and reflecting back, so that the sound was not coming from the stage but from everywhere, from the walls and the ceiling and the three centuries of music that had seeped into the plaster and the gilt. One woman. One instrument. No safety net.

He did not know what she was playing. He knew only that the sound was reaching a place in him that nothing else had reached -- not the fund's terrors, not Jeannie's contempt, not twenty years of living inside a lie. The music walked through every door he had ever closed and stood in the room behind them and said, simply: here.

He wept. The tears came without permission, without sound, running down his cheeks and into the corners of his mouth, and he did not wipe them away because wiping them would have meant moving his hand and he could not move. He was held by the sound the way a river holds a stone. He had not cried in years. Not at his father's funeral, not during the worst nights of the fund. He cried now because the sound had found the thing he had buried and had called it by its name.

## Chapter 13: "Home"

His left hand curled around the neck. His chin lowered toward where the chinrest should be. The muscle memory fired -- not skill, not technique, but the pure animal knowledge of what this object was and where it belonged against his body.

He was home. He was holding a broken violin in a room in a house he had bought with money that was not his, in a marriage that was not a marriage, in a life that was not a life. ## Chapter 14: "The Restoration"

The luthier's workshop was in Fremantle, in a lane behind the cappuccino strip, in a building that had once been a sail loft and still smelled, beneath the varnish and the glue, faintly of canvas and salt. Bob had buckled the violin case into the passenger seat. He had trained at the Newark School of Violin Making in England and worked a decade in London before returning to Perth because his wife missed the ocean and because, he said, the light was better. He meant this literally. The workshop's north-facing windows had been cut to his specification, flooding the benches with the even, shadowless light that a restorer needs to see what he is seeing rather than what he hopes to see.

Bob opened the case, lifted the violin with both hands -- gently, as though the instrument were asleep -- and set it on the cloth David had spread across his workbench. David looked at it.

His first reaction was professional scepticism. He picked it up the way a doctor picks up an X-ray -- with detachment, with the intention of seeing what was there rather than what someone wanted to be there. He turned it in the light. He looked at the scroll from the side, from below. He ran his thumb along the purfling -- three strips of wood, black-white-black, set into a channel a millimetre from the edge -- and his thumb slowed at the upper bout, slowed again at the corners, and stopped.

He reached for a loupe. A small brass eyepiece on a chain around his neck. He fitted it to his eye and leaned in, and for the next minute he did not speak. He examined the f-holes, their shape, the way the cuts tapered at each end. He examined the varnish -- sniffing, then tilting the instrument so the light caught the surface at different angles. He turned it over and studied the back, the maple's figured grain rippling like water beneath the surface as he tilted the wood.

He set the violin down. He removed the loupe. He looked at Bob.

"Where did you get this?"

Bob told him. Austria. A village called Absam. A luthier named Franz Kaltenbrunner.

David nodded. He did not speak for a moment. His hands rested on either side of the violin, not touching it, framing it, and his face had the expression of a man recalculating something.

"Do you know what you have?" he said.

"I know it's old," Bob said.

"It's more than old." David picked the violin up again, turning it slowly. "The purfling is hand-cut. Not machine, not stamped -- hand-cut, by someone who knew exactly what they were doing. The f-holes are --" He paused, choosing his words. "Consistent with something very fine. The varnish, the way it sits on the wood -- that's not a nineteenth-century imitation. That's not German trade work. That's something else."

He set it down. His hands had changed. They had been professional hands, competent, unhurried. Now they were careful hands. Reverent. The hands of a man who has been told that the object on his bench might be by someone whose name he knows.

"This needs work," David said. "The cracks need internal studs, glued from inside -- weeks of careful work. The soundpost reset. New bridge, new strings, the pegs checked, the seams re-glued. It's substantial." He looked at Bob. "But it's worth doing. It's very worth doing."

## Chapter 15: "First Lesson"

## Chapter 16: "Cremona, 1715"

The hands woke before the man. They always did. In the dark of the bedroom above the workshop, Antonio Stradivari lay on his back and felt his fingers stir against the linen sheet — the knuckles swollen, the joints stiff with the particular cold of late autumn in the Po Valley, where the damp rose from the river and settled into the stone walls and from the stone walls into the bones of anyone old enough to have stopped resisting it. He was seventy-one. He had been making violins for fifty-three years. Every morning now began with this negotiation between his will and his hands, and every morning the hands won eventually, loosening by degrees, the index finger of the left hand uncurling from the permanent crook that decades of holding a chisel against maple had carved into the tendon. He waited. Beside him, Antonia breathed the slow, even breath of a woman twenty years younger who did not need to bargain with her body for the right to begin the day.

He rose without waking her. Down the stairs, the stone cold under his bare feet — a cold he had felt ten thousand times, and which no longer registered as discomfort but simply as the texture of descent, of the passage from sleep to work, from the horizontal life to the vertical one. The workshop occupied the ground floor of the house on Piazza San Domenico, the same room he had worked in since he was a young man copying the arching templates of his teacher Nicola Amati and thinking, with the quiet certainty that was his defining characteristic, that he could do better. He had done better. He had done better for half a century, and he was not finished.

He lit a candle. Then a second, placing it on the far side of the bench so the light fell across the work from two angles, the shadows cast by the instrument's curves doubling and shifting as the flames steadied. The smell of the workshop entered him the way a prayer enters a man who has prayed the same words for fifty years: not through attention but through the body, through the lungs and the skin. Spruce shavings. Hide glue, its faintly animal warmth. The mineral tang of the varnish ingredients in their stoneware jars along the back wall — the dragon's blood, the turpentine, the linseed oil, the colophony, and the others, the ones he did not name even to his sons, the ground coat that penetrated the wood and did something to its voice that he could hear but not explain and would never write down.

He put on the white linen apron. The white cap. He stood before the bench.

On it, clamped in a cradle of his own design — a simple thing, two curved blocks of walnut lined with felt, holding the body without touching the plates — was a violin in the late stages of construction. The top plate of Alpine spruce was fitted and glued, the bass bar set, the purfling inlaid in its narrow channel around the edge, three strips of wood — black, white, black — bent and laid with a precision that would survive three centuries of handling and still be identifiable as his. The back plate was maple, the flame figure running diagonally across the surface in a pattern that caught the candlelight and released it, caught it and released it, as though the wood were breathing. Today he would work on the scroll.

The scroll was the signature. Every maker's hand could be read in the volute's curve, in the depth of the fluting, in the tightness of the final turn — the way every man's character could be read in his handwriting, if you knew how to look. Stradivari's scrolls were deeper-cut than Amati's, more assertive, the chamfers sharper, the eye of the volute slightly larger. He had been carving them for fifty years. Each one was slightly different. Each one was unmistakably his. He did not think of this as artistry. The word would not have occurred to him, and if it had, he would have found it imprecise. He was a maker of instruments. His father had been a maker of instruments. The genius — if it was genius, and he would not have used that word either — was in the hands, in the accumulated knowledge of tools and materials and the ten thousand small decisions that separated a good violin from a great one, and a great one from whatever his instruments were, the ones that left his workshop and went into the world and never came back because no one who owned one would willingly let it go.

He picked up the gouge. He tested the edge against his thumb — a habit so old it preceded thought, the way breathing precedes thought. The steel was sharp. He turned the scroll toward the light, studied the line of the volute where he had stopped yesterday, and began to cut.

The maple parted without sound. The curl fell to the bench in a tight, pale spiral. And Antonio Stradivari, seventy-one years old, in the forty-first year of his workshop on Piazza San Domenico, in the city of Cremona, in the Duchy of Milan, in the early morning of a day in late autumn in the year seventeen hundred and fifteen, did the thing he had done every day of his adult life. He made a violin. He made it a little better than the last one. And then, when this one was finished, he would make the next.

## Chapter 17: "The Cliff Face"

The studio was above a Thai restaurant on Rokeby Road, and the stairwell always smelled of lemongrass and coconut milk, a combination that Bob would associate for the rest of his life with the sound of the violin. He climbed the stairs on a Thursday afternoon in March, six months into his lessons with Katarina, carrying the case in his left hand and a bottle of water in his right, and he was thinking about the Gigue — the passage in the second half where the key shifted and his third finger had to stretch for a note he could not yet reach cleanly — and he was not thinking about anything else. Not the fund. Not the fourteen messages on his phone from the associate, or the three from investors he had not returned, or the compliance document on his desk that needed a signature he kept forgetting to provide. For the forty minutes of the lesson, and the fifteen minutes before it when he warmed up in the car, and the twenty minutes after it when he sat in the car park and replayed what Katarina had told him, the fund did not exist. It was the only hour in his week when he was not afraid.

He opened the door. The studio was a single room with a north-facing window that let in the last of the afternoon light — a warm, golden light in summer that was now, in early autumn, beginning to soften and cool. A music stand. A straight-backed chair. A metronome on the windowsill that Katarina never used because, she said, a metronome teaches you to keep time and she wanted to teach him to keep music, which was not the same thing. The walls were bare except for a framed photograph of Janacek that Bob had learned not to ask about, and a small mirror she sometimes positioned so he could watch his bowing arm. The room smelled of rosin and old carpet and, faintly, of the lemongrass rising through the floor.

Katarina was sitting with her violin on her lap, which was unusual. Normally she stood when he arrived, greeted him, asked about his practice week — a ritual of transition, she called it, the bridge between the world outside and the world in this room. Today she was seated, her instrument across her thighs, her bow resting against the chair leg, and she was looking at him with an expression he could not read. Not the attentive, slightly amused expression of a teacher assessing a student. Something else. Something that had weight.

"Sit down," she said.

He sat. He had not opened his case. He held it on his knees like a man in a waiting room.

She lifted her violin. No preamble. No explanation. She placed it under her chin with the economy of a woman who had done this ten thousand times, and she drew the bow across the strings.

The sound that came out was not like anything he had heard in this room. He had heard her demonstrate passages — a phrase here, a correction there, always in service of his playing, always pedagogical. This was different. She was not demonstrating. She was playing.

Four notes. A chord. D, A, D, F-sharp, struck from the bottom and rolled upward, the bow breaking the chord because a violin cannot sustain four notes at once and must choose, must move, must let the lower voices die so the upper ones can sing. The chord filled the room the way a stone fills a pool — not gradually but all at once, displacing everything, the air, the silence, the lemongrass, the traffic on Rokeby Road, the entire ordinary world pushed to the walls and held there by the sound. Then the descent. The bass voice stepping down — D, C-sharp, B-flat, A — and above it the melody, the upper voice, beginning its long fall into the theme that would, Bob did not yet know, consume the next two years of his life and then end it.

She played eight bars. Two statements of the theme. Then she stopped.

The bow lifted from the string. The last note — a D, the tonic, home — hung in the room for a moment and then was gone, and the silence that followed was not the absence of sound but its ghost, the shape the music had made still pressing against the walls, still displacing the air.

Bob did not speak. His hands, resting on the violin case, were trembling.

"That," Katarina said, "is where we're going. Eventually."

She set her violin down. She looked at him. He understood later that she had chosen this moment deliberately — had waited six months, had assessed his progress, his seriousness, his capacity for what she was about to unleash — but in the moment he understood nothing except that the sound she had made was the sound he had been listening for his entire life without knowing it existed, and that he would do anything, sacrifice anything, to make it himself.

"What is it?" he said. His voice was wrong. Too quiet, too tight. As if the music had compressed something in his chest.

"The Chaconne. Bach. Partita Number Two in D minor. The last movement." She paused. "Fifteen minutes of unaccompanied solo violin. I've been working on it myself for years. I'm not ready to perform it." She looked at the window, at the last of the gold draining from the sky. "No one is ever really ready."

"Play it again," he said.

It was not a question.

She did not play it again. She told him to go home. She told him to find the Milstein recording — the 1972 one, she specified, not the earlier version — and to listen to it alone, at night, with headphones. Then they would talk.

He drove home. He did not remember the drive.

## Chapter 18: "Recordings"

The headphones were Jeannie's — Bose, noise-cancelling, bought for a flight to Bali three years ago and abandoned in a drawer because she preferred to sleep on planes rather than listen to anything. Bob had found them while looking for a phone charger. They fit too tightly across the crown of his head and pressed against his temples with a low, constant ache that he would later be unable to separate from the music itself, as though Bach required a certain amount of pain as the price of admission.

It was past midnight. The house on Marine Parade was asleep — Jeannie in her room with the door closed, Chase somewhere out, Shanel's light off since ten. Bob sat at the desk in the study, which was not really a study anymore but a room where Jeannie stored overflow clothes and Bob kept the computer he used for fund administration and, now, for the only work that mattered. The desk lamp was off. The screen was the only light, its blue glow turning his face into something hollowed and strange, like a man seen underwater. YouTube was open. The search bar read: "Bach Chaconne Milstein 1972."

He pressed play. Not for the first time. Not for the fifth. He had been listening to the Chaconne for eleven days, since the afternoon in Katarina's studio when she had played the opening eight bars and the floor had dropped out from under his life. Eleven days of the same piece, the same recordings, the same fourteen minutes repeating like a loop in his skull, and each time he heard something the previous listening had hidden, as though the music were a building with rooms he had not yet entered, corridors he had not yet walked, and each repetition opened another door.

He had a notebook. A ruled Moleskine he'd bought at the newsagent in Claremont, the kind of thing he had never owned and had no use for in his professional life, where everything was digital, trackable, deletable. In this notebook he wrote in handwriting that had not improved since school:

*2:14 — chord change, Milstein waits before resolving. Why? The silence before the note is part of the note.*

*3:47 — bass voice disappears for four bars, then returns. You don't notice it's gone until it comes back. Like breathing.*

*5:22 — the run in sixteenths. Hahn plays it like a river. Milstein plays it like a man walking through a river. Different thing entirely.*

*9:12 — THE SHIFT. D major. Bar 133. Every recording, every time, this is the moment. Like stepping from a dark room into sunlight. Milstein plays it quieter than anyone else. This is wrong, it should be louder, but it is right. The light doesn't shout. The light just arrives.*

He was on his seventh recording now. He had started with Milstein because Katarina told him to, and Milstein had broken him open — the tone autumnal, philosophical, the playing of an old man who had lived with this piece for fifty years and no longer needed to prove anything, who played it the way you tell a story you have told a thousand times, not for the listener but for the story itself, because the story needs to be told. Then Hilary Hahn, and the shock of her: seventeen years old on her debut album, technically flawless, the notes struck with a clarity that was almost violent, as though she were cutting the music into the air with a scalpel. Where Milstein was warm, Hahn was luminous. Where Milstein breathed, Hahn burned.

Then Perlman. The tone like honey poured over wood — gorgeous, generous, almost too beautiful, so beautiful that Bob found himself listening to the sound rather than the architecture, and he wrote in the notebook: *Perlman makes you love the violin. Milstein makes you love the music. Not the same thing.* Then Heifetz, and the terror of Heifetz: the speed, the ferocity, the sense of a man who played the Chaconne the way a general takes a city, with total commitment and no mercy and no interest in whether the conquered territory loved him for it.

He turned the page. He wrote: *What am I hearing? Not the notes. The person. Each recording is the same 256 bars and each one is a different life.*

## Chapter 19: "The Soundproofed Room"

At seven-thirty on a Tuesday morning in May, Bob Harrington swiped his key card at the ground-floor entrance of 108 St George's Terrace, walked through the lobby past the security guard who nodded without interest, and took the lift to the fourth floor. The corridor was empty. It was always empty at this hour — the accountancy firm on level three did not start until nine, and the recruitment agency at the far end had been vacant since February, its frosted glass door dark, a small drift of uncollected mail fanning across the carpet beneath the slot. Bob turned left at the fire-exit sign, unlocked the office, and stepped inside.

The office was two rooms divided by a wall he had paid a contractor to install eighteen months ago, citing the need for a private meeting space. The contractor, a sun-ruined man from Balcatta who chewed gum and did not ask questions, had also installed the acoustic foam — fifty panels of dark grey polyurethane, glued in staggered rows to the walls and ceiling of the inner room, transforming it from a standard commercial space with a window into something that looked like the inside of a recording studio, or a padded cell, depending on your disposition. The foam absorbed reflections. It ate echoes. It turned the room into a sealed acoustic environment where sound went in and did not come out, and where the outside world — the traffic on the Terrace, the construction crane on the next block, the phone that rang and rang on the other side of the wall — could not enter.

Bob set his briefcase on the desk in the outer room. The desk held a Bloomberg terminal, dark. A telephone with a red message light blinking — nine messages, he knew without checking, because there had been nine yesterday and he had not listened to them and by tomorrow there would be eleven. A stack of compliance documents in a manila folder. A framed photograph of Jeannie and the children at Rottnest that Jeannie had placed there six years ago and that Bob had not moved because moving it would require having a reason, and having a reason would require a conversation he did not want to have. He did not turn on the Bloomberg. He did not check the messages. He opened the inner door and stepped into the soundproofed room.

The transformation of this space had happened gradually, then all at once, in the way of things that are denied and then surrendered to. First the foam. Then the music stand — a heavy Manhasset, not the flimsy wire kind, positioned at the centre of the room where the acoustic response was most even. Then the metronome, a Wittner mechanical that he wound each morning with a small key, preferring its wooden tick to the sterile pulse of a digital app. Then the mirror — a full-length mirror propped against the far wall so he could watch his bowing arm, his posture, the angle of his left elbow, all the things Katarina corrected each week and that crept back into error between lessons like weeds reclaiming a garden. Then the notebook, open on the stand beside the score. Then the stretching mat on the floor, because his shoulders had begun to ache after the first hour. Then the ice pack in the small bar fridge he had moved from the outer office, because his forearm had begun to burn after the second.

He opened the case. The violin lay in its blue velvet — Franz's violin, the one from Absam, its varnish the colour of dark honey, the scroll carved with a precision that still stopped him each time he looked at it, the volute's spiral tightening into an eye that seemed to watch him as he lifted the instrument free. He rosined the bow. He tightened the hair — a quarter-turn, the tension something his thumb had learned to measure without thought, the first of the body's small capitulations to the instrument. He placed the violin under his chin. The chinrest pressed the spot on his jaw that was now permanently marked — a reddish-brown patch of roughened skin that Jeannie had not noticed, or had noticed and not mentioned, or had noticed and filed away with the other accumulating evidence that her husband was becoming someone she did not recognise.

He played a D minor scale. Slowly. Two octaves up, two octaves down. Each note placed with deliberate care, the bow drawing the sound from the string the way a brush draws paint from a palette — not scraped, not forced, but invited. He listened. The E was slightly sharp. He adjusted. The B-flat had a thinness he did not like, a reedy quality in the upper half of the bow, and he corrected the pressure, adding weight from the index finger, feeling the string resist and then yield. The room absorbed every imperfection and returned nothing but the truth.

This was his real work now. The fund — the emails, the calls, the fabricated quarterly report that sat in the outer room like a grenade with the pin half-pulled — was the interruption. The fraud that had grown around him like scar tissue, layer upon layer of lies calcified into a structure that looked from the outside like a business, was the thing that kept him from the only honest labour of his life. In this room, with this instrument, there was no performance. The violin did not care about his net worth or his wife's opinion or the blinking message light. The violin cared only about the truth of the sound, and the sound could not be faked.

## Chapter 20: "Injuries"

He woke at three in the morning with his left forearm on fire.

Not fire — that was wrong, that was the language of metaphor, and the pain was not metaphorical. It was specific, anatomical, a deep burning ache that ran from the inner crease of his elbow along the belly of the forearm to the base of his wrist, following the line of the flexor tendons with a precision that would have impressed the anatomy textbook he had ordered from Amazon and read at his desk between practice sessions, trying to understand what was happening inside his own arm. He knew what this was. He had been reading about it for weeks with the morbid fascination of a man studying a disease he suspects he already has. Tendonitis. Flexor tendinopathy. The violinist's occupational curse — the inflammation of the tendons that connect the forearm muscles to the fingers, caused by repetitive gripping, by hours of pressing steel strings against an ebony fingerboard, by the relentless demand of the left hand to hold shapes it was not designed to hold for durations the body was not built to sustain.

He lay in the dark and tried to straighten his fingers. They would not straighten. The ring finger and the little finger had curled inward during sleep, contracting into a loose fist, and when he attempted to extend them the pain flared from a burn to a shriek — a bright, electric shriek that ran up the tendon and into the muscle belly and stopped his breath. He let them curl back. He lay still. He breathed.

Jeannie was asleep in her room. The door between their bedrooms — the connecting door that had been installed when they renovated in 2016, and that Jeannie kept closed from her side, the lock unnecessary because the closed door was itself the statement — was shut. No light beneath it. No sound. She slept well, Jeannie. She slept the deep, medicated sleep of a woman on sertraline and sauvignon blanc, a chemical partnership that carried her under each night at eleven and released her at seven, refreshed, functional, armoured for the day. Bob envied that sleep. He had not slept through a night in months — not since the practice had intensified, not since the Chaconne had moved from something he was learning to something that was learning him, colonising his hours, reshaping his body, demanding from his muscles and tendons and joints a payment he was discovering he could not afford.

He held his left arm against his chest with his right hand, cradling it the way you cradle a wounded animal, and he thought: *I can't play.*

The two most terrifying words he knew. More terrifying than *ASIC inquiry* or *structural insolvency* or *redemption request*. More terrifying than anything Jeannie had ever said to him, or anything the silence between them had ever meant. He could survive the fund's collapse — he had been surviving it in slow motion for a decade, carrying the knowledge of it like a stone in his chest, and the stone had become so familiar that he sometimes forgot it was there. He could survive Jeannie's contempt, because he had been surviving it for twenty years, and survival was the thing he was best at, the thing his entire life had been organised around: the absorption of damage without visible effect, the quiet endurance that passed for competence, for stability, for the kind of dependable blandness that people trusted with their money and their daughters.

But he could not survive silence. Not this silence — not the silence of the soundproofed room with the violin in its case, unplayed. Not the silence of the Chaconne interrupted at bar ninety-seven, or bar one hundred and forty, or wherever the tendon chose to seize, the music stopping mid-phrase like a sentence cut off before the verb, the meaning left hanging in the acoustic foam with nowhere to go. That silence would kill him. Not literally, not yet, but in the way that matters — the slow death of the only part of himself he had ever found, the part that had been buried for thirty years under chinos and compliance documents and the smooth, practised smile of a man who managed other people's money because he could not manage his own life.

He turned onto his back. The ceiling was dark. Somewhere outside, the Indian Ocean moved against the shore — the low, constant sound of Cottesloe at night, the sound that had accompanied every sleepless hour in this house, every 3 AM calculation of the fund's shortfall, every predawn walk across Marine Parade to stand on the terraced grass and not-think. He listened to the ocean. He held his arm.

Tomorrow he would see the physiotherapist — the one in Subiaco, not the sports medicine clinic in Claremont where the rugby players went and where he might be recognised. He would explain that he played the violin, that he played four to six hours a day, that the pain had been building for three weeks and was now acute. The physiotherapist would tell him to stop. Everyone would tell him to stop. Katarina would tell him to reduce his practice to ninety minutes, with breaks, with stretching, with ice. The internet forums — the violin forums he read late at night, the threads titled "Tendonitis: How long before I can play again?" and "Left arm pain — should I be worried?" — would tell him to rest for two weeks minimum, to do gentle stretches, to apply ice twenty minutes on, twenty minutes off, to take ibuprofen, and above all, above everything, to stop playing until the inflammation subsided.

Two weeks. Fourteen days without the Chaconne. Fourteen days without the room, the stand, the scroll watching him from the case.

He closed his eyes. The pain in his forearm pulsed with his heartbeat, a slow, deep throb that felt like a clock counting down to something he could not name.

## Chapter 21: "Busking"

The hoodie was from Vinnies — the St Vincent de Paul on Stirling Highway in Claremont, two suburbs from his own, chosen specifically because no one he knew would be caught dead in a Vinnies. It was grey, size large, shapeless in the way of charity-shop clothes that have been washed and donated and washed again until all memory of their original owner has been laundered out of them. The jeans were from the same rack — dark blue, stiff, the wrong cut, sitting too high on his waist in a style no one under sixty would choose deliberately. The baseball cap was plain black, no logo, pulled low. The sunglasses were aviators from a chemist, the lenses dark enough to hide his eyes and cheap enough that the frames pinched the bridge of his nose. He had dressed in the office bathroom, folding his linen shirt and chinos into his briefcase and standing for a moment in front of the mirror in clothes that cost eleven dollars and made him look like a man who might sleep in a park, or wait in line at Centrelink, or do any of the things that Bob Harrington had spent his entire adult life arranging not to do.

He did not recognise himself. That was the point.

It was a Thursday, just past noon. The lunch crowd on Murray Street Mall was at its densest — office workers streaming out of the glass towers on St George's Terrace and the Hay Street arcades, crossing into the pedestrian zone with its plane trees and its public art and its buskers spaced at intervals along the paving, each occupying a patch of territory staked out by long custom and unspoken agreement. A man with a guitar at the Hay Street end, playing something by Fleetwood Mac. A woman with a cello near Forrest Place, her case open, a hand-lettered sign reading BACH & BAROSSA: TIPS APPRECIATED. Bob had studied the mall's geography over three lunchtime reconnaissance trips, watching from a bench with a takeaway coffee, noting the foot traffic patterns, the sight lines, the spots where the acoustics bounced off the shopfronts and carried versus the spots where the sound bled into the open air and died.

He had chosen his position: midway along the mall, between the entrance to the Carillon City arcade and the old post office, where a slight indentation in the building facade created a shallow alcove that caught the sound and projected it outward. He set the case on the ground. Open, lid back, the blue velvet lining facing up. He placed a single gold coin in the case — a two-dollar piece, for suggestion, the way Katarina had told him buskers did it in Prague, a seed to show that payment was welcome and its denomination didn't matter.

He lifted the violin. His hands were shaking, but not from tendonitis — that had subsided after the two-week rest, the stretches, the ice, the reduced practice regime that Katarina had imposed and that he had followed with the desperate obedience of a man who had learned what it meant to lose the thing he loved. The shaking was something else. Stage fright. The terror of exposure that had stopped him performing at fifteen, that had kept the violin in its case for twenty-seven years, that had driven him to build a soundproofed room rather than let a single note escape into a world that might judge it.

He raised the bow. He placed it on the D string. He drew the opening chord of the Chaconne.

The sound bounced off the glass shopfront behind him and rolled out into the mall — four notes, broken from bass to treble, the D minor chord that Bach had written three hundred years ago for a single player in an empty room, now sounding in the open air of a Perth shopping precinct on a Thursday lunchtime among a thousand people who had never heard of the Chaconne and did not look up from their phones.

No one stopped. A woman in a charcoal suit walked past so close her handbag brushed the open case. A man in high-vis workwear glanced in his direction, registered nothing, looked away. Two teenagers with frozen yoghurt wandered past, their earbuds in, sealed inside their own music. The Fleetwood Mac guitarist down the mall was getting more attention, which was right, which was the order of things — you play what people know, what people can hum, what people can drop a coin for without breaking stride. You do not play the most demanding piece in the solo violin repertoire to a crowd that wants "Dreams" and a flat white.

Bob played on. He played the opening theme — the four bars, the descending bass, the first variation — and the sound was rough, imperfect, the outdoor acoustics stripping away the warmth that the soundproofed room had given him, exposing every flaw in his intonation, every unevenness in his bow, the way strong light exposes the cracks in a face that looked fine in the bathroom mirror. But he played. He stood in his Vinnies hoodie and his chemist sunglasses and he played Bach on Murray Street Mall, and the anonymity was a liberation he had not expected — no one knew him, no one cared, he was invisible in the way that buskers and homeless people and anyone without economic utility is invisible in a city that measures human value in square footage and school fees.

## Chapter 22: "The Gap"

She had told him to play the D major section, and he had played it — bars 133 through 208, the middle movement of the Chaconne, the passage where the music lifts from D minor into D major and the entire emotional landscape shifts, the way weather shifts when a cloud bank moves and the sun finds the ground. He had played it from memory, which was itself new — until a month ago he had needed the score in front of him, the notation a safety net he clung to even when his fingers knew the way, afraid that without the printed page the music would abandon him midphrase and leave him standing in silence with the bow raised and nothing to say. Katarina had taken the score away. She had closed it and placed it on the windowsill and said, "The music is in you. Stop reading it and start hearing it." He had been terrified. He had played the first eight bars from memory, stumbled on a chord in bar 141, and stopped.

"Again," she said.

He had played it again. The same eight bars, and then further — bar 141, the chord clean this time, the voices separating, the soprano floating above the bass the way Katarina had demonstrated week after week, "hear the upper voice as a singer, a woman's voice, and the bass as the ground she walks on." He played through bar 150, bar 160, the variations growing in complexity, the double stops climbing, and somewhere — bar 168, or bar 172, he could not later say exactly where — something happened.

The music breathed.

Not the way it had breathed before, which was the breathing of effort, of a man managing the mechanics of bow and string and left hand and intonation and dynamics while also trying to remember the next note and the note after that and the shape of the phrase and the shape of the section and the shape of the whole fifteen-minute arc. That breathing was the breathing of a swimmer counting strokes. This was different. This was the breathing of a man who had stopped counting. For six bars — maybe eight, he could not say, because the quality of the experience was precisely that he was not counting, not measuring, not managing — the violin sang and Bob disappeared into it the way he used to disappear as a boy in Mrs Albrecht's studio in the music wing at Scotch, the way he had disappeared in the car on Rokeby Road the first time he heard Milstein play the Chaconne, the way he disappeared into the ocean when he swam at dawn off Cottesloe in the winter, when the water was so cold it erased everything except the body's animal insistence on being alive.

He came back. The bow reached the end of bar 176, or bar 178, and the spell broke — his third finger missed a shift, the E was flat, and he was suddenly a man playing a violin again, aware of the room, the acoustic foam, the mirror, his own reflection with its furrowed brow and the red mark on his jaw and the sweat on his upper lip. He lowered the instrument.

Katarina was silent. She stood by the window, her own violin hanging at her side, and she did not speak for a long time. Long enough that the silence became its own presence in the room, the way a rest in music is not the absence of sound but its frame, the thing that gives the notes around it their meaning.

"You stopped trying," she said.

He looked at her.

"You let the music play you." She paused. "That's what I hear in the great recordings — Milstein, Szeryng, the very best. Not a person playing the Chaconne. The Chaconne playing through a person. You did that. For six bars, maybe eight. You did that."

Bob sat down. His legs were trembling. Not from exertion — from the aftermath of something he did not have language for, the same wordless trembling he had felt in the car on Rokeby Road, the same trembling that had seized him in Franz's workshop in Absam when he first drew the bow across the strings of this violin and the sound that came back was not his but the instrument's, three hundred years of resonance stored in the grain of Alpine spruce and released into the air by the contact of horsehair and rosin and the imperfect, desperate, fully human pressure of his hand.

"Can I do it again?" he said.

Katarina smiled. It was not the polished smile of a teacher praising a student. It was something rarer — the smile of a musician who has witnessed something she had begun to think she would never see.

"You can't make it happen," she said. "That's the whole point. You can only stop preventing it."

## Chapter 23: "The Authentication"

He sat at the desk in the outer office — the fund side, the side with the Bloomberg terminal and the blinking phone and the compliance folders and the photograph of Jeannie and the children at Rottnest — and he composed an email. He had been composing it for three days. Not writing it — composing it, the way a man composes a letter to a lover, or a resignation, or an apology that might save his life: choosing each word, weighing each sentence, reading it back and deleting and starting again, because this email mattered more than any communication he had ever sent, more than the quarterly reports with their fabricated returns, more than the investor letters with their reassuring prose, more than the love note he had written Jeannie on their first anniversary that she had kept in a drawer for a decade and then, during one of her reorganisations, thrown away.

The recipient was a name he had found on a website: Bein & Fushi, Chicago, one of the foremost dealers and authenticators of fine stringed instruments in the world. He had also drafted a version addressed to Peter Biddulph in London, and a third to Tarisio, the auction house that had sold more Stradivari instruments than anyone alive. He would send only one. He did not want the enquiry to circulate, to become gossip in a world that was, he had learned from his reading, surprisingly small — a few hundred people worldwide who could tell a Stradivari from a Guarneri from a Stainer from a good Cremonese copy, and who talked to each other constantly, the way people in any rarefied field talk, trading rumours and sightings and the occasional breathless story of a lost instrument surfacing in an attic or a garage sale or a small shop in a village in the Austrian Alps.

*Dear Mr. Bein,*

*I am writing to request your professional assessment of a violin in my possession. The instrument was purchased in 2023 from a luthier in Absam, Austria, who described it as a fine Tyrolean instrument of uncertain age. Since acquiring it, I have had it examined by a Perth-based luthier who believes it to be of early eighteenth-century Cremonese origin, possibly from the school of Stradivari.*

He stopped. Deleted "possibly from the school of Stradivari." Too much. Too soon. The man would stop reading, or worse, he would laugh — the weary laugh of an expert who receives a hundred emails a year from hopeful amateurs who think their grandfather's violin is a Strad because the label says *Antonius Stradivarius Cremonensis Faciebat Anno 17—* and a date that may or may not be legible. Bob knew the label meant nothing. Millions of violins carried Stradivari labels — they had been pasted into cheap factory instruments since the nineteenth century, a tradition of aspirational forgery so widespread that it was not even considered fraudulent, merely conventional, the violin world's version of a designer knockoff. He was not writing about the label.

He was writing about the wood. The varnish. The scroll.

He opened the photographs on his laptop. He had taken them two nights ago, alone in the soundproofed room after the last practice session, using the desk lamp to create raking light across the surfaces, the kind of oblique illumination that revealed what direct light concealed. The scroll from four angles — front, left, right, and from below, looking up into the pegbox where the chisel marks were visible, the precise cuts of a hand that knew exactly how deep to go and exactly when to stop. The f-holes in close-up, their elegant S-curves slightly asymmetric in the way that all hand-cut f-holes are asymmetric and that the best authenticators can read like a signature. The varnish in raking light, its surface crazed with a network of fine cracks — craquelure, the word was, the pattern of aging that forms over centuries as the organic compounds in the varnish oxidise and contract, a pattern that cannot be faked because it cannot be hurried, because it is the signature not of a maker but of time itself.

The back plate. The maple's flame figure, the tight curls of grain running diagonally across the surface, catching the light and releasing it in waves. The interior, photographed through the left f-hole with a phone torch: the bass bar, the linings, the corner blocks, the subtle graduation marks where the maker had carved the plates to their final thickness, the wood thinner at the edges and thicker at the centre in a pattern that determined the instrument's voice as surely as the shape of a larynx determines a singer's.

He attached the photographs. He resumed writing.

*I attach photographs taken in controlled lighting conditions. I am particularly interested in your assessment of the scroll carving, the arching profile, the varnish composition and craquelure pattern, and the f-hole geometry. I understand that a definitive attribution would require physical examination and dendrochronological analysis, and I am prepared to arrange both.*

He read it back. It was too formal, too stiff, the language of a man who had spent his career writing investor correspondence and could not shed the register. But it was also precise, and precision was what these people respected — not enthusiasm, not hope, but the careful, evidence-based language of someone who understood the difference between asking "Is this a Stradivarius?" and asking "What can you tell me about this instrument?"

He changed the subject line. He had tried *Instrument Assessment Request* and *Authentication Enquiry* and *Violin — Possible Early 18th Century Cremonese*. He settled on: *Request for Assessment — Violin of Unknown Provenance, Possibly Cremonese*.

His finger hovered over the send button. His hand was shaking. Not from tendonitis — that had receded, managed now, a chronic companion rather than an acute crisis. His hand was shaking because this email would begin a process he could not control and could not take back, a process that would end in one of two verdicts: the violin was what he suspected it was, and everything changed, or the violin was not what he suspected, and nothing changed except the loss of a hope he had not admitted to himself he was carrying.

He pressed send.

## Chapter 24: "The Invitation"

The last note — a D, low, open, the final chord of the Chaconne resolved to its single tonic voice — hung in the soundproofed room and then was gone, absorbed by the acoustic foam, eaten by the silence, and Bob stood with the violin at his side and the bow in his right hand and did not move.

He had played the complete Chaconne from memory. All 256 bars. All fifteen minutes. No stops, no restarts, no moments where the music collapsed and he had to find his place in the score and begin the passage again. He had played it from the opening chord — the four-note D minor break that he had spent a year learning to voice, the bottom two notes dying as the bow rolled to the upper two, the sound not clean, never entirely clean, but clear enough now that the chord's architecture was audible, the harmonics ringing in the room like a bell struck underwater — through the thirty-two variations of the first section, the descending bass, the building intensity, the arpeggiated climbs and the thirty-second-note runs that had defeated him for months and that he could now navigate, if not with grace then with conviction. Through the D major section — the passage that still, every time, stopped his breath, the shift from minor to major that Katarina called the most beautiful transition in all of music and that he played now with a tenderness he could not explain and did not need to, because the tenderness was not his but the music's, the quality inherent in the notes that emerged when a player stopped imposing himself on the sound and let the sound impose itself on him. Through the return to D minor, the bariolage — the obsessive string crossing in the final section that sounded, when it was played well, like a single voice screaming and singing at once, and that sounded, when he played it, like a man trying very hard to do something impossible and almost, almost, succeeding. The bariolage was uneven. The high-position passage in variation twenty-two was slightly flat. The final chords, the great stacked harmonies that brought the piece home to its opening key, were rough at the edges, the bow pressure a fraction too heavy, the voicing imperfect.

But he had played it. Beginning to end. Fifteen minutes without a break.

He stood at the window. The soundproofed room had one window, facing west, and through it he could see the Swan River turning gold in the last light, the water shifting from grey to amber as the sun dropped toward the Indian Ocean beyond Fremantle. The Norfolk pines on the South Perth foreshore were silhouettes now, black against the burning sky. A ferry crossed the river, small and white, leaving a wake that caught the light and held it. ## Chapter 25: "Kothen, Autumn 1720"

The ink had dried unevenly on the final bar. He noticed this as he noticed everything -- the slight pooling where the nib had rested a fraction too long on the downstroke of the last note, the faint bleed where the iron gall had found a weakness in the paper's sizing. He would need to touch it up. Not now. Later, when the page had set and he could lay a straightedge along the staff line and correct the stem without smudging what was beneath it. These were the concerns that occupied him: the practical, the material, the solvable. A man could lose himself in such concerns, and Bach had been losing himself in them for three months, ever since the afternoon he had walked into his house in Kothen and found it rearranged by grief into a place he did not recognise.

The study was cold. October had come in dry and sharp, the light through the leaded window arriving at a lower angle each morning, throwing the shadow of the mullion across the desk in a slow diagonal that he had learned to read like a clock. He had been here since before dawn, working by candle and then by the thin yellow light that crept over the rooftops of the town and found his window and lay across the ruled paper like a hand. The candle was still burning. He pinched it out between his thumb and forefinger -- the callus there thick enough that he felt only pressure, not heat -- and the smoke rose in a single thread and dissolved.

On the desk before him, arranged in the order he had copied them, lay the pages of the Sei Solo. Six works for violin without accompaniment. Three sonatas, three partitas, each in its own key, each complete, each copied in the careful hand he reserved for fair copies -- the hand that was not composing but transcribing, setting down in permanent form what had already been worked out in his head and in his fingers and in the rough drafts that lay in a separate pile, crosshatched with corrections, and which he would burn when the fair copy was finished, because he had no use for process, only for results.

The second partita lay open. D minor. Five movements: Allemande, Courante, Sarabande, Gigue, and then the thing at the end, the final movement, the one that had taken longer than the other four combined and that he had finished -- or believed he had finished -- in the early hours of this morning, writing the last bars by candlelight while the house slept above him and the town slept around him and the only sound was the scratch of the quill and his own breathing and, somewhere outside, a dog barking at nothing.

He did not know what he had written. This is the truth that must be set down plainly, because everything that follows -- three centuries of performance and scholarship and reverence and argument -- depends on it. Johann Sebastian Bach, Kapellmeister to His Serene Highness the Prince of Anhalt-Kothen, aged thirty-five, seated at his desk on a cold October morning in a small German town, did not know that he had just completed the greatest single movement ever composed for solo violin. He knew that the movement was long -- longer than any chaconne he had written or encountered, longer than the four movements that preceded it combined. He knew that it was technically formidable, that it demanded from the player a sustained concentration and a polyphonic imagination that went beyond anything the instrument was designed to produce. He knew that the central section, where the key shifted from D minor to D major, achieved something he had been reaching for -- a luminosity, a sense of arrival, as though the music had been travelling through a dark passage and had emerged into open air. He knew these things because he was a craftsman of supreme skill and experience, and a craftsman knows when the work is good.

But he did not know what the work meant. He did not know that it would be called the Everest of the violin repertoire, that Brahms would write to Clara Schumann that the mere conception of it would have driven him out of his mind, that violinists would spend their entire careers preparing to play it and never feel ready. He did not know that scholars would hear in it a lament for his dead wife, that a German musicologist would claim to find hymns of mourning encoded in its notation, that the word *chaconne* would become, for many who heard the piece, synonymous with grief made bearable through form. He did not know any of this because he was not thinking about posterity. He was thinking about the ink.

Maria Barbara's chair stood against the wall by the window. It had been her chair -- the straight-backed wooden chair where she sat in the evenings while he worked, mending clothes or reading or simply being present in the room, her presence a warmth he had not understood as warmth until it was gone. No one sat in it now. Catharina Dorothea had tried, once, in the first week, and he had said nothing, but something in his face -- or in the room's silence -- had told her not to do it again. The chair remained. A shawl hung over its back, something dark and woollen that still held, if you pressed your face to it, which he did not, a trace of the woman who had worn it.

He stood. The stiffness in his lower back -- hours of sitting, the forward lean of a man who writes with his whole body -- announced itself as he straightened. He gathered the pages of the partita, aligned their edges with a tap against the desk, and placed them on the shelf beside the others. The Sei Solo was nearly complete. He needed only to write the title page, and then it would be finished, and he could turn to other things. There were always other things. The prince wanted new chamber works for the ensemble. The organ at the Jakobskirche needed attention. Wilhelm Friedemann's keyboard studies required supervision -- the boy was gifted and lazy, a combination that Bach found more alarming than either quality alone.

He walked to the door of the study. In the kitchen, he could hear Catharina Dorothea's voice, low and patient, directing her brothers through the morning routine that had once been managed by hands other than hers. The sound of small beer being poured. A spoon against a bowl. The ordinary machinery of a household that had broken and was learning, with the slow, graceless determination of all broken things, to function again.

He would go to them. He would eat. He would check on the children and then return to the study and write the title page of the Sei Solo and date it 1720, the year his wife died, the year the greatest music he would ever write poured out of him in a silence so deep that he mistook it for concentration. And then he would go on, because going on was what he did, what his faith required, what his craft demanded, and because the children needed shoes and the prince needed music and the world -- indifferent, continuous, ordinary -- was waiting for him outside the study door.

He did not look back at the chair by the window. He did not need to. It was always there.

## Chapter 26: "The Party (Setup)"

## Chapter 27: "The Chaconne"

He stood where the terrace met the grass, the ocean behind him, the sun going down into it like a coal sinking into ash. The Norfolk pines threw their shadows east across the party -- long, thin, trembling shadows that reached past the marquee and the caterer's tables and the eighty-odd guests holding their glasses of Margaret River shiraz and their small plates of kingfish ceviche and their conversations about property and schools and the market, conversations that would, in a moment, stop.

The violin was in his left hand, the bow in his right. He had carried them out from the house without a case, holding them the way he had learned to hold them -- the violin by the neck, lightly, the scroll pointing down, the bow loose in his fingers with the hair facing away from his palm. Katarina had taught him this. You do not grip the instrument, she had said, in her careful English with its Czech consonants. You accompany it. It walks beside you. He had not understood this for months, and then one afternoon in the soundproofed office on St George's Terrace he had understood it all at once, the way you understand a sentence in a foreign language not word by word but whole, and after that his hands had changed, and the sound had changed, and everything that mattered had changed.

He raised the violin. Tucked it under his chin. The chinrest found the mark on his jaw -- the callused hollow that had formed over two years of practice, the brand that Jeannie had never asked about again after the first time. He felt the instrument's weight settle against his collarbone, the slight downward pull of the scroll, the warmth of the wood where his neck met the lower bout. The varnish was warm. It was always warm, as though the instrument remembered the hands that had made it, the workshop, the Italian sun. He adjusted the shoulder rest with a small motion of his left shoulder. Drew a breath.

The guests had begun to notice. The conversations nearest him thinned and stopped, the way a pool goes still when something enters the water. A few faces turned. He saw Jim -- his father, eighty years old, in a suit that was too large for him now, the shoulders sloping, the collar standing away from his neck, a man diminished by age into a version of himself that was gentler and more bewildered than the original. Jim was holding a glass of beer he had not drunk. Beside him, a woman Bob did not recognise -- a wife or a friend, someone's guest, someone who did not matter. Chase was near the marquee, a beer in each hand, one his and one belonging to the girlfriend Bob had met twice and whose name he could not at this moment recall. Shanel was at the edge of the terrace, alone, her arms crossed, watching him with an expression he could not read. And Katarina -- he found her without looking for her, the way you find a note without looking at the fingerboard when you have played it a thousand times -- Katarina was standing apart from the crowd, near the low limestone wall that separated the garden from the terraced grass, and her phone was in her hand, and she was already recording. She knew. She had known since the moment he told her, two weeks ago, that he wanted to play at the party. She had said nothing except: Play it all. Do not stop. Whatever happens, play it to the end.

He spoke. His voice came out steady, which surprised him, because inside the steadiness there was a vibration, a tremor that ran from his diaphragm to his throat and that he controlled the way he controlled the tremor in his bow arm during the bariolage passages -- not by suppressing it but by incorporating it, letting it become part of the sound.

"Thank you all for coming. I love my wife. I love my children. I want to play you something."

Someone laughed -- the nervous, social laugh of a person who expects a joke and is preparing to respond to it. The laugh died. The silence that replaced it was not the silence of attention but the silence of confusion, the held breath of a crowd that has been told something it does not know how to process. A man at his fiftieth birthday party, in a navy Italian suit, with a violin under his chin, saying he wants to play something. The collective mind of the crowd was cycling through possible explanations -- a gag, a midlife novelty, a party trick -- and finding none adequate, and falling silent because silence was the only response available.

Bob raised the bow. Set the hair against the strings. The contact point -- Katarina's voice in his ear, as it always was when he played, the patient Czech voice correcting, guiding, insisting: *contact point, Robert, contact point is everything* -- the contact point was midway between the bridge and the fingerboard, where the resistance was firm but not unyielding, where the string would speak without being forced. He felt the four strings through the bow's tension: G, D, A, E. Four voices. Four lives waiting to sound.

He played the opening chord.

D, A, D, F-sharp. The four notes broke across the strings -- the bow catching the G and D first, the lower pair, and then rolling upward with a pressure that was not pressure but weight, the weight of the arm transferred through the stick and the hair to the string and from the string through the bridge to the belly of the instrument, where the spruce -- three-hundred-year-old spruce, cut from a tree that had grown in the Alps during the coldest decades of the Little Ice Age, shaped by hands in a workshop in Cremona -- vibrated, and the sound that came out was not a chord in any ordinary sense. It was a door opening. It was the sound of a room that had been sealed for three centuries suddenly filling with air.

The chord hung in the evening. It spread across the terrace, past the marquee, over the heads of the guests, and out toward the ocean, where the sun was half-gone now, the sky banded in copper and rose and a deep, aching violet that was not quite dark. The Norfolk pines received it. The limestone walls of the house received it. The glasses of wine on the caterer's tables trembled, faintly, at a frequency that was felt rather than heard. And the eighty-odd guests, who had been holding their confusion like a weight they did not know how to set down, set it down. The confusion left them. What replaced it was not understanding -- they did not understand what they were hearing, would never understand it, most of them -- but attention. Pure attention. The rarest thing in the world: a room full of people, listening.

Bob played the second bar. The descending line -- the bass voice stepping down from D to C-sharp, the upper voice answering, the theme stating itself with the grave deliberation of a man who knows he has something to say and will not be hurried. His left hand found the positions. His bow arm, the arm that had cramped and seized and failed him ten thousand times in the soundproofed office, moved with a fluency that was not mastery but something adjacent to it -- the fluency of a body that has practised a motion so many times that the motion has become autonomous, a thing the body does while the mind goes elsewhere, goes deeper, goes into the sound itself.

The first four bars. The theme. A ground bass in D minor, four bars long, upon which everything that follows is built. Sixty-four variations. Fifteen minutes. A universe constructed on a foundation of eight notes.

He played.

## Chapter 28: "The Gossip"

## Chapter 29: "The Smashing"

The last note of the Chaconne hung in the air the way a bell's sound hangs after the clapper has stilled -- not fading so much as thinning, spreading outward in concentric rings until it became indistinguishable from silence, until the silence itself seemed to vibrate with the memory of what had just passed through it. The open D string. The final chord resolved to a single voice, the lowest voice, the bass that had been walking beneath the music for fifteen minutes like a heartbeat beneath the body's noise, and now the heartbeat was the only sound, and then it too was gone, and the silence that replaced it was unlike any silence the terrace on Marine Parade had ever held.

It was a silence with weight. A silence that pressed down on the eighty-odd people standing in the blue dusk with their glasses and their plates and their half-formed thoughts, pressed them into stillness, into a kind of paralysis that was not discomfort but awe -- or not awe exactly, because most of them did not have the vocabulary for awe, had never been asked to stand in the presence of something that exceeded their categories, but something in the neighbourhood of awe, something that made the body quiet and the mind stop and the breath hold and the ordinary machinery of social performance -- the scanning, the positioning, the ceaseless calibration of how one appeared to others -- go still.

Jim Harrington was weeping. He stood near the edge of the marquee in his oversized suit, the collar standing away from his diminished neck, and the tears ran freely down the vertical creases of his face, and he made no effort to stop them or to hide them, because he was eighty years old and had spent his entire adult life practising restraint and the restraint had finally broken, not from weakness but from the force of what he had just witnessed, which was his son -- his only child, the boy he had steered toward commerce and common sense and the sensible life, the boy who had become a man who had become a stranger -- his son, standing on a terrace with a violin, playing music that Jim did not understand and did not need to understand because understanding was not the point, the point was that the music was beautiful and his son had made it and Jim had never known this was inside him, had never asked, had never thought to ask, and the recognition -- thirty years late, a lifetime late -- was what the tears were for.

Chase stood ten feet from his father. He had not moved for fifteen minutes. The two beers he had been holding were both on the ground now -- he had set them down at some point during the performance, he could not remember when, an act so out of character that his girlfriend had looked at him with concern and then looked at the man playing the violin and then looked back at Chase and said nothing, because whatever was happening to Chase's face was not something she could help with. His face was open. Open in the way a door is open when someone has kicked it in -- not gently, not by invitation, but by force, the force of the music, which had entered him through his ears and his chest and his skin and had rearranged something fundamental in the architecture of his understanding. His father was not a stockbroker. Or: his father was a stockbroker the way the ocean was a surface -- there was something beneath it, something enormous and dark and moving, and Chase had been walking on the surface his entire life without knowing what was under his feet.

Shanel was at the far edge of the terrace. She had her arms wrapped around herself, a gesture that was not cold but protective, the posture of a person holding something fragile inside her body -- a feeling, a recognition, a small and terrifying bloom of understanding that she could not yet name. Her eyes were wet but she was not crying. She was standing very still, looking at the ocean behind where her father stood, the last light dying on the water, and she was thinking -- or not thinking, feeling -- that the sound her father had just made was the same thing as the photograph she had deleted from her phone three months ago, the one that was beautiful and that she had erased because it did not match her feed, and the same thing as the passage in Virginia Woolf that had lit her up in a tutorial and that she had not mentioned to anyone, and the same thing as something inside her that she had been editing out for as long as she could remember, and that the editing was the lie, and that her father, playing a violin at his birthday party in his good suit while the sun went down, had just told the truth.

Katarina stopped the recording. She slid the phone into her pocket. Her hand was steady. Her face was not. She looked at Bob across the terrace -- he was standing with the violin in one hand and the bow in the other, his arms at his sides, his face carrying the expression of a man who has been somewhere very far away and has not yet fully returned -- and she said, quietly, so that only Bob could hear: "That was the best you have ever played." She meant the notes, the intonation, the phrasing. She also meant the other thing, the thing that had no technical name, the thing that separated what Bob had just done from what a thousand more accomplished violinists would have done with the same piece. He had played it as though his life depended on it. Because it did.

The violin lay on the table. Bob had set it down on a caterer's napkin beside a tray of uneaten canapes -- the Stradivarius, the instrument whose authentication report was in his email, whose value exceeded the price of every house on this street combined, resting on a white cloth napkin on a trestle table in a garden in Cottesloe. The bow beside it. Unprotected. Unguarded. The casualness was not carelessness. It was the gesture of a man who had just given everything he had to give and had nothing left to protect.

Then Jeannie came through the glass doors.

## Chapter 30: "The Beach"

Bob walked. The grass was cool under his shoes -- the good shoes, the Italian leather, the pair Jeannie had chosen for him from a shop in Claremont whose name he had never learned because the names of shops where Jeannie bought things were Jeannie's knowledge, not his, part of the vast and intricate catalogue of the life she had built around them both, the life that was behind him now, up the slope, on the terrace, where the sound of voices had taken on the particular quality of voices processing catastrophe: high, broken, uncertain, the social machinery stripped of its gears and spinning free.

He descended the first terrace. The grass was thick here, the couch grass that the council maintained, and it gave slightly under his weight, the blades springing back behind him as if he had not passed. The fairy lights strung along the terrace railing were above and behind him now, their warm yellow glow reaching only partway down the slope before giving way to the blue dark of the April evening. The second terrace. The Norfolk pines rose on either side of him, their trunks straight and dark, their canopies high and still, and the air smelled of pine resin and salt and, faintly, jasmine from a garden to the south, the night-blooming jasmine that grew along the fences of the Marine Parade houses and that released its scent at dusk as if the darkness were a signal.

He was carrying the scroll. He had picked it up from the terrace floor -- the carved head of the violin, still attached to the pegbox, the four tuning pegs jutting at angles from the shattered neck, the scroll's volute intact, the spiral that the luthier had carved by hand, the spiral that had no beginning and no end. He had picked it up without deciding to pick it up, the way a man picks up his child's shoe from a wreck. His right hand closed around it and the wood was warm and smooth and it fit his palm the way it had always fit his palm, and he carried it down the grass toward the sea.

The third terrace. The esplanade. The low stone wall that separated the grass from the footpath, and he stepped over it, and the concrete was hard and flat under his shoes after the give of the grass, and then the sand.

He stopped at the edge of the sand and took off his shoes. He did this carefully, methodically -- loosening the left shoe with the toe of the right, then the right with the toes of the left, stepping out of each one and placing them side by side on the concrete. The socks he pulled off and tucked inside the shoes. He took off his jacket -- the navy Italian jacket, the jacket that matched the trousers, the jacket Jeannie had hung on the back of his chair that morning with a hanger she kept specifically for occasions when his clothes needed to be ready and unwrinkled and correct. He folded it. He placed it on the sand beside the shoes. He put the scroll in the jacket's inside pocket, where it fit as though the pocket had been made for it.

The sand was cool and fine under his bare feet. Cottesloe sand -- white, almost powder, the quartz grains ground by the Southern Ocean over millennia into a softness that felt, against the sole of a bare foot, like something between silk and ash. He walked across it. The dry sand shifted and gave way, and then the firm sand near the waterline, wet and packed and cold, and the water came to meet him -- the first thin sheet of it running up the beach and over his toes and retreating, and it was cold, colder than the air, the Indian Ocean in April carrying the memory of the deep water beyond the continental shelf, the water that came from the south, from the Southern Ocean, from Antarctica, from places where the sea was not a surface but a body, vast and dark and indifferent to the things that lived and died upon its skin.

He walked into it. The water rose around his ankles, his shins, his knees. The trousers -- the good Italian trousers, the ones that matched the jacket folded on the sand -- darkened and clung and the fabric billowed around his calves in the small current, and the sensation was of weight being added, the water pulling at him gently, claiming him by increments. He kept walking. The water reached his waist. His chest. The cold pressed against his ribs and he drew a breath and the breath was sharp and the air tasted of salt and he could feel his heart adjusting, the rhythm changing, the body's ancient response to immersion, to the crossing of the boundary between the element that sustained it and the element that could take it away.

He began to swim.

The shore behind him. The lights of Marine Parade, small and warm, the terrace where he had played, where the violin had been smashed, where Jeannie's voice had risen and broken and he had heard it the way you hear a sound from underwater -- muffled, distant, belonging to a world you are no longer in. The lights grew smaller. The houses grew smaller. The Norfolk pines became silhouettes, became shapes, became suggestions, became nothing.

He swam west. The sky ahead was dark now, the last colour gone from the horizon, and the stars were appearing -- not the faint suburban stars of Cottesloe but the real stars, the ones you saw when you got beyond the light, the ones that had been there all along behind the glare of the life you had built, the life with its terrace and its fairy lights and its eighty guests and its music and its ruin. He swam toward them. His strokes were steady, unhurried, the freestyle of a man who had grown up on this coast and had swum in this ocean all his life, the arms reaching and pulling and reaching and pulling, and the water was dark and he could not see the bottom and he did not look.

Something moved beneath him. A shadow. A displacement in the dark water, something large and slow, passing below and to the left, there and then not there. A dolphin, perhaps. They were common here -- the bottlenose dolphins that rode the swells off Cottesloe, that surfers saw in the mornings, grey shapes rising and turning in the green water inside the break. Or a seal. Or something else. The coast was home to other things, things that moved in the dark water with a patience that was not patience but hunger, the ancient hunger of a body designed for one purpose in a sea that had been dark for four billion years.

Bob may have seen it. He may not have. The novel does not say, because the novel is no longer with him. The point of view has risen, pulled back, lifted like a bird from the shoulder of a man swimming alone in the Indian Ocean at night, and what it sees from this height is only a shape in the water, a shape that is growing smaller, a shape that is difficult to distinguish from the dark water and the dark sky and the dark, and the strokes are still steady, still unhurried, still reaching, and then the shape is very small, and then the shape is not there.

---

The morning came the way mornings come to coastlines: slowly, then all at once. The ocean was pale blue, glassy, a sheet of hammered silver under a sky that had not yet decided whether to be clear or overcast. A jogger on the sand, earbuds in, not looking at the water. A woman walking a Labrador that strained toward the shallows and was pulled back. The Norfolk pines casting their long morning shadows east, away from the sea, toward the houses and the gardens and the lives that were waking behind their limestone walls.

At the waterline, just above the high-tide mark, a pair of shoes and a folded jacket.

In the jacket pocket, a carved wooden scroll.

The beach was closed by eight. A shark report. A helicopter circling offshore, low, the thudding of its rotors carrying across the water. A lifeguard on a jet ski, scanning. The yellow tape.

The body was not recovered.

## Chapter 31 (Coda): "The Recording"

The last student left at four. A twelve-year-old girl named Mia who was working on the Vivaldi A minor and who had, this afternoon, for the first time, played the second movement's slow theme with something that was not merely correct but felt -- a quality of attention in the phrasing, a willingness to let the note breathe before moving to the next, that Katarina had heard and acknowledged with a nod and had not praised aloud, because praise given too soon teaches a student to perform for approval rather than for the music, and Katarina had learned this the hard way, from Kovar, who had never praised her at all, and whose silence she had mistaken for disappointment until the afternoon he told a colleague, within her hearing, that she was the most musical student he had taught in twenty years, and the shock of it -- the recognition arriving sideways, unintended, real -- had taught her something about sincerity that she had never forgotten and that she practised now, daily, in the way she taught.

The studio was quiet. The particular quiet of a room that has held music and is now holding the absence of music, the way a glass holds the shape of the water after the water has been poured out. The light came in through the west-facing window -- late afternoon, autumn, the low gold light of a Perth April that was indistinguishable from the light of the afternoon three weeks ago when everything had changed and nothing in this room had changed at all. The music stand. The straight-backed chair. The shelf of scores arranged by period and difficulty -- Suzuki at one end, the Bach Sonatas and Partitas at the other, the progression of a violinist's education laid out in spine widths, from the slender first position exercises to the fat, dog-eared volumes that contained the music she had spent her life learning to play and that she would spend the rest of her life learning to play, because the learning was not a journey toward a destination but a practice, a discipline, a way of being in the world that was its own justification.

The rosin dust on the floor caught the light. A fine amber powder, almost invisible, that accumulated over weeks and that she swept up on Sundays and that was already, three days after sweeping, beginning to reappear -- the residue of horsehair on gut, the physical trace of the friction that produced sound, the evidence that work had been done here, that a bow had been drawn across strings and the air had moved and something that did not exist before -- a note, a phrase, a child's first true phrase -- had briefly existed and was gone.

She sat in the straight-backed chair. Her violin was on her lap -- the Czech instrument, the one she had played since she was seventeen, the one Kovar had helped her choose from a maker in Brno who worked in a converted garage and whose instruments had a darkness of tone that Kovar admired and that Katarina had come to love the way you love a voice you have heard every day for twenty years: not because it is the most beautiful voice, but because it is yours.

Her phone was on the music stand.

She had not watched the recording since the first time, three days after it happened, when she had sat in this chair at this hour in this light and pressed play and listened to the whole thing -- fourteen minutes and thirty-seven seconds, the duration displayed in small white numbers at the bottom of the screen -- and had heard, through the tiny speaker's compression and the ambient noise of the wind and the glasses and the cough of someone standing too near, the sound of a man playing the Chaconne on a terrace in Cottesloe while the sun went down behind him, and the sound had been imperfect and true and she had wept, not for the first time and not for the last, but with a completeness that had left her emptied and calm, the way a storm leaves the air emptied and calm, and she had put the phone down and not watched it again.

She picked it up now. She found the recording. The thumbnail showed a blur of sunset -- overexposed, the phone's camera overwhelmed by the light behind Bob, so that his figure was a dark shape against a white-gold wash, a silhouette, a man made of shadow holding a violin.

She pressed play.

The opening chord came through the speaker. Thin, compressed, stripped of the resonance that had filled the terrace that evening -- the sound of a great instrument reduced to the bandwidth of a phone's microphone, the warmth flattened, the overtones lost. But the chord was there. D, A, D, F-sharp. Bob's chord. The slight unevenness of the bow roll that was not a flaw but a signature, the way a voice has a grain that is not an imperfection but an identity. She closed her eyes.

She listened. Not to the whole recording -- she could not, not yet, not today. She listened to the opening, and then she skipped forward, her thumb on the screen, to the place she knew was there, the place where the key changed, where the D minor broke open into D major and the music stepped from darkness into light, and she heard it, and the sound that came through the small speaker was so diminished and so compressed and so far from what it had been in the air that evening that it should have been nothing, a ghost of a ghost, and instead it was everything, because the thing that had made it beautiful was not the instrument or the acoustics or the sunset but the man, the specific man, the man who was gone now, the man whose hands had shaken and whose intonation had slipped and who had played the D major section of the Chaconne with a sincerity that she, with all her training, with all her years, had never achieved and perhaps never would, because sincerity of that order required not technique but extremity, the extremity of a man who had nothing left to lose and therefore nothing left to hold back, and Bob had had nothing left, and he had held nothing back, and the music was the proof, and the phone in her hand was the only record, and she was the keeper.

She put the phone down. She picked up her violin. She rosined the bow -- three long strokes, the familiar gesture, the rosin's faint sweet smell rising. She stood. She needed to stand for this, needed the full body, the breath from the diaphragm, the feet on the floor.

She placed the bow on the string. She played the opening chord of the Chaconne.

Her chord was clean. Technically superior. The bow roll smooth, the intonation precise, the four notes ringing with the clarity of a musician who has spent twenty-five years training her hands to do exactly this. She played the descending line. She played the first variation. The sound filled the studio -- a different sound from Bob's, rounder, more controlled, more beautiful in the way that a polished stone is more beautiful than a broken one, and less true, and she knew it, and the knowing was the grief, because the grief was not for Bob alone but for the thing that separated her playing from his, the thing she could name but not acquire: the willingness to be broken in the act of making sound.

She played through the first section. She played into the D major. The key changed and the light changed -- the afternoon sun dropping below the roofline of the building opposite, the studio dimming, the gold leaving the room the way colour leaves a photograph held too long in the light -- and she wept. The tears came without sound, running down her face, and she did not stop playing to wipe them, because the bow arm must continue, because the music does not pause for the musician's grief, because Bach did not pause, because the Chaconne is not a piece that permits you to stop and collect yourself and resume. It asks you to carry everything -- the technique and the feeling and the memory and the loss -- simultaneously, and to keep going, and she kept going.

She played the bariolage. She played the return to D minor. She played the final bars. The last chord. She lifted the bow.

Silence.

The studio was dim now. The sun had gone. The light that remained was the grey-blue light of a Perth autumn evening, the light that comes after the gold, the light that is not dark but is no longer bright, the light in which everything looks both clear and far away. Outside the window: Rokeby Road. A car. A magpie, the last call of the day, liquid and descending. And beneath it, distant, constant, the sound of the Indian Ocean -- the sound that was always there in this city, beneath everything, even when you could not hear it.

She stood in the silence after the last note. Her violin at her side. The phone on the music stand, its screen dark. The recording inside it, the only recording, the only proof that Robert James Harrington had played the Chaconne on the evening of his fiftieth birthday and that the playing had been imperfect and true and that it had mattered.

The silence held.

It held the way the silence holds after every performance of the Chaconne -- the same silence that held in whatever room Bach first played it in 1720, the same silence that held in San Antonio when Isaac Stern lowered his bow and wept and asked four thousand people not to applaud, the same silence that held in a Washington Metro station when Joshua Bell finished playing and the commuters walked on and the music hung in the tiled corridor like a question no one had heard. The silence after the last note is not empty. It is full. It is full of everything the music said and everything it could not say and everything the listener brings to it -- every loss, every love, every moment of recognition, every time a door opened into a room you did not know existed and you stood there and you listened and you knew that the sound you were hearing was the sound of your own life, heard clearly, perhaps for the first time.

Katarina did not move. The silence continued. Outside, the city. The ocean. The ordinary evening.

The music was over. The music was not over. The music was never over.
