# CHACONNE -- Wave 1: Scene-Level Outline

## ACT I -- THE SURFACE LIFE

Chapters 1-8 | ~24,000 words
Establishing the world, the marriage, the fund, the buried self.

---

## Chapter 1: "The View from Marine Parade"

**Type:** Narrative (present-tense establishing shot)
**POV:** Third-person close, Bob
**Word count:** ~4,000
**Time:** Sunday morning, late autumn 2025 (approximately five months before the birthday)
**Primary location:** Marine Parade, Cottesloe -- the house, the terraced grass, the beach

---

### Scene 1 -- The Grass (pp. 1-4)

**Location:** The terraced grass between Marine Parade and Cottesloe Beach. Early Sunday morning, around 7am. Autumn light, pale gold. The Norfolk pines throwing long shadows west toward the ocean. The Indian Ocean flat, glassy, pewter-coloured before the sun gets to it.

**Characters:** Bob alone.

**Beat by beat:**

- Bob stands on the grass in bare feet, coffee in hand, wearing yesterday's chinos and a linen shirt he slept in. He has come out the back gate of the house and crossed Marine Parade and is standing on the first terrace of the stepped lawn that descends toward the sand. This is his morning ritual on days when he cannot sleep, which is most days now.

- The view: describe Cottesloe Beach in precise, loving detail. The terraced grass stepping down to the esplanade. The Norfolk pines -- their strange vertical architecture, like columns holding up nothing. The Indiana restaurant below to the south, closed at this hour, its white colonial facade catching the first light. The ocean beyond -- the Indian Ocean, which is not the Pacific, not the Atlantic, but something older and less named, stretching unbroken to Africa. The sky enormous. The silence almost total -- a magpie, the distant hiss of small surf, the creak of a Norfolk pine.

- Bob's physicality: establish him. Six foot, sandy hair going grey at the temples, the extra ten kilos sitting on his waist and softening his jaw. A face that belongs on a bank advertisement -- handsome going bland. The RM Williams boots by the back door (he came out barefoot). The hands: large, well-kept, the hands of a man who has never made anything with them.

- His interior: he is not thinking about the fund. He is not thinking about Jeannie. He is doing the thing he does out here, which is not-thinking -- letting the view fill the space where thought would be. The view costs him four and a half million dollars (what he paid for the house in 2010, at the peak of the mining boom, stretching further than he should have because Jeannie wanted Marine Parade and Marine Parade is what Jeannie got). He does not think about this either. He looks at the ocean and drinks his coffee and for a few minutes he is no one.

- Plant: a brief, almost subliminal physical detail -- his left hand, the one not holding the coffee, curls slightly, the fingers closing on nothing. As if gripping a neck that isn't there. He does not notice this. The reader should barely notice it.

**What the reader learns:** The world. The money. The beauty of the setting. The solitude of a man who has everything visible and nothing interior. The first hint -- in the curling hand -- that something is buried.

**What is planted:** The hand gesture (violin muscle memory, unconscious). The house price and the mining boom context. The phrase "not-thinking" -- Bob's lifelong strategy of avoidance.

---

### Scene 2 -- The House (pp. 4-8)

**Location:** The Harrington house on Marine Parade. Five-bedroom limestone, ocean views from the upper floor, a garden that Jeannie has had landscaped three times. Interior: Jeannie's taste -- white walls, Italian tile, furniture from a shop in Claremont that closed two years ago. Everything curated, everything clean, everything slightly impersonal, like a display home that people happen to live in.

**Characters:** Bob, then Jeannie (heard before seen), then briefly Chase and Shanel.

**Beat by beat:**

- Bob comes back through the gate and into the house through the kitchen. The kitchen: marble island, Miele appliances, a wine fridge Jeannie insisted on (two zones, one for white, one for red, though the red zone is mostly empty now because Jeannie only drinks white). Morning light through the east-facing window. He rinses his coffee cup.

- Sound from upstairs: Jeannie's voice, on the phone. He can hear the tone but not the words -- the bright, performative register she uses with her friends, the one that is ten degrees warmer than the voice she uses with him. He has lived with this woman for twenty-one years and he can map her emotional state by the pitch of her phone voice. This morning: she is organising something. A lunch, a charity thing, an event. She is in her element.

- Bob moves through the house. The living room: describe it as a set, a stage. The photographs on the console table -- the family in Bali (2019), the family at Rottnest (2022), the family at a charity gala (2023). In every photograph they are smiling. In every photograph Bob is standing slightly apart, as if he wandered into someone else's portrait.

- The children: establish their presence through absence. Chase's bedroom door is closed (he is twenty-one, still living at home between semesters, sleeping until noon on Sundays). Shanel's room: the door is open, bed made, she is already out -- she runs on Sunday mornings, a habit she picked up from a university friend, one of the few things she does that Jeannie did not initiate. Bob notices the made bed, the neatness. He thinks: she is more disciplined than any of us.

- A small cruelty: Jeannie comes downstairs. She is already dressed -- white linen pants, a silk top, her hair pulled back. She looks at Bob (still in yesterday's clothes, barefoot, hair uncombed) and says something precise and lacerating. Not a shout. A observation. Something like: "You look like you slept on the lawn." Or: "Are you planning to be presentable today, or is this it?" The line should be delivered casually, almost fondly, the way you'd comment on the weather. Bob absorbs it. He does not respond. He goes to make more coffee. This is the dynamic: she cuts, he absorbs. It has been this way for so long that neither of them registers it as abnormal.

- Establish the day's plan: they are taking the Riviera to Rottnest. Chase is bringing friends. Jeannie has packed hampers. The boat is at RFBYC -- the Royal Freshwater Bay Yacht Club in Peppermint Grove, the most exclusive yacht club in Perth, where membership requires a proposer and seconder of five or more years and the waiting list is a polite fiction. Bob joined because Jim was a member. Jeannie joined because it mattered.

**Key dialogue moment:** Jeannie, looking at her phone: "The Hendersons are already across. They left at six. We'll miss the good mooring." Bob: "There's always a mooring." Jeannie: "There's always a mooring in Thomson Bay for people who don't care where they end up." This is not about moorings. This is about everything.

**What the reader learns:** The marriage. Jeannie's cruelty -- subtle, constant, normalised. The house as performance. The children as presences defined by their parents' failures. The social world: RFBYC, the Riviera, Rottnest, the competitive leisure of wealthy Perth.

**What is planted:** Jeannie's phone voice (the performance that will become her undoing when the gaggle turns on her). The wine fridge (the drinking). Chase sleeping until noon (the aimlessness). Shanel's morning run (the discipline that separates her from her mother). The Hendersons (minor characters who will reappear at the birthday party).

### Scene 3 -- The Riviera (pp. 8-12)

**Location:** RFBYC jetty, then the Riviera 48 "Chardonnay" crossing to Rottnest. Late morning. The Swan River opening out past the Fremantle traffic bridge into the open ocean. The swell picking up as they pass the groyne.

**Characters:** Bob, Jeannie, Chase, Chase's two mates (unnamed, interchangeable -- board shorts, Patagonia fleeces, slabs of beer). Shanel absent -- she declined, which Jeannie noted with a tightening of the jaw.

**Beat by beat:**

- At the jetty: Bob prepping the boat. This is the one domain where he is competent in a way Jeannie cannot diminish -- he knows engines, he knows tides, he knows the crossing. The Riviera 48: describe it. Forty-eight feet of fibreglass and teak, twin Caterpillar diesels, three cabins, a flybridge. Named "Chardonnay" -- Jeannie's choice, which Bob found funny once and now finds merely accurate. The boat cost $420,000 second-hand in 2015 and runs $40,000 a year in maintenance, berthing, and insurance. Bob has not thought about whether he can afford it in years. This is because he cannot, and not-thinking is what Bob does.

- Chase arrives with his mates. He is physical, loud, taking up space. Six-one, broad, dark hair, Jeannie's jawline. He greets Bob with a half-hug and immediately takes over the helm prep -- he has his Recreational Skipper's Ticket, he has been driving this boat since sixteen, and the handover from father to son at the wheel is a ritual they both enjoy without examining. The mates load slabs of Emu Export into the fridge below. It is 10am.

- The crossing: Bob at the helm for the river section, then handing off to Chase for the open water. Jeannie on the flybridge with a glass of something -- sauvignon blanc, it's before noon so she's performing moderation. She is taking photographs. The wind in her hair. The ocean behind her. Content for the feed.

- Bob watches Chase drive the boat. A moment of uncomplicated love -- his son's hands on the wheel, the concentration on his face, the way he reads the swell. Chase is good at this. Bob thinks: this is the version of me that worked. The physical, confident, outward version. The version that doesn't need a sealed room.

- The open water. The swell off Cottesloe -- describe it. The Indian Ocean is not gentle here. The continental shelf drops away and the swells come in from the Southern Ocean, long and rolling, and the Riviera lifts and slams in a rhythm that is either exhilarating or nauseating depending on your relationship with the sea. Bob loves it. He has always loved the crossing. The moment when the coast falls away behind and Rottnest appears ahead -- low, scrubby, surrounded by water that shifts from deep blue to impossible turquoise as the reef shelf rises -- is the closest Bob comes to transcendence in his surface life.

**What the reader learns:** The boat as status object and genuine refuge. The father-son dynamic -- physical, wordless, warm. Chase's social world (mates, beer, Rotto weekends). Jeannie performing even at sea. The crossing as metaphor -- leaving the constructed world behind, entering open water.

**What is planted:** Chase's competence with the boat (he will inherit nothing financial, but he knows how to navigate). The phrase "sealed room" -- Bob's interior, which will become literal when he soundproofs his office. The ocean itself -- the thing that will end the novel.

### Scene 4 -- The Music (pp. 12-16)

**Location:** Thomson Bay, Rottnest. Late afternoon. The boat moored, Chase and his mates ashore at the pub. Jeannie below, napping (sleeping off the wine, though she would call it resting). Bob alone on the flybridge.

**Characters:** Bob alone.

**Beat by beat:**

- Thomson Bay: describe the anchorage. Thirty-odd boats on moorings, rafted up in twos and threes. The water absurdly clear -- you can see the sandy bottom four metres down, the shadow of the boat, a school of herring drifting beneath the hull. The settlement ashore: low white buildings, the general store, the bakery, the Rottnest Hotel where Chase and his mates are currently drinking mid-strength and talking about football. The afternoon light slanting in from the west, turning everything gold.

- Bob is sitting on the flybridge with his feet up. He has a beer but he's not drinking it. He is looking at the water and he is, for once, not not-thinking. He is thinking. Or rather: something is thinking him. A restlessness he cannot name, a feeling like the moment before you remember a word that's on the tip of your tongue. He has been feeling this more often lately -- in the car, in the shower, in the moments between sleep and waking. Something knocking at a door he sealed shut a long time ago.

- The music: from somewhere across the water -- another boat, or the shore, a radio, a speaker -- comes a sound. A violin. Not a recording he recognises, not a piece he can name. Just a few bars of something, carried on the wind, distorted by distance and salt air. It could be anything. It could be a child practising. It could be a busker at the pub. It could be his imagination.

- Bob's reaction: he goes still. Completely still, the way an animal goes still when it hears a predator. Except this is not fear. It is recognition. His left hand, resting on his thigh, curls again -- the same unconscious gesture from the morning, the fingers closing around a neck that isn't there. His breath changes. Something in his chest opens, or tries to open, like a door swollen shut from years of disuse.

- The music stops. Or the wind shifts. Or it was never there. Bob sits for a long time. The sun drops. The sky turns copper, then violet, then dark. The lights of the other boats come on. He hears Jeannie moving below -- the click of a wine glass, the flush of a toilet, the sound of a woman maintaining herself in a small space. He does not go down. He stays on the flybridge and looks at the stars, which are extraordinary out here, away from the city, and he feels -- for the first time in years, or perhaps for the first time ever -- the specific grief of a man who has lost something he never quite had.

- Ending beat: Chase returns from the pub, loud, slightly drunk, happy. He climbs aboard and sees his father on the flybridge and says something -- "You right, Dad?" -- and Bob says, "Yeah, mate, good," and the ordinariness of the exchange seals the moment shut. Whatever opened does not close entirely. But Bob cannot speak of it, and Chase would not know how to hear it.

**What the reader learns:** That Bob has an interior life he cannot access or articulate. That music is the key to it. That the grief is not new but newly stirring. That he is profoundly, invisibly alone.

**What is planted:** The violin sound (whether real or imagined, it triggers the return of the buried self). The hand gesture (now established as a motif -- this is the muscle memory of a fourteen-year-old boy who played until Year 12 and then stopped). The stars at Rottnest (will rhyme with the stars at the beach in Chapter 30). Chase's "You right, Dad?" (the question he will wish he'd asked differently, after).

**Thread status at end of Chapter 1:**
- **Fund:** Not yet mentioned directly. Present only in subtext -- the house price, the boat cost, the phrase "not-thinking."
- **Violin memory:** Stirring. The hand gesture (twice). The distant music. The door swollen shut.
- **Jeannie's state:** Established. The small cruelties, the wine, the performing, the phone voice. Functional, controlled, cutting.
- **Kids:** Chase physical and present but unknowing. Shanel absent but characterised (the made bed, the morning run -- discipline and separation from Jeannie).

## Chapter 2: "Nedlands, 1987"

**Type:** Narrative/flashback
**POV:** Third-person close, Bob
**Word count:** ~3,000
**Time:** 1987-1993, with the frame set in 2025 (a brief return to present before and after)
**Primary locations:** Pearse Street, Cottesloe (the childhood home); Scotch College, Swanbourne; Mrs Albrecht's teaching studio

---

### Scene 1 -- The Frame (p. 1)

**Location:** Marine Parade house, a weeknight, late 2025. Bob in the living room after dinner. Jeannie upstairs. The house quiet.

**Characters:** Bob alone.

**Beat by beat:**

- A brief present-tense frame. Bob is sitting in the living room. The television is on but muted -- a financial news channel, habit rather than interest. He is holding his phone, scrolling without purpose. The YouTube algorithm has learned something about him: in between the market commentary and the football highlights, it has begun suggesting violin performances. A thumbnail catches his eye -- a woman on a stage, the instrument tucked under her chin, the bow raised. He does not tap it. He puts the phone down. But the image stays.

- Transition: the memory arrives not as narrative but as sensation. The weight of a violin under his chin. The smell of rosin. The particular ache in the fingertip of his left index finger after an hour of practice. He has not thought about this in years. He thinks about it now.

**What this frame does:** Connects the present (the YouTube algorithm, the stirring from Chapter 1) to the past. Establishes that the memory is being pulled to the surface by something happening now, not retrieved voluntarily.

---

### Scene 2 -- Pearse Street (pp. 1-3)

**Location:** The Harrington house on Pearse Street, Cottesloe, 1987. A four-bedroom limestone cottage three blocks from the beach. Comfortable but not grand -- a distinction that will matter more to Jeannie than to Bob. The garden: natives and roses, Wendy's domain. The carport: Jim's Volvo wagon. The front room where Bob does his homework at a table by the window.

**Characters:** Bob (eleven), Jim Harrington, Wendy Harrington.

**Beat by beat:**

- Establish the family. Jim: a stockbroker at Patersons Securities, a man who wore a tie every day of his working life and drove a Volvo and believed that consistency was the highest virtue. Not cold -- capable of warmth, of a hand on his son's shoulder, of pride in small accomplishments -- but contained. A man whose emotional range ran from mild approval to mild concern, with nothing at either extreme. Wendy: a former legal secretary who never went back to work after Bob was born. She kept the garden, played bridge on Thursdays, and loved her son in the way she loved everything -- quietly, without drama, with a competence that asked for nothing in return.

- The violin decision. Scotch College offered instrumental lessons beginning in Year 7. Wendy thought it would "round him out" -- the phrase she used, meaning: give him something beyond football and the beach, something that signalled cultivation without requiring commitment. Bob chose the violin. The reasons, as he remembers them: the piano was too heavy to carry. The trumpet seemed aggressive. The cello was too big for the bus. The violin fit in a case he could sling over his shoulder, and so the most consequential decision of his life was made for logistical reasons. This is very Bob.

- The first lesson: a music room at Scotch, the smell of floor polish and brass cleaner. The teacher -- not Mrs Albrecht yet, a younger woman whose name Bob cannot remember -- placing the instrument under his chin, adjusting the chin rest, showing him how to draw the bow across the open D string. The sound: horrible. A scraping, whining protest. The other boys in the group lesson giggling. Bob's face burning. But something: the vibration of the string transmitted through the chin rest into his jaw, into his skull, into some chamber of his brain that had never been opened. A physical sensation before it was a musical one. The first hint.

**What the reader learns:** The ordinariness of Bob's origins. The parents: decent, limited, loving in their contained way. The accidental nature of the violin -- no destiny, no prodigy narrative, just a boy choosing the lightest instrument.

**What is planted:** The vibration through the jawbone. This physical, bodily relationship with the instrument (as distinct from intellectual or emotional) will be central to his return to playing in his forties. Wendy's phrase "round him out" -- the expectation that music is supplementary, decorative, not central.

---

### Scene 3 -- Mrs Albrecht (pp. 3-6)

**Location:** Mrs Albrecht's studio -- a converted sunroom in her house in Nedlands, near the university. Afternoon light through frosted glass. A music stand, a metronome, a poster of Yehudi Menuhin on the wall. Shelves of sheet music. The faint smell of cats.

**Characters:** Bob (twelve to sixteen), Mrs Albrecht.

**Beat by beat:**

- Mrs Albrecht. Her real name was Ingrid. She was German-born, came to Perth in the 1960s with a husband who taught mathematics at UWA, and supplemented the household income by teaching violin to western suburbs children whose parents wanted them "rounded out." She was patient in the way that people who have been disappointed by life are patient -- not from serenity but from the understanding that nothing will go as planned and there is no point in being angry about it. She was a good teacher. Not inspired, not severe, but observant. She noticed things.

- The progression: Bob through the grades. Year 7 to Year 10, four years. Describe the trajectory in compressed time -- the squeaking of early exercises giving way to simple melodies, the Suzuki books, the first real pieces (a Handel sonata, a simplified Bach gavotte). Bob was not gifted. His intonation was unreliable; his bowing arm was stiff; he lacked the discipline for scales and the competitive instinct for exams. He reached Grade 4 by Year 10, which was respectable but not remarkable. Boys with less natural ability but more structure had passed him.

- What Mrs Albrecht saw: the thing that no examination could measure. When Bob played -- not exercises, not scales, but actual music -- something happened. He disappeared. Not into competence or achievement, but into the sound itself. His body relaxed. His face changed. The tension that he carried in his shoulders (a tension she recognised, because she had seen it in other children whose homes were placid but airless) released, and for the duration of a phrase he was entirely present in a way he was not present anywhere else in his life. She told Wendy: "He has a genuine musical ear but not the discipline to develop it." This was accurate. She did not say what she also believed, which was that the ear was the rarer thing, and that discipline could be taught but the ear could not, and that it was a small tragedy that no one would teach him.

- The key moment: a practice session alone in his bedroom, age fourteen or fifteen. Not a lesson, not a recital -- a Tuesday evening, homework done, the door closed. Bob playing a slow movement from a Handel sonata. And the experience: the sound filling the small room, the vibration in his fingers and his jaw, and the sensation of the music opening a door into a room he hadn't known existed. A room where he was not Robert Harrington of Pearse Street, not Jim's son, not a B-average student at Scotch, not anything -- just a consciousness inside a sound. He played the phrase again. And again. And the door stayed open as long as the music lasted and closed the moment he stopped.

- He never spoke of this. He practised when he felt like it, which was often, but always alone, always with the door closed, always stopping when someone knocked. The music was private in the way that certain essential things are private -- not because they are shameful but because they cannot survive exposure. He understood this without articulating it.

**Key dialogue moment:** Mrs Albrecht, after hearing him play the Handel with unexpected feeling: "Robert, do you enjoy playing?" Bob, embarrassed: "It's all right." Mrs Albrecht, watching his face: "Hmm." She knew. He knew she knew. Neither of them ever addressed it directly.

**What the reader learns:** The nature of Bob's relationship with music -- physical, private, transcendent in a way he cannot articulate. The gap between his musical ear and his technical ability. Mrs Albrecht as witness -- someone who sees him clearly and cannot help him.

**What is planted:** The door metaphor (music as access to a hidden room). The privacy of it -- always alone, always with the door closed. This is the same man who will soundproof his office thirty years later. The pattern is set in childhood.

---

### Scene 4 -- The Concert and the Closing (pp. 6-9)

**Location:** Scotch College chapel or auditorium, an evening concert, approximately 1992 (Bob in Year 11). Then: the Harrington house, Year 12.

**Characters:** Bob (sixteen-seventeen), Jim, Wendy, schoolmates, Mrs Albrecht (in audience).

**Beat by beat:**

- The school concert. Bob's one public performance. He played a Handel sonata -- the one he had practised alone in his room, the one that opened the door. On stage, under the lights, in front of two hundred parents and students, the door did not open. It slammed shut. His hands shook. His intonation wobbled. He got through it -- he did not stop, did not break down -- but the experience of playing music he loved in front of people who were merely polite was so exposing, so unbearable, that he felt as though he had been turned inside out under fluorescent light. The applause was kind. Jim patted his shoulder afterward and said, "Well done, mate." Wendy smiled. Mrs Albrecht caught his eye from across the room and gave him a look he would not understand for thirty years: not pity, not disappointment, but a kind of recognition. She had been there. She knew what it cost.

- He never performed again. This was not a dramatic decision. He did not announce it or explain it. He simply never signed up for another concert, and no one asked why, because no one had expected anything in particular from Bob Harrington's violin playing.

- Year 12: the closing. Bob's final year at Scotch. Football, exams, schoolies at Dunsborough. The violin receded. Not violently -- it simply became less frequent, the way exercise becomes less frequent when life fills up with other demands. Jim's expectation was clear without being stated: Commerce at UWA, then Patersons, then a life. The violin was a hobby that had served its purpose (rounding out) and could now be put away.

- The last time: a night in the summer after Year 12, after the exams, after the results. Bob in his room. He took out the violin -- a student instrument, a Czech factory violin worth perhaps $400 -- and played the Handel one more time. The door opened. He stood in the room for the duration of a slow movement. Then he put the violin in its case, closed the clasps, and put the case in the top of his wardrobe. He did not play again for twenty-seven years.

- The chapter's final beat (return to present frame): Bob in the living room on Marine Parade, 2025. The television still muted. His phone on the couch beside him. The YouTube thumbnail still visible -- the woman with the violin. He picks up the phone. He opens the video. The sound fills the room: a violin, playing something he doesn't recognise, something in a minor key, something that pulls at the door he sealed shut in 1993. He listens for thirty seconds. Then Jeannie's footsteps on the stairs. He closes the app. The screen goes dark. He sits in the dark for a long time.

**What the reader learns:** Why the violin stopped. Not trauma, not prohibition -- something quieter and more devastating: the understanding that the thing you love most is the thing you cannot share. The concert as the proof that Bob's musical life cannot survive in the world he lives in. Jim's gravitational pull toward commerce. The putting-away as a small, quiet death.

**What is planted:** The violin in the wardrobe (a literal Chekhov's gun -- it will still be there in Chapter 7, when Jeannie finds it). The Handel sonata as Bob's ur-piece (before the Chaconne, this was the music that opened the door). The YouTube algorithm as the agent of return -- technology doing what Bob's will cannot: bringing the music back. Jeannie's footsteps as the sound that closes the door -- this will be inverted when she stands outside the soundproofed room in Act III and hears him through the wall.

**Thread status at end of Chapter 2:**
- **Fund:** Not yet present. The chapter is pre-fund, pre-career. Jim's expectation of Commerce at UWA is the seed.
- **Violin memory:** Fully established. The arc: discovery, private joy, one failed public performance, suppression. The Grade 4 level fixed as Bob's technical ceiling when the instrument goes into the case.
- **Jeannie's state:** Present only in the frame -- the footsteps, the closing of the app. She is the ambient pressure that keeps the door shut.
- **Kids:** Not present. This is Bob's childhood, before family.

## Chapter 3: "The Gaggle"

**Type:** Narrative (character study)
**POV:** Third-person close, Jeannie
**Word count:** ~3,000
**Time:** A Wednesday, mid-week, late 2025. Approximately the same period as Chapters 1 and 2.
**Primary locations:** A restaurant in Claremont; Napoleon Street, Cottesloe; the Marine Parade house.

---

### Scene 1 -- The Lunch (pp. 1-6)

**Location:** A restaurant on Bay View Terrace, Claremont. White tablecloths, or no tablecloths (the places that are expensive enough to not need them). French doors open to the pavement. Midday. The particular light of a Perth autumn afternoon: warm, gold, generous, making everything look better than it is.

**Characters:** Jeannie and the gaggle -- four or five women, mid-forties, western suburbs. They should be distinct but not individually named in detail at this stage. They are: the one who always knows (gossip conduit -- she will deliver the information at the birthday party), the one with the husband who drinks (providing Jeannie with a mirror she refuses to look into), the one who runs the charity (competitive philanthropy), the one who is new to the group and still performing eagerness (the current object of collective assessment). All PLC or MLC or St Hilda's. All married to men in mining, finance, or law. All wearing the western suburbs uniform: good linen, quiet jewellery, expensive sunglasses pushed up on their heads.

**Beat by beat:**

- Jeannie arriving. She is precise about arrivals: not first (eager), not last (rude), but second or third (correct). She parks the Range Rover on the street -- not the Evoque but the full-size, which she lobbied for and Bob bought -- and checks her face in the rear-view mirror. A habit so ingrained it is physiological. What she sees: dark hair, green eyes, fine-boned face. Still beautiful at forty-five, though "still" is a word she would reject. The Botox is subtle. The filler is restrained. She looks like herself at thirty-eight, which is the goal -- not youth but the arrest of time.

- The table. Jeannie reads the room instantly -- who is sitting where, who arrived together, what has already been said. This is her genius, her real intelligence: the ability to decode social arrangements at a glance and position herself at the centre without appearing to do so. She sits, orders a sauvignon blanc (it is 12:15; this is the first glass, the legitimate glass, the one that doesn't count), and the performance begins.

- The conversation: a social ballet. Topics rotate with practised rhythm: children (achievements, anxieties, school politics), holidays (recently returned, currently planning, competitive destination-dropping), real estate (who sold, for how much, the horror of the eastern suburbs), bodies (subtle references to weight, skin, procedures, always framed as health rather than vanity), and -- the main event -- other people. The gossip is the point. It circulates like a currency: you pay with information and you receive social standing. Jeannie is the central bank. She receives the most and distributes the least, which is how power works.

- Jeannie's interior during the lunch: beneath the performance, the calculations are running. She is assessing the new woman (acceptable but eager; needs to be trained). She is monitoring the one who always knows (what has she heard? what is she holding back?). She is managing her own presentation -- laughing at the right moments, touching arms, leaning in with the appearance of intimacy that is actually control.

- The drinking: Jeannie's second glass arrives without her ordering it. The waitress knows. By the third glass (ordered as "just a splash more"), the performance becomes slightly louder, the laughter slightly more generous, the edges slightly softer. This is Jeannie's sweet spot -- two and a half glasses in, where the wine rounds off the sharp corners of her anxiety and she can genuinely enjoy herself for an hour. She is funny. She is warm. She is the version of herself she wishes she were all the time. The reader should feel this -- should understand that the cruelty is the sober Jeannie, and the wine-Jeannie is closer to the person she might have been.

- A moment of real feeling: the woman with the drinking husband says something about her marriage -- not a confession, just a crack, a half-sentence that reveals more than intended. The table goes briefly silent. Jeannie touches her hand and says something kind. Not performed kindness -- genuine kindness, the kind that surprises everyone, including Jeannie. It passes. The conversation resumes. But the crack was there.

**Key dialogue moment:** The one who always knows, leaning in: "Have you heard about the Marcos?" (Referring to the Chris Marco Ponzi scandal, which is in the news -- the trial, the $250 million, the fourteen-year sentence.) The table discusses it with the delicious horror of people who believe it could never happen to them. Jeannie says: "Bob always says, if the returns look too good, they are." She believes this. The irony should be invisible to her and deafening to the reader.

**What the reader learns:** Jeannie's world from the inside. The social mechanics of the gaggle. The hierarchy, the currency of gossip, the performance of ease. Jeannie's relationship with alcohol -- functional, daily, escalating, unacknowledged. The flash of genuine warmth beneath the carapace. The Chris Marco reference -- establishing the real-world context of financial fraud in Perth.

**What is planted:** The gaggle as a Greek chorus (these women will reassemble at the birthday party and deliver the verdict). The one who always knows (she will tell Jeannie about the affair rumour and the fund). The Chris Marco discussion (the reader now knows what a Perth Ponzi looks like and what happens when it collapses). Jeannie's line about Bob -- dramatic irony that will detonate in Act IV.

---

### Scene 2 -- Napoleon Street (pp. 6-8)

**Location:** Napoleon Street, Cottesloe. After the lunch. Jeannie walks from her parked car to -- what? The site where JEANNIE used to be. She is not going inside (it closed in 2017). She is walking past.

**Characters:** Jeannie alone.

**Beat by beat:**

- Napoleon Street in the afternoon. Describe it: 250 metres of village heart. FLANNEL with its bias-cut slip dresses in the window. Picnic, the local label that has survived thirty years. The cafes -- Vans, Cimbalino. Women in activewear carrying yoga mats and takeaway lattes. The particular Cottesloe quality of it: expensive without trying, beachy without being casual, a place where a $200 linen shirt is displayed next to a surfboard wax candle and both seem natural.

- The shopfront where JEANNIE was. It is something else now -- a wellness studio, or a homewares shop, or another boutique. Jeannie walks past it. She does not slow down. But the reader should feel what she feels: the phantom limb of her one attempt at professional identity. The shop that lost $40,000 in its best year. The shop she described as "basically breaking even." The shop that was, for eight years, the only thing in her life that was hers -- not Bob's money, not Bob's name, but a space where she could exercise her genuine talent for aesthetics, for display, for making beautiful things desirable. It failed. She attributed this to the retail downturn. It was actually because she opened a luxury boutique in a suburb where women already drove to Claremont to shop.

- The Instagram: Jeannie stops at a cafe. Orders a long black (she has switched from wine to coffee -- this is the afternoon pivot, the recalibration). She checks Instagram. 4,200 followers. She scrolls through her recent posts: the boat, the house, a curated shot of the garden. The likes. She checks the likes. This is described without judgment -- the checking is compulsive, physiological, the same gesture as checking a watch. She posts a photo of her coffee. Caption: something breezy, something that performs spontaneity. She watches the first likes arrive and feels the tiny dopamine pulse that has replaced actual happiness.

- Interior: beneath the Instagram, beneath the coffee, beneath the performance of an afternoon, Jeannie is tired. Not sleepy-tired but existentially tired. The lunch was work. The walking past the shop was work. The Instagram is work. Everything is work, and the work produces nothing except the maintenance of a surface that requires more maintenance every year. She does not think this explicitly -- Jeannie does not do explicit self-reflection, which is both her tragedy and her defence -- but the reader should feel it. The wine is wearing off. The sertraline (prescribed by a GP in Subiaco she chose specifically because none of her friends go there) is doing its flat, grey, levelling work. She is forty-five years old and she is performing a life she no longer enjoys but cannot imagine replacing.

**What the reader learns:** The boutique -- its rise, its fall, its meaning. Jeannie's genuine aesthetic intelligence, trapped in a life that has no use for it. The Instagram as replacement for the shop, the shop as replacement for a self. The sertraline (mentioned for the first time, briefly, without dwelling).

**What is planted:** The empty shopfront (the loss of JEANNIE the boutique prefigures the loss of everything else). The sertraline (will become significant as the drinking worsens). Jeannie's aesthetic sense -- she does have an eye, a talent; the tragedy is that it was never developed into anything that could sustain her.

### Scene 3 -- Shanel (pp. 8-10)

**Location:** The Marine Parade house, late afternoon. Jeannie arrives home. Shanel is in the kitchen.

**Characters:** Jeannie, Shanel.

**Beat by beat:**

- Jeannie comes through the door and Shanel is there -- at the kitchen island, laptop open, textbook beside it, a mug of tea. She is studying. She is in her second year of a BA at UWA, Communications, and she is reading something -- the reader glimpses the page or the screen -- that interests her. (It could be a Woolf essay, or a photography monograph, or something about film. The specific text matters less than the fact that Shanel is engaged with something intellectual for its own sake, which neither of her parents has ever been.)

- The dynamic: Jeannie shifts into mother-mode, which for Jeannie means protege-mode. She comments on Shanel's appearance (approving but calibrating -- "You look tired, darling" or "That colour washes you out"). She asks about her day, but the questions are directives disguised as curiosity: "What did you have for lunch?" (monitoring food). "Did you see Olivia?" (monitoring social life). "What are you reading?" (assessing whether it's useful).

- Shanel's responses: practised, automatic. She gives the right answers. She closes the laptop slightly -- not shutting it, not hiding it, just angling it away from Jeannie's gaze. This is the self-censoring the biography describes: unconscious, pre-verbal, the withdrawal of the authentic self before it can be judged. She has learned this the way children learn language -- by immersion.

- A moment of almost-connection: Jeannie, still slightly softened from the wine and the genuine warmth she showed at lunch, sits down next to Shanel. She touches her daughter's hair. The gesture is tender. Jeannie says something about Shanel's beauty -- "You have my bone structure" -- which is simultaneously a compliment, a claim of ownership, and an instruction. Shanel smiles. The smile is correct. Beneath it, something else -- not rebellion, not resentment, but a faint, unnameable unease, the feeling of being curated rather than loved.

- Shanel, briefly, shows Jeannie something she's found -- a photograph, a passage, an image that interests her. She starts to explain why. Jeannie listens for approximately one sentence, then redirects: "That's nice, darling. Have you decided what you're wearing to the Hendersons'?" The moment closes. Shanel returns to her textbook. The thing she was interested in is filed away, unshared, in the growing archive of things she doesn't bother mentioning.

**Key dialogue moment:** Shanel, half to herself, about whatever she was reading: "It's about how people construct identities that--" Jeannie, already reaching for the wine fridge: "Hmm? Oh, that sounds interesting. Is it for your media thing?" The misunderstanding is total and mutual: Shanel cannot explain, Jeannie cannot hear, and both of them have been trained to let it go.

**What the reader learns:** The mother-daughter dynamic. Jeannie as narcissistic mirror -- investing in Shanel as a reflection, not a person. Shanel's buried intellectual/artistic life -- the things she self-censors. The closeness between them that is also a prison.

**What is planted:** Shanel's engagement with ideas about constructed identity (this is the intellectual framework that will eventually allow her to understand her family). The self-censoring habit (which will break open when she hears the Chaconne at the birthday party). The wine fridge -- Jeannie reaching for it the moment she gets home, automatic as breathing.

**Thread status at end of Chapter 3:**
- **Fund:** Present only through the Chris Marco conversation at lunch -- dramatic irony, the reader connecting what Jeannie cannot.
- **Violin memory:** Not present in this chapter. This is Jeannie's world, where the violin does not exist.
- **Jeannie's state:** Fully established. The drinking (functional, escalating, unacknowledged). The sertraline. The performance. The genuine warmth beneath. The exhaustion.
- **Kids:** Shanel established as the daughter being curated. Chase not present (this is deliberate -- Jeannie's chapters centre on the women).

## Chapter 4: "The Fund"

**Type:** Narrative (exposition)
**POV:** Third-person close, Bob
**Word count:** ~3,500
**Time:** A weekday, late 2025. A working day.
**Primary locations:** Harrington Capital, St George's Terrace (QV1 or a nearby tower); a restaurant on the Terrace; the drive home.

---

### Scene 1 -- The Office (pp. 1-5)

**Location:** Harrington Capital. An office in a tower on St George's Terrace -- not QV1 (that's Euroz Hartleys, the establishment), but a building a few doors down, respectable but not dominant. Level twelve, maybe. A view of the Terrace below, the glass and steel of the mining company headquarters, the park at the western end. The office itself: a reception area (unmanned most days now), a meeting room, Bob's office, and a smaller office for the part-time administrator who processes the paperwork. This is not a large operation. It never was.

**Characters:** Bob; the administrator (a woman in her fifties, competent, asks no questions); a client (Richard -- a name to remember; he will reappear at the birthday party); briefly, Jim Harrington (by phone).

**Beat by beat:**

- Morning. Bob arrives at the office. He drives in from Cottesloe -- the route along Stirling Highway, then the freeway, then into the city. He parks in the basement of the building, a reserved space with "Harrington Capital" on the sign. The German car -- a Mercedes, not the newest model, not flashy. Jeannie wanted a Porsche. Bob chose the Mercedes because his father drove Volvos and a Mercedes felt like an appropriate escalation: substantial, reliable, a car that says money without shouting it.

- The office routine: Bob at his desk. The desk is clean -- not compulsively clean, but the clean of a man who does not have enough real work to create mess. Two screens: one shows the market (ASX, scrolling tickers, green and red), the other shows email. The fund's assets under management were, at peak, around $85 million. They are now -- in reality -- somewhere closer to $30 million in actual assets backing approximately $75 million in reported obligations. The gap is the Ponzi. Bob does not keep a spreadsheet of the gap. He keeps two sets of books: the one the investors see (steady returns, quarterly reports, the reassuring fiction) and the one in his head (the real positions, the losses, the quiet transfers). The second set is not written down. If it were written down, it would be evidence.

- The mechanics of the fraud: described without drama. This is the chapter's tonal key -- the banality of it. Bob opens his email. There are three redemption requests this month. One is small ($50,000 -- he can meet it from the operating account). One is medium ($200,000 -- he will need to liquidate a real position, which will widen the gap). One is from Richard -- $500,000, requested politely, with a note about a property purchase. Bob reads Richard's email twice. He does not respond immediately. He will call Richard later. He will suggest a meeting. In the meeting he will explain that the fund's investment cycle favours a twelve-month redemption horizon and that early withdrawal would mean missing the next quarter's returns. He has said this before, to other investors. It works because people don't want to miss returns, and because Bob's face, his voice, his manner, make everything sound reasonable. He is lying, and the lie is fluent because it is the only fluent thing about him.

- How the Ponzi grew: a compressed backstory within the scene. Bob at his desk, not working, remembering. The mining boom: 2003-2011, iron ore from $32 to $180, everything Bob touched turning to gold, the fund tripling in four years. He was not a genius. He was a man in a boat on a rising tide. The bust: 2012, the iron ore price crashing, exploration companies evaporating, Bob's private equity positions becoming worthless. The first lie: a quarterly report that smoothed a loss into a flat return. Not fabricated -- adjusted. The word matters. Then: a redemption he couldn't meet, a transfer from one account to cover another, the promise to himself that he'd fix it next month. The next month: another transfer. And another. By 2015: structurally insolvent. By now: a decade of compounding deception, a machine that runs on new money and Bob's ability to look people in the eye.

- The emotional register: Bob does not feel guilt in the conventional sense. He feels something closer to vertigo -- the perpetual, low-grade dizziness of a man standing on a surface he knows is not solid. He compartmentalises. This is the skill. At the office, he is the fund manager. At home, he is the husband. The two realities do not touch. The space between them is where Bob lives, which is to say: Bob lives nowhere.

**What the reader learns:** The fund. How it works, how it broke, how the Ponzi operates day to day. The banality of financial crime -- not dramatic, not cinematic, just emails and spreadsheets and the slow accretion of lies. Bob's competence at deception and his horror at it. The mining boom/bust cycle as context.

**What is planted:** Richard (the $500,000 redemption -- he will be at the birthday party, and his inability to get his money out will be part of the gossip that Jeannie hears). The two sets of books (one real, one fictive -- this mirrors the two Bobs, the two marriages, the two lives). The phrase "fix it next month" (the addict's logic that will escalate through Act III).

---

### Scene 2 -- The Client Meeting (pp. 5-9)

**Location:** A restaurant on St George's Terrace -- one of the business lunch venues. Wildflower, or Petition, or something slightly below that tier (Bob is careful not to appear too lavish; the performance of the responsible steward). Early afternoon.

**Characters:** Bob, Richard.

**Beat by beat:**

- Richard: establish him briefly. Older than Bob -- late fifties, maybe sixty. A retired mining executive who made his money in the real economy and entrusted a portion of it to Bob because they were at the same yacht club and their wives sat on the same charity committee. Richard is not stupid. He is not aggressive. He is simply a man who wants his money and is beginning to wonder, with the quiet alarm of a person who has always assumed the world works the way it appears to work, why it is taking so long.

- The lunch: Bob performs. This is the scene where the reader sees Bob lie, in real time, with the full force of his charm. He is warm. He is attentive. He asks about Richard's grandchildren (he remembers their names). He orders a bottle of wine (not the most expensive -- the second most expensive, which signals confidence without extravagance). He steers the conversation away from the redemption for twenty minutes, building rapport, creating the atmosphere of two friends having lunch rather than a client confronting a manager.

- The ask: Richard, eventually, brings it up. He needs the $500,000. The property purchase. His tone is apologetic -- he doesn't want to be difficult. Bob listens with the patient, nodding attention of a man who takes your concerns seriously. Then he speaks.

- The lie: Bob explains that the fund's current portfolio is weighted toward positions with a twelve-to-eighteen-month horizon. Liquidating now would crystallise losses and reduce Richard's overall return. He recommends waiting until Q2 next year. He has projections -- not on paper (never on paper), but in his head, which he shares with the casual authority of a man who lives and breathes this. The projections are invented. The positions they describe do not exist, or do not exist in the amounts Bob implies. But the delivery is flawless. Bob's voice is calm, his eye contact steady, his body language open. He is not sweating. He is not nervous. He is doing the thing he is best at, which is making people feel that everything is fine.

- Richard's response: he accepts. Not happily, but he accepts. He trusts Bob. This is the word -- trust. It sits between them on the table like a loaded weapon. Bob pours more wine. They talk about the cricket. They shake hands at the door. Richard walks away reassured. Bob walks back to the office and sits at his desk and stares at the wall for fifteen minutes.

**Key dialogue moment:** Richard, over the second glass: "I'm not worried, Bob. I just need to know it's there." Bob, without hesitation: "It's there, Richard. It's always been there." The simplicity of the lie. The sincerity with which it is delivered. The reader should feel sick.

**What the reader learns:** How Bob lies. The social machinery of trust -- the yacht club, the wives, the lunches, the wine. The specific anatomy of a Ponzi lie: not dramatic, not even particularly clever, just the confident assertion that the money exists when it does not. Richard as a human being whose trust is being exploited -- the reader should feel the weight of what Bob is doing to him.

**What is planted:** Richard's growing suspicion (not yet suspicion -- more like unease. It will grow). The phrase "It's there" -- one of the novel's load-bearing lies, which will echo when the fund collapses and it manifestly is not there.

---

### Scene 3 -- The Drive Home / Jim (pp. 9-12)

**Location:** The freeway from the city to Cottesloe. Late afternoon. Then: a phone call.

**Characters:** Bob alone; Jim Harrington (by phone).

**Beat by beat:**

- The drive: Bob in the Mercedes, heading west on the Mitchell Freeway. The late-afternoon light. Kings Park on the left, the escarpment of bushland above the freeway, the city skyline receding in the mirror. The traffic: Perth traffic is not Sydney or Melbourne traffic, but the freeway bottleneck at the Narrows Bridge is real, and Bob sits in it every day, in the sealed cabin of his car, alone with the fraud.

- Bob's interior on the drive: the weight of it. Describe the specific, physical sensation of carrying the lie. It is not dramatic. It is not a thriller. It is more like a chronic illness -- a heaviness in the chest, a tightness in the throat, a low-frequency anxiety that never fully resolves. He has lived with this for a decade. He has become accustomed to it the way you become accustomed to chronic pain: it doesn't go away, but you learn which movements to avoid.

- The phone rings. Jim. Bob's father: eighty now, retired for fifteen years, living in the same house on Pearse Street where Bob grew up. Wendy died three years ago (mention this briefly -- Bob's grief for his mother was quiet and genuine and is one of the few authentic emotions he has allowed himself in adulthood). Jim calls twice a week. The calls follow a pattern: weather, cricket, the market. Jim still reads the financial pages. He still has opinions. He still thinks of Bob as the boy who went to Patersons.

- Jim asks about the fund. Not suspiciously -- with the pride of a father whose son is doing the thing he taught him to do. "How's the book looking?" "Markets are choppy, must be keeping you busy." Bob answers in the language Jim taught him -- calm, competent, measured. He tells his father the fund is performing well. He tells his father the clients are happy. Every word is a lie delivered in the vocabulary of the man who made him.

- The cruelty of this: Bob is not only defrauding his clients. He is defrauding his father -- the man whose approval he has spent his entire life seeking, the man whose expectations created the path that led to the fraud. Jim is proud of Bob. Jim tells his friends at the bowling club that his son runs a fund. This pride is built on nothing, and Bob maintains it with the same careful, fluent dishonesty he uses on Richard, on Jeannie, on everyone. The difference is that lying to his father costs him something the other lies do not. He can feel the weight of it in his hands on the steering wheel. His left hand tightens. Not the violin grip this time -- just a fist.

- Bob arrives home. Marine Parade. The house. He sits in the car for a moment after turning off the engine. The garage is quiet. The house above him is lit -- Jeannie's silhouette moving past an upstairs window. He closes his eyes. The reader should feel, in this moment, the full claustrophobia of Bob's life: the fraud at work, the performance at home, the lie in every direction, and no door that opens into a room where he can be real. Not yet.

**What the reader learns:** The emotional cost of the fraud. Jim as the person Bob most fears disappointing. The drive home as daily purgatory -- the transition from the lying self to the performing self, with no authentic self in between.

**What is planted:** Jim's pride (will make the eventual exposure devastating -- not just public shame but the destruction of his father's image of him). Bob in the car, eyes closed, the sealed space (this is the man who will eventually build a soundproofed room -- he is already seeking enclosure, already looking for a space where the world cannot reach him). The fist on the steering wheel (contrasted with the violin grip -- the two gestures that define Bob's hands and what they hold).

**Thread status at end of Chapter 4:**
- **Fund:** Fully established. The mechanics, the scale (~$45M gap), the daily practice of lying. Richard as a specific victim with a name and grandchildren.
- **Violin memory:** Present only as physical ghost -- the hand on the steering wheel. The violin is beneath the surface of this chapter, as the fraud is beneath the surface of Bob's life.
- **Jeannie's state:** Glimpsed from outside (the silhouette in the window). She is part of the structure Bob must maintain.
- **Kids:** Not present. This is a chapter about work, about the masculine world of the Terrace, about the inheritance from father to son.

## Chapter 5: "Rottnest"

**Type:** Narrative (family dynamics through Chase's eyes)
**POV:** Third-person close, Chase
**Word count:** ~2,500
**Time:** A Saturday, late 2025. A Rotto weekend with Chase and his mates, Bob joining for the day.
**Primary locations:** The Riviera 48 at Thomson Bay mooring; Rottnest Island shore; the boat at dusk.

---

### Scene 1 -- The Crossing (pp. 1-3)

**Location:** The Riviera "Chardonnay," motoring from RFBYC across to Rottnest. Saturday morning, early. The open water between Fremantle and the island.

**Characters:** Chase at the helm; two mates (interchangeable -- one is louder, one is quieter; they don't need to be deeply drawn); Bob on the flybridge.

**Beat by beat:**

- Chase's world: the chapter opens in his head and the difference should be immediate. The prose tightens, becomes more physical, less introspective. Chase thinks in action, in movement, in the language of bodies and boats and weather. He does not think in abstractions. His inner monologue is terse, confident, a little shallow, and entirely unaware of its own limitations.

- The crossing from Chase's perspective. He is at the helm. The twin Cat diesels rumbling beneath him, 680 horsepower per side, the throttle vibrating under his palm. The boat is his. Not legally, not financially, but practically -- he has driven it more in the last three years than Bob has. He knows the crossing by feel: the swell pattern south of the groyne, the wind shift at the halfway point, the moment when Rottnest resolves from a smudge on the horizon into an actual island, low and scrubby and ringed with that insane turquoise water.

- His mates: established through action, not description. They are loading beers into the cooler. They are talking about football -- the West Coast Eagles, or Fremantle, or the local footy team one of them plays for. The conversation is competitive, profane, affectionate. This is Chase's language. He is fluent in it the way his father is fluent in investor reassurance.

- Bob: visible but separate. He is on the flybridge, sitting in the skipper's chair that Chase has vacated. He has a coffee. He is looking at the ocean. Chase registers this peripherally -- Dad's in one of his moods, that blank look he gets, like he's tuned to a radio station no one else can hear. Chase doesn't think about it further. He has learned not to examine his parents too closely, because examination leads to questions and questions lead to the kind of emotional territory that his family does not visit.

- The arrival at Thomson Bay: Chase navigates the entrance, picks up the mooring, kills the engines. The silence when the diesels stop is always a shock -- the sudden presence of wind, birds, water lapping against the hull. Chase is good at this. He is physically competent in a way that gives him genuine satisfaction, and the reader should feel this: whatever else is wrong with Chase's world, his relationship with the boat is authentic.

**What the reader learns:** Chase's voice -- faster, more physical, less reflective than Bob's. His relationship with the boat. His mates. The way he sees his father: fondly, distantly, without curiosity. The reader should note the lack of curiosity -- it is the space where Chase's story will eventually break open.

**What is planted:** "Tuned to a radio station no one else can hear" -- Chase's description of Bob's abstraction, which will become literal when Bob starts playing the violin. Chase's competence with the boat (the one inheritance that will survive).

---

### Scene 2 -- Ashore (pp. 3-6)

**Location:** Rottnest Island -- Thomson Bay settlement, the Rottnest Hotel pub, the beaches. Saturday afternoon.

**Characters:** Chase, his mates, briefly other boat people (the Rotto weekend crowd).

**Beat by beat:**

- The Rotto weekend ritual, from Chase's perspective. They take the tender to shore. The settlement: the general store, the bakery, the bike hire. The Rottnest Hotel -- the pub, specifically the beer garden, which is the social centre of Rotto weekends for western suburbs twenty-somethings. Mid-strength beer (the island's rule, which everyone supplements with full-strength from the boat). The scene is a catalogue of Chase's social world: the boys from Scotch, from Aquinas, from Hale; the girls from PLC, from Iona, from St Hilda's. Everyone knows everyone. Everyone's parents know everyone's parents. This is Perth's closed loop -- the school system feeding into the university system feeding into the yacht club system feeding into the professional system, and at every stage the same two hundred families circulating through the same rooms.

- Chase among his peers. He is popular -- big, confident, the boy with the boat. He buys rounds. He tells stories. He performs the version of masculinity that his world rewards: physical, generous, slightly aggressive, never vulnerable. He has a girl interested in him -- she is here, somewhere, with her friends, and the dance of approach and retreat is happening on the edges of the afternoon. Chase does not pursue her directly. He waits. This is the power dynamic he learned from Jeannie (though he would be horrified to know this): control through the withholding of attention.

- The anti-women posture: established subtly, not as a manifesto but as a texture. The way Chase talks about girls with his mates -- not viciously, but with a casual objectification that treats their interest as a given and their feelings as an inconvenience. He has had three serious girlfriends. All were attractive. All were eventually bewildered by his emotional unavailability. He loses interest the moment a woman shows vulnerability, because vulnerability in a woman looks to Chase like what vulnerability in Jeannie looks like: a weapon, a demand, a trap. He does not know this about himself. The reader should.

- A moment of youth and beauty: the afternoon on the island should also be vivid, sensory, alive. The water. The white sand. The quokkas (the little marsupials that tourists photograph). The particular quality of Rotto light -- hard, bleached, the sky so blue it looks manufactured. Chase jumping off the jetty with his mates, the water cold and clear, their bodies strong and careless. There is something genuinely joyful here. Chase's world is limited, but within its limits, it has pleasures that are real.

**What the reader learns:** Chase's social world -- the closed loop of Perth's western suburbs. His performative masculinity. The anti-women posture (traced, by the reader, to Jeannie). The claustrophobia of a small city's social network. But also: the genuine pleasures of being twenty-one and strong and in the water on a Saturday.

**What is planted:** The girl (unnamed -- she may or may not reappear; her function is to demonstrate Chase's pattern). The closed social loop (these people will all be at the birthday party; there is no escaping your social world in Perth).

---

### Scene 3 -- The Boat at Dusk (pp. 6-8)

**Location:** Back on the Riviera, moored in Thomson Bay. Dusk. The mates below, asleep or on their phones. Chase and Bob on the flybridge.

**Characters:** Chase, Bob.

**Beat by beat:**

- Chase comes up to the flybridge and finds Bob there. His father has been on the boat all day -- he didn't come ashore. Chase registers this without alarm (Bob sometimes does this; he says he prefers the quiet). Bob has a beer, barely touched. He is looking at the ocean -- west, toward the horizon, where the sun is setting. The sky is doing the thing it does in Perth: copper, then rose, then violet, the colours deepening as the light fades, the Indian Ocean turning from blue to silver to black.

- Father and son, together. They do not have a conversation about feelings. They talk about the boat -- the port engine is running slightly rough, might need new injectors. They talk about football -- the Dockers' season, a game last weekend. They talk about Chase's degree, briefly, in the language of obligation: "How's uni going?" "Yeah, good." "Keeping up?" "Yeah." The conversation is a mutual performance of normality, and both of them are comfortable with it because both of them have been trained (by Jim, by Jeannie, by Perth itself) to treat emotional surfaces as sufficient.

- The moment: Bob falls silent. Chase, who has been talking about something -- a mate's job, a property deal -- realises his father is not listening. He looks at Bob and sees something he has no framework for. Bob is staring at the ocean with an expression Chase cannot name. It is not sadness, not exactly. It is something larger and more frightening: the look of a man who is seeing something that isn't there, or remembering something he has lost, or measuring the distance between where he is and where he should have been. Chase has never seen this face on his father. He has seen the performance face, the charm face, the tired face, the angry face (rare). This is different. This is Bob without any face at all.

- Chase says: "You right, Dad?" And Bob turns and the face disappears and the performing self returns and he says, "Yeah, mate. Good sunset." And Chase nods and lets it go. Because what else can he do? He is twenty-one. He does not have the language for what he just saw. He files it away in the place where he files things he cannot process, which is the same place Bob files his grief and his music and his fraud -- the sealed room, the closed door, the place where the things you cannot speak of wait.

**Key dialogue moment:** The exchange above. "You right, Dad?" / "Yeah, mate. Good sunset." -- the inadequacy of language between men who love each other and cannot say so.

**What the reader learns:** The tenderness between Bob and Chase -- real, inarticulate, structured by silence. The limits of Chase's understanding. The terrifying blankness of Bob's unguarded face.

**What is planted:** "You right, Dad?" will echo through the novel. Chase has seen his father's real face once and did not know what to do with it. After the birthday performance, he will see it again and begin to understand. The ocean at sunset -- this specific image (Bob looking west, at the water, at the horizon) will recur in the final chapter.

**Thread status at end of Chapter 5:**
- **Fund:** Not present in this chapter. The fund does not exist in Chase's world (yet -- his question at dinner in Chapter 22 will change this).
- **Violin memory:** Present in Bob's abstraction -- the blankness Chase sees is the same blankness of a man hearing an internal music. But Chase cannot decode it.
- **Jeannie's state:** Not present. She is not on this trip. Her absence is itself a statement (she chose not to come; the boat is Bob's domain, and she cedes it).
- **Kids:** Chase fully established as a POV character. Shanel absent (she will get her own moment later).

## Chapter 6: "The Terrace"

**Type:** Narrative
**POV:** Third-person close, Bob
**Word count:** ~3,000
**Time:** A weekday, approximately a month after Chapter 4. Late 2025, moving toward Christmas.
**Primary locations:** Harrington Capital office; briefly, Jim's house on Pearse Street.

---

### Scene 1 -- The Phone Call (pp. 1-4)

**Location:** Bob's office, St George's Terrace. Morning.

**Characters:** Bob; an investor (on the phone -- a new voice, not Richard, someone sharper, more insistent).

**Beat by beat:**

- The chapter opens with the phone ringing. Not the mobile (the mobile rings with messages Bob can triage) but the office landline, which investors use when they want formality, when they want a record, when the tone has shifted from inquiry to demand.

- The caller: an investor Bob has been avoiding. Not Richard -- someone else, someone whose redemption request Bob deferred three months ago with the twelve-month-horizon speech. This person has done what Bob feared someone would eventually do: they have spoken to their accountant, or their lawyer, or another investor. They have compared notes. They have questions Bob cannot answer.

- The questions: specific, technical, the kind of questions that reveal the caller has been thinking. "What's the fund's current NAV?" "Can I see the most recent audited statements?" "Who is your auditor?" Bob answers smoothly -- he has answers for all of these, prepared answers, answers that are technically accurate in ways that obscure the truth. But the caller is not satisfied. The caller asks a question that cuts through the prepared responses: "Bob, I just want to hear you say it. Is the fund solvent?"

- Bob's response: a pause. The pause is fractional -- half a second, maybe less -- but it is the first time in ten years that Bob has hesitated. He says: "The fund is performing within its mandate. I can send you the latest quarterly." The caller is not reassured. The call ends with a polite tension that Bob recognises as the beginning of the end, or at least the beginning of the beginning of the end.

- After the call: Bob sits at his desk. His hands are shaking slightly. He opens the real spreadsheet -- the one in his head, the one he never commits to paper -- and runs the numbers. The gap has widened. Three more redemptions have come in this week. He can meet one. He cannot meet all three. He needs new money. New money requires new lies, new lunches, new performances of confidence. The machine is speeding up. The machine has always been speeding up, but the acceleration was gradual enough to be normalised. Now it is not.

**What the reader learns:** The fund is deteriorating faster. The careful compartmentalisation is breaking down. Someone is asking questions Bob cannot answer. The clock is ticking.

**What is planted:** The investor who compared notes (this is how Ponzis unravel -- not through investigation but through the simple, devastating act of investors talking to each other). The pause before Bob's answer (his first crack). The phrase "beginning of the beginning of the end."

### Scene 2 -- Jim's Visit (pp. 4-8)

**Location:** The office, then Jim's house on Pearse Street, Cottesloe. Afternoon.

**Characters:** Bob, Jim Harrington.

**Beat by beat:**

- Jim arrives at the office. He does this occasionally -- drops in, the way retired men drop in on the places they used to matter. He is eighty, physically diminished (slower, thinner, the Volvo replaced by a Toyota he drives too cautiously), but mentally sharp in the specific way of old stockbrokers -- he reads the AFR, he has opinions, he remembers the names of Bob's major clients because they were his clients first.

- Jim in the office: describe the visit through Bob's eyes. Jim looks around with the pride of a man surveying what his son has built, not knowing that what his son has built is a lie. He asks about the market. He asks about specific stocks. He gives unsolicited advice that is twenty years out of date but delivered with the authority of a man who once ran his own book. Bob listens, nods, performs the role of son-receiving-wisdom.

- The hiding: while Jim talks, Bob is acutely conscious of everything in the office that could betray him. The real numbers are not on paper, but the quarterly reports are -- the fabricated ones, beautifully formatted, that go to investors. Jim picks one up from the credenza. "Mind if I have a look?" Bob's heart rate spikes, then settles. The quarterly is designed to be convincing. Jim reads it, nods approvingly. "Twelve percent annualised? In this market? That's strong, son." Bob says: "We've had a good run." The lie, delivered to his father, in his father's voice, using his father's language.

- Jim tells a story. About the old days, about Patersons, about a client who panicked during the '87 crash and sold everything at the bottom. The moral of the story is about steady hands, about not panicking, about the long view. Jim is, without knowing it, describing the opposite of what Bob has done -- Bob did not panic and sell; he panicked and lied, which is worse. Bob listens to the story and smiles and nods and feels, beneath the performance, a grief so specific and so deep that he cannot look at it directly: he has taken the life his father gave him and turned it into a fraud, and his father's pride is the cruelest punishment because it is undeserved.

- Bob drives Jim home. Pearse Street. The limestone cottage. The garden Wendy kept, now maintained by a service Jim hired because he cannot manage it alone. Bob walks his father to the door. Jim says something at the threshold -- "You're doing well, Bob. Your mother would be proud." Bob drives back to the office, pulls into the basement car park, and sits in the dark with the engine off.

**Key dialogue moment:** Jim, reading the quarterly: "Twelve percent annualised? In this market? That's strong, son." Bob: "We've had a good run." -- The generational lie. The son performing for the father, using the father's own values to deceive him.

**What the reader learns:** Jim as the source of everything Bob is -- the broker's language, the values, the path. The generational weight. The specific pain of deceiving the person whose approval you most need. The house on Pearse Street as the origin point (where the violin once lived in a wardrobe, where the boy became the broker).

**What is planted:** Jim's pride (this will shatter). The Pearse Street house (the childhood home, the violin in the wardrobe -- though this is not mentioned here, the reader who remembers Chapter 2 will feel its presence). Wendy's absence -- Bob's mother, the woman who signed him up for violin, is dead. The one person who might have seen him clearly is gone.

### Scene 3 -- The Gap Widens (pp. 8-10)

**Location:** The office, late afternoon. Then the drive home.

**Characters:** Bob alone.

**Beat by beat:**

- Back at the office after dropping Jim home. The day is ending. The Terrace below is emptying -- the suited workers heading for the parking garages, the after-work drinks at the Palace Hotel or one of the rooftop bars. Bob is at his desk. He should be working on the problem -- the redemptions, the gap, the fund's accelerating decline. Instead he is doing something else.

- He is on YouTube. The algorithm has refined its understanding of him. Tonight it offers: a video titled "Bach Chaconne -- Hilary Hahn." He does not play it. He reads the title. He reads the description. He does not know what a chaconne is. He does not know why the algorithm is showing him this. He closes the tab.

- The drive home: the same route, the same freeway, the same bottleneck at the Narrows Bridge. But something has shifted. The phone call this morning -- the investor with questions -- has cracked something. Bob is, for the first time, consciously imagining the end. Not planning for it (planning requires agency, and agency is what Bob lacks) but imagining it. What would happen. Who would know. What Jeannie would say. What his father would say. What the headline would be. He imagines the Chris Marco trial, which he has followed with the horrified fascination of a man watching someone else crash the same car he is driving. Fourteen years. $250 million. 151 investors. Bob's fraud is smaller -- perhaps $45 million in actual losses -- but the mechanics are the same, and the law does not care about scale.

- He arrives home. Jeannie is out (a dinner, a event, something). The house is empty. He stands in the hallway, in the silence, and the silence is total -- no children (Chase at a mate's, Shanel at the library), no wife, no phone ringing, no one needing anything from him. He stands in the hallway of the house that cost $4.5 million and is empty, and he feels the specific loneliness of a man who is surrounded by the evidence of a successful life and knows that none of it is real.

**What the reader learns:** The fund is approaching crisis. Bob is beginning to imagine the end. The Chris Marco parallel -- the reader sees the future. The loneliness that is the true cost of the fraud: not the money but the isolation.

**What is planted:** The YouTube algorithm (it will succeed eventually -- the Chaconne is circling, approaching). The imagined headlines (which will become real headlines). The empty house (which will be the house where, in Act IV, the violin is smashed and Bob walks out).

**Thread status at end of Chapter 6:**
- **Fund:** Accelerating crisis. An investor asking hard questions. Redemptions mounting. The gap widening. The Chris Marco parallel made explicit.
- **Violin memory:** The YouTube algorithm pushing violin content. Bob not yet clicking. The approach is glacial but inevitable.
- **Jeannie's state:** Absent from this chapter (she is out, performing elsewhere). Her absence itself is a data point -- she and Bob are leading parallel lives.
- **Kids:** Absent. This is a chapter about the Terrace, about fathers and sons (Bob and Jim), about the masculine world of finance and fraud.

## Chapter 7: "What Jeannie Doesn't Know"

**Type:** Narrative (dual perspective)
**POV:** Alternating interiors -- Jeannie, then Bob, then Jeannie
**Word count:** ~2,500
**Time:** An evening, late 2025 / early 2026. The family at home.
**Primary location:** The Marine Parade house.

### Scene 1 -- Jeannie's Evening (pp. 1-3)

**Location:** The house. Upstairs -- Jeannie's bedroom (she and Bob sleep separately; she calls the spare room "my retreat," which sounds like a lifestyle choice rather than the collapse of physical intimacy it actually represents). Then the kitchen.

**Characters:** Jeannie alone, then Jeannie and Shanel briefly.

**Beat by beat:**

- Jeannie in her retreat. Late afternoon, post-lunch, the dangerous hour. She has been out with one of the gaggle and is home now, slightly lit, transitioning from social wine to domestic wine. She changes clothes -- from the lunch outfit to what she thinks of as home clothes, which are still expensive, still curated, because Jeannie does not have an off switch. She catches her reflection in the full-length mirror and performs the daily assessment: face (holding), body (thin enough), hair (needs colour). She is forty-five. She is maintaining.

- The pills: she opens the drawer of her bedside table. The sertraline is there, in a prescription bottle from the Subiaco GP. She takes one. She has been taking them for seven years. They work, or she believes they work, or the combination of sertraline and sauvignon blanc produces a chemical state she has accepted as normal. She does not think of herself as medicated. She thinks of herself as managed.

- Downstairs. Jeannie in the kitchen, pouring a glass. Shanel passes through -- on her way out, or on her way to her room, a brief transit. Jeannie asks where she's going (monitoring). Shanel answers (deflecting). They exchange a look that contains twenty years of choreography: Jeannie checking, Shanel complying, both performing the gestures of intimacy without its substance.

- The boutique: Jeannie's phone shows a memory -- Instagram's "on this day" function, showing her a photo from 2016, the boutique in full swing, Jeannie behind the counter in a silk blouse, champagne flute in hand, a customer smiling. The memory arrives like a knife. She swipes past it. She pours more wine.

**What the reader learns:** Jeannie's private world -- the separate bedroom, the pills, the wine, the Instagram memories of a life she no longer has. The architecture of her denial.

**What is planted:** The separate bedroom (the marriage's physical deadness). The Instagram memory function (the app as a weapon, reminding her of what she's lost).

### Scene 2 -- Bob's Evening (pp. 3-5)

**Location:** Downstairs. The living room, then the home office (not yet soundproofed -- this is a regular office, a desk, a computer, a door that closes).

**Characters:** Bob alone.

**Beat by beat:**

- Bob is home. He has been home for an hour. He has changed out of his office clothes and is in the living room, the television on, muted (his habit -- the flicker of images without the demand of sound). He is on his phone. He is on YouTube.

- The algorithm: tonight, it has offered him something specific. Not just violin performances but a specific video: a masterclass, a teacher working with a student on a Bach partita movement. He watches. The teacher adjusts the student's bow hold. The student plays a phrase and the teacher says, "Listen to the singing quality -- let the bow find the string." Bob watches this for twenty minutes. He does not know he is learning. His body knows: his left hand, resting on his thigh, is moving, the fingers pressing against nothing, finding positions on an invisible fingerboard.

- The search: Bob opens a browser. He types: "violin teacher Perth adults." He looks at the results. He does not click. He closes the browser. He opens YouTube again. He finds the video he closed two chapters ago: "Bach Chaconne -- Hilary Hahn." He hovers over it. He does not click.

- His phone buzzes. A message from the associate (or from an investor, or from Richard). The fund. He reads it, responds with something brief and reassuring, and the real world closes over the crack like water closing over a stone.

**What the reader learns:** The YouTube sessions are becoming a ritual -- Bob's secret nighttime life. He is approaching the violin from the outside, through screens, through the algorithm, the way a man might approach something he desires but cannot justify. The search for a teacher -- the first almost-act.

**What is planted:** "Violin teacher Perth adults" -- the search that will eventually find Katarina. The Hilary Hahn Chaconne video -- he has not clicked it yet. When he does, the novel will pivot. The hand on the thigh, pressing invisible strings -- the body remembering before the mind allows it.

### Scene 3 -- Dinner (pp. 5-7)

**Location:** The kitchen. Evening. A meal neither of them has cooked (something from a delivery, or something the part-time housekeeper left in the fridge).

**Characters:** Bob, Jeannie. (Chase is out; Shanel is in her room.)

**Beat by beat:**

- They eat together. This is rare -- perhaps twice a week, when their schedules align and neither has found a reason to eat separately. The kitchen island, two stools, two plates, the wine bottle between them (Jeannie's; Bob has a beer). The conversation is transactional: the house (a plumbing issue), the kids (Chase's university enrollment, Shanel's car service), the Europe trip.

- The Europe trip: Jeannie raises it. She has been planning. Three weeks, she says. London first, then Paris, then "maybe Austria -- everyone says Vienna." She has hotels bookmarked. She has an itinerary colour-coded in a Google doc. She has outfits planned for each city. Bob listens. He nods. He says: "Sounds great." He does not tell her that when she said Austria, something moved in his chest. He does not know what moved or why. But the word -- Austria -- sits in the air between them like a note struck on a string.

- The distance: describe the physical reality of two people in a kitchen who have been married for twenty-one years and have run out of things to say. The silences are not hostile. They are simply empty. Jeannie scrolls her phone between bites. Bob stares at a point above her head. They do not discuss feelings, or the fund, or the future, or anything that exists below the surface of logistics. They discuss renovations. They discuss the Hendersons' upcoming party. They go to bed.

- The bed: or rather, the beds. Jeannie to her retreat. Bob to the master bedroom, alone. The width of a king-size mattress and approximately thirty years of accumulated silence. He lies in the dark and thinks about Austria.

**Key dialogue moment:** Jeannie, over dinner: "I was thinking Vienna, then maybe some little towns. Salzburg. You know." Bob: "Austria sounds good." The flatness of his response conceals the earthquake beneath it. Jeannie interprets his compliance as indifference. The reader knows it is something else entirely.

**What the reader learns:** The marriage as parallel solitudes. The utter absence of intimacy -- not even conflict, just emptiness. The Europe trip taking shape as Jeannie's project. Austria as the word that triggers something in Bob he cannot name.

**What is planted:** Austria. This is the mechanism that will crack everything open. The word sits in the novel like a seed. The Europe trip -- Jeannie's vision (Instagram content, luxury hotels, the performance of cultured travel) versus Bob's secret hope (unnamed, even to himself, but the reader should feel it: something about Austria, about Alpine towns, about a place where violins are made).

### Scene 4 -- The Wardrobe (pp. 7-8)

**Location:** The master bedroom, or a hallway cupboard. Late at night or early morning.

**Characters:** Jeannie alone.

**Beat by beat:**

- A brief, sharp scene. Jeannie, unable to sleep (the wine wearing off, the sertraline's flat grey hum, the 3am anxiety that is her truest companion), gets up. She goes to find a blanket, or a jumper, or something from the top of a wardrobe -- the wardrobe in the hallway, or the storage cupboard, or the back of Bob's closet.

- She finds the violin case. The old one. Not a case she has seen before -- or rather, a case she saw once, years ago, when they moved to Marine Parade, a battered black case that Bob carried in himself and put on the top shelf of the wardrobe without explanation. She asked about it then. He said: "Old stuff from school." She forgot about it.

- Now she pulls it down. She opens it. Inside: a student violin, a Czech factory instrument, the bow loose, the strings slack, the horsehair yellowed. It smells of rosin and old wood and something else -- the specific, slightly sweet smell of an instrument that has not been played in decades. Jeannie looks at it.

- Her response: she mocks it. Not aloud (there's no one to perform for) but internally, and then, when she sees Bob the next morning, a small, cutting remark. "I found your fiddle. Shall I put it in the charity bin?" Or: "I didn't know you played the violin. How very Year Seven." The remark is designed to diminish, to make the object small and silly, to ensure that whatever it represents cannot threaten the world she has constructed. Bob's response: something neutral, deflecting. "Just kids' stuff. Leave it." He does not defend the violin. He does not explain. He closes the door on it, as he has always closed the door.

- But: Jeannie noticed something. Not in the violin but in Bob's face when she mentioned it. A flicker. A contraction around the eyes. The same expression she saw when Chase fell off the boat as a toddler and Bob dove in after him -- fear, not for himself but for something he loved. She files this away. She does not understand it. She will remember it later.

**Key dialogue moment:** Jeannie: "I found your fiddle in the wardrobe. Shall I chuck it?" Bob: "Leave it. It's nothing." -- The instrument dismissed. But the reader hears what Jeannie does not: "It's nothing" is the voice of a man protecting something by denying it.

**What the reader learns:** The violin has been in the house all along, in a wardrobe, waiting. Jeannie's instinct to mock and diminish anything that threatens her. Bob's protective denial. The marriage's power dynamic distilled: Jeannie attacks, Bob absorbs, the real thing goes back into hiding.

**What is planted:** The violin in the wardrobe (Chekhov's gun from Chapter 2, now physically present). Jeannie's flicker of recognition -- she saw something in Bob's face. She will weaponise this much later, when the new violin arrives from Absam and the stakes are infinitely higher. The "fiddle" vs. "violin" -- Jeannie's word choice is deliberate diminishment; it will contrast with the reverence the instrument receives in Acts II and III.

**Thread status at end of Chapter 7:**
- **Fund:** Present in the background -- Bob's phone buzzing with messages. The fund exists as ambient pressure in every scene.
- **Violin memory:** Surfacing. The YouTube sessions. The search for a teacher (not yet clicked). Austria triggering something. The old violin found in the wardrobe. The approach is accelerating.
- **Jeannie's state:** Fully dimensional. The pills, the wine, the separate bedroom, the Instagram memories. The mockery of the violin as defence mechanism. But also: the flash of recognition, the moment when she sees Bob's face and registers something she cannot name.
- **Kids:** Peripheral. Shanel passing through. Chase absent. The family as a set of solitudes sharing an address.

## Chapter 8: "The Trip"

**Type:** Narrative (transitional)
**POV:** Multiple -- Jeannie (primary), then Bob, then brief touches of Chase and Shanel
**Word count:** ~3,000
**Time:** Weeks before departure, then the departure itself. Early-to-mid 2023 (the trip happens in European summer, per the timeline).
**Primary locations:** The Marine Parade house (planning scenes); Perth Airport.

**Note on timeline:** The PLAN.md places the Europe trip in summer 2023, with the Absam discovery. The earlier chapters (1-7) are set in late 2025 -- this creates a chronological issue. Resolution: Chapters 1-7 are establishing the world as it is NOW (late 2025, two years into Bob's violin obsession, the fund deteriorating). Chapters 2, 5, and 7 contain flashbacks/memories. Chapter 8 is a FLASHBACK chapter -- it takes us back to the planning and departure of the 2023 Europe trip, which is the hinge between the surface life and the discovery. The narrative signals this shift explicitly.

---

### Scene 1 -- Jeannie Plans (pp. 1-4)

**Location:** The Marine Parade house. The kitchen, the living room, Jeannie's retreat. Over a period of weeks: late winter / early spring 2023.

**Characters:** Jeannie (primary), Bob (present but passive), Shanel (briefly -- she is sixteen, still at PLC).

**Beat by beat:**

- The opening signals the time shift: "Two years earlier..." or the equivalent. The prose should feel slightly different -- lighter, less pressured. In 2023, the fund is in trouble but not yet in crisis. Jeannie's drinking is steady but not accelerating. The children are younger -- Chase nineteen (just started at UWA), Shanel sixteen (Year 11 at PLC). The world is intact.

- Jeannie planning the trip. This is Jeannie in her element: organisation as identity, logistics as love language. She has been researching for weeks. She has a spreadsheet. She has bookmarked hotels on Instagram (she chooses hotels partly for their interiors and partly for their feed-worthiness). She has a content strategy, though she would not use that phrase -- she thinks of it as "capturing memories," but the truth is she is planning her posts before she has the experiences they will document.

- The itinerary: three weeks. London first (Harrods, the V&A, a restaurant someone from the gaggle recommended). Paris (the Marais, Saint-Germain, a patisserie she saw on a food blog). Then south -- "maybe the Amalfi Coast" -- or east: Vienna, Salzburg. The itinerary shifts as Jeannie researches. Vienna enters the picture because someone posted about the Hotel Sacher and the Opera and Schonbrunn, and the images were the kind of images Jeannie wants to replicate: herself in a grand European setting, cultured and at ease, the mining money transmuted into old-world sophistication.

- Bob's response: total compliance. Jeannie presents the itinerary over dinner and Bob says, "Sounds wonderful." He means it, or a version of it -- he would like to be away, to be somewhere other than the Terrace and the fund and the phone calls. But when Jeannie says "Austria," the word lands differently. He feels the same stirring he felt in Chapter 7 -- something in his chest, a contraction, a pull. He does not know why. (The reader knows: Austria is Absam, is the Inn Valley, is Jakob Stainer and the violin-making tradition he does not yet consciously know about but that some part of him, some deep-buried part, has been circling toward for thirty years.)

- The kids' reactions: Chase, at the dinner table, performatively indifferent. "Sounds cool." He will come for the first week (London, maybe Paris) and then fly home because he has work and mates and Rotto and no interest in museums. Shanel is more engaged -- she wants to see Paris, genuinely, and she asks about Vienna with the curiosity of a sixteen-year-old who has read something somewhere (Klimt? Music? Architecture?) and wants to see it for real. Jeannie approves: "We'll do the Belvedere, darling. You'll love it." The approval is conditional -- Shanel's curiosity is acceptable because it aligns with Jeannie's aesthetic program.

- Jeannie packs. Describe the ritual: the outfits laid out on the bed, photographed for reference, rolled into packing cubes. One outfit per major location, plus two "flexible" pieces. The luggage: matching Rimowa cases (a gift from Bob, chosen by Jeannie). The preparation is meticulous, excessive, and deeply anxious -- she is not packing clothes but packing a self, the version of herself she wants Europe to see.

**What the reader learns:** The trip as Jeannie's project. Her vision: curated, Instagram-ready, a performance of cultural engagement. The kids' different responses (Chase indifferent, Shanel curious). Bob's compliance concealing something he cannot name. The 2023 timeline -- the world is lighter here, not yet in crisis.

**What is planted:** Austria entering the itinerary through social media and the gaggle network (the same machinery that will eventually destroy the family is the machinery that leads them to the discovery). Shanel's curiosity about Vienna (she has an intellectual life that will matter later). Chase's early departure from the trip (he will miss the Absam discovery -- he was not there when his father found the violin, which adds to the tragedy of never knowing his father).

### Scene 2 -- Bob's Quiet Hope (pp. 4-6)

**Location:** Bob's home office. A night, a few days before departure.

**Characters:** Bob alone.

**Beat by beat:**

- Bob at his desk. Not working (or working -- the fund requires maintenance, even from abroad; he has arranged for the administrator to handle routine calls and has pre-signed several documents). He is online, doing something he has not done before: researching Austria. Not the tourist Austria -- not Vienna, not the Opera, not the Sacher Hotel. The other Austria. He has typed "violin making Austria" into Google and is reading about Absam.

- What he finds: a Wikipedia article, or a travel blog, or a cultural heritage site. Absam, a village of three thousand in the Inn Valley of Tyrol. Home of Jakob Stainer, the greatest luthier outside Cremona. A tradition of violin-making stretching back to the seventeenth century. Workshops that still operate. He reads about Stainer, about the instruments, about the spruce from the Alpine forests. He reads about the Mittenwald school just across the German border. He reads and reads and does not know why he is reading.

- Or rather: he knows. He is not ready to admit it, even to himself, but the reader should see it clearly. Bob is planning a detour. Not consciously -- he has not drawn a route or booked an appointment. But when the family is in Austria, when they are in the Inn Valley (it is close enough to Innsbruck, which is close enough to Salzburg, which is on Jeannie's itinerary), he will find a reason to visit Absam. He will walk into a violin shop. He does not know what will happen then. He knows only that he must go.

- The emotional register: this is not excitement. It is closer to dread -- the dread of a man who senses that something is about to change and cannot stop it. The same feeling as the investor's phone call, but inverted: not the dread of collapse but the dread of opening. What happens when the door he sealed shut in 1993 opens and he cannot close it again?

- He closes the laptop. He goes to bed. He lies in the dark (alone -- Jeannie in her retreat) and thinks about the Inn Valley and the spruce forests and the weight of a violin under his chin, and he sleeps, and he dreams of music, and in the morning he does not remember the dream but his left hand aches.

**What the reader learns:** Bob is not a passive participant in the trip. Beneath Jeannie's curated itinerary, he is running a secret one. Absam is already calling to him through the algorithm, through the research, through the buried memory. The trip is not just Jeannie's performance -- it is Bob's pilgrimage, though he does not yet know to call it that.

**What is planted:** The Absam research (Bob will "stumble" into Franz's shop, but the reader knows it was not a stumble -- it was a pilgrimage disguised as chance). The aching left hand in the morning -- the body insisting on what the mind denies. The Alpine spruce, the Inn Valley, Stainer -- the world Bob is about to enter.

### Scene 3 -- Departure (pp. 6-10)

**Location:** The Marine Parade house, then Perth Airport (Terminal 1, International). A morning in June or July 2023.

**Characters:** Bob, Jeannie, Chase (briefly -- he is coming for the first week), Shanel. Jim Harrington (by phone, briefly).

**Beat by beat:**

- The morning of departure. The house in controlled chaos -- Rimowa cases by the front door, Jeannie checking passports, Chase arriving late (he slept at a mate's), Shanel ready and waiting, sitting on her suitcase reading something on her phone. The mundane machinery of family travel: the taxi (or the car service), the argument about luggage weight, the second-check of doors and lights and the alarm system.

- Bob's last act before leaving: he goes to his home office and locks the door. Not to work -- to stand in the room for a moment. The office where, in two years, he will install soundproofing and spend hours learning the Chaconne. For now it is just an office: a desk, a computer, a filing cabinet. He stands in it and feels the strange, anticipatory hum of a man who is about to do something he cannot name. He locks up. He leaves.

- A phone call from Jim. Brief: "Have a wonderful trip, son. You deserve it." Bob thanks him. The word "deserve" sits in his chest like a stone.

- Perth Airport. The international terminal: departures, the Emirates or Singapore Airlines check-in, the crowds of Perth families heading to Bali, to Singapore, to Europe. The Harringtons in the queue: a handsome family, well-dressed (Jeannie has coordinated their travel outfits -- not matching, but harmonised). They look like what they are supposed to be: a successful Perth family going on a European holiday. The surface is perfect. It has always been perfect.

- Jeannie at the gate: already posting. A photo of her passport and boarding pass, artfully arranged with a coffee cup. Caption: something about "adventure" or "three weeks of culture." The first likes arrive before they board.

- Bob at the gate: looking out the window at the planes. The Indian Ocean visible beyond the runway, the same ocean he looks at every morning from the grass on Marine Parade. He is about to cross it. He is about to go to the other side of the world, to the place where violins are made, to the village where Stainer lived, and something is going to happen that will change everything, and he knows this in his body if not in his mind, and the knowledge sits in him like music -- like the opening chord of a piece he has not yet heard, vibrating, waiting to resolve.

- Final beat: the plane taking off. Perth falling away below -- the river, the suburbs, the coast, the ocean. Jeannie adjusting her neck pillow. Chase already asleep. Shanel reading. Bob looking out the window as Australia disappears, and feeling, for the first time in years, something that is not dread and not resignation and not the flat grey hum of a managed life. Something that might, if he were braver, if he had the language for it, be called hope.

**What the reader learns:** The departure. The family in transit. The contrast between Jeannie's curated trip and Bob's secret pilgrimage. The hope -- fragile, unnamed, perhaps the most dangerous emotion in the novel, because hope is what will drive Bob to the violin and the violin to the Chaconne and the Chaconne to the performance and the performance to the smashing and the smashing to the beach.
