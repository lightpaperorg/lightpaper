## Chapter 1: "The View from Marine Parade"

**POV:** Bob | **Target:** ~700 words

---

The grass was cold under his feet. It was the cold of late autumn in a place that did not really have seasons, a mild affront, barely noticed, the dew soaking through the soles of his feet and into the soil beneath, as if the earth were drawing something out of him. Bob stood on the first terrace of the stepped lawn that descended from Marine Parade toward the beach, a coffee in his right hand, wearing yesterday's chinos and a linen shirt he had slept in, and he looked at the ocean.

It was early -- seven, or just past it -- and the light had that quality it only had at this hour: pale gold, provisional, as though the day had not yet committed to itself. The Norfolk Island pines threw long shadows westward across the grass, their strange columnar shapes dark against the brightening sky, like columns built to hold up nothing. Below and to the south, the Indiana sat closed and white on the beachfront, its colonial facade catching the first horizontal light. The esplanade was empty. A magpie picked at the edge of the path. The ocean beyond the sand was flat, glassy, a pewter colour that would burn off into blue within the hour, and it stretched -- as it always stretched, as it had stretched for every one of the fifteen years he had stood on this grass with a coffee in his hand -- unbroken to Africa, or to nothing, or to whatever existed on the far side of eleven thousand kilometres of Indian Ocean. The sky above it was enormous. That was always the thing you forgot about Perth until you stood outside before the day started and looked up: the sky was enormous, and the silence was almost total, and for a few minutes you could believe you were the last man alive, or the first, and that nothing had gone wrong yet.

This was his ritual on the mornings when he could not sleep, which was most mornings now. He would come out the back gate and cross Marine Parade -- the street empty, the houses on either side still shuttered, the Range Rover and the Mercedes parked nose-to-tail in the drive -- and he would stand on the grass and drink his coffee and do the thing he had done out here for years, which was not-thinking. Letting the view fill the space where thought would be. The view had cost him four and a half million dollars. That was what he had paid for the house in 2010, at the peak of the mining boom, stretching further than he should have because Jeannie wanted Marine Parade and Marine Parade was what Jeannie got. He did not think about this. He did not think about the fund, or about the phone call he needed to return, or about the quarterly reports that were due on Friday and contained numbers that did not correspond to anything real. He looked at the ocean and he was no one for a while, and the no one felt like the truest thing about him.

He was forty-nine. Sandy hair going grey at the temples. Six foot, with an extra ten kilos that had settled around his waist and softened the line of his jaw, so that his face, which had once been handsome in the way that suggested competence and trustworthiness, had gone bland -- a face for a bank advertisement, a face for the back page of a financial newsletter, a face that made people feel their money was safe. He was wearing RM Williams boots when he dressed, but he had come out barefoot, and the grass was cold, and his hands were large and well-kept, the hands of a man who had never built anything with them.

His left hand -- the one not holding the coffee -- curled slightly at his side. The fingers closed, not into a fist but around something that was not there, the thumb bracing against an invisible neck, the fingertips pressing against nothing. It was a gesture so small and so unconscious that Bob himself did not notice it, and it lasted only a moment before his hand relaxed and hung at his side again. But it was there. The hand remembered what the mind had locked away.

He turned and looked back at the house. Five bedrooms. Limestone. Ocean views from the upper floor. A garden Jeannie had landscaped three times. It sat above him on the rise of Marine Parade, lit by the early sun along its eastern wall, and from here it looked like what it was supposed to look like: the house of a man who had done well. Jeannie's bedroom curtain -- her retreat, as she called it, the second bedroom she had colonised three years ago -- was still drawn. The house was quiet. The house was always quiet in a way that had nothing to do with sound.

## Chapter 2: "Nedlands, 1987"

**POV:** Bob | **Target:** ~600 words

---

He found it on a Tuesday night, or a Wednesday -- a nothing evening in late November, the kind of Perth evening where the heat has broken but the air still holds the memory of it, and the house is quiet because Jeannie is upstairs and the children are elsewhere and the television is on but muted, the way Bob always has the television, the images washing over the room like light through water. He was on his phone. He was always on his phone in the evenings now, scrolling with the glazed purposelessness of a man who does not know what he is looking for but cannot stop looking, and the YouTube algorithm -- which had learned things about him he had not learned about himself -- had begun sliding violin performances between the market commentary and the football highlights. Thumbnails of women on stages, instruments tucked beneath their chins, bows poised. He scrolled past them. He scrolled past them every night. But tonight a thumbnail stopped him: a girl, young, twelve or thirteen, playing on a stage that looked like a school hall, and the angle of her left hand on the neck of the instrument -- the wrist flat, the fingers curving down over the strings with that particular concentrated stillness -- sent something through him that was not thought and not memory but something older than both: a sensation in his own hand, a weight beneath his own chin, the pressure of a string against the pad of his index finger, and he was eleven years old and it was 1987 and the bus was grinding up the hill to Scotch College with the sun coming through the windows and his school bag wedged between his knees and inside the bag, in a case that smelled of rosin and old cloth, was a violin.

He did not tap the video. He put the phone face-down on the couch and sat in the flickering light and let the memory arrive, which it did not as narrative but as a series of physical impressions: the weight of the case on his shoulder. The bus jolting over the speed bumps on Stirling Highway. The music room at Scotch -- its particular smell, floor polish and brass cleaner and the dusty warmth of instruments stored in cupboards that were never quite closed. And the teacher, the first teacher, a young woman whose name he could not retrieve -- not Mrs Albrecht, that was later, this was earlier, Year Seven, the beginning -- placing the violin under his chin and adjusting the chin rest with her thumb, and showing him how to draw the bow across the open D string.

The sound had been horrible. A scraping, nasal whine that made the other boys laugh and made his face burn and made the teacher say something encouraging that he did not hear, because beneath the horrible sound, transmitted through the chin rest into his jawbone and from his jawbone into his skull and from his skull into some chamber of his brain that had never been opened, was a vibration. Not a musical vibration -- not yet, not for months -- but a physical one, as if the string and the wood and the bone were all the same material and the bow had set all of it resonating at once. He did not know what to do with this sensation. He was eleven. He stored it in the place where children store things they cannot explain, below language, below thought, in the body itself, where it would remain for thirty-eight years like a seed in dry ground, waiting for water.

The phone buzzed. A notification from the fund's administrator -- something routine, something about quarterly filings. Bob picked it up, read the message, typed a brief response, and the room reassembled itself around him: the muted television, the empty couch, the Marine Parade house with its ocean views and its silence. He put the phone down. He sat in the dark for a long time.

## Chapter 3: "The Gaggle"

**POV:** Jeannie | **Target:** ~700 words

---

She arrived second, which was correct. Not first -- first was eager, first was the new girl, first was the woman who had nowhere else to be -- and not last, because last was Margot's privilege and Margot was never late so much as delayed, which was a different thing, a thing that communicated that one's time was valuable and one's entrance was worth waiting for. Jeannie parked the Range Rover on Bay View Terrace -- the full-size, not the Evoque; she had lobbied for the upgrade and Bob had signed the lease without discussion, which was how most things in her life arrived -- and checked her face in the rear-view mirror. This was not vanity. Or it was vanity, but it was also something else: a diagnostic, a systems check, the way a pilot scans instruments before takeoff. Hair: pulled back, the colour still good, the grey managed by Dario at his salon in Subiaco every five weeks. Face: the Botox was holding; the filler along the jawline was subtle enough that no one had mentioned it, which meant it was either invisible or so common among her friends that it no longer registered as a procedure. Eyes: green, clear, no puffiness, the concealer doing its work. She was forty-five years old and she looked like herself at thirty-eight, which was not the same as looking young but was the goal -- not the reversal of time but its arrest, the preservation of a particular vintage, like a wine held at the right temperature in the right cellar.

The restaurant was one of the places on Bay View Terrace that had survived by understanding precisely what Claremont women wanted, which was good food served in a room that looked expensive without being ostentatious, with French doors open to the pavement so that arriving and departing could be witnessed by passersby. The table was in the window. Jeannie took the seat facing the door -- a habit so ingrained she no longer noticed it, though it served the same purpose as the rear-view mirror: a position from which to see everything and be seen seeing it.

Karen was already there, which made Karen first, which was fine because Karen was always first and everyone had stopped reading anything into it years ago. Karen had the look of a woman who had been pretty once in a way that suggested health -- good skin, wide smile, the kind of blonde that came from the sun and not from Dario -- and was now handsome in a way that suggested maintenance. Her husband sold industrial pumps to mining companies and drank too much at dinner parties, a fact the group acknowledged through a shared vocabulary of euphemism: Colin was "tired," Colin had "a big week," Colin was "letting off steam." Karen bore this with a composure that Jeannie simultaneously admired and found repulsive, because the composure was real -- Karen genuinely did not seem to mind -- and Jeannie could not understand a woman who did not mind. Not minding was, to Jeannie, a failure of standards.

They ordered. Jeannie asked for a sauvignon blanc -- it was twelve-fifteen, this was the first glass, the legitimate glass, the glass that did not count because everyone was having one and the sun was out and it was a Wednesday and lunch without wine was not lunch but a meeting. The wine arrived and she took the first sip and felt the familiar softening, the slight recession of the sharp thing that lived behind her sternum, the thing she had no name for and medicated with fifty milligrams of sertraline every morning and an undisclosed number of glasses of Doyen Bay every afternoon. The first sip was always the best. The first sip was the moment the day became manageable.

Amanda arrived next, then Pip, then the new woman -- what was her name, Renee, or Rachael, something with an R -- and the table filled and the noise rose and the performance began in earnest. The conversation moved with the practised rhythm of women who had been having this lunch, at this table or one like it, every second Wednesday for six years: children first (achievements, anxieties, the inexhaustible subject of school politics), then holidays (who had been where, who was going, the careful calibration of destinations to signal wealth without vulgarity), then real estate, then bodies, and then, always, inevitably, the main event: other people.

## Chapter 4: "The Fund"

**POV:** Bob | **Target:** ~700 words

---

The key card beeped and the light went green and Bob pushed through the glass door into the lobby of the building on St George's Terrace with the same movement he had made every weekday morning for eleven years, a movement so habitual it had ceased to be an action and become a reflex, like breathing, like lying. The lobby was marble and steel, the kind of corporate entrance that exists to communicate seriousness -- the security desk, the turnstiles, the lift bank with its brushed aluminium doors reflecting the shapes of people who did not look at each other. He pressed the button for level twelve. The lift rose. A woman from one of the law firms on fourteen stood beside him holding a takeaway coffee and looking at her phone, and they rode in the particular silence of people who share a building and nothing else, and the doors opened and she continued upward and Bob stepped out into the corridor that led to the frosted glass door with HARRINGTON CAPITAL in matte grey lettering, and he felt what he always felt at this moment, which was nothing. A nothing so practised, so thorough, so carefully maintained that it had become its own kind of expertise.

The office was small. That was the first thing a visitor would notice, if visitors came, which they rarely did anymore -- Bob preferred to meet clients at restaurants, at the yacht club, in environments he controlled and that communicated the right things. The office itself communicated very little. A reception area with a desk that was unmanned three days out of five. A meeting room with a view of the Terrace below. Bob's office beyond that: a window looking east toward the park at the top of the city, two screens on the desk, a bookshelf containing financial texts he had read once and regulatory documents he consulted when he needed the precise language of compliance. The carpet was grey. The walls were pale. There was a print of something -- a nautical scene, boats in a harbour -- that Jeannie had chosen when the office was fitted out in 2012. It was the most personal object in the room.

He sat down. He turned on the screens. The left screen showed the ASX -- tickers scrolling, the small red and green movements of a market that had nothing to do with him and everything to do with the performance he maintained. The right screen showed his email. There were forty-two unread messages. He scanned the subject lines the way a man scans a crowd for the face he is afraid to see: quickly, peripherally, looking not at the messages themselves but for the particular words that signalled danger. *Redemption. Statement. Query. Meeting request.* He found three redemption requests. One was small -- fifty thousand, manageable, he could meet it from the operating account. One was two hundred thousand, which would require liquidating a real position, which would widen the gap between what the fund owned and what the fund owed. The third was from Richard, requesting five hundred thousand, politely, with a note about a property purchase, and Bob read it twice and then minimised the window and looked at the wall.

The wall held no answers. It never had. But Bob looked at it the way he looked at the ocean from the grass on Marine Parade -- as a surface onto which he could project nothing, a blankness that asked nothing of him, a space in which he could practise the only skill he had truly mastered, which was the postponement of the unbearable. The fund's assets under management had been, at their peak in 2011, approximately eighty-five million dollars. In the quarterly reports that went to investors -- the beautifully formatted documents that Sandra printed and posted in cream envelopes with the Harrington Capital logo -- the figure was seventy-four million, reflecting a managed and dignified decline that suggested prudent rebalancing in uncertain markets. In reality -- in the set of numbers that existed only in Bob's head, because to put them on paper would be to make them evidence -- the fund held approximately thirty million in actual assets backing seventy-four million in reported obligations. The gap was forty-four million dollars. The gap was the lie. The gap was the distance between the man Bob pretended to be and the man he was, expressed in a currency everyone understood.

He opened the email from Richard and began composing a reply. The words came easily. They always came easily. That was the terrible thing.

## Chapter 5: "Rottnest"

**POV:** Chase | **Target:** ~600 words

---

The swell hit them side-on coming out past the groyne and the Riviera pitched hard to port and Macca's beer went flying off the dash and Chase laughed and opened the throttle and felt the twin Cats dig in, thirteen hundred horsepower of diesel churning the water white behind them, and the bow rose and steadied and the boat found her line and they were out, properly out, the river and the traffic bridge and Fremantle falling away behind them and nothing ahead but eighteen kilometres of open Indian Ocean and Rottnest sitting low and scrubby on the horizon like something that couldn't be bothered to be impressive.

This was the thing. This was the actual thing, and Chase did not have words for it and did not need them. The throttle under his palm, vibrating. The spray coming over the bow in sheets when they punched through a swell. The sound of the diesels, not a roar but a sustained deep growl that came up through the deck and through his legs and settled in his chest like a second heartbeat. He was twenty-one years old and he was driving a forty-eight-foot motor yacht across open ocean on a Friday afternoon in late autumn and his two best mates were below deck loading slabs of Emu Export into the fridge and arguing about the Dockers and the sky ahead was enormous and blue and the water was that dark, almost navy blue it went when the continental shelf dropped away, and he was happy in the uncomplicated way that he was only ever happy on this boat, which was the one place in his life where his body and his mind were doing the same thing.

The Riviera 48 was called *Chardonnay*. His mother's choice. Chase had wanted *Full Send* or *Rip Tide* or something his mates would find funny, but the boat was not his -- it was his father's, technically, paid for in 2015 when Chase was eleven, though by now Chase had driven it more hours than Bob had and knew the crossing by feel: the swell pattern south of the groyne, the wind shift at the halfway point where the Fremantle Doctor met the Southern Ocean rollers and the chop turned ugly for ten minutes, the moment when Rottnest resolved from a smudge into an actual island with that insane turquoise water ringing it like something out of a travel ad. Forty-eight feet of fibreglass and teak, twin Caterpillar C-12s, three cabins, a flybridge, a tender on the swim platform. She was not new and she was not flash -- the blokes with real money had sixty-footers, Sunseekers and Princesses with crew -- but she was solid and she was fast enough and she was his, in every way that mattered.

His dad was up on the flybridge. Chase had registered this without really looking: Bob sitting in the skipper's chair with a coffee, staring at the ocean with that blank look he got sometimes, like he was tuned to a radio station no one else could hear. Dad did this. He went somewhere inside his own head and you could talk to him and he'd nod and say "yeah mate" but he wasn't there, not really, and Chase had learned a long time ago not to examine his parents too closely because examination led to questions and questions led to the kind of territory that his family did not visit. His mum had a word for it -- she said Bob was "in one of his moods" -- and Chase had adopted the phrase without investigating what it meant, the way you adopt a local expression when you move to a new country: you use it, it works, you don't ask about the etymology.

## Chapter 6: "The Terrace"

**POV:** Bob | **Target:** ~600 words

---

The phone rang at nine-fourteen on a Monday morning in November, and Bob knew. He did not know in the way that a person knows a fact -- with certainty, with evidence, with the architecture of reason supporting the conclusion -- but in the way a body knows: the phone vibrating against the desk surface, the sound of it thin and industrial in the quiet office, and something in his chest cavity dropping, a physical descent, as though a trapdoor had opened between his ribs and his organs had shifted downward by a centimetre. He looked at the screen. The number was not in his contacts, which meant it was either a cold call or it was the other thing, the thing he had been waiting for without admitting he was waiting for it, because admitting you are waiting for something means admitting it exists, and Bob's entire life was built on the premise that certain things could be kept from existing by the simple act of not looking at them.

He let it ring. Once, twice, three times. The office was empty -- Sandra came in on Tuesdays and Thursdays, and on the other days the reception desk sat unmanned and the meeting room sat unused and Harrington Capital consisted of one man in a room on the twelfth floor of a building on St George's Terrace, looking at a phone he did not want to answer. The morning light came through the east-facing window and fell across the desk in a long rectangle, illuminating the phone, the two screens (both dark -- he had not yet turned them on), a pen, a glass of water, the ordinary objects of a working life arranged with the neatness of a man who did not have enough real work to create disorder.

He picked up. He did this not because he wanted to but because not picking up was worse. Not picking up meant the call would come again, and then again, and then it would come as a letter, and then it would come as something else entirely, something with a letterhead and a reference number, and by then the architecture of not-looking would have collapsed under its own weight. Better to answer. Better to perform.

"Bob Harrington."

The voice on the other end was male, measured, and precise in the way that professional voices are precise when they are building toward something. Not angry. Worse than angry: informed. The man identified himself -- an investor, not Richard, someone else, someone whose name Bob recognised from a redemption request he had deferred three months ago with the twelve-month-horizon speech. The speech had worked at the time. Speeches like that always worked, because they were constructed from the same materials that Bob used in every client interaction -- warmth, authority, the reassuring cadence of a man who lives and breathes this -- and because people did not want to believe that the person managing their money was lying to them any more than Bob wanted to believe he was the person lying.

But this man had done something. Bob could hear it in the voice before the words confirmed it: the particular tone of a person who has been thinking, who has compared notes, who has spoken to his accountant or his lawyer or, worst of all, to another investor. The questions were specific. Technical. They arrived in a sequence that suggested preparation -- not hostility, not yet, but the careful architecture of a man who wants to give you the opportunity to explain before he escalates. "What's the fund's current NAV, Bob?" "Can I get the most recent audited statements?" "Who do you use for the external audit?"

Bob answered. The answers were fluent, practised, technically accurate in ways that obscured the truth. He had delivered them before, to other callers, in other years. The machine ran.

## Chapter 7: "What Jeannie Doesn't Know"

**POV:** Jeannie | **Target:** ~600 words

---

The room was hers. That was the point of it, and Jeannie was conscious of this every time she closed the door -- not locked it, never locked, locking would be a declaration and declarations were not how Jeannie operated; she closed it, which was a suggestion, a boundary stated in the passive voice. She had taken the second bedroom three years ago, moving her things across the hall in stages -- first the reading lamp, then the cashmere throw, then the pillows (her pillows, the European goose-down ones from The White Company that Bob's neck could not tolerate and that she refused to replace with anything from a department store), then the phone charger, and finally, one Tuesday evening without announcement or discussion, herself. Bob had said nothing. This was his principal talent: saying nothing when something was happening, absorbing the event like water absorbing a stone, registering no disturbance on the surface. She had expected a question. She had prepared an answer -- something about sleeping better alone, something about his snoring, something plausible and face-saving that they could both pretend was the reason. He did not ask. She did not explain. The marriage rearranged itself around the new configuration the way a family rearranges itself around a death: silently, practically, with everyone pretending the empty chair has always been empty.

The room, then. It was ten o'clock on a Thursday night. Jeannie was in bed -- her bed, queen-size, white linen, the Diptyque candle on the nightstand still burning because she liked the smell and because the small orange flame gave the room a quality she thought of as European, though she had never articulated this even to herself. On the nightstand beside the candle: her phone, a glass of sauvignon blanc that was not her first, the prescription bottle of sertraline from the GP in Subiaco. She had taken the pill an hour ago. The pill and the wine had met in her bloodstream and were performing their nightly collaboration, a chemical détente that lowered the volume on everything -- the anxiety, the restlessness, the low hum of something she might have called despair if she were the kind of woman who used that word, which she was not. She was the kind of woman who called it being tired.

She was on Instagram. The screen glowed against her face in the dim room, and she scrolled with the practised, frictionless movement of a thumb that had performed this gesture ten thousand times, past the photographs of other women's lives -- the interiors, the holidays, the children in school uniforms, the flat lays of coffee cups and novels and cashmere socks arranged on white marble with the artful carelessness of a production that had taken twenty minutes. She was looking for something. She did not know what she was looking for. She was always looking for something on this screen and she never found it and she never stopped looking, and this was the rhythm of her evenings: the wine, the pill, the screen, the scroll, the thin blue light on her face in the dark, and the feeling -- beneath the medication, beneath the alcohol, beneath the performance of a woman at rest in a beautiful room -- of a tide going out and leaving behind it not sand but something harder and more desolate, the exposed and ugly architecture of a life that looked, from the outside, like everything anyone could want.

Her follower count was 4,187. It had been 4,200 on Monday. She had noticed the drop the way you notice a hairline crack in a wall -- not alarming in itself, but suggestive, evidence of some structural shift you cannot see and do not want to investigate. Thirteen people had decided, over the course of a week, that Jeannie Harrington's curated life was no longer worth watching. She did not know who they were. She wanted to know who they were.

## Chapter 8: "The Trip"

**POV:** Bob | **Target:** ~500 words

---

Perth Airport's international terminal had a particular quality of light that Bob had never been able to name -- not quite fluorescent and not quite natural, a compromised brightness that flattened everything, so that the families in the departure lounge all looked like versions of the same family: sunburned, slightly overdressed, clutching passports and boarding passes with the anxious reverence of people who do not travel often enough for the ritual to become routine. The Harringtons were not that family. Jeannie had organised them into a tableau of practised ease: matching Rimowa luggage arranged in a formation that was not accidental, she and Shanel in linen that would crease before Singapore but looked immaculate now, Chase in the one good shirt Jeannie had laid out on his bed that morning with a note that said *Wear this.* They looked like a family in a magazine. They looked like what they were supposed to be.

Bob sat slightly apart. Not dramatically apart -- not across the lounge, not making a statement -- but one seat removed from the cluster of his family, holding his boarding pass in his left hand with a looseness that suggested calm and concealed something else. He was watching the planes through the floor-to-ceiling windows. The Indian Ocean was visible beyond the runways, the same ocean he looked at every morning from the grass on Marine Parade, the same pewter expanse that had been the backdrop of his entire life, and in a few hours he would be above it, crossing it, leaving it behind. Jeannie was photographing the luggage arrangement. Chase was on his phone, body angled away from his mother in the universal posture of a twenty-year-old who has not yet forgiven his parents for existing in public. Shanel was reading a novel -- Bob could not see the cover from this angle, but she was absorbed in it, her body curled into the plastic airport chair with a self-containment that reminded him, with a tenderness so sudden it startled him, of his own mother reading in the garden on Pearse Street.

He felt something. It was not a large feeling and it did not arrive with drama. It arrived the way a change in air pressure arrives: subtly, without announcement, altering the quality of everything without changing its appearance. He felt light. Or lighter. The fund was nine thousand miles behind him, or would be by tonight. Sandra had the office. The quarterly was filed. The machine would run without him for three weeks, the way machines run when no one is watching -- which is to say, it would run exactly the same, because the machine was automatic now, self-sustaining, requiring only his face and his voice to maintain the fiction, and his face and his voice would be in Europe, where no one knew him, where no one needed anything from him, where he could be -- and here the feeling sharpened, became almost painful in its specificity -- no one.

In his search history, undeleted, was the word *Absam*.

He did not know what he would find there. He knew only that he would go.
