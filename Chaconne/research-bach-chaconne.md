# Research: Bach's Chaconne — The Piece, Its History, Famous Performances

## Key Facts for the Novel

- **Full name**: Chaconne from Partita No. 2 in D minor, BWV 1004
- **Composed**: Fair copy dated 1720, likely conceived 1717 or earlier
- **Duration**: 14–17 minutes (256 bars)
- **Structure**: 64 four-bar variations in three sections: D minor (bars 1–132), D major (133–208), D minor (209–256)
- **The opening**: Four-note chord. You can't sustain all four on a violin — must be "broken"
- **The D major shift** at bar 133: One of the most stunning moments in all music

## The Chaconne as a Musical Form

Origins in the New World — a "sexily swirling dance" from New Spain (modern Mexico), arriving in Spain in the late 1500s as a quick-tempo, suggestive dance performed with castanets. Cervantes and Quevedo document it. Called *chacona* in Spanish, *ciaccona* in Italian. Considered vulgar and disreputable.

Lully domesticated it at Versailles into stately dances. By the early 18th century: slow triple-meter, purely instrumental, built on a repeated short harmonic progression (ground bass).

**How it works**: Rigid constraint below (repeating bass), infinite freedom above (variations). The composer is locked into the same harmonic cycle but can explore an entire universe within it.

**Chaconne vs Passacaglia**: Baroque composers didn't consistently distinguish them. Modern attempts at distinction are "arbitrary and historically unfounded."

**Other famous chaconnes**: Purcell's "Dido's Lament," Vitali's Chaconne in G minor (authenticity disputed), Handel's Chaconne in G major, Biber's Passacaglia from the Rosary Sonatas.

## Bach's Partita No. 2

Five movements: Allemande (~4 min), Courante (~2.5 min), Sarabande (~3.5 min), Gigue (~4 min), **Chaconne (~15 min)**. The Chaconne's 256 bars exceed the four preceding movements combined. Unprecedented in scale.

### Musical Architecture

- **Part I — D minor (bars 1–132)**: Theme statement, builds in complexity, arpeggiated passages climax, highest note (G5) at variation 22
- **Part II — D major (bars 133–208)**: "Like stepping from a dark cathedral into sunlight." Pastoral, angelic, hymn-like. Gradually rebuilds complexity.
- **Part III — D minor (bars 209–256)**: Return to darkness. The bariolage passage (variations 58–62) — "the most beautiful thing in the history of Western music." Final theme statement completes the circle.

**Golden ratio**: The 33-19-12 variation distribution approximates phi. Bar 158 (0.618 × 257) features trumpet-call motifs.

### Technical Difficulty — "The Everest of the Violin Repertoire"

- Polyphonic writing on a monophonic instrument — four voices on an instrument designed for one
- Sustained chordal playing across 15+ minutes
- Melodic continuity through cramp-inducing chords
- No rests, no accompanist, total exposure
- Interpretive depth: "learning the notes is possible; learning to interpret it is a lifetime's work"

## The Maria Barbara Bach Story

**Maria Barbara Bach** (1684–1720): Bach's first wife, his second cousin. Married 1707. Seven children (three died in infancy), including Wilhelm Friedemann and Carl Philipp Emanuel.

**Her death**: Summer 1720. Bach was away with Prince Leopold at Carlsbad spa for ~2 months. When he returned, she had died and already been buried (7 July 1720). Cause of death undocumented.

**The memorial theory**: Romantic narrative — Bach poured his grief into the Chaconne. Evidence is circumstantial: the 1720 date, D minor's association with grief, the piece's emotional arc. But: the piece was likely conceived before her death (possibly 1717); Bach's generation didn't compose autobiographically; no documentary evidence links them. Verdict: **possible but unproven.**

**Helga Thoene's hidden chorale theory** (1994): Hidden mourning chorales encoded in the notation. The Morimur recording (ECM, 2001) realizes this artistically. Mainstream musicologists reject the theory as methodologically unsound, but the recording is beautiful.

## Famous Quotes

**Johannes Brahms** (letter to Clara Schumann, 1877):
> "On one stave, for a small instrument, the man writes a whole world of the deepest thoughts and most powerful feelings. If I imagined that I could have created, even conceived the piece, I am quite certain that the excess of excitement and earth-shattering experience would have driven me out of my mind."

**Yehudi Menuhin**: "The greatest structure for solo violin that exists."

**Joshua Bell**: "Not just one of the greatest pieces of music ever written, but one of the greatest achievements of any man in history. It's a spiritually powerful piece, emotionally powerful, structurally perfect."

**Helene Grimaud**: "A dance of life and death — a work of cathedral-like architecture, which, through its variations, appears like coloured light through stained glass windows."

## Famous Performances

- **Joshua Bell, Washington Metro (2007)**: Played the Chaconne incognito during rush hour on a $3.5M Stradivarius. 1,097 people passed; 7 stopped. Earned $32.17. Pulitzer Prize-winning story ("Pearls Before Breakfast").
- **Isaac Stern, San Antonio (22 Nov 1963)**: Was in Dallas when Kennedy was shot. Next night, told 4,000 people he would pray by playing the "soul-cleansing" music of Bach. Wept uncontrollably during the Chaconne. Asked the audience not to applaud.
- **Midori, Köthen Castle (2016)**: Performed the complete Sonatas and Partitas in the rooms where Bach composed them.

## Major Recordings

Heifetz, Milstein (1972–74, considered definitive), Menuhin, Szeryng, Grumiaux, Perlman, Kremer, Hilary Hahn (debut album at 17), Isabelle Faust, Julia Fischer, Viktoria Mullova (baroque setup).

## Transcriptions

Brahms (left hand piano), Busoni (full piano — essentially a separate work), Segovia (guitar), Stokowski (orchestra), Mendelssohn and Schumann (piano accompaniment).

## Place in the Canon

Consensus among violinists is near-unanimous: the greatest piece of solo violin music ever written. No serious challenger in 300 years. Standard requirement in competitions and auditions. Performed from memory. Not well known outside classical circles — "most people on the street won't recognize it."
