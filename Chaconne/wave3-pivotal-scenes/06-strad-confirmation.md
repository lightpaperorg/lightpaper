# Scene 6: The Authentication

## Chapter 23

---

The email arrived at 5:47 a.m. on the morning of his fiftieth birthday.

Bob was already awake. He had been awake since four, lying in the guest room with his eyes open and the ceiling above him and the silence of the house pressing down like a hand. Not the silence of peace -- the silence of a machine that has been switched off, all its parts still present but none of them moving, the refrigerator's hum and the tick of the hot water system and the oceanic whisper of Jeannie's white noise machine through the wall the only evidence that this was a house where people lived and not a museum of a life that had already ended. He had lain there and thought about nothing, which was what he had been best at for most of his life -- the not-thinking, the strategic vacancy, the ability to fill his mind with the blankness of a television tuned between stations. But the not-thinking was harder now. It had been harder since the violin, since the Chaconne, since the soundproofed room on St George's Terrace where he had spent two years becoming someone Jeannie would not recognise and the world would not forgive. The not-thinking kept being interrupted by music. By bars of the Chaconne playing themselves behind his eyes, the way a song gets stuck in your head, except this was not a song but a universe, and it did not get stuck so much as move in and refuse to leave.

His phone buzzed on the bedside table. The buzz was soft -- he had set it to the lowest vibration, the one that would not carry through the wall to Jeannie's room, because the habits of concealment were so deep in him now that they operated without conscious direction, the way a hunted animal sleeps with one eye open. He reached for it. The screen lit his face in the dark.

One new email. From David Saunders, Saunders & Associates, Melbourne. Subject line: *Final Report -- Harrington Instrument (Ref: SA-2025-0847).*

Bob sat up. His heart was doing something it had not done since the first time he drew the bow across the strings in Franz's workshop in Absam -- a thick, heavy knocking, as if the organ had swollen to fill his entire chest. He swung his legs off the bed. His feet found the floor -- the jarrah floorboards cool and smooth, the floor of a house that had cost four and a half million dollars and that he could not afford and that he had bought because Jeannie wanted Marine Parade and Marine Parade was what Jeannie got. He sat on the edge of the bed in his undershorts and a t-shirt and opened the email.

The body was brief. Three sentences of covering note in the measured tone of a professional who understands that what he is about to deliver will alter the trajectory of the recipient's life and who has therefore chosen his words with the care of a surgeon choosing where to cut. David Saunders thanked Bob for his patience during the extended examination period. He noted that the attached report represented the consensus findings of his firm in consultation with two independent experts whose credentials were listed in the appendix. He recommended that the full report be read in conjunction with the executive summary on page three.

The attachment was a PDF. Forty-three pages. Bob opened it. His hands were shaking. Not the tremor of tendonitis, which he knew intimately -- the deep ache in the forearm extensors, the burning in the base of the left thumb that came after three hours of practice and that he managed now with ice and ibuprofen and the careful stretching routine Katarina had taught him. This was different. This was the shaking of a man holding a door that might open onto salvation or onto nothing, and who cannot bring himself to push.

He scrolled to page three. The executive summary.

The language was the language of expertise deployed with extreme caution. Saunders & Associates had examined the instrument submitted by Mr. Robert Harrington of Perth, Western Australia, previously assessed by Malcolm Firth of Fremantle as a possible early-eighteenth-century Cremonese instrument. The examination had encompassed visual inspection by three certified appraisers, dendrochronological analysis of the spruce top plate conducted by the Berger Laboratory in Hamburg, CT scanning at the University of Melbourne's imaging facility, and varnish composition analysis via infrared spectroscopy and scanning electron microscopy.

Bob read each line the way he had learned to read the Chaconne -- bar by bar, note by note, not rushing ahead, not allowing his eye to skip to the conclusion the way his bow sometimes tried to skip to the resolution. He read the dendrochronology results: the youngest tree ring in the top plate dated to approximately 1695, consistent with wood harvested in the Alpine region during the final decades of the Little Ice Age, a period of cold temperatures and slow growth that produced the dense, uniform-grained spruce used by the Cremonese masters. The ring pattern matched, with a 97.6% confidence interval, a master chronology established from authenticated instruments produced in the Stradivari workshop between 1700 and 1725. He read the varnish analysis: a two-layer system comprising a mineral ground coat and an organic coloured upper layer, the composition consistent with varnish samples taken from authenticated Stradivari instruments, including trace elements of potassium silicate, iron oxide, and zinc that matched the chemical fingerprint of the so-called *vernice bianca* described in historical Cremonese workshop inventories. He read the construction analysis: plate graduation patterns, corner block configuration, internal linings, and bass bar placement consistent with the methodologies documented in the Stradivari templates held by the Museo del Violino in Cremona.

And then he read the conclusion.

*Based on dendrochronological analysis, varnish composition, wood treatment residues, construction methodology, and stylistic comparison with authenticated exemplars, it is our considered opinion that the instrument submitted for examination is consistent with the workshop of Antonio Stradivari, Cremona, circa 1710-1720, and most probably dates to the period 1714-1716, corresponding to Stradivari's "Golden Period" of production.*

He read it again.

*Consistent with the workshop of Antonio Stradivari.*

He read it a third time. His phone trembled in his hands, the screen's blue light catching the moisture on his fingers, and the words did not change, did not rearrange themselves into the polite disappointment he had prepared himself for -- *a fine instrument of uncertain provenance, probably Tyrolean, possibly nineteenth century, worth perhaps thirty to fifty thousand dollars* -- the words he had rehearsed receiving so many times that the rehearsal had become its own kind of grief, the preemptive mourning of a hope he had not admitted to himself he was carrying. The words did not change. They said what they said.

He scrolled down. Page four. The valuation.

*Based on recent comparable sales, including the Baron Knoop Stradivarius (1715) sold in 2025 for USD $23 million, the Da Vinci ex-Seidel Stradivarius (1714) sold in 2022 for USD $15.34 million, and the Lady Blunt Stradivarius (1721) sold in 2011 for USD $15.9 million, and taking into account the instrument's condition, which is good to very good with evidence of professional restoration and no significant structural damage, we estimate the current market value of the Harrington instrument at USD $15,000,000 to $20,000,000.*

Bob put the phone down on the bed. He put his hands on his knees. He bent forward, the way you bend forward when you have been punched in the stomach, and he breathed, and the breath was ragged and shallow and insufficient, and he pressed his hands against his kneecaps and closed his eyes and the number was there behind his eyelids, white on black, floating: fifteen to twenty million dollars.

He laughed.

The sound came out of him without permission -- a single, involuntary bark of laughter that was not amusement but the body's response to an overload it could not process through any other channel. He pressed his hand over his mouth. The house was silent. No one had heard. He sat on the edge of the bed and laughed into his palm and the laughter was wet and strangled and it went on for longer than he expected, the convulsions rising from his diaphragm in waves, and when it stopped he was crying, and he did not know when the laughing had become crying, because the two were the same thing, the same overwhelmed animal sound, the same body trying to discharge a current it was not built to carry.

Fifteen to twenty million dollars.

The fund's deficit was five million. Perhaps six, if the latest redemption requests were included -- the iron ore man's four million, the two smaller requests he had been deferring, the accrued management fees he had not earned and could not justify. Call it six million. The fund had three million in liquid assets. The gap was three million, or six million, depending on how you counted and what you were willing to admit. The violin was worth fifteen to twenty million.

The violin was a fund asset. Bob had bought it with fund money -- ten thousand euros, wired from the fund's operational account to Franz Kaltenbrunner's bank in Innsbruck in August 2023, recorded in the fund's books as "Musical instrument -- investment purpose," a line item so absurd that any competent auditor would have flagged it, except that the fund had not had a competent audit in three years because Bob had been filing the returns himself, signing the compliance declarations, maintaining the fiction with the methodical diligence of a man who understood that a Ponzi scheme was, at its core, an administrative exercise. The violin belonged to the fund. The fund belonged to the investors. If the violin was sold -- properly, transparently, through Tarisio or Christie's or one of the handful of dealers who handled instruments at this level -- the proceeds would flow to the fund, and the fund would be solvent. More than solvent. Spectacularly, absurdly, miraculously profitable. An investment of ten thousand euros that had returned fifteen to twenty million dollars. A return of roughly one hundred and fifty thousand percent. The investors would be made whole. The investors would be *enriched*. The fraud would become, retroactively, the luckiest accident in the history of Australian financial mismanagement.

Bob laughed again. Quietly this time, a sound that was closer to a gasp, and he stood and walked to the window and looked out at the dark. Marine Parade was empty. The Norfolk pines were black silhouettes against a sky that had not yet begun to lighten. The Indian Ocean was out there, invisible, making its sound. He could not hear it through the glass, but he knew it was there the way he knew the bass line of the Chaconne was there beneath the variations -- constant, structural, the thing everything else was built upon.

He could save it. He could save everything. If he called a lawyer today -- not the corporate lawyer who handled the fund's paperwork, but a real lawyer, a criminal defence lawyer, someone who understood ASIC and the Corporations Act and the precise calibrations of self-reporting and cooperation that could mean the difference between prosecution and a negotiated settlement -- if he called that lawyer and said: *The fund is insolvent, I have been operating it as a Ponzi scheme for approximately three years, but the fund holds an asset worth fifteen to twenty million dollars, here is the authentication report, I want to liquidate the asset and make every investor whole and cooperate fully with ASIC* -- if he did that, today, on his fiftieth birthday, before the party, before the performance, before Jeannie's caterers arrived and the marquee went up and the eighty guests assembled on the terrace to celebrate the life of Robert James Harrington, fund manager, husband, father, citizen of the western suburbs, man of no particular distinction -- if he did that, he might survive. Not unscathed. There would be consequences. Loss of his financial services licence, certainly. ASIC enforcement proceedings, probably. A possible criminal referral, though the voluntary disclosure and the full restitution would weigh heavily in his favour. The house might have to be sold. The boat would certainly have to go. Jeannie would --

He stopped.

Jeannie would leave him. This was not a hypothesis. It was a certainty as fixed and structural as the bass line of the Chaconne. Jeannie had married a fund manager on Marine Parade. She had not married a disgraced former fund manager in a rented apartment in Scarborough, which was what Bob would become once the fund was unwound and the assets were distributed and the ASIC investigation ran its course. She would take the children. She would take the Instagram. She would take the narrative -- *My husband was a fraud, I had no idea, I'm rebuilding my life* -- and she would perform it with the same conviction she had brought to every other narrative she had ever constructed, and within six months she would be having lunch in Claremont with the gaggle and the story of Bob Harrington would be a cautionary tale to be deployed between the second and third glass of sauvignon blanc.

But the children would know he had tried. The investors would have their money. And the fraud would be over. The lie that had been growing inside him like a tumour for three years, the lie he carried to bed and woke with and fed with fabricated returns and evasive phone calls and the queasy, ceaseless performance of competence -- the lie would be over. He could breathe. For the first time in three years, he could breathe.

He picked up the phone again. He opened the email. He looked at the photograph on the final page of the report -- a high-resolution image of the violin taken by Saunders's team during the examination, the instrument resting on a white cloth against a neutral background, lit from two angles, the varnish glowing with a warmth that the photograph could capture but not convey, the way a photograph of a sunset can show you the colours but not the heat. The scroll was centred in the frame -- the carved volute that Franz had handled with such reverence in the workshop in Absam, the spiral that had no beginning and no end, cut by a hand that had been dead for three hundred years and that had left behind, in the precision of its cuts and the confidence of its curves, an object of such beauty that Bob's chest ached to look at it the way his chest ached when the D major section of the Chaconne opened like a door into the light.

Antonio Stradivari had made this violin. A man in a white apron in a workshop in Cremona had cut this spruce, carved this scroll, mixed and applied this varnish, fitted these plates and ribs and linings with hands that had performed these operations ten thousand times and that had, sometime around 1715, achieved a mastery so absolute that the instruments produced in those years had never been equalled. The violin had survived three centuries. It had survived the collapse of the Cremonese workshop tradition, the Napoleonic wars, two world wars, the scattering of European collections, the Nazi looting that had swallowed at least ten Stradivari instruments into the darkness. It had ended up in a village in the Austrian Alps, in the hands of a luthier who suspected what it was but could not bring himself to confirm it, because confirmation would turn an instrument into a commodity. And from Franz's hands it had come to Bob's. Ten thousand euros. A purchase made with stolen money, in a moment of grace that Bob had not earned and could not repay.

He set the phone down. He walked to the study at the end of the hallway -- the small room that had been a sewing room and then a wardrobe overflow and was now, by the slow accretion of his retreat from the rest of the house, his room. The violin case was on the desk. He opened it. The instrument lay in its blue velvet lining, and the first light of the dawn -- barely there, the faintest violet suggestion at the eastern window -- caught the varnish, and the varnish responded the way it always responded: with a glow that seemed to come from inside the wood, as if the instrument were lit by a fire that had been burning for three hundred years and would burn for three hundred more.

He lifted the violin. He tucked it under his chin. The chinrest found the mark on his jaw -- the fiddler's neck, the callused hollow that had formed over twenty-four months of daily practice, the physical evidence of his transformation that Jeannie had asked about once and never again. The shoulder rest settled against his collarbone. The weight of the instrument, familiar now as the weight of his own hand, distributed itself through his neck and shoulder and left arm in the way Katarina had taught him -- not held but carried, the way you carry a child, the weight borne not by grip but by structure, by the architecture of the body arranged in service of the sound.

He drew the bow across the open D string.

The sound filled the small room. It went out through the window, which was cracked open an inch, and into the morning, and it was the sound he had been hearing for two years without knowing what it was. The sound of a Stradivarius. The sound of wood that had been vibrating for three centuries, its cellular structure rearranged by the passage of sound waves through its grain until the instrument had become, in a way that defied any materialist explanation, a living thing -- responsive, warm, capable of nuance that exceeded anything the player could consciously intend. When Bob played a note on this violin, the note he heard was not the note he had played but something larger, richer, more complex, as if the instrument were adding its own voice to his, the accumulated resonance of every hand that had drawn a bow across these strings in the three hundred years since the wood had been shaped and the varnish had dried in the Italian sun.

He played the opening chord of the Chaconne. D, A, D, F-sharp. The bow caught the lower two strings and rolled to the upper pair, and the sound that filled the room was the sound of a door opening -- the same door that had opened in a bedroom on Pearse Street when he was fourteen, the same door that had closed when he put the violin in its case after Year 12, the same door he had broken open in a soundproofed office on St George's Terrace two years ago and that had stayed open ever since, and behind the door was the room where the only true thing in his life lived, and the true thing was this: a man could spend fifty years being someone else and then, in the final years, find the person he was supposed to be, and the finding could cost him everything, and it was still worth it.

He played the first variation. The second. The descending bass line, the upper voice answering, the polyphony emerging the way it always emerged -- not as two separate lines but as a single thought expressed in two voices, the way a person can hold grief and love simultaneously and the holding does not diminish either but makes both more real. His left hand found the positions. His bow arm, the arm that had failed him ten thousand times, moved with the fluid, autonomous confidence of a body that has rehearsed a motion so often that the motion has become a part of its nervous system, wired in, permanent, inerasable.

He played for twenty minutes. He played the entire Chaconne, start to finish, alone in the study on the morning of his fiftieth birthday, with the authentication report open on his phone and the ASIC letter unopened in his office and the party twelve hours away and Jeannie asleep behind her white noise machine and his children asleep behind their closed doors and the sun coming up over the Indian Ocean, turning the sky from violet to rose to gold.

When he finished -- when the last chord, the great D minor resolution, rang in the small room and faded into the silence that always followed the Chaconne, the silence that was not empty but full, full of everything the music had said and everything it could not say -- he lowered the bow and stood at the window and looked out at the light on the water and did the calculation one more time.

The violin was worth fifteen to twenty million dollars.

The fund's deficit was five to six million.

The ASIC letter was in his office, postmarked nine days ago, demanding the production of documents within twenty-eight days. Nineteen days remained.

If he sold the violin, the fund was saved. Every investor would be made whole. The fraud would be erased -- not morally, not legally, but financially, which was the only form of erasure that mattered in the world he inhabited. He could walk into ASIC's offices with a cheque and an apology and a lawyer and the authentication report, and the worst that would happen would be a fine and a ban and the loss of the life he had built, which was not a life but a performance, and the performance was killing him.

But if he sold the violin, the music would stop. Not the Chaconne -- he could play the Chaconne on any instrument, on the student violin still sitting in the wardrobe of the Pearse Street house, on the Chinese factory instrument he had owned before Franz's. The notes were in his hands now, in his body, in the neural pathways that two years of obsessive practice had carved into his brain. No one could take the notes from him. But the sound -- the specific, unrepeatable, three-hundred-year-old sound of this instrument, the sound that had taught him what music could be, the sound that had opened the door -- that sound would belong to someone else. A collector. A museum. An institution that would display it behind glass and bring it out for special occasions and never play it the way Bob played it, with the desperate, imperfect, all-consuming love of a man who had found his voice at forty-eight and knew he was running out of time to use it.

He could not sell it. He understood this the way he understood the ASIC letter: as a fact, structural and immovable, like the bass line of the Chaconne. The violin was not an asset. It was not an investment. It was not a get-out-of-jail card concealed inside a case lined with blue velvet. It was the only beautiful thing he had ever done. It was the sound of his own soul, heard for the first time at the age of forty-eight, in a soundproofed room, on an instrument made by a man who had spent seventy-five years learning how to make a wooden box sing.

And tonight he was going to play it in front of everyone he loved.

He put the violin back in its case. He closed the latches. He picked up his phone and read the authentication report one more time -- the dendrochronology, the varnish analysis, the construction methodology, the conclusion, the valuation -- and then he opened his other email, the one he had been avoiding, the one from ASIC, and he read that too. It said what he knew it would say. Documents. Twenty-eight days. The language of institutional machinery engaging, the impersonal bureaucratic voice that would, within months, dismantle everything he had built and expose the hollow core of his life to the light.

Two emails on the morning of his fiftieth birthday. One that could save him. One that would destroy him. Both sitting in the same inbox, separated by a scroll of his thumb, the distance between redemption and ruin measurable in pixels.

He closed the phone. He stood in the study. The light was strengthening. Downstairs, the refrigerator hummed. A magpie called from the Norfolk pines -- the first of the morning, its song liquid and clear, the song of a bird that had no knowledge of authentication reports or managed investment schemes or the particular agony of a man standing between two futures, both of them impossible, both of them real.

The caterers would arrive at two. The guests at five. The DJ at nine. Jeannie would wake at seven and begin the performance of the day -- the preparation, the arrangement, the curation of a fiftieth birthday party for a man she did not love, on a terrace overlooking the ocean, in a house they could not afford, in a life that was about to end. And sometime between the speeches and the DJ, after the sun had set and before the dancing began, Bob would pick up the violin -- the Stradivarius, the fifteen-to-twenty-million-dollar instrument, the impossible gift from a small man in a leather apron in a village in the Alps -- and he would play the Chaconne. All of it. Fifteen minutes. For Jeannie, who would not hear it. For Chase and Shanel, who had never known him. For Jim, who had wanted him to be a stockbroker. For Katarina, who had taught him to listen. For Franz, who had sold him a Stradivarius for the price of a second-hand car because the old man had seen something in Bob's playing that Bob himself had not yet seen -- a genuine musical ear embedded in an undertrained body, a soul embedded in a life that was not its own.

He would play it, and then he would face whatever came after.

He went downstairs. He made coffee. He stood on the terraced grass in bare feet and watched the sun come up over the Indian Ocean, the light spreading across the water like a hand opening, and he held the phone in one hand and the coffee in the other and the two emails sat inside the phone like two futures inside a life, and the Indian Ocean made its sound, and the magpies called, and the Norfolk pines stood black against the gold, and Robert James Harrington turned fifty years old and was, for a few minutes, standing in the space between everything and nothing, and the space was very narrow, and the morning was very beautiful, and he drank his coffee, and he waited.
