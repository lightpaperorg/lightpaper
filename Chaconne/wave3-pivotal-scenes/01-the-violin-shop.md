# Chapter 11: "Absam"

The road from Innsbruck ran east along the valley floor with the Inn River on the left and the mountains on both sides, close and enormous, their lower slopes dark with pine forest and their upper reaches bare grey rock that caught the morning sun and held it. Bob drove. Beside him Jeannie slept, her face in the unguarded softness of sleep looking younger and kinder than it did when she was awake and composing it. In the back seat Chase had his earbuds in and Shanel was reading -- actually reading, a paperback from the hotel lobby, her hair falling across her face in a way that made Bob think, with a small sharp ache, of the girl she had been at six.

He had told them he wanted to stop at a village for coffee. The mildest possible lie, and yet his hands on the steering wheel were damp. He had been carrying this destination for eleven days -- since the Musee de la Musique in Paris, since the moment on the Metro when he had typed "violin makers Tyrol" into his phone. Eleven days of knowing he would come here. Eleven days of not knowing why.

He passed Hall in Tirol, and then the sign: ABSAM. White letters on green. Houses built in the Tyrolean style, white plaster and dark timber, balconies heavy with geraniums. The mountains rose directly behind the village, so close that the upper houses seemed to lean against them, and the air through the open window carried the cold mineral smell of altitude and snowmelt and stone.

He parked on a side street near the church. The engine ticked in the silence. In the back seat Chase had not looked up. Shanel turned a page.

"I'll catch up," he said. "There's a cafe somewhere. Get coffees."

Jeannie opened her eyes. "Where are we?"

"Absam. Little village. I just want to stretch my legs."

"Fine." She was already reaching for her phone, checking the screen with the automatic urgency of a woman who has been offline for forty minutes. "We need to be back by one. I booked lunch."

"Won't be long."

He got out of the car. The air hit him -- cold for July, the mountains holding winter in their upper reaches -- and he stood on the cobblestones breathing. The village was quiet with the particular quiet of small places on weekday mornings: a woman sweeping a step across the street, a delivery van idling outside a bakery, the sound of a church bell marking the half-hour with a single, clear stroke that hung in the mountain air and did not fade so much as thin, spreading outward until it became part of the silence it had interrupted. Three thousand people lived here. It had the clean, maintained look of Austrian villages -- window boxes, swept gutters, no litter -- and the faint melancholy of a place whose greatest days were four centuries past.

He walked up the street toward the church and then turned left, following a sign he had memorised from Google Maps. Past a brass plaque mounted on a stone wall -- Jakob Stainer, Geigenbauer, 1617-1683 -- the German words half-legible to him, but Geigenbauer he knew. Violin maker. The plaque was tarnished and modest, the kind of memorial a village erects for a figure it respects without fully understanding, the way a town might honour a poet whose work no one in the town has read. Bob stood in front of it for a moment. He had read the Wikipedia entry on his phone in Paris, and then the longer articles, and then a chapter of a book he had downloaded at two in the morning in the hotel room while Jeannie slept, reading with the intensity of a man studying a map to a place he suspects he has been before.

Jakob Stainer. Born in this village. Made violins that were, for a century after his death, considered the finest in Europe -- valued above Stradivari, above Amati, above every instrument produced in Cremona. Then taste shifted, concert halls grew larger, and the bigger, brighter sound of the Italian instruments displaced the Tyrolean ones, and Stainer's name receded into the specialist knowledge of luthiers and collectors. But he had been born here, on this street or one near it, and he had learned to shape wood in this air, in this light, with these mountains at his back.

Bob walked on. And then the workshop. A low building, older than the houses around it, with small windows set deep in thick walls, and in the nearest window, catching the morning light: the scroll of a violin, its curve precise and dark against the glass.

He stopped. His heart was doing something it did not normally do -- a fast shallow rhythm he associated with the fund, with the moments when a client called and the number was a number he could not cover. But this was not dread. It was something closer to the feeling of standing at a door you have been walking toward without admitting you were walking toward it, and reaching for the handle, and knowing that once you opened it you could not close it again.

The door was wooden, heavy, paint-stripped to grey. A brass bell on a spring, the kind that had not been fashionable for fifty years. He pushed it open.

---

The bell rang -- bright, small, quickly dead, absorbed by the room. And then the smell.

Spruce shavings. Hide glue. Varnish. Rosin, and something resinous and ancient beneath all of it, something he could not name but that his body knew -- not from memory but from some deeper recognition, the way a sleeping dog's legs move to the dream of running. The smell entered him and opened something, a chamber behind his sternum that had been sealed so long he had forgotten it was there. He stood in the doorway and breathed. He breathed again. The air in the workshop was different from the air outside -- warmer, denser, saturated with decades of making -- and he felt, with a certainty that had no rational basis, that he had been in this room before.

He had not. He had never been in any room like this. The closest thing in his experience was a furniture showroom in Osborne Park where Jeannie had spent four hours choosing a dining table, but that had smelled of new pine and carpet adhesive and the showroom's air conditioning had created a sterile chill that made everything in it feel temporary. This room was the opposite of temporary. Everything in it had been here a long time and intended to remain.

The workshop was small and cluttered and organised in a way that made sense only to its inhabitant. A workbench ran the length of one wall, its surface scarred and stained and covered in tools -- chisels of different widths laid out in descending order, planes with their blades catching the light, clamps in various sizes, pots of glue with their lids off and a faint haze of warmth rising from them, brushes standing upright in jars. The floor was covered in wood shavings -- fine pale curls that lay in drifts against the bench legs and crunched softly underfoot when Bob took a step. Violins hung from the ceiling at various stages of completion: a raw white body with no varnish, its spruce top carved but not yet smoothed; a nearly finished instrument with its varnish still tacky, the colour a deep amber that seemed to hold the light rather than reflect it; two completed violins, strung and polished, hanging by their scrolls on brass hooks. A viola. A cello in the far corner, leaning against the wall like a quiet, dignified person waiting to be spoken to. And the light -- the light came from a large window facing north, constant and even, the light of a craftsman who needed to see what he was seeing and not what shadows told him he was seeing.

On the varnishing bench, a cat. Large, grey, asleep, its paws tucked under its chest. It opened one eye when the bell rang, assessed Bob with the slow disinterest of an animal that has seen too many visitors to be impressed by another, and closed the eye again.

A man appeared from the back room. Small -- five foot six, perhaps less -- with white hair and a checked flannel shirt and a leather apron and hands that were disproportionately large, the fingers thick and extraordinarily steady. His face was deeply lined, brown from sun and weather, with pale blue eyes that had the unsettling clarity of a person who has spent decades looking at things closely and has learned that most of what matters is invisible until you learn where to look. He moved without hurry. He wiped his hands on the apron -- a gesture so habitual it had become involuntary, the hands cleaning themselves before they did anything else -- and looked at Bob with the unhurried assessment of a man who had been sizing up visitors for sixty years.

"Guten Morgen."

Then, registering something -- Bob's clothes, his posture, the RM Williams boots that marked him as neither European nor American but something from the English-speaking periphery -- he said: "English?"

"Australian," Bob said.

The man nodded. He had had Australians before. "Come in," he said. "Close the door. The cat goes out."

Bob closed the door. The cat did not go out. The cat had not moved. The man glanced at it and shrugged with the tolerance of a person who has long since stopped winning arguments with this particular opponent.

"I am Franz," the man said. "This is my workshop. You are looking for something?"

Bob opened his mouth to answer and found that he did not know what to say. He had rehearsed something in the car -- a tourist's inquiry, something casual and vague about the village's violin-making history, a question he could ask without revealing that he had driven here deliberately, that he had been carrying this address in his phone for eleven days, that his heart was hammering against his ribs. But the rehearsed words would not come. The room had disarmed him. The smell, the light, the wood shavings on the floor, the instruments hanging from the ceiling like the frozen bodies of birds -- all of it was operating on a part of him that the rehearsed words could not reach.

"I played the violin," he said. "As a boy. A long time ago."

Franz waited. He had the patience of a man for whom silence was a professional tool -- a luthier who listened to wood, who tapped a plate and waited for the sound to tell him what it needed, who understood that the most important information came after the speaker thought he had finished.

"I stopped," Bob said. "In school. Year twelve. I haven't played since."

"How long?"

"Thirty years."

Franz nodded. Thirty years was not unusual. He had heard this before -- the returners, the men and occasionally women who had put the instrument away at sixteen or seventeen and carried the absence of it through decades of other lives. Some of them came to buy a violin for a grandchild. Some came because they were dying and wanted to hear the sound one more time. Some came because they did not know why they had come, and those were the ones Franz paid attention to, because they were the ones the instrument had called back.

He took a violin from the wall. A modern instrument, one of his own -- a good workshop violin, the varnish a warm brown-orange, the wood well-chosen and well-made. He held it out.

"Play," he said.

Bob stared at the violin. "I can't. I mean -- I haven't played in thirty years. I wouldn't know where to --"

"Play." Franz did not withdraw the instrument. He held it out with the steady, extended arm of a man who was prepared to stand there all morning.

Bob took the violin. The weight of it. The shape against his collarbone, the neck in his left hand. His fingers curled around it and the muscle memory fired -- not technique, not skill, but the pure physical knowledge of what this object was and where it went against his body. His thumb found the back of the neck. His jaw lowered toward where the chinrest met his shoulder. Twenty-seven years fell away like a door swinging open, and behind the door was a fourteen-year-old boy in a music room at Scotch College with Mrs Albrecht's metronome ticking on the piano and the afternoon light coming through the high windows and the sound -- the sound of the string under his finger, the vibration travelling through the wood into his jaw, into his skull, into the centre of his head where it became not sound but feeling, not music but the memory of music, not a thing he heard but a thing he was.

He picked up the bow Franz offered. His grip was wrong -- he knew it was wrong, his hand remembered enough to know that, the way a man who has not ridden a bicycle for thirty years knows the moment he sits on one that his weight is in the wrong place. He straightened the bow, set the hair against the D string, and drew it across.

The sound was raw. Unsteady. The bow skated, the pressure uneven, and the note that emerged was thin and wavering, the sound of a body remembering something the mind had tried to forget. He drew the bow again. Better. The D string opened up, the note rounding, the overtones beginning to bloom in the small room with its wooden walls and its high ceiling and its hanging instruments that vibrated faintly in sympathy, as though every violin in the workshop were leaning in to listen.

He played a scale. D major. His intonation was rough -- the third finger landed flat, the shift to second position was clumsy, the high E at the top of the second octave was sharp enough to make him wince. His bowing arm was stiff, the wrist locked, the elbow too low. But the notes came. One after another, each one a small recovery, a fragment of a language he had spoken as a boy and never entirely lost. He reached the top of the scale, paused, and came back down. The descending scale was better -- his hand relaxing, the fingers finding the intervals with something closer to instinct, the bow drawing a longer, more even tone.

He played a melody. Not from the page -- from memory, from somewhere so deep he did not know it was there until it came out. A simple tune, a Handel minuet from Suzuki Book 2 that Mrs Albrecht had taught him in Year 8, and that he had played at the school concert that year -- the only concert, the one under the lights where he had felt so exposed he had never performed again. He played it now in a luthier's workshop in an Austrian village with wood shavings under his feet and a cat asleep on the varnishing bench, and it was terrible. The intonation wandered. The bow changes were audible -- small bumps where the direction reversed, the sound thinning and then recovering. His vibrato was nonexistent. His left thumb pressed too hard against the neck, tensing the whole hand, locking the fingers into a grip that was effortful where it should have been loose.

But he was playing. He was playing, and something in the way he played made Franz, who had been standing with his arms folded and his face neutral, uncross his arms and lean forward slightly, the way a person leans toward a sound they want to hear more clearly.

It was not talent. Franz had heard talent -- the prodigies who came through this workshop once or twice a decade, the children whose fingers moved with a facility that defied their age, who played in tune the way birds sing, without apparent effort, as though intonation were not a skill but a birthright. Bob was not talented. His playing was rough and halting and technically poor. But he was doing something that Franz had seen perhaps a dozen times in sixty years of listening to people play in this room, and it was this: he was listening.

Most people played at the violin. They pushed sound into it, forced the bow across the string, muscled the notes out of the instrument with the determination of a person trying to make a stubborn machine comply. The sound they produced was the sound of effort -- technically adequate, sometimes, but fundamentally adversarial, the player and the instrument engaged in a negotiation that neither could win. Bob did not play at the violin. He played into it. He drew the bow and waited. He waited for the sound to come back to him, and when it came, he adjusted -- not his fingers, which did not have the training to respond that quickly, but something else, something in the angle of his head, in the pressure of his jaw on the chinrest, in the weight of his arm on the bow, that said: I hear you. I am listening. Tell me more.

Franz watched him play the Handel minuet to its end -- a simple, square, eight-bar melody that a competent twelve-year-old could play in their sleep and that this tall Australian in his expensive clothes played as though it were the last sound on earth, as though the small room with its wood and its tools and its hanging violins were the only room that existed, as though nothing outside the door -- not the village, not the mountains, not the sleeping wife in the car, not the fund or the fraud or the life he had built on a lie -- were real. The instrument was real. The sound was real. And Franz, who believed that instruments had souls, who used the German word Seele and meant it not as metaphor but as observation, saw something in Bob's playing that he had learned to recognise the way a doctor learns to recognise a symptom: the sound of a man who had been separated from the thing he was meant to do, and who was dying of the separation without knowing its name.

The minuet ended. The last note -- a D, open string, the simplest sound a violin can make -- rang in the workshop and faded. Bob lowered the instrument. His face was flushed. His hands were shaking.

"I'm sorry," he said. "That was --"

"No," Franz said. He said it quietly, without force, but the word stopped Bob mid-sentence. "No. Do not apologise." He took the violin back and hung it on the wall. He stood for a moment with his back to Bob, looking at the instruments on the wall, at the bench with its tools, at the window with its pale north light. The cat stirred on the varnishing bench and resettled. Franz touched the cat's head -- a single, absent stroke -- and turned back to Bob.

"Wait," he said. "I show you something."

---

He went into the back room. Bob stood alone in the workshop. He could hear Franz moving behind the wall -- a drawer opening, the soft thud of something being lifted, the careful footsteps of a man carrying something he did not want to drop. Bob looked at the instruments on the wall. He looked at his hands. His left hand was still trembling, a fine, involuntary vibration that seemed to come not from his muscles but from somewhere deeper, from the bones themselves, as though the act of holding a violin after thirty years had shaken something loose in the architecture of his body.

Franz came back carrying a case. Not a modern case -- an old one, battered, the leather cracked, the clasps tarnished to a dark bronze. He set it on the workbench and stood beside it for a moment without opening it. He looked at Bob. His pale blue eyes had an expression Bob could not read -- not the professional neutrality of a dealer showing stock, but something more private, something that cost him.

He opened the case.

Inside: a violin. Or what had once been a violin. No strings. No bridge. The tailpiece was detached, hanging from the endpin by the gut loop. Through the f-holes Bob could see the soundpost -- a small wooden dowel, the critical structural member that transmitted vibrations from the top plate to the back -- lying on its side against the back plate, dislodged, the instrument's internal architecture collapsed. Cracks ran from the f-holes toward the edges of the top plate, fine dark lines that followed the grain of the spruce, two on the treble side and one on the bass. The varnish was dark with age and grime, chipped along the edges where the wood showed through in pale crescents, but beneath the dirt there was a colour -- a deep reddish-amber that caught the light from the north window and held it, not reflecting but absorbing, as though the surface were not opaque but translucent, as though you could look into the varnish the way you look into deep water and see something moving underneath.

The whole thing looked like junk. Like something you would find in an attic clearance and leave in the pile for the tip.

Franz lifted it from the case with both hands. He handled it with a tenderness that contradicted its appearance -- the way you handle a bird with a broken wing, knowing the bones are fragile and the creature is frightened and the wrong pressure in the wrong place will cause a damage that cannot be undone. He held it up to the light. He turned it.

"Look," he said. "The scroll."

Bob looked. The scroll -- the carved spiral at the top of the pegbox, where the tuning pegs were seated -- was extraordinary. Not because it was ornate or unusual but because it was confident. The volute spiralled inward with a precision that was almost mathematical, each turn tightening at exactly the rate the eye expected, the curves smooth and clean and certain, carved by a hand that had known exactly what it was doing and had not hesitated. Bob had seen scrolls on the instruments hanging on the wall -- Franz's own work, accomplished and fine -- and they were good. This was different. This was the difference between a person who could draw a circle freehand and a person whose freehand circle was a circle, geometrically, actually, the Platonic form of the thing rather than an approximation of it.

"Now the wood," Franz said. He turned the violin over. The back was a single piece of maple -- not two pieces bookmatched, as was common, but one -- and the figure in the wood was extraordinary. Flame, they called it in English. The grain of the maple running at an angle to the surface so that when the light caught it, bands of dark and light rippled across the wood like the surface of a lake in wind, shifting as you moved, alive. This maple had a flame so deep and so regular that the bands seemed to pulse, to breathe, to respond to the light as though the wood were not dead but merely sleeping.

"Feel," Franz said.

Bob touched the back plate. The wood was smooth, almost greasy from the varnish and the centuries of handling, and warm. Not room temperature -- warm, as though something inside the wood were generating heat, which was impossible, which was the varnish and the friction of Franz's hands and Bob's imagination filling in what his senses could not explain. But the warmth was there. He ran his fingers across the surface and felt the slight undulations of the arching -- the curve of the back plate, higher in the centre and tapering to the edges, the geometry that gave a violin its acoustic shape. The curve fit his palm. His hand knew this shape. His hand had always known this shape.

"The f-holes," Franz said. He turned the instrument again, holding it so the light fell across the front plate. The f-holes -- the two sound-holes cut into the spruce top, their shape giving the violin its face -- were cut with the same terrifying precision as the scroll. The curves flowed without interruption, the notches at the waist of each f were sharp and clean, and the taper at the top and bottom of each hole ended in a point so fine that the wood seemed to have been not cut but parted, as though the knife had found a line already present in the grain and simply followed it.

"This," Franz said, and paused. He was choosing his words with the care of a man who understands that what he is about to say will change the shape of the conversation. "This is not an ordinary violin."

Bob waited. His hands had stopped trembling. Or rather, his hands were trembling in a new way -- not with the nervousness of a man who has played badly in front of a stranger but with the vibration of a man who is holding something and does not yet know what it is but whose body knows, the way a dowsing rod knows water, the way a compass needle knows north.

"I have had this instrument for many years," Franz said. "It came from an estate. A family in Salzburg -- the father died, the house was cleared. The daughter brought three violins to a dealer in Vienna. Two were nothing. Factory instruments, nineteenth century, worth a few hundred euros each. This one was with them. No strings, no bridge, the soundpost fallen. The dealer sold it to me for two thousand euros because he did not look carefully, and because the instrument, in this condition --" he gestured at the cracks, the missing bridge, the collapsed interior -- "looks like what it is not."

He set the violin down on the bench, on a cloth, with care.

"I have studied it. Three years now. The wood. The varnish. The scroll. The construction." He touched the edge of the top plate, where the purfling ran -- three strips of wood, black-white-black, inlaid in a channel a millimetre from the edge. His thick finger traced the line where it rounded the upper bout, and Bob could see what Franz was showing him without being told: the purfling turned the corner in a smooth, continuous curve, not mitred, not spliced, not faked. Hand-cut, by someone who knew what they were doing to a degree that precluded error.

"I believe this instrument is very old," Franz said. "Eighteenth century. Possibly seventeenth. The varnish is consistent with the finest Tyrolean or north Italian work. The wood --" He looked at the maple back again, the flame rippling in the light. "The wood is from the high Alps. Spruce and maple from the forests above the Inn Valley, cut during a period when the winters were very long and very cold, and the trees grew slowly, and the grain is tight and even in a way that modern wood is not." He paused. "I believe this violin may be exceptional."

The word sat in the room. Exceptional. Bob looked at the instrument on the bench. He looked at the cracks, the missing strings, the fallen soundpost visible through the f-hole. It looked like junk. It looked like the kind of thing Jeannie would tell him to throw away.

"Have you had it authenticated?" Bob asked.

Franz shook his head. "No."

"Why not?"

Franz looked at him. The pale blue eyes were steady, and the expression in them was one Bob would think about for years afterward -- would think about on the plane home, in the study at three in the morning, in the soundproofed room on the Terrace, in all the hours of practice and doubt and terror that lay ahead. The expression was not secretive. It was not evasive. It was the expression of a man who had made a decision that he understood would seem irrational to anyone who lived in a world where objects had prices and prices determined value.

"If I know what it is worth," Franz said, "it becomes money. I do not want money. I want it to be played."

He said the German word then. Seele. He said it the way you say a word that is not a word but a fact -- the way you say gravity or light or time, naming something that exists whether or not you name it.

"Every instrument has a Seele," he said. "A soul. Not metaphor. I have built instruments for sixty years. I have heard it. The great violins -- the ones the old makers built, the ones that have survived -- they have something in them that is not wood, not varnish, not geometry. Something the maker put in that cannot be explained and cannot be reproduced. This violin --" He touched the scroll. One finger, resting on the volute's outermost curve. "This violin has a Seele that has been silent for a long time. It needs to be heard. It needs to be played."

Bob was looking at the violin. He could not stop looking at it. The reddish-amber varnish beneath the grime, the flame of the maple shifting in the light, the scroll with its impossible precision. He did not know what he was looking at. He did not know the difference between a Stradivarius and a student instrument, between a seventeenth-century masterpiece and a nineteenth-century copy. He had no vocabulary for the things Franz was showing him -- the purfling, the f-holes, the arching. But his body knew. His hands knew. The trembling in his fingers had resolved into something steadier, something that felt less like nervousness and more like recognition -- the vibration of a tuning fork that has found its pitch.

"Can I hold it?" he said.

Franz picked up the violin and placed it in Bob's hands.

The weight was different from the instrument he had just played. Lighter, somehow, despite the damage, as though the centuries had refined the wood, burned away everything that was not essential. The curves settled into his palms. He turned it, looking through the f-hole at the dark interior, the fallen soundpost lying at an angle, the fine grain of the spruce visible in the shadows. He could not play it -- there were no strings, no bridge, no way to make it sound. But he tapped the back plate with his knuckle, gently, the way he had once seen a piano tuner tap the soundboard of a Steinway in a hotel lobby, and the wood responded. A resonance. Low, warm, complex, a sound that was not a note but the ghost of every note the instrument had ever made, stored in the wood and released by the touch of a hand. The sound lasted longer than it should have. It lived in the air of the workshop for a moment, and every violin on the wall vibrated in sympathy -- a faint, collective hum that Bob felt rather than heard, a chorus of instruments responding to one of their own.

His eyes were wet. He did not know when that had happened.

"How much?" he said.

---

Franz named the price without hesitation. "Ten thousand euro."

The number landed in the room and Bob felt it settle. It was more than he had expected -- more than a broken, unstrung, unplayable instrument should cost by any rational measure. A functioning workshop violin cost five to eight thousand euros. This was a wreck. A project. A thing that would need months of restoration before it could make a single sound.

But the price was not irrational. It was deliberate. Bob understood this without being told, the way he understood client psychology -- the way a price communicates not just cost but meaning. Franz was not pricing the violin as junk. He was pricing it as an instrument. An instrument with potential, with history, with something inside it that deserved to be found. And at the same time, he was pricing it within reach. Ten thousand euros was a lot of money. It was not an impossible amount of money. It was the kind of price a man could pay if he wanted to badly enough, if the wanting was real.

Bob did not negotiate. He reached for his wallet. The card was there -- the black Amex linked to the fund's operating account, the card he used for everything because using it was easier than maintaining the fiction of separate finances, because Jeannie expected a lifestyle and the lifestyle had a cost and the cost was paid from the only account that had money in it, which was the account that belonged to other people. He had paid for the flights with this card. The hotels. The rental car. Jeannie's shopping in Paris and London. The entire European holiday was funded by the savings of people who believed their money was invested in the Australian equities market and did not know that it was paying for a woman to buy handbags in the Marais and a man to buy a broken violin in an Alpine village.

He held the card. He hesitated. Not at the price -- the price was irrelevant, the price was a number, and Bob had spent the last decade manipulating numbers into shapes that bore no relationship to reality. He hesitated because this purchase was different from the flights and the hotels and the shopping. Those were maintenance. They were the cost of sustaining the lie, of keeping the surface intact, of ensuring that Jeannie and the children and the investors and the world at large continued to see what they expected to see. This purchase was not maintenance. This was the first thing he had bought with stolen money that was not about maintaining the lie but about reaching through it toward something true. The irony was exquisite and he did not pause to appreciate it. He put the card on the bench.

Franz took it. He had a small card reader, old, the kind with a chip slot and a printed receipt. He processed the transaction with the unhurried competence of a man who sold instruments rarely and handled each sale as a discrete event, not a line in a ledger. The receipt printed. Bob signed. Ten thousand euros. The number would appear on the fund's statement at the end of the month: KALTENBRUNNER GEIGENBAU, ABSAM, AT. A charge at a violin workshop in Austria, on a fund account, discoverable, traceable, one more thread in the web of fraud that would eventually be pulled.

Franz tore off the receipt and placed it on the bench. He did not offer it to Bob. He placed it there, as though it were a document of secondary importance, as though the transaction that mattered had already occurred and the card and the receipt were merely its administrative shadow.

Then he wrapped the violin. He did not put it back in the old case. He took a piece of soft cotton cloth from a drawer -- old, washed many times, the colour of cream -- and laid the instrument on it. He folded the cloth over the belly, over the back, tucked the edges around the scroll and the base. He moved with the slow, specific care of a man who had wrapped a thousand instruments and understood that the wrapping was not packaging but protection, not commerce but tenderness. He placed the wrapped violin in a cardboard box and packed it with tissue paper, pressing the paper into the gaps, ensuring that nothing would shift. He closed the box and taped it with brown packing tape and wrote on the lid, in pencil, in a neat, small hand: VIOLINE -- VORSICHT.

"You need a luthier," he said. "In Australia. Someone to restore it. The cracks need internal studs. The soundpost reset. A new bridge, new strings, the seams re-glued." He looked at Bob. "Find someone who listens. Not someone who fixes. You understand?"

Bob nodded. He did not trust his voice.

Franz held out the box. Bob took it. It was lighter than he expected. The weight of an instrument that had been stripped of everything that made it functional -- no strings, no bridge, no fittings, just the body, the box, the resonating chamber that an unknown maker had shaped from spruce and maple in a workshop not unlike this one, in a time when the mountains outside the window were the same mountains and the light was the same light and the smell of wood shavings and hide glue was the same smell, and the only thing that had changed was everything else.

They shook hands. Franz's grip was firm, his hand enormous around Bob's, the fingers rough with calluses and varnish residue, the hand of a man who had held chisels and planes and the necks of violins for sixty years and whose strength was not the strength of force but of patience, the accumulated pressure of ten thousand days of careful work.

"It needs to be played," Franz said. "You will play it."

He said it simply. Not as encouragement. Not as prophecy. As fact. The way he said Seele -- naming something that existed whether or not you believed in it. Bob held the box against his chest and looked at the old man in the checked flannel shirt and the leather apron, in the workshop on Stainerstrasse in a village of three thousand people at the foot of the Alps, and he understood, without the words for it, that something had just happened that he would spend the rest of his life trying to deserve.

"Thank you," he said. The words were inadequate. Every word was inadequate. Franz nodded, as though he understood this too, and turned back to his bench, to the chisel and the piece of spruce he had been shaping when Bob walked in, and the cat opened its eyes and watched Bob cross the workshop to the door and push it open and step out into the bright, cold, Alpine morning.

---

The bell rang behind him. The door closed. He stood on the cobblestones with the box in his arms and the mountains above him and the village quiet around him and his heart beating with a force that seemed to exceed the capacity of his chest. He walked to the car. He walked slowly, not because the box was heavy but because his legs were unreliable, as though the ligaments that connected his bones had loosened and the architecture of his body was being rebuilt as he moved, reorganising itself around a new centre of gravity.

Jeannie was not in the car. The car was empty, the doors locked. He put the box on the roof and looked around and saw them -- Jeannie, Chase, Shanel -- through the window of a cafe across from the church, sitting at a table with coffees. Jeannie was on her phone. Chase was eating something. Shanel was watching the street, her chin on her hand, and she saw Bob through the glass and raised her hand in a wave that was half greeting, half question: where have you been?

He waved back. He took the box from the roof and put it in the boot, beneath the suitcases, in the dark. He locked the car. He crossed the street and pushed open the cafe door and the smell of coffee and pastry replaced the smell of spruce and varnish and he was back in his life, the life he had left an hour ago, the life that was the same life and was not the same life.

"Where were you?" Jeannie said. Not looking up from her phone. The question was administrative, not curious.

"Found a little shop. Antiques, sort of. Craft things."

"Did you buy something?" She looked up now, her face sharpening into the expression she wore when money was involved -- an alertness that no other subject could produce.

"A violin. Old one. Broken, actually. No strings or anything. Just thought it was interesting."

"How much?"

"Couple of hundred euros."

The lie came out as smoothly as water. He heard himself say it and felt nothing -- no guilt, no hesitation, the ease of a man who had been lying about money for so long that the mechanism was automatic, a reflex as involuntary as blinking. Jeannie looked at him for a moment. Her eyes moved across his face the way they moved across an Instagram post -- assessing, evaluating, deciding within a second whether the content merited further attention. It did not. A broken violin for a couple of hundred euros was exactly the kind of harmless, slightly pathetic purchase she expected from Bob. It confirmed her model of him: a man who pottered, who browsed, who bought small useless things because he did not have the taste or the ambition to buy large useful ones.

"Fine," she said, and looked back at her phone.

Chase had not looked up. Shanel was still watching him through the window's reflection, her chin on her hand, and there was something in her expression -- not suspicion, not curiosity, but a faint attentiveness, as though she had registered a detail she could not yet name. Bob sat down. He ordered a coffee. He drank it. The coffee was good -- strong, dark, Austrian -- and it tasted like the workshop smelled, like wood and warmth, and his left hand, resting on his thigh beneath the table, was curled around a shape that was not there.

They drove back to Innsbruck. Bob drove. The box was in the boot. The mountains slid past, enormous, indifferent, permanent. In the back seat Shanel had returned to her book. Chase was asleep. Jeannie was posting a photograph of the cafe -- the table, the coffees, the geraniums in the window box, the caption: "Austrian village mornings" with a pin-drop emoji.

Bob's hands on the steering wheel were steady. His heart had slowed. The fast, shallow rhythm had been replaced by something deeper and slower, a pulse that seemed to originate not in his chest but in his hands, as though the trembling that had begun in the workshop had resolved into a new resting frequency, a vibration so low and so constant that it was indistinguishable from stillness.

He glanced at the rear-view mirror. The village was gone, swallowed by the valley and the road and the mountains that closed behind it like a door. Absam. He would not come back. He knew this with the same certainty with which he knew that the thing in the boot was not a broken violin for a couple of hundred euros but something else, something whose true nature and true cost he could not yet calculate, and that the calculation, when it came, would change everything.

His left hand lifted from the steering wheel and curled, the fingers closing softly, finding positions on strings that were not there, and this time he did not straighten them. He let them curl. He let them hold the shape of the thing he had been reaching for his whole life and had finally, in a workshop on a street named after a dead violin maker in a village at the foot of the Alps, touched.
