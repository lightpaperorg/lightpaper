# Scene 5: The Breakthrough

## Chapter 22: "The Gap"

---

She had told him to play the D major section, and he had played it -- bars 133 through 208, the middle movement of the Chaconne, the passage where the music lifts from D minor into D major and the entire emotional landscape shifts, the way weather shifts when a cloud bank moves and the sun finds the ground. He had played it from memory, which was itself new -- until a month ago he had needed the score in front of him, the notation a safety net he clung to even when his fingers knew the way, afraid that without the printed page the music would abandon him midphrase and leave him standing in silence with the bow raised and nothing to say. Katarina had taken the score away. She had closed it and placed it on the windowsill and said, "The music is in you. Stop reading it and start hearing it." He had been terrified. He had played the first eight bars from memory, stumbled on a chord in bar 141, and stopped.

"Again," she said.

He had played it again. The same eight bars, and then further -- bar 141, the chord clean this time, the voices separating, the soprano floating above the bass the way Katarina had demonstrated week after week, "hear the upper voice as a singer, a woman's voice, and the bass as the ground she walks on." He played through bar 150, bar 160, the variations growing in complexity, the double stops climbing, and somewhere -- bar 168, or bar 172, he could not later say exactly where -- something happened.

The music breathed.

Not the way it had breathed before, which was the breathing of effort, of a man managing the mechanics of bow and string and left hand and intonation while trying to remember the next note and the shape of the phrase and the shape of the whole fifteen-minute arc. That was the breathing of a swimmer counting strokes. This was different. This was the breathing of a man who had stopped counting. For six bars -- maybe eight, he could not say, because the quality of the experience was precisely that he was not counting -- the violin sang and Bob disappeared into it the way he used to disappear as a boy in Mrs Albrecht's studio, the way he had disappeared in the car on Rokeby Road the first time he heard Milstein play the Chaconne, the way he disappeared into the ocean when he swam at dawn off Cottesloe in the winter, when the water was so cold it erased everything except the body's animal insistence on being alive.

What happened in those bars was not a technical event. It was a perceptual one. For eighteen months, he had listened to himself playing the Chaconne -- monitoring, evaluating, comparing the sound he produced against the recordings in his memory, measuring the gap and finding himself, always, on the wrong side of it. That listening was the listening of self-consciousness. It was the way he listened to himself speak at investor lunches, choosing words, managing tone, projecting the competence he did not feel. It was the listening of a man who lived inside the gap between who he was and who he pretended to be.

Now the gap closed. Not permanently -- he would feel it open again in seconds. But for these bars, there was no Bob Harrington playing the Chaconne. There was only the Chaconne, sounding in a room, through an instrument, via a body that had ceased to obstruct it. The concentrating self -- the self that monitored and managed and corrected and judged, the self that stood beside the music and said faster, slower, wrong note, flat, sharp, fix it -- that self released its grip. What remained was the body. The hands. The bow arm. The instrument.

His bow pressure lightened of its own accord. His vibrato widened, slowed, became the gentle pulsing that the Italians call the vibrato del cuore -- an undulation of pitch so subtle it does not change the note but warms it, the way candlelight warms a face. The double stops separated into their constituent voices and the soprano floated above the bass and the two lines sang independently, a conversation between high and low, between grief and its transfiguration, between the thing that was lost and the thing that remained after the losing.

The melody climbed. The arpeggio figures in bars 145 through 152 cascaded upward, the tone brightening, and his bow arm drew the sound with a freedom it had never had -- the elbow swinging, the wrist flexible, the whole mechanism of bowing functioning, for the first time, as a unified system. The bow did not press the string. It breathed across it. The string did not resist. It sang. His fingers found the positions and the positions were correct, not because he was placing them correctly but because the music wanted to go there and his hands followed, the intonation governed not by the conscious placement of the fingertip but by the body's deep agreement with the harmonic series, the mathematical relationships between frequencies that the inner ear recognises the way it recognises gravity -- without thought, without effort, without the intervention of the managing self.

He came back. The bow reached the end of bar 176, or bar 178, and the spell broke -- his third finger missed a shift, the E was flat, and he was suddenly a man playing a violin again, aware of the room, the acoustic foam, the mirror, his own reflection with its furrowed brow and the red mark on his jaw and the sweat on his upper lip. He lowered the instrument.

Katarina was silent. She stood by the window, her own violin hanging at her side, and she did not speak for a long time. Long enough that the silence became its own presence in the room, the way a rest in music is not the absence of sound but its frame, the thing that gives the notes around it their meaning.

"You stopped trying," she said.

He looked at her.

"You let the music play you." She paused. Her English was precise, accented, the syntax occasionally inverted in a way that gave her statements the quality of pronouncements. "That's what I hear in the great recordings -- Milstein, Szeryng, the very best. Not a person playing the Chaconne. The Chaconne playing through a person. You did that. For six bars, maybe eight. You did that."

Bob sat down. He did not decide to sit -- his legs folded, the chair catching him. His hands were trembling. Not from exertion -- from the aftermath of something he did not have language for, the same wordless trembling he had felt in the car on Rokeby Road, the same trembling that had seized him in Franz's workshop in Absam when he first drew the bow across the strings of this violin and the sound that came back was not his but the instrument's, three hundred years of resonance released into the air by the imperfect, desperate, fully human pressure of his hand.

"Can I do it again?" he said.

Katarina smiled. It was not the polished smile of a teacher praising a student. It was something rarer -- the smile of a musician who has witnessed something she had begun to think she would never see. Not from this man. Not from any of her students -- the twelve-year-olds with their Suzuki books, the Scotch College boys doing their AMEB exams, the retired professionals learning folk songs. She had heard good playing. She had heard talented playing. She had not, in twelve years in Perth, heard a moment where the music itself seemed to take over, where the player became the instrument and the instrument became the voice and the voice said something that was true.

"You can't make it happen," she said. "That's the whole point. You can only stop preventing it."

---

He raised the violin. He placed the bow on the string and began the D major section again, bar 133, and this time the concentrating self was fully awake -- monitoring, managing -- and the music that emerged was correct and controlled and dead. The transparency was gone. The vibrato was tight. The double stops were clean but undifferentiated, the voices merged into a texture rather than separated into a conversation. He played through to bar 160 and stopped.

"Gone," he said.

"It will come back. Not because you chase it. Because you've been there now. Your body knows the way. Your body will take you there again, when your mind stops standing at the door."

She picked up her own violin and played the D major section -- not the pedagogical playing she normally did but the way she would play it for herself, alone, at performance tempo, with the tone and phrasing she had spent twenty-eight years developing, the sound of a conservatory-trained violinist who had placed fourth in a national competition and had not placed first and who carried that distance -- between fourth and first, between very good and great -- the way Bob carried his own gap, as a wound that was also the source of everything she understood about music. She played bars 133 through 180. It was expert playing. Beautiful playing. It was not, quite, what Bob had done for those six bars.

She lowered her violin.

"I've been playing the Chaconne for twenty years," she said. "I have performed it twice. Both times, I played it carefully. And carefully --" She stopped. She looked out the window, where rain was falling on St George's Terrace, the drops visible against the glass, the city grey and wet, the river invisible behind cloud. "Carefully is death on the concert stage. That's what my professor told me. Kovar. He was a bastard, but he was right. The great performers are not careful. They are reckless. They put everything at risk -- intonation, tone, the whole structure of the piece -- in order to find something true. And you --" She turned back to him. "You are not reckless, Bob. You are the most careful man I have ever met. But for eight bars, you forgot to be careful. And what came out was more honest than anything I have heard in that piece in twelve years."

She began packing her case. The Prokop went into its velvet. The bow, loosened, alongside.

"Next week, we'll work on the bariolage. Bars 89 to 120. The hardest passage technically. I want you to play it badly."

"I play everything badly."

"No. You play everything carefully. There's a difference. I want you to play the bariolage and not care where the notes land. Just move the bow. Crossing, crossing, crossing -- the right arm does the work. Let the left hand find the notes on its own. Don't place them. Let them arrive."

She stood at the door.

"And Bob. What happened just now -- it will happen again. You won't know when. You can't schedule it. But when it comes, don't grab for it. Don't try to hold it. Let it pass through you. The moment you reach for it, it's gone."

She left. He heard the outer door close. The lift. Then nothing. The building was quiet. The rain tapped on the window behind the acoustic curtains, a soft percussion, barely audible through the foam.

---

He did not play again that afternoon. He sat in the chair in the centre of the soundproofed room with the violin in his lap and the bow resting against his knee and he sat in the silence and felt the residue of those six bars in his body, the way you feel the residue of a wave after it has broken over you -- the salt on your skin, the chill, the buoyancy still in your limbs.

He had done something he could not do. The paradox was simple enough that a child could have understood it: the music was better when he stopped trying to play it. The careful, managing, correcting self -- the self that had gotten him through forty-eight years of life, through a commerce degree he didn't want and a career he hadn't chosen and a marriage he couldn't leave and a fraud he couldn't stop -- that self was the obstacle. It was the wall between him and the music. It was the soundproofing, not of the room, but of the soul. All those years of accommodation, of adjustment, of being what the situation required -- all of it was a technique for preventing exactly the kind of unmediated experience he had just had. And the Chaconne had found the one passage, the D major section, the passage of transcendence, where the defensive structure gave way and something came through.

Not skill. Not mastery. Not the virtuosity that Milstein had or Hahn had or Katarina had in her restrained, precise, fourth-place way. What had come through was Bob. The Bob who had listened to himself play at fourteen in a bedroom in Cottesloe with the door closed. The Bob who had wept in a car on Rokeby Road. The Bob who had walked into a violin shop in Absam and picked up an instrument and heard a voice inside the wood that was also, impossibly, his own voice. For thirty years that Bob had been sealed inside the accommodating, performing, lying Bob, the way the Chaconne's D major section is sealed inside its D minor frame -- present, luminous, inaudible until the structure opens and the light pours through.

He lifted the violin. He did not play the D major section. He played a single open D -- the first note he had played in this room, the note he played every morning, the drone, the tonic, the ground beneath everything. The note rang in the foam-deadened air. He held it. The vibration travelled through the bridge, through the body of the instrument, through his collarbone and his jaw, into the bone of his skull, into the place where sound becomes thought becomes feeling becomes the thing that has no name, the thing that music is for.

He held the note until the bow reached the tip. Then he sat in the silence that followed.

---

That evening, he drove home along the Mitchell Freeway in the rain. The wipers beat a rhythm on the windscreen. The traffic was slow -- the Friday afternoon exodus, headlights on wet tarmac, the freeway narrowing at the Loftus Street exit, the long curve past the university and down into the western suburbs.

His phone buzzed on the passenger seat. A voicemail from Henderson, one of the fund's investors, a man who had put in $1.2 million in 2014 and had been asking pointed questions at the last quarterly meeting, questions Bob had answered with the smooth, reassuring fluency that was the most polished of his lies. Henderson wanted to talk. Henderson's tone had the quality of a man who has spoken to his accountant and his lawyer and is now making the call he has been advised to make.

Bob did not return the call. He turned off the phone.

The rain eased as he crossed the bridge at Dalkeith. By the time he reached Marine Parade, the sky was clearing from the west, the clouds breaking to reveal a strip of pale gold above the Indian Ocean. The Norfolk pines along the esplanade were black against the gold. The ocean was grey and churning, the swell pushed up by the front, the waves breaking white against the groyne.

He parked in the garage and sat with the engine off. The violin case on the seat beside him. The phone dark in his pocket. He thought about what Katarina had said. You stopped trying. You let the music play you.

He thought about his wife inside the house, pouring a glass of wine, scrolling the feed that confirmed, image by image, the life she believed she was living. He thought about Chase in his flat in Subiaco, eating takeaway in front of the football, his commerce degree half-finished, his father's trajectory ahead of him like train tracks. He thought about Shanel in her room, the door closed, the made bed, the morning runs, the part of her that was not her mother and did not yet know it.

He thought about the birthday party. Three months away. The house full of people. The caterer, the DJ, the marquee, the ocean view. And the thought that had been forming beneath the surface of his consciousness for weeks -- not as a plan but as a recognition, the way you recognise a face in a crowd before you know whose face it is -- surfaced.

He would play the Chaconne. He would play it for them. All of it. All fifteen minutes. He would stand up in front of the people who thought they knew him and play the most honest thing he had ever done, and they would hear him -- really hear him -- for the first time.

The thought was insane. He knew it was insane. He could hear the voices already -- Jeannie's incredulity, the polite bafflement of the guests, the second-hand embarrassment of watching a middle-aged stockbroker perform the most demanding piece in the violin repertoire at his own birthday party. It would be a spectacle. It would be a disaster. It would be, in the language of Jeannie's social world, a mortification.

But the thought would not go away. It sat in his chest with the weight and warmth of those six bars, the passage where he had stopped being Bob Harrington and become something true, something buried for thirty years that had finally, in a soundproofed room on St George's Terrace, on a Thursday morning in the rain, found its way to the surface.

If I can do it for eight bars, he thought. Maybe I can do it for fifteen minutes. Maybe I can play this for the people I love. Maybe they will hear what I heard.

He got out of the car. He walked through the garage and into the house, where the lights were on and the kitchen smelled of something reheated and his wife stood at the island bench with a glass of wine and her phone and the practiced composure of a woman who had not, in twenty years of marriage, heard a single true thing from the man she had married.

"You're late," she said.

"Meeting ran over," he said.

He put the violin case in the study and closed the door. He ate dinner. He watched television without seeing it. He went to bed. He lay in the dark and the D major melody played itself behind his eyelids, the soprano voice climbing, the bass descending, the hymn-like quality that Bach had written three hundred years ago in a room in Kothen after his wife had died and been buried while he was away, the passage of transcendence, the vision of light in the middle of darkness, the thing that cannot be taken back -- not by time, not by failure, not by the woman who would smash the violin, not by the ocean, not by anything.

The music played. Bob slept.
