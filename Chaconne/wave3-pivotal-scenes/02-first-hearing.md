# Chapter 17: "The Cliff Face"

The studio was above a Thai restaurant on Rokeby Road, and the stairwell always smelled of lemongrass and coconut milk, a combination that Bob would associate for the rest of his life with the sound of the violin. He climbed the stairs on a Thursday afternoon in March, six months into his lessons with Katarina, carrying the case in his left hand and a bottle of water in his right, and he was thinking about the Gigue -- the passage in the second half where the key shifted and his third finger had to stretch for a note he could not yet reach cleanly -- and he was not thinking about anything else. Not the fund. Not the fourteen messages on his phone from the associate, or the three from investors he had not returned, or the compliance document on his desk that needed a signature he kept forgetting to provide. For the forty minutes of the lesson, and the fifteen minutes before it when he warmed up in the car, and the twenty minutes after it when he sat in the car park and replayed what Katarina had told him, the fund did not exist. It was the only hour in his week when he was not afraid.

He opened the door. The studio was a single room with a north-facing window that let in the last of the afternoon light -- a warm, golden light in summer that was now, in early autumn, beginning to soften and cool. A music stand. A straight-backed chair. A metronome on the windowsill that Katarina never used because, she said, a metronome teaches you to keep time and she wanted to teach him to keep music, which was not the same thing. The walls were bare except for a framed photograph of Janacek that Bob had learned not to ask about, and a small mirror she sometimes positioned so he could watch his bowing arm. The room smelled of rosin and old carpet and, faintly, of the lemongrass rising through the floor.

Katarina was sitting with her violin on her lap, which was unusual. Normally she stood when he arrived, greeted him, asked about his practice week -- a ritual of transition, she called it, the bridge between the world outside and the world in this room. Today she was seated, her instrument across her thighs, her bow resting against the chair leg, and she was looking at him with an expression he could not read. Not the attentive, slightly amused expression of a teacher assessing a student. Something else. Something that had weight.

"Sit down," she said.

He sat. He had not opened his case. He held it on his knees like a man in a waiting room.

She lifted her violin. No preamble. No explanation. She placed it under her chin with the economy of a woman who had done this ten thousand times, settled it against her collarbone, closed her eyes for a moment -- the brief inward turn of a musician gathering the music before the bow touches the string -- and drew.

Four notes. A chord. D, A, D, F-sharp, struck from the bottom and rolled upward, the bow breaking the chord because a violin cannot sustain four notes at once and must choose, must move, must let the lower voices die so the upper ones can sing. The chord filled the small room the way a stone fills a pool -- not gradually but all at once, displacing everything. The air. The silence. The lemongrass. The traffic on Rokeby Road. The fourteen unreturned messages and the fund and the fraudulent quarterly and the compliance document and the marriage and the children and the twenty years of lying -- all of it pushed to the walls and held there by the sound. A single chord, four notes, and Bob's hands tightened on the case in his lap because the sound was doing something to his body that sound had never done before, not even in the Musikverein, not even in Franz's workshop -- the sound was reaching past his ears and his brain and into the place where his life actually lived, the sealed room, the room behind the door that had opened half an inch in Absam and had been opening, millimetre by millimetre, through six months of scales and exercises and simple Bach movements and the slow, agonising recovery of a skill he had abandoned at seventeen.

Then the descent. The bass voice stepping down -- D, C-sharp, B-flat, A -- four notes falling like a man walking down a staircase in the dark, each step deliberate, each step taking him deeper, and above it the melody, the upper voice, beginning its first long phrase, a soprano singing over the descending bass, the two voices moving in contrary motion, one falling and one rising, the counterpoint creating a space between them that was not silence but tension, the sound of two forces pulling against each other and holding.

She played eight bars. Two statements of the four-bar theme. The first statement was the theme itself -- raw, unadorned, the harmonic skeleton. The second varied it: the bass line descending the same path but the upper voice now ornamented, the melody turning back on itself in a way that was at once inevitable and surprising, as though the music knew where it was going and had decided to take a longer route.

She stopped.

The bow lifted from the string. The last note -- a D, the tonic, home -- hung in the room for a moment and then was gone, and the silence that followed was not the absence of sound but its ghost, the shape the music had made still pressing against the walls, still displacing the air, still holding the fourteen messages and the fund and the marriage at the edges of the room where they could not reach him.

Bob did not speak. He could not speak. His hands on the violin case were trembling with a violence he could not control. Something had happened. Something had happened in eight bars of music that he did not have the vocabulary to describe and that he would spend two years trying to play and the rest of his life failing to explain. The music had named him. Not his name -- not Robert James Harrington, fund manager, fraud, husband, father -- but the thing beneath the name, the thing that had been crouching in the sealed room since he was seventeen years old, the thing that had stirred when he walked into Franz's workshop and that had been growing through six months of lessons in this room above a Thai restaurant, feeding on scales and Handel minuets and simplified Bach, and that now, hearing those eight bars, had stood up to its full height and pressed its face against the door and said: let me out.

"That," Katarina said quietly, "is where we're going. Eventually."

She set her violin down. She looked at him. He understood later that she had chosen this moment deliberately -- had waited six months, had assessed his progress, his seriousness, his capacity for what she was about to unleash. She had heard him play the Gigue from Partita No. 3 and the Gavotte en Rondeau and the Sarabande from Partita No. 1, and she had heard in his playing -- in the rough intonation and the stiff bowing and the musical ear that shone through both like light through a dirty window -- something that told her he was ready. Not ready to play it. Ready to hear it. Ready to be destroyed by it. There was a difference, and Katarina knew the difference, because she had been destroyed by it herself, at fifteen, in a practice room at the Brno Conservatory, when Professor Kovar had played the Chaconne for her without warning and she had sat in the same stunned silence that Bob was sitting in now, understanding nothing except that the sound was the sound she had been listening for her entire life.

"What is it?" Bob said. His voice was wrong. Too quiet, too tight. As though the music had compressed something in his chest and the words had to squeeze past it to get out.

"The Chaconne," she said. "Bach. Partita Number Two in D minor. The last movement." She paused. Her hands rested on her violin, one on the neck and one on the body, the way a person's hands rest on a sleeping animal. "Fifteen minutes of unaccompanied solo violin. I've been working on it myself for years. I'm not ready to perform it." She looked at the window, at the last of the gold draining from the sky, the autumn light softening into the blue hour that meant the Fremantle Doctor had died and the evening was coming in from the ocean. "No one is ever really ready."

"Play it again," Bob said.

It was not a question.

She did not play it again. She looked at him for a long moment -- the same assessing look she had given him at their first lesson, when he had stood in his office on the Terrace with the violin case in his hands and the expression of a man confessing something he had not yet committed -- and she made a decision.

"Go home," she said. "Find the Milstein recording. Nathan Milstein, the 1972 recording -- not the earlier version. Listen to it alone. At night. With headphones." She paused. "Then we'll talk."

He nodded. He stood. He picked up the case and walked to the door and his legs were uncertain beneath him, as though the eight bars had shifted the floor of the room and the floor had not yet settled back. At the door he stopped.

"Katarina."

She looked up.

He wanted to say something. He did not know what. Something about the sound. Something about the way it had reached him. Something about the sealed room and the door and the thirty years and the fourteen-year-old boy who had played a Handel sonata under the lights at Scotch College and been so exposed by the experience that he had put the violin away and not taken it out again until a luthier in an Austrian village had said Play and he had played. He wanted to say that the sound she had made in this room was the sound of that boy, still alive, still waiting, and that he was forty-seven years old and his fund was insolvent and his marriage was dead and his children did not know him, and that none of it mattered, none of it had ever mattered, except the sound.

He did not say any of this. He said: "Thank you."

She nodded. The nod said: I know.

He went down the stairs. The lemongrass. The evening air. The car.

---

He sat in the car and did not start the engine.

Rokeby Road at dusk: the traffic thickening with the end of the working day, headlights coming on in the blue light, a woman in activewear walking two dogs past the Thai restaurant, the neon sign of a bottle shop glowing green and red on the corner. Bob sat in the driver's seat with his hands on the steering wheel and his eyes open and his mind entirely elsewhere. His mind was in the room upstairs. His mind was still sitting in the chair with the case on his knees, hearing the four-note chord, the descent, the second statement with its ornamental turn, the silence after.

His hands on the steering wheel were trembling. He looked at them. They were his hands -- large, soft, the hands of a man who handled paper and telephones and other people's money -- and they were shaking with a fine, continuous vibration that seemed to come from somewhere deeper than his muscles, from the bones, from the marrow, from whatever part of the body stores the memory of sounds it has heard and cannot forget.

He took out his phone. He opened YouTube. He typed, with fingers that fumbled on the screen: Bach Chaconne Milstein 1972.

The search returned a video. A black-and-white photograph of an older man with a violin. Nathan Milstein. The duration: 14:23. He plugged in his earbuds -- the cheap white ones that came with the phone, not proper headphones, the left one slightly loose in his ear -- and pressed play.

The opening chord.

The same chord Katarina had played, the same four notes -- D, A, D, F-sharp -- but different. Different in the way that a photograph of a mountain is different from the mountain. Milstein's chord was not louder or softer or more technically accomplished than Katarina's. It was deeper. It came from further away. It came from a man who had been playing this piece for fifty years and who no longer played it the way you play a piece of music but the way you breathe, the way you walk, the way you live inside a structure so familiar that you have forgotten it is a structure and experience it simply as the shape of being alive. The chord rolled from the bass to the treble and each note rang with a warmth that the cheap earbuds could not diminish, and then the descent -- the same descent, D, C-sharp, B-flat, A -- and above it Milstein's soprano voice, singing the melody with an evenness and a patience that made Katarina's version, accomplished as it was, sound like a sketch for a painting that Milstein had completed.

Bob listened. He did not move. The four-bar theme stated itself, and then the first variation: the bass line still descending but the upper voice now running in faster notes, sixteenths, the melody becoming more elaborate, the ornamentation thickening like a vine growing around a column. Then the second variation: the rhythm shifting, the bow working harder, the chords beginning to assert themselves, each one a small collision of notes that resolved into harmony. The music was building. Not in volume -- Milstein kept the dynamics restrained, the bow close to the fingerboard, the sound contained -- but in density. More notes. More voices. More meaning per bar, as though the music were a language and each variation were a sentence that contained more words than the last, and the paragraph was growing, and the essay was growing, and somewhere in the distance -- bar thirty, bar fifty, bar eighty -- the essay was becoming a book and the book was becoming a library and the library was becoming a world.

He lost track of time. He lost track of the street. The woman with the dogs was gone. The traffic had thinned. The sky had darkened from blue to indigo to the deep purple-black of a Perth autumn evening, and the bottle shop's neon was brighter now, casting its green-red light across the bonnet of his car, and he did not notice. He was inside the music. Not listening to it -- inside it, the way you are inside a building, surrounded by walls and ceilings and corridors, moving through rooms that open onto other rooms, each one different and each one connected, the architecture holding him.

The first section built to a climax. The chords thickened, the double-stops multiplied, Milstein's bow pressing into the strings with an increasing urgency, the voices climbing, the tension accumulating like water behind a dam. And then -- bar eighty-nine, ninety, somewhere in there, he did not know the numbers, did not know the structure, knew only what his body was telling him -- the bariolage. The bow began to cross the strings in rapid alternation, the same two notes repeated obsessively, the melody trapped on a single pitch while the harmony shifted beneath it, a shimmering, drilling, relentless figure that sounded like a machine, like a prayer, like a man saying the same word over and over until the word lost its meaning and became pure sound and the pure sound became something larger than meaning, something that precedes language and survives it. The bariolage drilled into him. It found the sealed room and pressed against the door not with force but with repetition, the same two notes, the same crossing, the same obsessive return, and he felt the door bending inward.

And then the section ended. The music descended into its final cadence, the chords darkening, the soprano falling, and a silence -- a silence that lasted less than a second but felt like a held breath, the entire piece gathering itself for what came next.

Bar 133. The key changed.

D major.

He did not know what had happened. He did not know the technical name for what he was hearing. He knew only that the music had shifted -- not gradually, not through modulation, but simply, completely, the way dawn is not a slow brightening but a moment, a specific instant when the light changes and the world is different. The minor third became a major third. The F-natural became an F-sharp. And the sound -- the sound that had been dark and dense and grief-stricken and relentless for nine minutes -- opened.

It opened the way a cathedral opens when you step through the door from the street. It opened the way the sky opens when a cloud bank moves and the sun finds the ground. It opened the way a life opens when you stop pretending it is someone else's and accept that it is yours. The D major section was quiet. Milstein played it quieter than the minor section, which was wrong, it should have been louder, it should have announced itself, but it didn't. It arrived. The light arrived. It did not shout. It just came.

Something broke in Bob.

He wept. Not the silent tears of the Musikverein, the tears that came without his permission and ran down his face while he sat motionless in a velvet seat. This was different. This was a sound -- a low, involuntary sound from somewhere in his chest, a sound he had not made since childhood, the sound of a man whose body is expelling something it has carried too long. He pressed his forehead against the steering wheel. His shoulders shook. The earbuds were still in. Milstein was still playing -- the D major variations, each one a hymn, each one a statement of something that Bob could not name but that felt like the opposite of everything he had done with his life. The music was kind. That was the word. In the midst of the greatest piece of music he had ever heard, in the midst of the most technically demanding passage of the most technically demanding work in the solo violin repertoire, the music was kind. It was gentle. It sang to him the way you sing to a child who has woken in the dark -- not to explain the dark but to say: I am here. The dark is not everything. There is also this.

He wept through the D major section. He wept through the transition back to D minor -- the return, the darkening, the grief reasserting itself over the hymn. He wept through the final bariolage, which was different from the first -- more desperate, more searching, the bow crossing the strings with an urgency that felt less like prayer and more like a man running out of time. He wept through the final statement of the theme -- the four bars, the descent, the soprano singing for the last time over the falling bass -- and through the final chord, which Milstein played with a softness that was almost unbearable, a dying fall, a door closing gently on a room you have been allowed to visit but cannot stay in.

Silence.

He sat in the silence for a long time. The earbuds fed him nothing. The screen had gone dark. Outside, Rokeby Road had emptied. The Thai restaurant had turned on its interior lights and he could see, through the window, a couple at a table, their faces lit by a candle, their hands touching across the plates. He watched them without seeing them. He was still inside the music. The music had ended and he was still inside it, the way you are still inside a dream for the first minutes after waking, the room around you less real than the room you have left.

He pressed play again.

---

He drove home along Marine Parade. The ocean on his right, black and moving, the Norfolk pines silhouetted against a sky still faintly lit in the west. He did not remember starting the car. He did not remember the drive from Subiaco -- the left turn onto Stirling Highway, the roundabout at Claremont, the long straight run through Cottesloe. He was driving on the muscle memory of ten thousand commutes, his body performing the sequence of turns and stops and accelerations that the route required while his mind replayed the Chaconne, not the recording but the memory of the recording, the ghost of the sound, the four-note chord sounding over and over in his skull.

He pulled into the driveway. The house was lit -- the kitchen, Shanel's bedroom, the blue glow of a television from the living room. He turned off the engine. He sat in the car. He sat in the car for ten minutes, or fifteen, or twenty, looking at the house he had bought with money that was not his, the house where his wife slept in a separate bedroom and his children lived their separate lives and no one knew -- no one had ever known -- that the man who pulled into this driveway each evening and ate dinner and watched television and went to bed and got up and drove to the Terrace and answered phones and told lies was not a stockbroker, not a fund manager, not a husband, not a provider, but a boy with a violin in a school assembly hall, playing a Handel sonata under the lights, disappearing into the sound, and being dragged back by a world that had no use for what he'd found.

He went inside. Jeannie was watching something on the television -- a renovation show, the kind where attractive people demolished perfectly adequate kitchens and replaced them with other perfectly adequate kitchens and called it transformation. She did not look up. The kitchen bench held an open bottle of sauvignon blanc, two-thirds empty, the condensation forming a pale ring on the stone.

"I'm going to do some work," Bob said.

"Mm."

He went to the study. The study that was not really a study -- that was the room where Jeannie stored overflow clothes on a rolling rack and where Bob kept the computer he used for the fund and, recently, for the only work that mattered. He closed the door. He sat at the desk. The room was dark except for the glow of the screen when he opened the laptop. On the desk, pushed to the corner: a compliance document, unsigned. A letter from the fund's accountant. A yellow Post-it in the associate's handwriting: "Three calls today -- please return." He did not look at any of it.

He opened the laptop. He found the headphones -- Jeannie's Bose, noise-cancelling, bought for a Bali flight and abandoned in a drawer. They fit too tightly across the crown of his head and pressed against his temples with a low, constant ache that he would later be unable to separate from the music itself, as though Bach required a certain amount of pain as the price of admission.

He opened YouTube. He searched: Bach Chaconne Milstein 1972.

He pressed play. Not for the first time. Not for the second. But this time was different from the car. In the car he had been ambushed. The music had hit him before he knew what it was, before he could prepare or defend or contextualise. Now he was seeking it. Now he was going in with his eyes open, the way a man who has been knocked down by a wave goes back into the surf, not to be knocked down again but to learn the shape of the wave, to understand the force that had overwhelmed him, to find, inside the chaos, a structure he could hold.

The chord. The descent. The first variation. He listened with the attention of a man studying a language he needs to learn. He did not have the vocabulary -- he did not know what a bariolage was, or a double-stop, or a variation, or a ground bass. He heard sounds. He heard patterns. He heard the bass voice descending and the soprano voice rising and the two of them creating, in their contrary motion, a space that was not empty but full, full of something he could feel but not name, something that lived in the tension between the falling and the rising, the grief and the hope, the weight and the lift.

The D minor section played. He listened to it all the way through. And this time he heard something he had not heard in the car: voices. Not one melody over a bass line, but voices -- three, sometimes four, moving independently, weaving through each other, the way a conversation has multiple speakers who interrupt and defer and overlap and finish each other's sentences. He could not track them all. The polyphony was too dense, the writing too complex, and his ear was too untrained. But he heard them. He heard the moments when a voice emerged from the texture and sang a phrase that was its own, that belonged to no other voice, and then submerged again into the harmony, and another voice rose. The music was not a solo. It was a choir. One instrument, one player, alone in a room, producing the sound of a congregation.

Bar 133. The shift to D major. He braced himself, knowing what was coming, and it did not help. It arrived with the same devastating gentleness, the same sense of a door opening into light, and his eyes filled again -- not with the violent, chest-deep weeping of the car but with a slow, steady welling that blurred the screen and ran down his face and he did not wipe the tears because his hands were resting on the desk, palms down, and he could not move them, would not move them, because moving them would mean leaving the music and he could not leave the music, not yet, not yet.

The D major section played. Hymn-like. Pastoral. A series of variations that had the quality of a man walking through a garden -- not hurrying, not lost, simply walking, looking at what was there, touching the leaves, feeling the sun. Milstein played it with a simplicity that was the opposite of simple, each note placed with the precision of a man who had spent fifty years deciding where to put it and had arrived, finally, at the only possible place. The section built slowly, the variations growing in complexity, the double-stops climbing, and then the return. The shift back to D minor. The garden door closing. The light withdrawing. The grief returning, but different now -- deepened by the memory of the major section, by the knowledge that the light existed even if you could not stay in it.

The final bariolage. The shimmering, obsessive string-crossing, the two notes alternating with a speed that seemed to exceed the capacity of a human arm, the sound drilling into the air, and beneath it the melody, struggling upward, straining against the pattern that held it. The bariolage was the most beautiful and the most terrible thing in the piece. It was the sound of a man trying to say something he cannot say, trying to reach something he cannot reach, the bow moving back and forth across the strings like a hand knocking on a door that will not open.

The final chord. D minor. The piece ended where it began.

Silence.

He pressed play again.

---

At some point -- midnight, one, later -- he switched from Milstein to Hilary Hahn. The shock of her. A different universe. Where Milstein was autumnal, valedictory, the playing of an old man making peace with something, Hahn was ablaze. She was seventeen on this recording -- seventeen, the age Bob had been when he put the violin away -- and she played the Chaconne with a technical ferocity that made the notes not flow but burn, each one struck with a clarity that was almost violent, the sound cutting into the air with a brightness that hurt, that made Bob's chest constrict, because this girl, this child, was doing with ease what Milstein had done with a lifetime of accumulated wisdom, and the effect was not lesser but different -- not a man looking back on a life but a girl hurling herself at one, and the Chaconne absorbed both, because the Chaconne absorbed everything, because the piece was not a container but an ocean, and every performer who entered it brought their own weather and the ocean accepted it all.

He found a notebook. Not the fund notebook, not the leather-bound diary Jeannie had bought him at Christmas that he had never written in. A lined Moleskine from the newsagent in Claremont, bought the next day. He wrote:

*Milstein: sounds like remembering. Hahn: sounds like discovering. Same notes. Different lives.*

He listened to Perlman. The tone like warm honey. Generous, indulgent, the sound so beautiful it became its own subject, and Bob found himself listening to the violin rather than the music, which was a distinction he could not have made a week ago and that the Chaconne was teaching him to make. He wrote:

*Perlman makes you love the violin. Milstein makes you love the music. Not the same thing.*

He listened to Heifetz. The terror of Heifetz. The speed, the ferocity, the ruthless forward motion of a man who played the Chaconne the way a general took a city -- total commitment, no mercy, no interest in whether the conquered territory loved him for it. The technical command was absolute. Every note in tune, every shift invisible, every chord voiced with crystalline precision. And yet something was missing. The kindness. The kindness that Milstein had found in the D major section, the gentleness that had broken Bob in the car -- Heifetz did not have it, or did not allow himself to have it, and the absence made the performance brilliant and cold, like a diamond that catches every wavelength of light and returns none of its warmth.

He wrote:

*Heifetz is terrifying. This is what it sounds like when perfection is not enough.*

He listened to Szeryng. The middle ground. Balanced, humane, the phrasing shaped with an intelligence that was neither Milstein's world-weariness nor Hahn's fire but something calmer, something that took the music at its own pace and did not impose a life upon it. Bob listened to Szeryng and understood, for the first time, that the Chaconne was not a fixed object. It was not a text that said one thing. It was a mirror. Each performer looked into it and saw their own face, and the piece reflected them back, and what you heard when you listened was not Bach but the person playing Bach, their life, their grief, their hope, their accumulated experience of being alive, filtered through 256 bars of music that had the capacity to hold all of it, every human life that had ever been lived, because the structure was large enough -- the ground bass descending, the variations rising, the minor yielding to the major and the major returning to the minor -- to contain the entire range of what it meant to be a person.

He turned the page. He wrote:

*What am I hearing? Not the notes. The person. Each recording is the same 256 bars and each one is a different life.*

And then, below it, in handwriting that was shakier than the rest, because his hand was trembling again, the fine, continuous vibration that had begun in Katarina's studio and that he was beginning to understand was not nervousness but recognition:

*The D major section. Bar 133. This is the moment. Every recording, every time. Like stepping from a dark room into sunlight. Milstein plays it quieter than anyone else. This is wrong -- it should be louder. But it is right. The light doesn't shout. The light just arrives.*

He was weeping again. He had not noticed when the tears began. They came without sound, without the chest-deep convulsion of the car. They came the way rain comes in certain seasons -- steady, continuous, the body releasing something it could no longer hold. The tears were not for the music. The tears were not for himself. The tears were for the thirty years he did not play. For the boy in the school assembly hall. For the fund and the fraud and the marriage and the children who had never heard their father make a sound that was true. For Jeannie in her separate bedroom with the door closed and the sauvignon blanc and the sertraline and the Instagram and the twenty years of performing a life that was nobody's. For Chase on the boat, for Shanel deleting photographs that were beautiful because they did not match her feed. For the life he had built, the whole gleaming fraudulent edifice of it -- the house on Marine Parade, the office on the Terrace, the German car and the yacht club membership and the tailored chinos -- all of it a lie, all of it a sealed room inside which the real thing, the only real thing, had been crouching in the dark for thirty years, waiting.

The Chaconne had named it. The Chaconne had found the sealed room and opened the door and said: this. This is what you buried. This is what you lost. This is what you have been mourning without knowing you were mourning. And here it is. Still alive. Still waiting. Still yours, if you have the courage to pick it up.

Around 2 AM, the study door opened. Jeannie stood in the doorway in her robe, her face creased with sleep, her hair flat on one side. The robe was silk, pale blue, a birthday gift from Shanel that Jeannie had said was "lovely" in the tone she used for gifts that were adequate but not what she would have chosen.

"Are you coming to bed?"

He took off the headphones. The silence of the house rushed in -- the hum of the refrigerator, the distant murmur of the ocean through the walls, the ticking of the hallway clock that Jeannie's mother had given them and that Bob had never liked and had never said so.

"Soon," he said.

She looked at the screen. Multiple browser tabs, all YouTube, all violin performances. The Moleskine open on the desk, his handwriting visible, the words illegible from the doorway but their existence registering -- Bob writing things down, Bob taking notes, Bob engaged in some private project that she had not been informed of and that she categorised, in the instantaneous filing system of a woman who had learned to assess threats by their proximity to her interests, as harmless. Not an affair. Not a financial crisis she didn't already suspect. Just Bob being Bob -- up late, fixated on something pointless, the violin hobby that she had tolerated the way she tolerated his taste in restaurants and his fondness for the dog they no longer had.

She shook her head. The gesture was small, reflexive, the muscular memory of twenty years of finding her husband mildly disappointing. She closed the door.

Bob put the headphones back on. He pressed play.

He stayed up until dawn. He listened to the Chaconne eleven times. By the fifth listening he had stopped weeping. By the seventh he was no longer hearing the piece from the outside -- no longer a listener sitting at a desk -- but from somewhere inside it, as though the variations were rooms and he was moving through them, and each room had a different light and a different temperature and each one showed him something about his own life that he had not seen before. The D minor opening was the fund. The building complexity of the first section was the lie, growing, thickening, the voices multiplying until the texture was so dense that the original theme was almost lost beneath its own elaboration. The bariolage was the obsessive repetition of a man maintaining a fraud -- the same two notes, over and over, the same lie told to the same people, the shimmering surface masking the static desperation underneath. And the D major section. Bar 133. The shift. The light.

The D major section was Absam. It was the workshop and the smell of spruce and the luthier's hands and the broken violin and the word Seele. It was Katarina's studio and the metronome on the windowsill and the sound of the Gigue coming together under his fingers. It was the feeling of the bow on the string, the vibration travelling through the wood into his jaw, the moment when the note opened and the sound came back to him and he was not playing at the instrument but into it, and the instrument answered.

The D major section was what lived underneath.

At some point the sky outside the window turned from black to grey to the pale, clean blue of a Perth autumn morning. The ocean became visible -- a dark line, then a silver line, then the full expanse of the Indian Ocean stretching west to Africa, the water catching the first light. He took off the headphones. His head ached from the pressure and the hours and the crying and the exhaustion. His eyes were swollen. His mouth tasted of nothing, of the stale air of a room where a man had been sitting all night without eating or drinking.

He closed the laptop. He sat in the silence. The silence after the Chaconne was not empty. It was full -- the way the silence after a conversation is full of everything that was said, the words still resonating in the air. He sat in the full silence and listened to it and the sun came up over the Indian Ocean and the house began its morning sounds -- the refrigerator cycling, the pipes clicking, the distant thud of Jeannie's alarm.

He opened the violin case. Franz's violin -- the one from Absam, restored now, the cracks repaired, the soundpost reset, the bridge carved and fitted, the strings new and tuned to concert pitch. The varnish gleamed in the early light, the reddish-amber depth that had been hidden under grime now visible, luminous, the surface catching the dawn and holding it the way it had held the workshop light in Absam, the way it had held, perhaps, every light that had ever fallen on it for three hundred years. The scroll watched him with its carved eye.

He lifted the violin. He placed it under his chin. He tightened the bow. He rosined the hair. He opened the score on the desk -- the Chaconne, the first page, the opening bars. He looked at the notation. Four notes stacked vertically: D, A, D, F-sharp. A chord. The chord that had changed everything.

He placed the bow on the D string. He pressed his left hand into the chord shape -- first finger on A, third finger on D, fourth finger on F-sharp, the stretch extreme, the tendons of his hand protesting, the fingers splayed across the fingerboard in a position his hand had never held and did not want to hold.

He drew the bow.

The sound was atrocious. The four notes that should have rung as a unified harmonic statement -- the same chord that Milstein had rolled from bass to treble with the unhurried authority of a man opening a door, the same chord that Katarina had struck in the studio with the clean precision of a professional -- came out of Bob's violin as a strangled, scraping crunch. The bow skidded across three strings simultaneously, the pressure wrong, the angle wrong, the left hand unable to maintain the chord shape, the fourth finger collapsing under the stretch and slipping flat, the F-sharp becoming something that was neither F-sharp nor F-natural but a queasy, indeterminate pitch that made the chord not dissonant but meaningless. The sound was not music. It was the sound of a man failing.

He tried again. He reset the bow. He reformed the chord, pressing his fingers harder, feeling the steel strings bite into the pads of his fingertips, the calluses not yet thick enough to protect him. He drew the bow. The sound was worse -- the increased pressure making the bow dig into the string and produce a scratchy, forced tone, the chord crunching, the voices fighting each other instead of blending.

He tried a third time. And a fourth. And a fifth. Each attempt was different and each was terrible. The bow pressure too light, the chord ghostly and thin. The bow pressure too heavy, the chord grinding and harsh. The angle wrong, the hair catching the D and A strings but missing the higher two. The left hand cramping, the stretch too wide, the fourth finger sliding sharp.

The sixth time, something happened. Not mastery. Not competence. Not even adequacy. But the bow caught the lower two strings cleanly, and the roll upward -- the break, the moment when the bow lifts from the lower strings and falls onto the upper ones -- had a quality that was, for a fraction of a second, not wrong. The D and the A rang, and then the upper D and the F-sharp rang, and for less than a heartbeat the four notes existed in the same air, the chord shape visible in the sound, and Bob heard it -- heard, through the noise and the error and the cramp in his hand, the ghost of what the chord was supposed to be. The ghost of what Milstein had played. The ghost of what Bach had written.

It lasted less than a second. Then the F-sharp slipped and the chord collapsed and the sound died and he was standing in the study in his socks with his hand aching and the morning light on the ocean and the house waking up around him.

He lowered the violin. He looked at the score. Two hundred and fifty-six bars. Sixty-four variations. Fifteen minutes of music. He could not play the first bar.

He did not know the mathematics of what he was attempting. He did not know that the Chaconne was Henle Level 9, the maximum difficulty rating. He did not know that professional soloists prepared it for months. He did not know that his childhood Grade 4 was to the Chaconne what a recreational jog around the block was to an Olympic marathon. He did not know the word bariolage. He did not know what a ground bass was, or a variation, or a polyphonic line. He did not know that Brahms had written to Clara Schumann that the piece would have driven him out of his mind, or that Joshua Bell had called it one of the greatest achievements of any man in history. He did not know any of this. He would learn it. He would learn all of it, in the weeks and months ahead, in the soundproofed room on the Terrace, in the lessons with Katarina, in the late nights with headphones and the Moleskine and the desperate, obsessive study of a man who has found his life's work at forty-seven and understands, with the terrible clarity of the latecomer, that there is not enough time.

But this morning, standing in the study with the violin under his chin and the ocean outside and the ruined chord still ringing in his ears, he did not need to know any of it. He knew one thing. He knew it with the certainty of a drowning man who has seen the shore and who understands that the distance is impossible and that the current is against him and that his arms are not strong enough and that none of this matters, because the shore is there, and it is real, and it is the only direction that exists.

He would play this piece. This one piece. Not the Gigue, not the Gavotte, not the simplified movements Katarina had been guiding him through for six months. The Chaconne. The whole of it. The 256 bars, the fifteen minutes, the three sections, the sixty-four variations, the opening chord to the final cadence. He would play it because it was the sound of his life -- the grief and the hope and the sealed room and the light that arrived without shouting -- and because playing it was the only thing he had ever wanted to do that was not for Jeannie, not for the fund, not for the investors, not for the children, not for anyone or anything except the sound itself, the vibration of the string under his finger, the note entering the air and the air returning it, the conversation between the player and the instrument that Franz had called Seele and that Bob, standing in the morning light with his aching hand and his swollen eyes, called the only true thing.

He raised the bow. He placed it on the D string. He drew the opening chord.

It was terrible.

He drew it again.
