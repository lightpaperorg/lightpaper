CHACONNE — CHAPTER EPIGRAPHS
==============================


1. THE VIEW FROM MARINE PARADE

"The voice of the sea is seductive, never ceasing, whispering, clamoring,
murmuring, inviting the soul to wander for a spell in abysses of solitude."

— Kate Chopin, The Awakening (1899)

[Chopin's novel ends with her protagonist walking into the Gulf of Mexico.
The resonance with the novel's final chapters is deliberate but not obvious
at the opening — it reads as atmosphere first, foreshadowing only in retrospect.]


2. NEDLANDS, 1987

"Music creates order out of chaos: for rhythm imposes unanimity upon the
divergent, melody imposes continuity upon the disjointed, and harmony
imposes compatibility upon the incongruous."

— Yehudi Menuhin

[Menuhin was a child prodigy violinist. The quote captures what music offers
a child: a world that makes sense when the real one doesn't.]


3. GAGGLE

"As we ascend the social ladder, viciousness wears a thicker mask."

— Erich Fromm

[Short, sharp, and exactly right for a chapter about the cruelty embedded
in social performance. Fromm was a social psychologist and philosopher.]


4. FUND

"The sad truth is that most evil is done by people who never make up their
minds to be good or evil."

— Hannah Arendt, The Life of the Mind (1978)

[Arendt's insight into the banality of evil — not grand villainy but the
failure to think — is perfect for white-collar fraud. Bob never decided
to be a criminal; he simply never decided not to be one.]


5. ROTTNEST

"I was much too far out all my life
And not waving but drowning."

— Stevie Smith, "Not Waving but Drowning" (1957)

[A son watching his father without understanding him. The poem's central
conceit — that what looks like confidence is actually desperation — maps
onto a boy's misreading of masculine performance. Also foreshadows the ending.]


6. TERRACE

"Evil appears as good in the minds of those whom god leads to destruction."

— Sophocles, Antigone (c. 441 BC)

[The classical Greek insight about hubris: the gods first cloud your
judgment, making ruin look like triumph. Bob's misplaced pride in his
son while the fund collapses around him.]


7. WHAT JEANNIE DOESN'T KNOW

"A great deal of intelligence can be invested in ignorance when the need
for illusion is deep."

— Saul Bellow, To Jerusalem and Back (1976)

[A marriage sustained by deliberate not-knowing. Bellow captures how
intelligence doesn't protect against self-deception — it enables it.]


8. TRIP

"The only true voyage of discovery would be not to visit strange lands but
to possess other eyes, to behold the universe through the eyes of another."

— Marcel Proust, The Prisoner (1923)

[Bob is leaving for Europe, but the real journey will be internal. Proust's
insight — that travel's value is perceptual, not geographical — sets up
the transformation ahead.]


9. KOTHEN, JULY 1720

"After thirteen years of blissful married life, the misfortune overtook him,
in the year 1720, upon his return to Cothen from a journey with his Prince
to Carlsbad, of finding her dead and buried, although he had left her hale
and hearty on his departure."

— Carl Philipp Emanuel Bach, Obituary of J.S. Bach (1754)

[Bach's own son describing the moment. No commentary needed — the bare
facts carry all the grief. The only epigraph drawn from historical record
rather than literary reflection.]


10. LONDON, PARIS

"We shall not cease from exploration
And the end of all our exploring
Will be to arrive where we started
And know the place for the first time."

— T.S. Eliot, "Little Gidding," Four Quartets (1942)

[Travel as a circuit back to the self. Eliot's most famous passage, but
earned here because Bob's European journey will eventually return him to
Perth utterly changed.]


11. ABSAM

"Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity."

— Simone Weil, letter to Joe Bousquet (1942)

[The luthier's gift is not just the violin — it is the act of truly seeing
Bob. Weil's insight reframes generosity as perception rather than material
giving. Grace as being noticed.]


12. VIENNA

"The beauty of the world, which is so soon to perish, has two edges, one
of laughter, one of anguish, cutting the heart asunder."

— Virginia Woolf, A Room of One's Own (1929)

[Weeping at a concert. Beauty that wounds. Woolf knew this duality
intimately — the ecstasy and the devastation arriving simultaneously.
She, too, walked into water.]


13. HOME

"There is nothing like returning to a place that remains unchanged to find
the ways in which you yourself have altered."

— Nelson Mandela, Long Walk to Freedom (1994)

[The simplest and most universal articulation of the experience of
homecoming after transformation. Perth hasn't changed; Bob has.]


14. RESTORATION

"There is a crack in everything. That's how the light gets in."

— Leonard Cohen, "Anthem" (1992)

[A broken violin made whole. The crack is not concealed but incorporated
into the instrument's new identity. Cohen's line works for objects,
people, and lives.]


15. FIRST LESSON

"Do not take up music unless you would rather die than not do so."

— Nadia Boulanger, advice to her pupils
    (quoted in Alan Kendall, The Tender Tyrant, 1976)

[The greatest music teacher of the twentieth century. Her standard was
absolute: music is not a hobby but a vocation. The teacher in this chapter
recognises that Bob has crossed that threshold.]


16. CREMONA, 1715

"Patience is also a form of action."

— Auguste Rodin

[Stradivari at his bench, selecting wood, applying varnish in dozens of
coats over months. Rodin — another maker whose hands created things that
outlived him — understood that waiting is not passivity but craft.]


17. CLIFF FACE

"On one stave, for a small instrument, the man writes a whole world of the
deepest thoughts and most powerful feelings. If I were to imagine how I
might have made, conceived the piece, I know for certain that the
overwhelming excitement and awe would have driven me mad."

— Johannes Brahms, letter to Clara Schumann (1877),
    on Bach's Chaconne

[The single most famous statement about the Chaconne, from one of the
greatest composers who ever lived. Brahms is describing exactly what Bob
feels: the piece is impossible, and he must attempt it anyway.]


18. RECORDINGS

"After silence, that which comes nearest to expressing the inexpressible
is music."

— Aldous Huxley, "The Rest is Silence,"
    Music at Night and Other Essays (1931)

[A chapter about listening to recordings — Stern, Milstein, Heifetz,
Hilary Hahn. Bob is researching the piece's history and performers.
Huxley places music at the boundary of the sayable.]


19. SOUNDPROOFED ROOM

"Don't only practise your art, but force your way into its secrets; art
deserves that, for it and knowledge can raise man to the Divine."

— Ludwig van Beethoven, letter to Emilie M. (1812)

[Beethoven writing to a young musician. The language of forcing,
deserving, raising — it captures obsessive practice as a spiritual
discipline, not mere repetition.]


20. INJURIES

"Perhaps all the dragons in our lives are princesses who are only waiting
to see us act, just once, with beauty and courage. Perhaps everything
terrible is in its deepest being something helpless that wants help from us."

— Rainer Maria Rilke, Letters to a Young Poet (1904)

[Tendonitis as dragon. Forced rest as the thing that looks like an obstacle
but is actually the teacher. Rilke reframes suffering as an invitation,
which is exactly what Bob's injury turns out to be.]


21. BUSKING

"Each passerby had a quick choice to make, one familiar to commuters in any
urban area where the occasional street performer is part of the cityscape:
Do you stop and listen?"

— Gene Weingarten, "Pearls Before Breakfast,"
    The Washington Post (2007)

[The Joshua Bell experiment: a world-class violinist plays incognito in a
metro station and almost no one stops. Bob does the same thing on the
streets of Perth. The parallel is explicit in the novel, so the epigraph
earns its place.]


22. GAP

"In the midst of winter, I found there was, within me, an invincible summer."

— Albert Camus, "Return to Tipasa" (1954)

[The breakthrough: the music plays the player. Camus found his invincible
summer after years of exile and war. Bob finds his after decades of
hollowness. The gap between inner and outer life is at its widest.]


23. AUTHENTICATION

"Artistic growth is, more than it is anything else, a refining of the sense
of truthfulness."

— Willa Cather, The Song of the Lark (1915)

[Two authentications happening simultaneously: experts examining whether
the violin is a Stradivarius, and Bob's life being examined by ASIC.
Cather's line cuts both ways — what is genuine, in art and in life?]


24. INVITATION

"There are more things likely to frighten us than there are to crush us;
we suffer more often in imagination than in reality."

— Seneca, Moral Letters to Lucilius (c. 65 AD)

[Bob has decided to play the Chaconne at his birthday party. The ASIC
letter has arrived. Hope and dread in equal measure. Seneca reminds us
that anticipation is usually worse than the event — but in Bob's case,
reality will prove worse than anything he imagined.]


25. KOTHEN, AUTUMN 1720

"Without music, life would be a mistake."

— Friedrich Nietzsche, Twilight of the Idols (1889)

[Bach completes the Sei Solo — "You Are Alone." He does not know what he
has written. Nietzsche's aphorism, placed here, gains weight: for Bach,
music was not decoration but the thing that made existence bearable
after Maria Barbara's death.]


26. PARTY

"Every artist makes himself born. It is very much harder than the other
time, and longer."

— Willa Cather, in Willa Cather in Person:
    Interviews, Speeches, and Letters (1986)

[The morning of Bob's fiftieth birthday. The Strad is confirmed. The
party is being set up. Bob has spent a year remaking himself, and
tonight he will be born as a musician in front of eighty people.]


27. CHACONNE

"Music is a higher revelation than all wisdom and philosophy."

— Ludwig van Beethoven, as reported by Bettina von Arnim
    in a letter to Goethe (1810)

[THE PERFORMANCE. The novel's climax. No qualification, no irony — just
Beethoven's absolute claim for music's supremacy. The epigraph steps
aside and lets the chapter do its work.]


28. GOSSIP

"For in other ways a woman is full of fear, defenseless, dreads the sight
of cold steel; but when once she is wronged in the matter of love, no
other soul can hold so many thoughts of blood."

— Euripides, Medea (431 BC)

[Jeannie hears about the "affair" and the fund collapse simultaneously.
Euripides understood that the most dangerous moment is not the act of
betrayal but the moment the betrayed person learns of it. Medea's face,
like Jeannie's, does not change.]


29. SMASHING

"All human nature vigorously resists grace because grace changes us and
the change is painful."

— Flannery O'Connor, The Habit of Being (1979)

[Jeannie destroys the violin. O'Connor's insight: grace is violent, and
we fight it. The violin was grace made physical — an unearned gift that
changed Bob — and Jeannie's destruction of it is human nature's
resistance to transformation it cannot control.]


30. BEACH

"The mass of men lead lives of quiet desperation."

— Henry David Thoreau, Walden (1854)

[Bob walks into the ocean. Thoreau's most famous line — written in a
chapter called "Economy" — is about the life Bob lived before the
violin. He escapes it, but we don't know whether he escapes into death
or into something else. The ambiguity is the point.]


31. RECORDING

"Heard melodies are sweet, but those unheard
Are sweeter."

— John Keats, "Ode on a Grecian Urn" (1819)

[The coda. Katarina alone with the phone recording. She plays the Chaconne
herself. The novel ends on silence — and Keats tells us that the music
we cannot hear is the most beautiful of all. The unheard melody is Bob's
absence, the recording's echo, and the silence after the final note.]
