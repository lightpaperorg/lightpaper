# CHACONNE — Character Biographies

---

## 1. BOB (Robert James Harrington)

Robert James Harrington was born in April 1976 at King Edward Memorial Hospital, the only son of Jim Harrington, a stockbroker with Patersons Securities, and Wendy Harrington (nee Collins), who had been a legal secretary before marriage and never went back. He grew up in a four-bedroom limestone cottage on Pearse Street, Cottesloe, three blocks from the beach — a house that was comfortable but not grand, a distinction that would matter more to Jeannie than it ever did to him. Jim drove a Volvo wagon and wore a tie every day of his working life. Wendy kept the garden and played bridge on Thursdays. They were decent, mildly conservative, entirely unexceptional people who loved their son in the only way they knew: by assuming he would become his father.

Bob entered Scotch College in Year 5, riding the bus from Cottesloe each morning. He was an adequate student — not stupid, never brilliant. He played First XVIII football without distinction, swam at interhouse carnivals, sat in the middle of every classroom. His school reports said things like "Robert applies himself well" and "a pleasure to have in class," which is what teachers write when a boy gives them no trouble and no reason to remember him. He had friends. He went to birthday parties and sleepovers. He was liked in the way that inoffensive, good-looking boys are liked — welcomed everywhere, essential nowhere.

The violin came in Year 7. Scotch offered instrumental lessons, and Wendy thought it would round him out. Bob chose the violin because the piano was too heavy to carry and the trumpet seemed aggressive. He was not naturally gifted. His intonation was unreliable; his bowing arm was stiff. But something happened when he played that did not happen anywhere else in his life: he disappeared. Not into competence or achievement, but into the sound itself, into the vibration of the string under his finger, into the way a phrase could open like a door into a room he hadn't known existed. He reached Grade 4 by Year 10. His teacher, a patient woman named Mrs. Albrecht, told Wendy he had "a genuine musical ear but not the discipline to develop it." This was accurate. Bob practiced when he felt like it, which was often, but always alone, always with the door closed, always stopping when someone knocked. He played at one school concert, a Handel sonata, and felt so exposed under the lights that he never performed again. The violin went into its case after Year 12 and did not come out for twenty-seven years.

He enrolled in a Bachelor of Commerce at UWA because Jim said, "You'll always have a career," and Bob had no counter-argument. He was not an intellectual. He did not read widely or think abstractly. But he had a quality that would serve him well and then destroy him: he was likeable. People trusted Bob. His face was open, his handshake firm, his laugh generous. He remembered names and birthdays. He asked about people's children. This is the raw material of a successful broker, and Jim knew it.

After graduating in 1997, Bob joined Patersons Securities under his father's supervision. The late nineties were kind to stockbrokers: the tech boom brought retail investors flooding in, and Bob's phone rang constantly. He was good at the job in the way a friendly bartender is good at his — not through expertise but through the ability to make people feel that everything would be fine. He moved to Jim's smaller firm on St George's Terrace in 2002, and when Jim retired in 2005, Bob inherited the client book and rebranded: Harrington Capital. The mining boom was erupting. Iron ore was $60 a tonne and heading for $180. Bob's clients were making money because everyone was making money, and Bob mistook the tide for his own swimming.

The fund formalized in 2008 — a managed investment scheme, 2% management fee, 20% performance fee, invested across listed equities, private exploration companies, and eventually a few unlisted ventures that a friend of a friend brought to him. Bob never intended to steal. The Ponzi grew the way most Ponzis grow: a bad quarter, a redemption he couldn't meet, a quiet transfer from one account to cover another, the promise to himself that he'd fix it next month. The mining bust of 2012 turned a manageable lie into an unmanageable one. By 2015 the fund was structurally insolvent, running on new inflows and fabricated statements. Bob knew. He carried the knowledge the way a man carries a tumour he refuses to have scanned — with silence, and dread, and the magical thinking that not naming it keeps it small.

He married Jeannie Wallace in 2004, at St George's Cathedral in the city, reception at the Royal Freshwater Bay Yacht Club. He was twenty-eight; she was twenty-four. He loved her. This is the simple and terrible truth of Bob's life: he loved his wife, and she did not love him. He loved her beauty, her confidence, the way she walked into a room as though she owned it. He mistook her certainty for strength. Over twenty years of marriage, as her contempt crystallized and her cruelty became routine, his love did not die — it merely went underground, the way his music had gone underground, pushed into a sealed room where no one could mock it.

Physically, Bob is the kind of handsome that ages into blandness. Tall enough at six foot, sandy-haired going grey, a face that belongs on a bank advertisement. By his late forties he carries an extra ten kilos that sits on his waist and softens his jawline. He dresses well because Jeannie dresses him — RM Williams boots, tailored chinos, linen shirts from a shop in Claremont. He looks like what he is supposed to be: a successful Perth money man in comfortable middle age.

His psychology is defined by a single, catastrophic passivity. Bob does not act; he accommodates. He accommodated his father's expectations, his wife's demands, his clients' greed, and the fraud's escalation. He is not weak exactly — he has a core of genuine warmth and decency that survives everything — but he lacks the mechanism that converts feeling into action. Until the violin. When he picks it up again at forty-seven, something breaks open. The Chaconne becomes the first thing in his adult life he pursues for its own sake, with full commitment, knowing it is impossible and doing it anyway. The irony is that Bob becomes most fully himself in the final two years of his life, when everything external — his marriage, his fund, his reputation — is collapsing. He does not become a great violinist. He becomes a man who finally refuses to be what others need him to be.

His children know a version of him: the provider, the peacekeeper, the man who absorbs Jeannie's fury so they don't have to. Chase sees money; Shanel sees stability. Neither sees the artist. At the birthday party, when Bob plays the Chaconne on a violin that might be a Stradivarius, his children hear their father for the first time. It is the only inheritance that matters.

## 2. JEANNIE (Jean Margaret Harrington, nee Wallace)

Jeannie Wallace was born in August 1980, the second daughter of Geoff Wallace, a mining engineer who made his real money not in engineering but in the shares he accumulated in companies he consulted for during the 1990s boom, and Patricia Wallace (nee Dempster), whose family had owned land in Dalkeith since the 1940s. The Wallaces lived in a double-storey Georgian revival on Watkins Road, Dalkeith, with river views and a swimming pool that Patricia maintained at a temperature suitable for her constitutionally disappointing circulation. Geoff adored Jeannie. He called her "Princess," bought her a pony at seven, a convertible Golf at seventeen, and never once asked her to justify her existence. Patricia, by contrast, competed with both daughters in ways she would have denied under oath — commenting on their weight, comparing their skin, treating their social successes as reflections of her own genetic contribution and their failures as evidence of Geoff's side.

Jeannie entered PLC in Year 5 and understood the social architecture of the school within a fortnight. She was pretty — genuinely pretty, with dark hair, green eyes, and the kind of fine-boned face that photographs love — and she was clever in the specific way that social environments reward: she could read a room, identify the anxious and the ambitious, and position herself as the person whose approval mattered. By Year 8 she had a group of six girls who ate lunch together, attended each other's parties, and quietly destroyed anyone who threatened the hierarchy. Jeannie was not the loudest or the cruelest in this group, but she was the centre. She decided who was in and who was out, and she did it with a smile that made exclusion feel like your own failure.

She met Bob through the Scotch-PLC shuttle bus in Year 11. He was older, in Year 12, handsome, and — crucially — he looked at her the way her father looked at her: with uncomplicated adoration. She did not love him. She liked being loved. This is a distinction Jeannie has never made and never will, because making it would require a kind of self-knowledge she has spent her entire life avoiding.

After PLC, Jeannie enrolled in a Diploma of Interior Design at Curtin, which she completed without distinction or interest. She had no intellectual life and did not pretend to one. Her mind was sharp but entirely directed outward — toward surfaces, appearances, the management of perception. She could arrange a room, choose a fabric, style an outfit with real flair. These are genuine skills, and in another life — a life with less money and more necessity — they might have become a career. Instead they became hobbies funded by her husband.

She opened JEANNIE on Napoleon Street, Cottesloe, in 2009 — a fashion boutique that sold European labels at prices that made the stock hard to move. The shop was beautiful. The fitout alone cost $180,000, which Bob paid without complaint. It never turned a profit. In its best year it lost only $40,000, which Jeannie described as "basically breaking even." She employed two part-time girls, hosted champagne evenings for her friends, and treated the shop as a social venue that happened to have a cash register. It closed in 2017, which Jeannie attributed to "the retail downturn" and not to the fact that she opened a luxury boutique in a suburb where women already drove to Claremont or the city to shop.

The drinking began in her early thirties — a glass of Leeuwin Estate chardonnay at lunch that became two, then a bottle, then a quiet switch to sauvignon blanc because it was "lighter." By forty she was drinking every day, beginning at noon, maintaining a functional buzz through the afternoon that hardened into something glassier by evening. She was never falling-down drunk. She was never visibly impaired at school pickup or charity lunches. She was simply never entirely present, never entirely sober, never entirely there when her children spoke to her. The antidepressants started at thirty-eight — sertraline, prescribed by a GP in Subiaco she chose specifically because none of her friends went there. She told no one. The pills and the wine formed a chemical partnership that kept her level, or what she understood as level: a smooth, medicated numbness that she mistook for coping.

Her social media presence intensified through her forties. Instagram became the primary venue for the performance that had previously required physical audiences. She posted curated images of the house (a five-bedroom limestone on Marine Parade, Cottesloe, with ocean views that Bob had stretched to buy in 2010), the children (always in uniform or activewear, always smiling), the boat (the Riviera 48, named *Chardonnay*, which Jeannie had lobbied for and Bob had financed), holidays (Bali, the Maldives, Margaret River), and herself (always in natural light, always filtered, always with a caption that performed effortless abundance). She had 4,200 followers, mostly women she knew or women who knew women she knew, and she checked her likes with the desperate frequency of a gambler checking a slot machine.

Her cruelty toward Bob is not sadistic; it is defensive. She married a man she did not love because he offered the life she wanted, and the cognitive dissonance of this transaction has curdled over twenty years into contempt. If Bob is worthy of love, then her failure to love him is her fault. If Bob is pathetic — a mediocre man, a bland provider, a soft and passive disappointment — then her coldness is justified. She needs him to be small so she can live with herself. She dismisses his opinions, corrects his grammar in public, rolls her eyes when he speaks at dinner parties, and sleeps in a separate bedroom she calls "my retreat." She is not aware that this is cruelty. She experiences it as standards.

Her children receive a version of this. Chase, the son, she criticizes — his posture, his grades, his friends — because he reminds her of Bob. Shanel, the daughter, she cultivates as an ally, a protege, a miniature version of herself. She takes Shanel shopping, teaches her to dress, shows her how to hold a room. This is the closest Jeannie comes to love: the reproduction of herself in her daughter.

When Jeannie hears at the birthday party that Bob has been taking weekly violin lessons from an attractive woman, and that the fund may be in trouble, two separate terrors converge into a single act of destruction. The affair she could perhaps survive — she does not love Bob, but she owns him, and infidelity is a property crime. The fund collapse she cannot survive, because it means public humiliation, the loss of the house, the boat, the life, the Instagram. It means becoming a woman people pity. Pity is the one thing Jeannie cannot endure, because beneath the performance, beneath the wine and the pills and the filtered photographs, she knows — has always known — that there is nothing underneath. She smashes the violin because it represents everything she cannot be: genuine, vulnerable, and brave.

She is forty-five years old. Her face is still beautiful, maintained now by subtle cosmetic work — Botox, a little filler, the kind of intervention that wealthy Perth women undergo so routinely it has become invisible. She is thin in a way that requires effort and white wine. She dresses impeccably. She is, by any external measure, a successful woman. She is miserable, and she does not know why, and she will not ask.

## 3. CHASE (Chase Robert Harrington)

Chase Robert Harrington was born in March 2004 at St John of God Hospital, Subiaco, and named after the bank — Chase Manhattan, now JPMorgan Chase — because Jeannie thought it sounded "strong and modern" and Bob did not object. The name is a small monument to Jeannie's aspirations: not old money (that would be "James" or "Edward") but new money, American money, money that moved fast and sounded like it. Bob sometimes wonders if naming your son after a financial institution was a kind of curse, but he has never said this aloud.

Chase entered Scotch College in Year 5, as his father had before him. He was a bigger kid than Bob had been — taller, broader, with Jeannie's dark hair and a jawline that hardened early. He played football with more aggression than skill, made the First XVIII in Year 11 through physicality rather than talent, and was popular in the loud, expansive way of boys who take up space. He was not a bully. He did not need to be. His size, his parents' money, and his easy confidence — inherited, though he would not know it, from Bob's social fluency — placed him in the centre of things without effort. He threw parties. He organized weekends. He was the boy who said "My parents' boat — Rotto — Saturday" and twelve boys would appear at the Royal Freshwater Bay Yacht Club jetty with slabs of beer and no sunscreen.

The Riviera 48 became Chase's identity-engine from the age of fifteen. Bob taught him to handle the boat in the river first — slow circuits of Freshwater Bay, docking practice at the yacht club — and then, when Chase was sixteen, took him across to Rottnest for the first time. The crossing is eighteen kilometres of open ocean, and the swell off Cottesloe can be ugly, and Chase loved it: the throttle in his hand, the diesels growling, the bow lifting and slamming. He got his Recreational Skipper's Ticket at sixteen and began taking the boat to Rotto without his parents on weekends, mooring in Thomson Bay, ferrying friends in the tender to shore for pub sessions at the Rottnest Hotel, sleeping in the master cabin while his mates crashed in the other two. These weekends were the crucible of his social life — performative, excessive, and entirely about being seen as the alpha, the provider, the man with the boat.

His relationship with Jeannie is the defining wound. She criticized him relentlessly through adolescence — his school results (average), his table manners (adequate), his girlfriends (never good enough, which meant never from families Jeannie respected). He internalized her contempt not as a reflection of her damage but as a reflection of women generally. His mother was cold, controlling, and impossible to please; therefore women were cold, controlling, and impossible to please. This is not a philosophy he articulates. It is a posture — a slight hardness in how he speaks to girlfriends, a preference for relationships where he holds the power, a tendency to lose interest the moment a woman shows vulnerability. He has had three serious girlfriends by twenty-one, all attractive, all eventually bewildered by his emotional unavailability.

His relationship with Bob is more complex and more tender than either of them knows. Chase respects his father in the language he understands: Bob makes money, Bob provides, Bob bought the boat. He does not know his father as a person. They do not have conversations about feelings or fears or what they want from life. They talk about football, the boat's engines, and whether the market is up or down. But there is a warmth between them that survives the silence — a physical ease, a shared laugh, a loyalty that does not need to be spoken. Chase would defend his father. He just wouldn't know how to ask him if he was happy.

Chase completed Year 12 at Scotch in 2021 and enrolled in a Bachelor of Commerce at UWA — the same degree, at the same university, as his father. This was not a decision so much as an inheritance. He did not consider alternatives because no one presented any. He is in his fourth year now, coasting, attending enough lectures to pass, working part-time at a mate's property development firm in Subiaco. He assumes he will end up in finance because that is what Harrington men do, and the assumption sits on him like a suit that fits but isn't his.

Physically, at twenty-one, Chase is what Bob was at twenty-one but more so: six-one, broad-shouldered, with the tan and ease of a boy who has spent every summer on the water. He wears what Cottesloe boys wear — boardshorts, Patagonia fleeces, R.M. Williams boots when he goes out. He drinks too much on weekends and not at all during the week, which he considers discipline.

At the birthday party, when Bob picks up the violin and plays the Chaconne, something happens to Chase that he has no framework for. He has never heard his father make music. He has never seen his father do something that was not for money, or for Jeannie, or for the maintenance of the life they all inhabit. The sound of the violin — imperfect, straining, but searingly sincere — opens a crack in the architecture of Chase's understanding. His father is not just a stockbroker. His father is something else, something Chase cannot name, something that has been hidden from him for his entire life. He feels pride, and beneath the pride, a terrible sadness he does not yet understand: the sadness of realizing you have never really known the person you love.

After Bob's death, Chase will inherit not money — there will be none — but the question his father's life finally asked: *What are you, when you stop being what they expect?* Whether Chase answers it is the open thread of his story.

## 4. SHANEL (Shanel Patricia Harrington)

Shanel Patricia Harrington was born in November 2006, and her name is spelled S-H-A-N-E-L because Jeannie wanted Chanel but also wanted it to be "unique," which in Jeannie's lexicon means distinguishable from the merely expensive. The misspelling is a tiny act of aspiration that reveals more than Jeannie intended: the desire for luxury combined with the fear of being seen to desire it too plainly. Bob suggested "Charlotte." Jeannie did not discuss it further.

She entered PLC in Year 5, arriving each morning in the green tartan uniform with the self-possession of a girl who has been told since birth that she is beautiful and that beauty is currency. She is beautiful — Jeannie's bone structure, Bob's warmer colouring, a face that at nineteen has not yet decided whether it will harden into her mother's angular composure or soften into something more generous. She is slender, careful about food (Jeannie has been commenting on portion sizes since Shanel was twelve), and dresses with a precision that looks effortless but is not. Her Instagram, which she started at fourteen, has 2,800 followers and is curated with an instinct she learned from watching her mother: every image is a statement about who she is, or rather, who she wants to be seen as.

At PLC she occupies the same social position Jeannie once held — the quiet centre of a group, the girl whose approval sets the weather. She is not cruel in the way Jeannie was cruel. She is subtler, more modern. She includes and excludes through the language of social media: who she tags, whose stories she shares, whose comments she likes. The mechanism is digital, but the architecture is her mother's — control through attention, punishment through withdrawal. She learned it the way children learn language: by immersion, not instruction.

Her relationship with Jeannie is the most complicated in the family. Jeannie treats Shanel as an extension of herself — dresses her, advises her, takes her to appointments and lunches and boutiques as though they are friends rather than mother and daughter. Shanel receives this attention as love because she has no other model for it. She does not yet understand that Jeannie's investment in her is narcissistic rather than maternal — that Jeannie is not raising a daughter but curating a reflection. The moments when Shanel diverges from the script — when she expresses an opinion Jeannie hasn't sanctioned, or shows interest in something Jeannie considers beneath them — are met with a withdrawal of warmth so swift and total that Shanel has learned to self-censor before she speaks. She does this unconsciously now. She does not know she is doing it.

Her relationship with Bob is the most tender and the most neglected thread in the family. Bob adores Shanel with a softness he cannot show Chase (because Jeannie has defined softness as weakness in men) and cannot show Jeannie (because Jeannie receives adoration as her due and returns nothing). He buys Shanel books she does not read. He asks about her classes and listens to the answers. He is the parent who attends her school plays and remembers her friends' names. Shanel takes this for granted in the way children take air for granted — it is simply there, constant and invisible, and she will not understand its value until it is gone.

Academically, Shanel is brighter than either parent. She achieved an ATAR of 91, well above the family average, and enrolled in a Bachelor of Arts at UWA, majoring in Communications and Media Studies — a choice Jeannie approved because it sounded creative without being impractical, and because UWA is where Western suburbs girls go. Shanel chose it because she genuinely doesn't know what she wants, and the degree is broad enough to postpone the question. She is nineteen, in her second year, living at home, driving a white Fiat 500 that was an eighteenth-birthday gift.

What Shanel does not know about herself — what the novel holds open as possibility — is that she has her father's buried artistic sensibility without his passivity. There are moments: a song that makes her cry and she doesn't know why; a photograph she takes that is genuinely beautiful and she deletes it because it doesn't match her feed's aesthetic; a conversation with a tutor about Virginia Woolf that lights something up and she changes the subject because none of her friends would care. She is standing at the edge of a life that could go either way — toward her mother's hollow performance or toward something real — and she does not yet know the edge is there.

Physically, she holds herself like Jeannie: straight-backed, composed, aware of being looked at. But there is a softness in her face that Jeannie lost long ago — a capacity for surprise, for openness, for being caught off guard. She has not yet learned to armor herself completely.

At the birthday party, when Bob plays the Chaconne, Shanel feels something she cannot categorize. It is not pride exactly, and it is not sadness. It is recognition — a dim, pre-verbal sense that the sound coming from her father's violin is the same thing as the photograph she deleted, the song that made her cry, the Woolf passage that lit her up. It is the part of herself she has been editing out. She does not cry. She does not speak. She stands very still and listens, and something shifts in her that will take years to understand.

After Bob's death, Shanel is the family member most likely to be changed by what she witnessed. She is young enough, and the armour is thin enough, and the seed her father planted — that a person can be more than their performance — may, in time, grow into something Jeannie could never give her: permission to be real.

## 5. THE LUTHIER (Franz Kaltenbrunner)

Franz Kaltenbrunner was born in 1947 in Absam, a village of three thousand souls in the Inn Valley of Tyrol, six kilometres east of Hall in Tirol and fifteen from Innsbruck. Absam has made violins since the seventeenth century. Jakob Stainer, whom many consider the greatest luthier outside Cremona, was born there in 1617 and is buried in the parish churchyard. The village carries this heritage lightly — there is a small museum, a street named for Stainer, and two or three workshops that still build instruments — but for Franz, growing up in the shadow of Stainer's legacy was not heritage but vocation. His father, Karl, repaired instruments. His grandfather, also Franz, had built them. The workshop on Stainerstrasse, with its smell of spruce shavings and hide glue and varnish, was the first room he remembers.

He apprenticed under his father from the age of fourteen, building his first complete violin at seventeen — a crude instrument with a thick neck and uneven f-holes that he keeps on a shelf in the workshop to this day as a reminder of how far he has come and how much he still does not know. He studied briefly at the Mittenwald School of Violin Making across the German border, thirty kilometres west, but returned to Absam after two years because he missed the workshop and because, he says with a shrug that contains no false modesty, "I learned more from my father." He took over the business when Karl died in 1981, and has run it since — building perhaps eight to ten instruments a year, repairing dozens more, buying and selling old violins from estates and attics and the occasional dealer who doesn't know what he has.

Franz is now seventy-eight. He is small, five foot six, with hands that are disproportionately large and preternaturally steady — the hands of a man who has held a chisel against maple for sixty years. His face is deeply lined, brown from walking in the mountains, with pale blue eyes that have the unsettling clarity of someone who spends most of his time looking at things very closely. His hair is white and thin. He wears the same clothes every day: a checked flannel shirt, dark trousers, a leather apron in the workshop. He lives alone — his wife, Hilde, died in 2011; their two children, a son in Vienna and a daughter in Zurich, visit at Christmas and call on Sundays. He is not lonely. He has the workshop, and the mountains, and the instruments, and a cat named Stainer who sleeps on the varnishing bench.

His spiritual life is quiet and unorthodox. He attends Mass at the Absam parish church most Sundays, not from conviction but from habit and a respect for ritual that is, in its own way, a form of faith. He does not believe in the catechism. He believes in something he cannot name — a quality he encounters in the finest instruments, a resonance that exceeds the sum of wood and varnish and geometry, something that the old makers put into their violins that cannot be explained by acoustics alone. He calls it *Seele* — soul — and he is not being metaphorical. He believes that a great violin contains something of its maker's spirit, and that this spirit can be heard by those with ears to hear it.

When Bob Harrington walks into his shop in the summer of 2023, Franz sees a man in expensive casual clothes carrying a case of medium-quality wine and a look of desperate hope. Bob has come to Absam because he read about Stainer online, and because he is on a holiday in Austria that Jeannie organized (Innsbruck, Salzburg, Vienna — culture as social performance), and because he has been playing the violin again for six months and wants a better instrument. His current violin is a Chinese factory instrument worth $800. He has not told Jeannie why he has detoured to this village.

Franz shows him several instruments. Bob plays each one — badly, with the halting intonation of a returning amateur, but with a touch that Franz recognizes. It is the touch of a person who listens. Most amateurs play at the violin, forcing sound from it. Bob plays into it, waiting for the sound to come back to him. Franz has seen this perhaps a dozen times in sixty years. It has nothing to do with technique and everything to do with the relationship between the player and the instrument.

The violin in the back room — the one Franz has been studying for three years, the one with the Stainer-style label that might be authentic and the wood that might be seventeenth-century spruce and the varnish that has that particular craquelure he has seen only on instruments of the highest provenance — this violin Franz has been unable to bring himself to send to a dendrochronology laboratory or to a Stainer expert, because knowing its value would turn it from an instrument into a commodity. He has insured it for $30,000, which is what it is worth if it is a good Tyrolean copy, and which is a thousandth of what it is worth if it is what he suspects.

He lets Bob play it. Bob's hands shake. The sound that comes out — even from Bob's imperfect bowing — fills the small workshop with a warmth and complexity that makes both men fall silent for a moment afterward. Bob's eyes are wet. Franz sees a man who is drowning in a life that is not his own, reaching for something real.

He sells it for ten thousand euros. Bob tries to pay more. Franz refuses. "It needs to be played," he says. "You will play it." This is not a commercial decision. It is not charity, exactly. It is something closer to what a priest does when he absolves a stranger — an act of grace extended to someone who has not earned it and cannot repay it, offered because the giver recognizes a suffering he cannot otherwise reach. Franz does not know Bob's name will appear in Australian newspapers after his death. He does not know the violin will be smashed by a woman he will never meet. He knows only that this man needs this instrument the way a man dying of thirst needs water, and that giving it to him is the right thing to do.

After Bob's death, when Franz learns what happened — through an email from the violin teacher, who found his address on the invoice — he sits in the workshop for a long time. He is sad. He is also, in a way he cannot fully explain, glad. The violin was played. The man found his voice. These things cannot be taken back, not even by death, not even by the woman who broke the instrument. The music happened. It was real. For Franz, that is enough.

## 6. THE VIOLIN TEACHER (Katarina Nemcova)

Katarina Nemcova was born in 1985 in Brno, the second city of the Czech Republic, a place of faded Austro-Hungarian grandeur and fierce musical tradition. Her father, Jiri, was a sound engineer at Czech Television's Brno studios. Her mother, Vera, taught piano at the Brno Conservatory's preparatory school and supplemented the family income with private lessons given in the front room of their apartment on Cejl, a long street that runs through the city's working-class north. Katarina grew up in that front room, falling asleep to the sound of children murdering Czerny exercises through the thin wall of her bedroom, and she swore she would never be a music teacher. She would be a soloist. She would play in the Rudolfinum in Prague, in the Musikverein in Vienna. She would be the thing her mother had wanted to be and failed.

She entered the Brno Conservatory at eleven, studying violin under Professor Emil Kovar, a demanding man with a legendary temper and the conviction that any student not practicing four hours a day was wasting his time and God's gift. Katarina practiced five hours a day. She was talented — genuinely, objectively talented, with a tone that Kovar described as "naturally dark, like good Czech beer," and a technique that by sixteen was cleaner than most university students'. She won the regional round of the Czech National Violin Competition at seventeen. She reached the national finals and placed fourth, which in the Czech Republic — a country that produces violinists the way Australia produces swimmers — was respectable but not sufficient. Fourth place does not lead to a concert career. Fourth place leads to a master's degree and a teaching position, which is exactly where Katarina ended up.

She completed her Masters at the Janacek Academy of Performing Arts in Brno, specializing in performance, and spent two years freelancing — a few concerts with the Brno Philharmonic, some chamber music, a summer festival in Litomysl, recording sessions. The work was irregular, the pay was poor, and the competition was merciless. By twenty-five she understood what Kovar had known but never said: she was very good, but very good is not enough. The soloists who fill concert halls possess something beyond technique and musicality — a charisma, a recklessness, a willingness to risk everything in performance that Katarina, for all her discipline, did not have. She played beautifully and carefully, and carefully is death on the concert stage.

In 2012, at twenty-seven, she came to Perth. The reasons were partly romantic — a Czech-Australian boyfriend she'd met at a festival, a relationship that lasted eighteen months after her arrival and ended without drama — and partly pragmatic. Australia needed music teachers. Perth, specifically, needed them. The mining boom had created a generation of wealthy families who wanted their children to play instruments, and the supply of qualified teachers had not kept pace. Katarina's qualifications were impeccable, her English was good (she'd studied it since childhood, as most educated Czechs do), and within six months she had a full teaching schedule: twenty-five students a week, mostly children, mostly in the western suburbs, mostly being driven to her rented studio in Subiaco by mothers in SUVs who wanted their daughters to play well enough for the school orchestra and not a note more.

She is good at teaching. This surprised her and still faintly disappoints her, because being good at teaching confirms that she was right to stop pursuing performance, which confirms that she was not quite good enough, which is the wound she carries. She is patient, precise, and genuinely invested in her students' progress. She does not shout, as Kovar shouted. She does not humiliate. She has a way of playing a phrase and then asking the student to describe what they heard, which teaches listening before it teaches playing, and which reveals, to those paying attention, her own deep relationship with music — not as a skill to be mastered but as a language to be spoken.

Physically, Katarina is striking rather than conventionally beautiful. She is tall — five-nine — with the lean, long-limbed build of a woman who walks everywhere and eats when she remembers to. Her hair is dark brown, worn long, usually pulled back when she teaches. Her face is angular, with high cheekbones and dark eyes that have a quality of serious attention that some people find intimidating and others find magnetic. She dresses simply — dark jeans, a good blouse, flat shoes — and wears no makeup except occasional lipstick. She looks like what she is: a European woman of intelligence and discipline who has not organized her life around being looked at.

She is thirty-nine when Bob Harrington contacts her in early 2024. He has found her through a web search — "violin teacher Perth adults" — and his email is endearingly awkward: he played as a boy, he wants to start again, he knows he's too old, he doesn't want to waste her time. She almost declines. Adult returners are usually a chore — men going through midlife crises who want to play "Meditation" from Thais at their daughter's wedding and give up after three months. But something in Bob's email — its honesty, its lack of pretension — makes her say yes.

Their first lesson is in Bob's soundproofed office on St George's Terrace, an arrangement Bob insisted on and that Katarina found unusual until she understood: he is hiding this from his wife. He plays for her — scales, a simple Suzuki piece — and she hears what Mrs. Albrecht heard thirty years earlier and what Franz Kaltenbrunner heard in Absam: a musical ear embedded in an undertrained body. His intonation is rough. His bow hold is wrong. His left hand is tense. But he listens to the sound he makes with an intensity she rarely sees in amateurs, and when she corrects his bowing and the tone opens up, his face transforms in a way that is almost painful to witness — the face of a man who has been starving and is finally being fed.

When he tells her, in their third lesson, that he wants to learn the Bach Chaconne, she laughs. She does not mean to be cruel. The Chaconne is one of the most demanding pieces in the violin repertoire — fifteen minutes of unaccompanied music that requires not just technique but philosophical stamina, a capacity to sustain and develop a single musical idea through sixty-four variations. Professional soloists prepare it for months. The idea that a Grade 4 returner could play it is, objectively, absurd. She tells him this, gently. He nods and says, "I know. But I want to try." And something in the way he says it — not with the bluster of a man who thinks he can do anything, but with the quiet resolve of a man who knows this is the only thing worth doing — makes her agree.

Over the next eighteen months, she watches Bob do something she has never seen an amateur do: improve through sheer love. He practices two hours a day, every day, in the soundproofed office. His technique remains limited — he will never play the Chaconne as a professional would play it. But his musicality deepens week by week, and by the end, he plays certain passages with a sincerity and emotional directness that Katarina, who has heard Perlman and Hahn and Kavakos play this piece, finds genuinely moving. He plays it like a man who means every note. This is rare. Most professionals play the Chaconne beautifully. Bob plays it truly.

She develops for him a feeling she has no word for in English or Czech. It is not romantic love — she is not attracted to Bob, though she can see he is attractive, and she is aware that Jeannie suspects otherwise. It is not friendship, exactly — they do not socialize, do not share meals, do not know each other's daily lives. It is the bond that forms between two people who share a profound experience of music — who have both, in different ways, organized their lives around the sound a wooden box makes when horsehair crosses a string. She understands what the Chaconne means to Bob in a way no one else in his world can, because she understands what music means when it is not a hobby or a performance but a necessity.

She records the birthday performance on her phone. She does this instinctively, without planning, because she knows it is significant — not because it is technically accomplished but because it is the moment Bob becomes who he was meant to be, in front of the people who never knew him. The recording is shaky, the audio imperfect. It is the only record of Robert James Harrington playing the violin.

After his death — after the fund's collapse, the scandal, the noise — Katarina is the keeper of what mattered. She has the recording. She has the memory of a man who, in the last two years of his life, found the courage to be real. She does not speak to journalists. She does not attend the memorial. She sits in her studio in Subiaco, plays the Chaconne herself — all of it, the full fifteen minutes — and weeps, not for Bob specifically but for the terrible waste of a life spent being someone else, and for the grace of those final months when he stopped.

She continues teaching. Her students do not know that the quiet Czech woman correcting their bow holds once witnessed something extraordinary in a soundproofed office on St George's Terrace. She does not tell them. Some things are not for telling. They are for keeping.
